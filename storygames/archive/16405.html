    \n Hey Peter, thanks for starting the thread.

    We decided to go open source for lots of reasons. Speaking to my own interest in it, I think that there are some outmoded ideas about publication and DRM and the way creative people feel a need to "protect" themselves from piracy. We've been all over giving the game away from the get go, albeit in a limited form with a direct feedback loop (The Adventurer's Guild) that we benefited from. One thing to remember is that the final, polished product isn't "free". We won't be posting the full PDF with all the art up on our site for download. That said, if people want the "source code" of DW, they can always get it and lay out the pieces they need, make micro-versions, mods, hacks, etc etc and make Dungeon World their own as a result of the XML data. Philosophically, it made sense to us to make that part of it CC because, honestly, we're hackers ourselves and we want to encourage more of it. Sage and I are both in the software industry professionally and have been working on DW like it was a piece of software all the way through (tracking bugs, releasing patches and beta versions and all that on the regular). DW was developed in an open space, why not leave the source code that way? A big part of it is just that we're really excited and want to see what happens.

    On the fiscal side, yeah, of course we'd like to see some money come back for the hours we've sunk into the game. We know that the PDF will end up on demonoid, we know that people have friends of friends and people will share legit copies with their group. We understand that it's not something we wanted to try and "protect" ourselves against. What we have to offer that people will want to pay for is the polish of the final product, you know? Cool art, the layout, awesome bonuses that you'll see in our kickstarter. PDFs are awesome, but speaking anecdotally, it seems to me that they're still a peripheral media engine. People still like the print artifact. The shelf candy. We think that's what people will pay for.

    DW is engineered for accessibility, you know? I remember being 14 and being too fucking poor to afford D&D books and it being incredibly shitty. You want to play DW? We're going to make sure you damn well get to. You want a .txt file of the rules? Go for it. You want to pirate a PDF? Well, we'd obviously prefer you didn't but we're not going to be unrealistic and hell, maybe you'll share it with someone else who can afford it. You want a print version, that option is going to be there, too, and it's going to have all the bells and whistles, etc. We didn't make DW to make a profit. We made it because we love D&D and we love RPGs and we wanted to make a game that other people would want to play and have a good time doing it. Money would be cool, but what kind of insane person says "I am going to quit my job and live off a single release of a role playing game?" Well, okay, maybe someone does, but that's not us.

    I bet Sage has a lot more to say, too, but that's my five cents. We're both totally open to answering any questions people might have about the game and our design / release process.

    \n No John, it doesn't work like that. What you're thinking of are infectious licenses that force derivative products to abide by the same license. This is a common set-up, especially in the software markets, but the Creative Commons license discussed here is not one of those. Pretty much the entire gist of the license is that you can reuse the material provided that you attribute properly. The new work does not need to be CC licensed. I know that the prior art is misleading - I, for example, licensed the third edition of TSoY with the same licenses that Clinton did, even as I didn't have to.

    (There is a CC variant that is infectious in this sense, but I understand that that's not what's going on here. Can't remember offhand what it's called.)

    OGL, on the other hand, places no restrictions on the content except the curious limitation that you cannot attribute without permission. (I'm sure that one makes sense in the misty mind of some lawyer somewhere.) However, if you got permission to attribute the Dungeon World material you use (or perhaps assumed that the requirement to attribute caused by the license is also a tacit permission), there is no conflict between the two licenses that I can see.

    \n I don't agree that anyone other than the copyright holders can license the Dungeon World text under the Open Game License.

    When a work is licensed under the OGL, that work can be legally copied and adapted if a third party complies with the OGL. Therefore, John Walton (or any person other than Adam and Sage) can only license Dungeon World under the OGL if every right reserved by Adam and Sage under CC BY is also reserved under the OGL. However, there are a few areas where the OGL is more liberal than CC BY; where the rights reserved by Adam and Sage under CC BY could be abrogated by someone using Dungeon World under the OGL. The ones I could find are:

    * Adaptations must be clearly labelled as such (CC BY 3.0 Universal 3(b)).
    * All notices that refer to the CC BY licence must be kept intact (4(a))
    * Works under CC BY cannot be distributed under DRM (4(a))
    * When works are adapted or collected, the attribution for the CC BY licensed work must be listed in the credits and at least as prominent as the credits for the other contributing authors (4(b))

    None of these are required for the OGL, so CC BY does not entitle anyone other than Adam and Sage to license DW under the OGL.

    Of course, since Adam and Sage are both talking in this thread and don't seem to have a problem with the OGL, a happy solution seems obvious!

    (PS I've said this privately but I'll say it again: I was really pleased by this development, and by Mist-Robed Gate taking this route earlier.)

    \n I think that there are two separate issues here: whether derivative material must come under the same licence as the original work (what I would call share-alike) and whether part of a work can be licensed differently to other parts.

    Material that is derivative of someone else's Open Game Content must itself be Open Game Content. However, Eero is quite correct in saying that non-derivative material that is included alongside Open Game Content does not have to be Open Game Content (having said that, the same is sometimes true for works licensed under Creative Commons licences. For example, if I remember correctly John Harper's AGON has a cover that is licensed CC BY-SA [because it is based on a CC BY-SA photo] but the rest of the book is all rights reserved).

    \n Hey Johnstone,

    I might not have been clear about this in my previous posts: there is no conflict (as far as I can see) if someone licenses a DW supplement partially CC BY (the parts derived from DW) and partially OGL (the parts derived from FATE, let's say).

    But what you appeared to suggest was a third party licensing parts derived from DW under the OGL. As soon as content is licensed under the OGL, it can be used exclusively according to the terms of the OGL - and the terms of CC BY can be ignored.

    But if that were to happen in this case, then some of CC BY's more restrictive clauses would not apply (because they are not present in the OGL). So people could get rights that Adam and Sage haven't given up - which would be a violation of CC BY.

    (In terms of whether 'effective technological measures' is a synonym for DRM, it's sometimes used by Creative Commons themselves in that way and sometimes not. Of course, the licences stand on their own; once they've been written down, Creative Commons' opinion is no more legally valuable than yours or mine).

    Anyway, I think we agree now! Keep content from the two sources quarantined, and you shouldn't run into problems.

      \n IANAL, but "brand identity" as a quasi-legal artifact is well distinct from the legal artifact which is "copyright".

      Copyright applies (automatically, by the law) to the letter of any text you write, the likeness of any image you paint, and so on. Copyright laws makes it so that people who'd like to, for example, print what you wrote, or make edits to it and circulate the modified version, are mandated by the law to ask for your explicit permission before they can do anything like that (and if they don't ask, you're in the position to sue).
      Licenses - such as CC BY, CC BY-SA, OGL and so on - are a legal tool you employ (leveraging, in fact, your copyright) to, basically, preemptively grant virtually anybody (as long as they comply with the terms of the license) permission to do certain license-specified things with your work \u2014 so that they don't have to ask you. By the way: in case people wish they could do things which are outside the scope of the license you employed, or when they just aren't sure they can understand it, then the default behavior of asking you is still available to them.

      As for "brand identity", "trademarks" and so on\u2026 I understand those are some different thing, but I don't believe I understand them well enough (legally or ethically) to school anybody on the topic, sorry.

      \nIt's not that difficult to write license texts. If that's what you want to do, just write up a clean and unambiguous licensing permission stating thus. Get some help and have a few people with their heads on their shoulders (lawyers maybe, if you believe in such) check out that you've written what you thought you did. It's shallow thinking to rely on the "officialness" of famous licensing models like OGL or Creative Commons to make them somehow more binding or serious than if you just write your own license. The only advantage those licenses have (aside from immediate recognizability) is that it'd take perhaps a bit more balls to flout the license, but I don't think that that's a very decisive advantage; the weakest part of your legal arsenal is your own willingness to enforce your rights, and that's something that licensing terms do nothing to improve. (Also obviously those licenses are written with less wormholes than an average bear's attempt at legalese, but because your ability to actually utilize your rights under a licensing scheme depends on your ability to understand the license, I don't think that anybody who understands what CC licenses do would do too badly writing their own.)

      Also, match your legal securities to the scale of your project. Unless you think that your IP is worth millions of dollars, you'll probably be more than fine enough with a layman-written license. And if you think that your IP is worth so large amounts of money that somebody will try to utilize some legal trickery to steal it, then you're presumably willing to pay for a corresponding amount of legal expertise in helping you write up your licensing terms. It seems to me in this regard that the system works reasonably well, and there really isn't a significant legal security hole at any scale of operation. These modern boilerplate public licenses are just an additional tool in the toolbox, and a great way to increase awareness about open culture; they do not enable you to do anything entirely different from what you've always been able to do.

      If you feel really uncertain about how to go about something like this, just write an informal public statement of intent and invite anybody who wants to use your work to contact you so you can talk out the details. Then you can license your work on a case by case basis, which is much easier, as you can just decide for each project whether it's something you want to allow. Saying "I'm willing to license this work for free to further rpg-related projects, so let me know if you'd like to do something" is not a
      license in itself, but I think it makes it pretty clear who should bother to contact you about it and who shouldn't.
