\n What\u2019s up with rpg and rewards? I was always awkward about this question, in an unconscious kind of way, but recent threads in SG brought it to the front of my mind (don't ask me why).

I see, and read about, mechanics to encourage players to adopt a specified behavior, mostly through rewards. I see mechanics that rewards players that \u201cplay the game (correctly)\u201d. Excuse my French, but c\u2019est quoi cette merde ?. Or, in other word, WTF? While I can (more or less) understand these things from a gamist perspective, what are their relations to narr-games / story-games?

Most of these rewards are of the \u201cif the player does this, the character gains that\u201d kind. Write a compelling backstory, gain XP. Describe a cool something, gain a bonus on your action/conflict test. Invent a part of the setting, gain spotlight time.

I believe I know where these rewards come from. They\u2019re an evolu-corruption of DD\u2019s character progression system, aka leveling. But, imo, leveling is not a reward (to the player). It is a part of the play experience. Leveling is fun in itself, it is a goal of the game. It\u2019s also changing the game\u2019s elements to prevent boredom from repetition. You learn to use your character\u2019s powers and defeat threats-of-his-level. When you\u2019ve done enough, things change before (hopefully) they become dull. It carried very well in video games, with or without character evolution.

When you play other games with your friends, you do not expect the game to reward you for \u201cappropriate behavior\u201d. You play to have fun (or for some complicated social reason). Everybody is expected to respect the spirit of the game (as it is locally, defined \u2014 expectations). If I play team sport, I do not get a game advantage for playing with my team (instead of alone), nor for doing my best to oppose the adversaries which are, in all probability, friends.

So why should any rpg give rewards for respecting the spirit of the game? I can find only two explanations:
  • The game designer simply followed the rewards tradition.
  • What is rewarded is a less fun or even unfun part of the game and the reward is there to mitigate that. (-> Is this part necessary? Can\u2019t it be made funnier instead? Can\u2019t it stay optional?)
\nIt is some years since I used any xp or other reward in any of the game I run. We do character progression by consensus, considering setting and story coherences and genre expectations. Most love it. Recently, I tried to re-introduce xp rewards in my games, to \u201cpromote good role-playing/storytelling\u201d (eg: I state a mood for a scene; players who contribute to the mood gain xp). Xp can be used to modify characters or act on the setting or on the story. It feels wrong, just wrong, plain wrong. Why should a player that is a good mood contributor have is character improve (that destroy story/setting coherence and spoil my fun)? Or have more rights than another to alter the story? (On the contrary, I believe that he is probably so good because the game suits him more than it suits the others, so should we not bring the game closer to the other players?) Or why limit somebody who has great ideas for the game because he did not earn the necessary rewards-resources?

The only virtue I find to these rewards is that they may add a gamist dimension to a game. However, trying to add these gamisms on a non-gamist game blights the game for purely-non-gamist players while simultaneously not making the game sufficiently interesting for gamist-only players. Not a winning combination.

I am very interested in your views on this matter.

TIA

Ajax

I think others are more comfortable with having rewards in the forefront of their vision and assumptions about social convention understood, but not in the center focus.

    Myself, I'm comfortable with those words - reward, currency - because I when I see them in play, they're ways of working within a social context, of sharing social messages around the table. When "rewards" work with my game play, they're either used to enhance or slightly modify the existing social patterns.

    On example for me, Fan Mail in PTA (using a mechanic to give players positive feedback). When I've played with it successfully, there's already a strong social understanding that positive feedback is what we all do, anyway. The Fan Mail availability is a reminder to keep it up, and provides another way to represent that message.

    Another spin on rewards: sometimes they're not about rewarding the player but encouraging certain world outcomes. "I get a +20 on my hacking roles when I activate into my flaw." This is saying we're in a fictional world where, if a player delves into their personal flaw, it's more likely they'll achieve some other kind of success at the same time.

    I think that something like "rewards" isn't antithetical to your style of play. But, I want to say that I definitely see what you saying. There are times where mechanical reward or impact has kinda crashed the social context in a group. (There are a few games where us freeforming it would have turned out better than that specific group following its mechanized rewards.) When I make rewards, it's clear that we're using the rewards of the system to find interesting outcomes and share information and get feedback (from each other and from the system), but we're ultimately at some level of consensus beyond that about what we're wanting exactly to do.

    \n Some forms of mechanical 'rewards' aren't really rewards at all; they are compensations. Examples are Fate Points earned in Spirit of the Century for Compells, or Artha earned in Burning Wheel for acting out Traits or Instincts even when they aren't in your character's best interest. They directly work against the traditional problem of "roleplaying is suboptimal tactics", which often leads to roleplaying (or using your character for maximum story fun) reducing your in-game power/protagonism.

    It's worth noting, too, that a lot of people have weird hybrid approaches to gaming that fit poorly into Big Model CAs. E.g. Burning Wheel - check out Luke Crane's recent post (here), or me trying to make sense of the BWHQ playing style (here).

    That aside - I, personally, like mechanical rewards because they're Fun! Winning things for doing things is fun, and adds to anything. Accumulating useful points is fun and exciting. This digs in deep with some instinctive "get reward" mechanism.

    This thread drives home for me, more than anything else I've seen, that any classification of reasons for gaming (such as the Big Model CAs) has it's work cut out for it. I can't really justify why I like mechanical rewards, any more than I can really justify why I like mechanised social conflict - at root, it's instinctive and I have no idea *why* like it. To me, it's just obviously something worth doing.

    (Although I *suspect* that both, at heart, are appealing because I want play a game and tell a story, rather than pretend to be a dwarf (I can act ok, but I can't character-immerse in any interesting sense).)

    This phenomena of inexplicable desire makes a lot of "justify mechanic X" threads quite frustrating - the reasons for having or not having a whole class of mechanics isn't, fundamentally, rational. The desire for (or hatred of) fundamental things like mechanical rewards or social mechanics has its roots in a deep-seated, fundamentally irrational (a-rational?), unreasoned "Hell yeah!".

    Or maybe a guttural roar with no real words in it.

And positive reinforcement (rewards) works the best and is the most pleasing, so it's no surprise that games, which are meant to be pleasing, revolve largely around rewarding players for following certain actions.

    \n David... how's that "unfun?" The reward systems reward the players, not the characters. Oh, in some RPGs the interests of the character and player are conflated. But in the games we write, we usually stay away from rewarding the character, and instead reward the player (in fact the detractors of our style of play often say that this dissociation is the most problematic part of play). This is how you can get systems where it's fun to have your character fail. Which is the sole key to most of these games.
    \n
    \nDissociaton of the player's interests from the character's interests, making the player the author of the character, and not responsible for his failures, is the primary shift between other forms and narrativism promoting games. As an example, in Universalis, we award the player Coins for creating "complications" that may end up hurting the characters that we ourselves made earlier in the scene. The player is rewarded for creating drama, instead of being rewarded for character success. And drama requires occasional failure.

    \n Let me try this zen approach:
    \n
    \nPeople here, generally, find "freeform" to be a valid approach to play. Freeform being defined as play sans any mechanics.
    \n
    \nThere are two "problems" with freeform and design:
    \n1. It's already been done. There's nothing new to design here.
    \n2. Some people don't find it nearly as interesting (myself for one).
    \n
    \n
    \nThe point is that... if you don't like mechanics that promote story, then there's a great way to create story that relies solely on social rewards (or none at all, other than the fun of play, in some cases). The rules we write are intended for an audience that demands that the rules do something more than say, "Go do it, be nice." That's far from 100% of the audience. In fact, I'd posit that, given some definitions of freeform play (collaborative fanfic, for instance), that TTRPG play is a small minority. TTRPG play where characterization and player-driven plot creation is central, is a minority inside of a minority.
    \n
    \nSo if you don't find yourself to be part of our tiny minority... well that's hardly unusual, is it? It may well be that you don't like this sort of game. And no explanation by ourselves for why we like it is going to change your mind.
    \n
    \nBut that's not the same as us saying that we don't like these games. Right? That is, you don't expect that, if you don't like these games, that nobody can. Right?
    \n
    \n
    \nThe condescending part, by the way, comes from comments like saying that you don't feel like you're being treated as an adult by rules like this. The subtext of this comment (intentional or otherwise) is something like, "You people don't trust your players to play well, and so put rules in place to prevent players from misbehaving. You're a fearful bunch."
    \n
    \nHopefully you don't feel this way, really. And you can see that the reason we play with the sorts of mechanics we do is no different than the reasons that people play basketball with foul rules. Because it's a part of the game as designed, and the result is fun.
    \n
    \nThe reward systems we put in place exist to try to make more of a good thing. To focus play. That is, we expect that players will do these things, even without the rewards. We include them so that an even greater proportion of the activities done in play will be this sort.
    \n
    \nBecause, yes, we are trying to promote one way to play.
    \n
    \nYeah, some people think this is controversial. But, you know what? Any RPG can be played "any way" by anyone playing it. That won't make the rules exceptionally fun for any particular way to play, however. That is, you can play Rolemaster as a player-theme-producing game, in theory. It's just that you'll be wading through the rules in order to get your goal.
    \n

    \n Here's a couple of examples off the top of my head how rewards have been with us in games for a very long time.

    In checkers when you reach the other side of the board you get castled, then you can move your piece much more freely. This enhances the forward momentum of the game already.

    In chess, manipulating the ways you can put the king into check reduces your opponent's move options, which rewards you by making an outcome easier to predict. Another example is the point value given to pieces. Giving up a piece to the opponent can reward you because it puts their piece in a specific location that you predict ahead of time. Since pawns are less valuable than knights, then by sacrificing a pawn in order to take a knight rewards you in the long term game.

    Games are driven by rewards. Without rewards, there's no reason to play.\n
    Posted By: Ajax AldwyneSo, rewarding the loosers surely changed the nature of the game but did not destroyed the game. Most people were not fundamentally playing to win, nor to be rewarded, but to play. The \u201cto win\u201d part was how you played. Most people assumed that rewarding the winner was what would generate the best play experience but, to my tastes and for my group, it was wrong.

    So here is an example of a reward I like. It rewards the player for the player behavior, it generates and sustains a desirable behavior and it is central to the system: it was not added as an afterthought or as another part and you can\u2019t remove it.
    \nBut you can remove it and it was added as an afterthought and is not at all central. The PlayStation and Tekken's game design was central. Needing two or more people to play was central. But who played and in what order wasn't central. You tried playing one way, and then changed it. If you stuck with the old way, it would have continued to be less enjoyable. So you see, the rules weren't static or central, but in terms of your enjoyment and having a cohesively fun experience, those rules were core. This is how rewards work in the better story games. It gets put in, play tested, and tweaked until the desired results. Whether that's to "reward the winner" or "give the losers a chance to improve" is up to the designer. But they end up being central in the game through proven and consistent fun by lots of people.

    But rewards are always towards the player and in many games, they are a result of player behavior. Even ones that go to the character, like experience, are more for the player to increase their character's abilities. In Dogs in the Vineyard, you get rewarded for facing odds that cause you to fail. Spirit of the Century rewards you for putting your character into specific situations that you define as an important part of your character. HeroQuest rewards you for pursuing actions that your character excels at. Shadow of Yesterday rewards you for pursuing built in character aspects or permanently changing them. These are not added in as an afterthough, and can't be removed without a significant reduction in at least the fun, and more often the core function of the game itself.

    So after all this, and especially after your Tekken example, where you clearly saw the rules improve the game, I don't see how you are generally irritated with them.

      Posted By: Ajax AldwyneTo me, none of these examples are rewards. I interpret these as risk vs. gain. But I guess it depends on your type of play. To take the fight against the monster example, the player who decide there is a monster to defeatmightplace a reward. In classic D&D style, most of the time the monster has a treasure that no in-setting reason nor meta-setting story reason justify. It is a bonus for defeating the monster and I probably would consider it a reward. The treasure doesn\u2019t even need to exist: the probability that there is a treasure, through hope, is an incentive and a reward in itself. Now, if the monster is there for a story or an exploration reason, there probably is no reward.

      Reward, to me and in this conversation, demands that there is intent of enticing the players to a given behavior. Gain is a consequence. Foreknown gain is announced consequence. Reward is a foreknown gain placed there with a motive (other than coherence).
      \nI think the problem here is that you\xb4re seeing believable-in-fiction rewards differently than those outside the fiction, and calling the first "consequences"; this being expressed in your "other than coherence" there.
      In my opinion, those two words aren\xb4t opposite: a positive consequence is a reward, or should i say works as a reward. Consider how those consequences push the person making the choices towards them: if our spear maker would\xb4ve perfected his cave-painting skill instead, he would\xb4ve made great paintings but he would\xb4ve had a hard time next winter. So he "chose" to make a better spear... The only difference here is that we don\xb4t see nature as being "designed" ( actually, some still do, and it\xb4s ok for them ); but that is not important. What matters is: if i have a decision to make, and some choices give better payouts than others; i have a reward system there. Game designers, and then game players, make this explicit; that\xb4s the only difference.

      Consider the "consequence" of getting the treasure after slaying the dragon. The key factor to see here is that both dragon and treasure ( and slayers ) are imaginary entities, created by us the players usually following a game rule or genre tropes or whatever. In the fiction, the dragon could\xb4ve been a bunny, or an illusion or an urban legend; the treasure could\xb4ve been discount cupons, rotten cookies, nothing, a picasso... Though most of those would instantly brake the contract of "hey, we\xb4re playing fantasy, not cartoonish!", they are options anyway. So someone made the choice of creating a system of "dragon+treasure" and therefore making the adversity and the gain; they weren\xb4t "there" because there\xb4s no objective "there" to begin with; the "there" is the product of our creative input alone. This someone ( say, the GM ) is then, at the moment, creating a reward for going in and killing the beast. The fact that gets explained in-game as consequence doesn\xb4t erase the fact that it is a reward; and neither does the degree of awareness of it being a reward by its creator: the GM might have set the treasure there "because it\xb4s logical" but it will ( again ) work as a reward.

      I think you only see "intent of entincing the players to a given behaviour" when it\xb4s detached from the fiction; but when "the force grows strong in you for your heroic actions" and when "you learn to be a better fighter out of your success in the fight" you\xb4re also being rewarded; it\xb4s just that it\xb4s coincident with some setting fact.
