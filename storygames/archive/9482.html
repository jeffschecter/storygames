    \n Great job, looking forward to my copy landing on my door step.

    Matthijs, H\xe5ken, perhaps you can summarize the process leading to the compilation. How where the 17 games selected? How did you organise translation, proof reading, editing & layout? What about licensing - and how did you manage to actually complete the project?
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
  • \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n\nMatthijs \n\n\n
    \n
    \n\nJune 2009 \n edited June 2009
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n Okay, here goes, short version.
    \n
    \nI've always been a freak for publishing, and when I saw how many games were being produced by competitions and collaboration in Norway (mostly on rollespill.net), I wanted to get them out there so the world could see them, because I thought there was so much good material. I posted a thread and sent out an e-mail and asked whether people wanted to be part of a compilation, and a lot of people said yes. I asked who wanted to be part of the editorial staff; four people, myself included, volunteered. Two dropped out fairly quickly, for personal reasons. We also asked for a layout volunteer - I think we pretty much asked H\xe5ken directly. He's done some amazing work in the past.
    \n
    \nSome people volunteered for proofreading, and we posted to ask for more; we got 4 or 5 people altogether, I think. Authors were asked to translate their games, which went surprisingly smoothly - I'd expected some resistance, since the idea from the start was that this would be done by the editors; we had to change that once we saw how much work was needed. We decided right from the start not to go into any kind of subjective judgement of quality or worthiness; we know all these people personally, and trying to select games based on fuzzy criteria was just not worth it. It's a small crowd, and we didn't want anyone to feel left out or anything. The criteria were that the games had to be finished and playtested. Also, no authors could have more than two games in the book. A few games were left out because they hadn't been played; some were dropped when the authors didn't have the time to finish them. One author passed away just before we started the book; we asked her family for permission to print her game, and received it.
    \n
    \nAt this stage, we had a lot of momentum; the plan was to publish in February 2008, I think. We read through the games, edited them, sent them back to the authors. When we got the texts back from the authors, the proofreaders read through them and sent them to the editors. Then they went to layout.
    \n
    \nWe (Even and I) had envisioned a fast process, where the main goal was just to get the book published. H\xe5ken is much more of a perfectionist, and there was some frustration on both sides, I think, in the process that followed. All of a sudden, we were thinking about author texts, splash pages with info about each game, colophons. We tried to get pictures of the authors, but that just didn't work out. Time passed, interspersed with discussion of details that (from my viewpoint) slowed everything down, and (from a design viewpoint) were necessary to make everything just right. There was a trial print, and Even and I proofread that pretty thoroughly over the course of a weekend, still trying to get things done in time for the deadlines that were constantly being moved.
    \n
    \nMore time passed. I stopped getting replies to e-mails and phone calls. After several months of banging my head against the wall, I dropped the project out of frustration. Anders Nygaard became the new guardian of the project, although I don't know exactly what he did. Four months later, I got an e-mail out of the blue saying that the book was available on Lulu. It had an additional game in it that I'd never read. I ordered five copies right away, just in case the file should disappear the next day.
    \n
    \nIn the end, I'm extremely happy with the book. I do wish we'd communicated better, and had more of a shared vision - of the book and the work processes - right from the start. But what matters now, after all that is over, is that H\xe5ken did an excellent job of designing the book and making sure all the details are just right. The authors put in a lot of work translating and re-writing their games. The whole book is a labor of love, and it shows.
    \n
    \nWe're all individuals, with our quirks and hang-ups and different ideas of how things should be done. Still, we manage to cooperate and create beauty. If that's not N\xf8rwegian Style, I don't know what is.
