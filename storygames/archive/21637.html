    \n Very interesting result. I find myself reading plot summaries of movies I want to watch to make sure they don't suck.

    Dramatic irony is already a well known technique in story telling, this seems to prove you can use it to improve any story. After all, historically people listen to the same stories and songs over and over.

    I think this knowledge could vastly improve the experience of most railroaded adventures. Someone should run an experiment where the major plot points are spoiled to the players and see if they still enjoy the adventure. At least the stupid struggle to "keep players on the right track" can finally end and without the GM needing to state clearly that "the plot is this way" the players will gladly follow it looking forward to the upcoming "twist".

    \n Surely it must depend on the nature of the story as well as the nature of the reader. Even if it is impossible to say for sure counterfactually, I can think of cases from my own experience where I think spoilers ruined a story and other cases where the spoilers didn't harm it much at all.

    Example A: In Game of Thrones, I was spoiled to the effect that certain characters would die in certain books. But I still enjoyed the books plenty well, perhaps more than without spoilers. Like Shakespearian tragedy, you can appreciate the dramatic irony as you see the character's mistakes draw them inexorably towards their fates.

    Example B: Lovecraft horror stories. The stories are written to very carefully control how much you know and realize at each point, making you fear that something terrible is coming without being certain what it is. I found the most famous stories like Call of Cthulhu pretty boring because I already knew the gist of what was up from popular culture allusions. But stories where I went in blind were thrilling to read.
