\n Maybe this is a project for 2009:

A bunch of people put their heads together and thrash out a loose setting. Maybe it's the sprawling generic fantasy continent or maybe it's 1903 but with robots. Something broad, easy to understand, and flexible enough to make a little corner of it your own. Probably fantasy, but hopefully interesting and unique shared fantasy.

We get Tony Dowler to make a map. We get somebody to set up a Web-based depository with a blog for news/AP reports, a place to dump files, and maybe a wiki. Custom Abulafia generators. Whatever makes sense.

Then we carve out territory. The idea would be to use it as a mutual playground, sort of like a system-less RPGA thing (that sounds horrible but you get the idea). People and game groups could come and go.

So here in NC, my friends and I grab a chunk of land and rock out using the Solar System. Somebody in Denmark hacks AGON and plays that in another corner. There's some D&D4 action on the virtual tabletop and Harper's running Savage Worlds out west. Jonathan Walton does something unsettling with his SG Boston peeps. And when I visit Boston, I can bring a dude with a shared history to play in another part of the world. I'll have to stat him for JWalt's weirdo home brew system, but we both know the world and its highs and lows. The game he runs is informed by events in my game. The crazy villain who shows up in my AP report becomes a shared object world-wide. When we cross boundaries or reincorporate, we can ping the original author and coordinate.

I know this isn't remotely ground-breaking, but it seems like it could be really fun. It is a Little Idea.

    \n Jason, the main takeaway: When you set up a collective creative project, there's a spectrum from too little frontloading to too much frontloading.

    It's easy to do too little,
    I'll tell you a story about too much,
    and it's easy to catapult from too little to too much.

    Some do too little and ask "Help us decide what we're doing, and how we'll go about it, and then we'll all work together to do it." Nobody wants to step on each others toes so if anything comes of it it's very weaksauce and disjointed - like bad freeform roleplaying.

    I was trying to avoid that problem, so I added a bunch of stuff, and I had a vision for the project and a style guide right out of the gate. Mike Holmes pointed out some really, really good points and I made that style guide even tighter. Some people were put off by that, but I carried on. Others were not sure how to do it the "right way" so I made some examples.

    I attracted really awesome stuff from the few people who grokked the Great Hundred, but couldn't hit critical mass because this awesomeness became a barrier to entry. Also, since the pages were hard enough to write, the pattern was "write a couple of pages, make a couple of comments, and come back later to see how the project is doing."

    Now this wasn't a complete failure, but it meant that for the project to continue I needed to constantly advocate for it (to both existing and current contributors). More importantly, I could get more contributors but not more advocates. That's a tale of too much frontloading.

    However, MOST collective worldbuilding projects (I've seen many) die by catapulting from too little to too much. There's too little, someone tries to be a leader and jumpstart the project by just putting stuff out there, and it's a turnoff or it's suddenly too much.

    \n There are two ways to handle big crossover play. I will give you my wisdom regarding shared tabletop gaming in a moment, but I'll handle the "what do we do when two games are about totally different things but they're in a shared world?" The answer comes from fanfic. There are two possibilities:

    1. Diligently and carefully look at themes and elements and see where they mesh and conflict. Identify which conflicts have narrative juice and discard the rest. Pump up the areas where they mesh even if they are minor and not very important. (Did you know that Lurch once leaned out of a window Batman was climbing past? Well, figure out why he was in Gotham City and now you've got a real story!)

    2. Get drunk and throw everything together in a big ridiculous mess and any time there's a conflict that's problematic, take a shot and change the scene.

    Both approaches work perfectly.

    \n The more I read about this, the more certain I am this could use a Feudal authority system.

    That is, there is a "King", a supreme authority, whose main concern is to oversee and divide his territory. To keep it consistent.

    Then he has his "Feudal Lords", beings who have a very broad and loosely defined authority, but who can order and organize things in their territories. And (this is the interesting part) they too have the power to divide and grant parts of their territories to others.

    So we have this small "Landlords" with some powers on their own. They have a lot of freedom and interactions, and are pretty much able to do as they please. But in the end they still have someone to answer to. And if things are not working (or that someone is unavailable) they can escalate and go to the "King".

    Note that this might seem like a common business hierarchy at first look. It is not. The feudal metaphor is precisely to stress how subjective, flexible and non-committing those charges are.

    Now, the thing is that the "Feudal Kingdom" doesn't need to be (exclusively) the "physical world". You could get a "feudal lord" about religion and myths, one about politics among the kingdoms, one for multi-regional societies... You get my drift.

    The thing is that people are actually working under a "mercenary banner". That is, I can work with anyone I feel like. I want to include some religious stuff about the world in my campaign/land/group/city/whatever? Then I simply announce it to the corresponding feudal. He can't forbid me from doing so (unless it completely breaks the spirit of the Campaign, as defined by those pillars people were talking about), but he can advise me about it, and he'll be the common source of knowledge (and organization) about that particular field.

    I am also thinking this can be adapted to be a rotating work. That is, after a while I can decide whether I want to resume my work as a lord to my domain, or change it to something else (with my King's approval, by the way). Even the King's post could be a rotating (and willing) one. Or even a meta-democracy of sorts.

    \n Perhaps you kick off the world-building not with a contest, but a collaborative lexicon game. However, an abbreviated version of the game, requiring only 8 entries per player, broken down as a telephone pad (one entry per player for a-b-c, then one for d-e-f, et cetera). Should take a little more than two weeks. Play on a wiki.

    Everyone write an encyclopedia entry, one due every 2 or 3 days. Roughly 500 words. Maximum: as much as you want.

    1. On the first turn, each player writes either an 'A', 'B', or 'C' entry, from the point of view of an amateur, armchair historian within the setting itself (feel free to develop the personality of said scholar) . The entry should be roughly 500 words minimum, and within it you embed two citations to an entry that hasn't been written yet. At the end of the entry, you sign your name, and list the two links to other "phantom" citations -- their names exist, but their content will get filled in only on the appropriate turn. No letter-group can have more entries than the number of players, either, so all citations made on the first turn have to start with non-A, B, or C letters.

    2. On the second turn, you write another entry. However:
    * Your entry must 'fill in' one of the phantom entries someone else already made.
    * You need to make THREE citations at the end. One must be a reference back to an already-written entry, and two must create unwritten entries that don't already exist. ((It's fine to also link to other phantom entries that someone else already made, but only as a 'bonus'.)) These links should be to things that in some way intersect the entry you just wrote.

    3. It's a sin to link only to your own work -- you can link back to an entry you've written, but only if you've ALREADY back-linked to someone else's entry, so that the link to your entry is a 'bonus'. (This forces everyone to intertwingle their entries, so that everybody depends on -- and is reading -- everyone else's stuff.)
    * Again: no fair writing the entry for a phantom citation that YOU created in the first place, unless there's NO other alternative.

    4. On the fourth (of eight) turns, you still make three citations, but unlike previous turns, TWO must be a reference back to already-written entries, and ONE must create a new phantom citation to be written.

    5. On the fifth, sixth, seventh, and eighth turns, you make two or more back-links, but unlike previous turns, ALL must be a reference to already-written entries or already-created-phantoms, because by this point you have enough phantom entries to carry through to the end.

    6. Despite the fact that your peers are self-important, narrow-minded dunderheads, they are honest scholars. No matter how strained their interpretations are, their FACTS are accurate as historical research can make them. So if you cite an entry, you have to treat its factual content as true. (Though you can argue vociferously with the interpretation and introduce new facts that shade the interpretation.)

      \n I think The Big Campaign is a great idea, and here's how I could see it working, kind of like Slobbovia. Slobbovia was a diplomacy variant published in a zine in the 70's and 80's that focused more on the story and characterization than game mechanics. Particularly since each group will have their own mechanics, we'll be spending most of our time on story and characterization.
      \n
      \nI know the original suggestion was to create a map first, but I think it would be better if each person had a square section of the world that they fleshed out on their own. Other people involved can use anyone's square, but aren't allowed to make changes to it without approval.
      \n
      \nIn order to make the map work, I think we should use Geomorphs. Let's say the world starts out as a 4 by 4 square grid (giving us 16 squares total). Each square inside would have at most 4 sides that are shared. These sides would be divided up by 2 (making 8 total shared sections), and the person working on the square would chose different types of geography, like water, tundra, mountain, grassland, etc for each shared side section. Anyone chosing an adjacent square would have to make sure the border that they shared matched their neighbors. As we get more people involved in the shared world, we can add more sections.
      \n
      \nAll we would really need is a wiki that people where people would add actual play and other information about their sections and a map that someone would manage as new people created sections of the world. I would recommend that people only be allowed to have one section to work on, at least at first. In would be fun to have a blog/newsletter where people could post news from the Big Campaign, but that might be something further down the road as the Big Campaign begins to grow.
      \n
      \nAll I think we would need to agree is the time period/technology level, but even that could vary throughout the world (as it does in our real one).
      \n
      \nI love this idea, and think it could be a lot of fun.

      \n So I tried to do this for about 2 years with the World of Darkness, a game that in its newest incarnation is specifically designed for and supports this kind of thing. Here was my experience:

      * The number one organizational problem is not getting people interested, as you can see from the thread, tons of people love to do this. The main problem is obtaining game organizers. In World of Darkness this is normally GMs. These people exist in every game group, normally one per group, but the main issue is that they are normally already playing their own games. Basically what you are asking is not "play this game with us!", but "stop what you're doing, organize and play a different game with us".

      * Once things are going, the main game issue is inconsistent portrayal of the characters and setting while pursuing the themes and tones of interest to the individual games. Like, it's not so much that the Prince is one way in this game and the Prince is another way in another game, because that's just a character with different facets. It's more that the events and decisions of one game express one group's vision of the setting and the other group doesn't share it. Things like tone (is this a splatterfest with yuks, or a gloomy rose-smelling poetry slam?) become extremely important to how you make decisions.

      \n Looks like this has died again, so let me have one last go at jump starting it.

      The problem seems to be the organization that needs to be done before the thing can get off the ground. So here is a version where most of the organization gets done in play as it goes along. I've deliberately chosen rules that restrict campaign making to those who play in it rather than people with time on their hands, so that the campaign doesn't grow out of proportion to the play it is designed to foster.

      Below are a set of draft guidelines. I\u2019m going to post them in a separate thread with a link back to this one. That thread is for AP only, so if you want to comment come back here, or--if this takes off--we\u2019ll make a separate thread for commentary. Eventually there may be a need for a wiki to organize information, but for now let's leave it to see what happens.

      The aim is to set up some basic ground rules for building the campaign together based on AP and then just to let it happen. If you have any comments or feedback, let me know and I\u2019ll edit them into the post at the top of the AP thread.

      Here are my suggested guidelines:

      How to play:

      1. Choose a game

      The campaign comes from actual play, so grab a game and start playing. You can use any system you\u2019d like, but to make coordination easier this is assumed to be a fantasy campaign, so pick something that will fit.

      2. Start small

      Build only what you need for you first session: small scale setting, situation, characters and maps. Leave large-scale politics, geography, and cosmology to develop as time goes on.

      3. Post Actual Play reports

      Post descriptions of your play in this thread. Everything you contribute should come from play. The other participants will only have your AP to go on, so if you want to contribute something to the setting mention it in your report. Aim for AP about the length of a Bite Sized AP post, maybe two short paragraphs: one with an outline of the events in play, another with a list of important setting detail established during play.

      4. Build on things others establish

      Take the setting details from other people\u2019s AP and incorporate it into you own play. As much as possible use \u201cYes, and \u2026\u201d as the template for your contributions. When making up information about places that are far away or long ago, make up stories or rumours that might be true or might be false. Let your players or other groups find out which in play.

      5. Build incrementally

      Don\u2019t make large additions or changes to the setting at one go. Introduce new material a bit at a time and give other groups a chance to contribute to how things turn out. No single session should radically change the setting as a whole. Keep even dramatic events relatively local so other groups have the option of including their effects in their part of the campaign or not. Big changes should only come when multiple groups take up and build on the same material.

      6. Have fun!

      Make material that excites you because it will also excite others. This is a shared setting, but that doesn\u2019t mean that you have to reign in your imagination. Go!
