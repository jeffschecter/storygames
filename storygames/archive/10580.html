\n So this is ok right, no controversy here:

I buy a PDF, and print it out at my house, on my printer.

So is this ok?:

I buy a PDF and have lulu.com print it out and ship it to me, because it's cheaper than buying the printed game itself.

Something about the second case feels despicable, but logically it shouldn't be suspect, at least if the first case is true. Right?

    Posted By: AndyThey won't have a problem with you printing a copy at Lulu and shipping a copy to yourself. But how about buying one copy of a PDF, and printing out 4 copies at Lulu/other for the members of your gaming group? Law aside (no interest in this discussion), what do you think of the moral implication of that action?
    I'd say that's immoral, because you are in effect making yourself a (not-for-profit) publisher, using Lulu as your print provider, but not sending any remunerations to the IP owner. This all assumes, of course, that it's not a free PDF, in which case you can usually do all that... but not SELL copies to your friends, only give away or sell at cost of printing. Seems pretty straight-forward to me; what's your source of wafflage? The notion that "well, we COULD all play with one book, but it's EASIER if everyone gets a copy" logic (which I find unsound off the bat: the ease of use is a convenience for which you pay when you buy four copies for your friends)?

    That said, let's dial it up another notch: you take the PDF and GIMP and make "cheat sheets" to print out for your friends to use as reference... and which, basically, have all the book's contents that one might need after one reading (i.e. the game is easy to recall procedurally, you just need occasional look-ups of stats or values). That will have the same net effect as printing out copies for everyone, but at a fraction of the cost and waste (of paper that is never referenced; e.g. fictional examples). In order to "do right" by the game maker, you'd have to "do wrong" by the trees of the world. (Let's assume the game maker doesn't sell a "GM Screen" or similar cheat sheets product.)

    Posted By: Jonathan WaltonI would say that if you're buying the cheaper PDF, knowing that you're going to eventually print it, that's straight-up dubious in my book. I wag my finger at you.
    \nWhy? Generally speaking, the reason the PDF is cheaper is that you're paying the author for content and layout only, while assuming the monetary burden of printing yourself... Not because the author is selling the PDFs at a loss, or giving them away as a preview of the print book.

    If an author/publisher releases a PDF of a book as an option for potential buyers, customers are supposed to choose whichever option is best for them - usually going for the overall cheaper option. If lived in the USA, or otherwise I had a shop close to me where they carry the RPGs I want, or if shipping was fast and free, I'd be happy to buy printed books. Instead, I buy the PDFs and print me, which probably costs me a little more than a US customer would pay for a print book (after you account for ink, paper, power, and time spent binding the pages together), but less than buying a physical book and having it shipped from overseas. Sure, I also end up with a less attractive item in my hands (and not very durable, so you have to account for printing a replacement copy at times), but I can satiate my thirst for reading the content almost immediately as opposite to waiting one month ore more (plus the very concrete possibility of having a damaged book delivered, or having to pay an extra for the Italian customs, or the package actually getting lost).
    If I could just walk to a shop in my neighborhood and get a print copy of IaWA or Grey Ranks for 20 bucks, instead of downloading the same for 10 bucks and printing it myself, I'd happily go with the former.

    The case in question, where the PDF sells for $5 and the book for $50, is weird. The numbers seem to indicate that the author extimated the cover price of the book as being 1/10 content, 9/10 physical item. Maybe the book has superb production values, thus by just printing out the PDF you get an inferior product? I dunno: color artwork, hardcover, high paper quality...

    \nInteresting question.

    Without looking it up, I'm pretty sure that anything you upload to print on Lulu has to be your personal and intellectual property. A PDF you purchased doesn't give you exclusive rights to that property, you can access the material (and if it's specified) print it out on your personal printer, but not reproduce it as if you are the original author of the content. Which is what you are doing when you upload it to Lulu.

    It's the same as photocopying copyrighted material, which isn't legal (it's actually an extreme example of this).

    Digital files are pushing copyright laws around in all sorts of new ways.

    \n Ok, here is how I understand it, and having it vetted by one IP lawyer I know, he agrees.
    \n
    \nPDF formatting came about as an evolution of adobe postscript, which was a means of transmitting "neutral" file to printers specifically for the intent of ripping the file for printing. Of course, this is now the digital era and digital printers don't need to rip files or create printing plates in order to print something. Our printer can tackle CYMK and RGB files equally well. But the evolution of the PDF, with its embedded fonts and all, remains a neutral and "standard" way to transmit a file for printing.
    \n
    \nEqually important, the majority of publishers selling content on PDF have some expectation that the items will be printed by the end user. This is especially true of games, where charts, important rules or whole books will be printed for reference in game use. Plus lets not forget PDF game tiles, 3d card stock build-it-yourself kits, strategy bard and wargame maps and counters, PDF card games, etc. PDF is a file format intended for print and the majority of PDF game releases are intended for print. Heck, some publishers even bundle ink friendly versions of their PDFs specifically to save their customers money in printing the items.
    \n
    \nNow, that said, it is copyright protected material. The owner, by industry standard, has an implied right to print their own PDF for their own personal use. This is no way extends their right to distribute said goods to any 3rd party. So printing it and giving it to your friends becomes a no no.
    \n
    \nNow, it gets stickier with regards to rights of transfer of physical goods. Something you typically always have the right to do. So technically, if you bought a PDF, you have a right to use it. If you give your legally purchased PDF to a friend, it would be considered duplication and distribution, which you don't have a right to do, unless perhaps if in giving it to them, you deleted all copies of the thing yourself. That _MIGHT_ still be ok under traditional rights of transfer, but I am unaware of any case precident clarifying that issue. Likewise, if you print your PDF, then give it to someone else, so long as you delete the PDF itself and don't keep any printed copies to boot, then that in theory is ok too under your right of transfer. In theory.
    \n
    \nOn the issue of having a third party printer print it. In you have the right to print it, you have a right to have a 3rd party print it for you. This, of course, runs into the issue of distribution. You must obviously give a copy of the file to said 3rd party in order for them to print it. Additionally, that third party, having no rights to the copyright also has no rights to make any alterations or ammendments to it and can only print those portions you tell them to. Ala, you can tell them to print only certain pages, only the front cover, print it in color or B&W, but you can't ask them to edit the text, change or alter any graphics, etc.
    \n
    \nSince we do print and bind PDFs as a professional service here in our store, this is how we approach it.
    \n
    \n1) You must show evidence of proper ownership or right to duplicate. This can be you being the owner of the copyright, you showing us proof of purchase, etc. For proof of purchase, we require any of a reciept, each page being properly watermarked by the seller and that matching the name of the would be print customer giving us the PDF, or showing proof of the PDF being freely distributed by the appropriate rights holder. On a couple occasions we've had folks come in and tell us they got permission from the publisher directly to duplicate and even make alternations to the original PDF and before we could do the job, we have e-mailed or called the publisher to get confirmation of that.
    \n
    \n2) You will sign a document declaring both your right to duplicate and clarify its intended usage. Right to duplicate does not equal right to sell and/or distribute. And even though if they have the right to duplicate it, we should be clear to print it, we want no part of printing it if they plan to then go on ti illegally sell or distribute it.
    \n
    \n3) Once we have the PDF, we check the first few pages to make sure the PDF did not come with some sort of restrictive license. i,e, declaring that the purchaser/owner of the PDF does not have duplication rights, can only duplicate it on saturdays, can only duplicate it in a domicile you own and only at a rate of one printed page per week, etc. Theory says, the owner of the copyright can clarify what duplication and/or distribution rights the purchaser of the PDF has and can apply any limits they so wish, excepting the usual (age, race, gender, etc).
    \n
    \n4) Once printed, we delete the file. Obviously in order to print it, we must have access to it. This is the one real grey area, since them giving it to us could in theory be considered distribution. But if it is simply for us to perform a service for duplication that they have a right to have done, then the distributed copy is deleted, then the arguement is that is was not distributed (since there was no intent to transfer ownership). This too is an area where there seems to be no case precident yet. We have proceeded on the principle that a right to duplicate and a right to have that duplication outsourced is no right at all if technical limitations make it so the 3rd party can not access the document necessary to provide that service and within that conflict, if reasonable action is taken to protect the IP holder in the doing of the service for which the owner has a right to utilize, then that should prove acceptable under the law. Logic doesn't always win court cases, but when encountered by a zone undefined by law, its a good place to plant yourself on the issue.
    \n
    \nOk, so that all being said. Any publisher here that doesn't want the customers who purchase their PDFs to have a right to duplicate, to be on the safe side, on the copyright page of that document, I would clarify any restriction on the right to duplicate that the customer will have. I would make sure that such a restriction is legal and I would make sure that said restriction is made known to the purchaser before they purchase. That would be the "safe" place to be on the issue, should you want to implement any kind of restrictive use license on your PDFs. At least until such a time any "defaults" are clarified under the law, you have a right to remove any potential ambiguity on rights to your IPs.

      Posted By: JaroslavThe fact that my PDF reader software has "Print" built into the file menu seems to contradict that, doesn't it? I've certainly seen software that doesn't have a "Print" option built in, so it seems like printing is an explicit function of PDF reader software to me.
      And yet one can set Distiller settings so that the file, in fact, can not be printed (easily, from within Acrobat). So that would tell me that if printing is not forbidden with a password, then the creator/provider doesn't mind.

      Hmmm... but if such a provider is ignorant of this functionality, does that give one tacit permission to print? (Obviously, express legal verbiage in the document that forbids printing would be a lgel protection; I'm asking, basically, about the obligation to use securing measures.)\n
      Posted By: bevedog(A) to ''circumvent a technological measure'' means to descramble a scrambled work, to decrypt an encrypted work, or otherwise to avoid, bypass, remove, deactivate, or impair a technological measure, without the authority of the copyright owner; and
      (B) a technological measure ''effectively controls access to a work'' if the measure, in the ordinary course of its operation, requires the application of information, or a process or a treatment, with the authority of the copyright owner, to gain access to the work.
      And these are only violated in a PDF if you crack the Read Password or Interaction Password(s) (print, copy, image copy, etc).

      \n How open is PDF's open standard, though? If the PDF format is freely available to everyone, everywhere, then as these things go you might as well consider it a plain ol' ASCII file.
