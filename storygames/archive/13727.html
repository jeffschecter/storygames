\n I had a weird dream last night. I was with friends, playing a roleplaying game. It was a zombie apocalypse type situation. When my turn came around, the GM asked me what sort of zombies I liked best. "George Romero zombies" I replied, and he set up a scene with shambling old Romero zombies. Then it was another player's turn, and he asked the same question, and the player replied "28 Days Later rage zombies", and that is what he got.

But this wasn't the GM tailoring his adventure to the tastes of his players, making a world full of different zombies - we shared a distinct, coherent universe that only had one type of zombie. But that type was filtered through the lens of player preference. I woke up going "huh".

As far as I know we don't do this, or when we do it accidentally it is considered a failure worthy of retcon. Why is that?

    \n Maybe because conversation is a kind of shared understanding, or an agreement on a set of standards, and an active disruption of that understanding, unless it's cumulative, breaches something important. Like, today I was talking to my girlfriend about the indefinite and definite article and I noticed how much agreement there needs to be between parties before articles change from isolated to shared experience. I'll explain that now, and then push the other point afterwards.

    For example, you and your mum are walking down the street, and she starts a story. Look at these two quotes: "So, this morning I kissed a horse." "So, this morning I kissed the horse." In one context, your mum does not own a horse. Or, alternatively, in one context your mum recognises that you do not understand that she does own a horse. Whether we talk about something generally or specifically is not only determined by whether we are talking about something generic or specific, but also by whether the person we are talking to understands this also. The use of articles seems to be a way to negotiate a shared understanding of a _________.

    Like an establishing shot.

    So, maybe that's partly why the confusion of a shared world space is, for folks like me, a conversational taboo.

    \n This makes me think of something that a guy who DMed for me once or twice in high school did. He would kind of "filter" each characters perceptions, like so:

    DM: You enter the dungeon room. You see Kyle's character! Kyle, describe your character.

    Kyle: I'm a dwarf with a bearskin cloak and a big axe. I'm ready, in case you make any sudden moves, but I'm not openly hostile.

    DM: Ok, so you see a small figure all wrapped in up on some kind of furs with a nasty-looking axe. He glares at you suspiciously as you enter. What's your character look like again?

    Me: I'm half-elven, but you can only see it in the tips of my ears and green eyes. I'm in serviceable mail, nothing too conspicuous, with a brown cloak and a bow over my back. My sword is drawn, obviously, but I stop when I see Kyle's guy.

    DM: Kyle, you see a tall man, sword in hand, step into the room as if expecting a fight.

    Then we would roleplay out our meeting, and so on. It was interesting because he always took players descriptions, which tended to be very matter-of-fact, and made them more ambiguous, and then tossed the ball back to us to run with. Because we were friends we always played out our meetings to be friendly in the end, because we had to get the party together, after all - but there were always opportunities to "misunderstand" the situation.

    So I guess this is somewhat like Eppy's comment, but a less situation specific.

    \n My suspicion is the majority of what we imagine in an RPG isn't shared at all. But the minority that is shared, is enough to keep it all together.

    A GM may say:

    1. "city"
    2. "a city like NYC"
    3. or never even say "city" but imply it with "you hear honking from cars stuck in traffic, see tourists jay walking, walk past the shadow of the tallest building you've ever seen, smell sweat and sewers, and feel the street rumbling below as crowded subways rush by."

    And it's very unlikely that everyone will actually imagine NYC. Even the people who actually live in NYC! There are a billion details unspecified. Mostly superficial but even seemingly important setting facts like time, season, day, and weather are glossed over. Often "where am I loosely", "why am I here", "what will help me get what I want" preoccupies many people's head space and we abstract everything else, ignore it, or fill in our own, often unshared, details.

    TV even does this. Vancouver often acts as a stand in for major locations all over the world from Hong Kong to NYC to the North Pole. We get just enough details for just enough time to communicate the minimal essentials and then move on. Often people don't even remember most of the characters' names in the show they are watching BUT it can still be 100% emotionally engaging and satisfying... moments you will never forget but you focus on the bits you care about and abstract the rest.

    I minored in Semiotics in college so I tend to over think this. And although my studies are likely a bit outdated now, from what I remember, even our physical input organs abstract information. Our eyes see a combination of color and b&w dots and then our brains blur it all together to form a 100% color image. We ignore superfluous details all the time (just think about how much advertising information gets thrown in our faces non stop, we have to shut out mass amounts of information just to keep focused). And even when we absorb details, often our mind alters that information to serves its own purpose. Many people's memories aren't completely trust worthy. People will sometimes remember what they want to and liberally, often without intending it, rewrite history in their own heads to fit their own preconceptions and agendas. Even how we rationalize information is driven by our emotions, not the facts.

    When I GM, I work under the idea that 90% of what I'm imagining isn't shared by anyone else and vice versa. And it doesn't have to, for the game to be successful. So I try my best not to input my vision into people's heads but work with theirs, even if everyone is contradicting each other. I will try to describe around the details they are imagining without contradicting them. And when those contradictions happen, I use it as an opportunity to add twists. You thought the zombie was slow, and you thought it was fast... you're both right and here's why... and I introduce a twist to smooth it over.

    \n I played in a game once where the GM intentionally shaded his descriptions of things to fit our character's preconceived notions and prejudices -- all without telling us, of course. It was a superhero setting with an overwhelming organization of high-powered entities that 'governed' superhumans so the rest of the world didn't have to. It's leadership was descended from the Greek Gods, and had an appropriately imperious 'style' of leadership. Newly-discovered superhumans were given to the organization to be taught to use their powers...

    One character's experience of this was described to him as 'English Boarding School' style hazing and benign neglect.
    Another character, with a rebellious nature, had her time with the organization described like Cinderella's treatment by her step-sisters; constant brutal labor punctuated by random, senseless punishment.

    My character encountered a group of runaway teen supers living on the streets of NYC, hiding out from the authorities. I perceived them as sort of 'junior X-men', protecting street people and rescuing other teens with powers from the organization.
    When the character with the 'English Boarding School' view of that organization encountered the same group of teen supers, he saw them as a predatory group of hyper-gangbangers terrorizing the neighborhood and recruiting super-criminals.

    These differing perceptions of the same phenomena were delivered to us via emails or in one-on-one gaming outside of the whole-group game sessions, for the most part. The in-game 'reality' wasn't fungible or mutable, but the GM's intention was to push the conflicts and struggles between the PCs by playing to our differing world views. It worked wonderfully, until we finally figured out how he was doing it. After that, we began to distrust our own character's perceptions, and fact-check everything we saw.

    The technique provided a richness to the gaming experience that I had seldom encountered previously. The differences in the PCs perception of 'objective reality' that we had encountered mirrored what seems to really happen when people unconsciously 'edit' their memories of events.

    \n I don't know if this is the same phenomenon you're talking about, but I really enjoy the play experience of when I'm able to move back and forth between my character's perspective, an audience member's outsider perspective, and if I'm really lucky, the perspective of a player who isn't me. In the first case it's a fictional creation, in the second it's the performance comprised of many fictional creations all interacting, and in the third it's me guessing what the real person is experiencing (and not knowing for sure if they're keyed into their creation or the stage itself at that moment).

    I think also one of the greatest GMing tools one can ever develop is being able to forcibly move between these perspectives and reconcile them all into a coherent whole. It's a poor GM who simply cannot connect with how the players are connecting with both their own character experiences, and how the stage looks from the outside. I strongly suspect most traditional frustrated-novelist GMs are mostly keyed into the action on the stage, at the expense of never empathizing with the characters.

    \n This may be an idea whose time has finally come; there have been intimations of its possibility for a long time.

    There was a game in the 2004 Game Chef on the Forge called "Snow Day" or maybe "Fort Joey Must Fall!"; it was written by a fellow named Hans Christian Anderson--a great-grandson of the fabulist, I think. In it, you played a kid outside on a snow day, trying to defend your snow fort from the neighborhood bullies. You could if you wanted summon up your imaginary snow monster friends to help you, but the way the mechanics worked it wasn't clear just how (fictionally) real that imaginary help was. As I think Mike Holmes pointed out, it was possible to be playing the game and imagining that that your PC's imaginary monster did in fact "really" come and save your fort, while someone else imagined that your PC was simply imagining the imaginary help, standing amid the ruins of your in-fictional-fact destroyed fort.

    And I remember talking to a fellow player after a badly railroaded D&D convention game several years ago who was not as badly upset as I was, because the game in his head was cooler than the one at the table--in other words, he had been able to imagine that some of the silly at-the-table stuff the DM had pulled had some interesting in-the-fiction explanations. As a concept, I thought that was an interesting idea: "The game in my head is awesome!"

    Another example may be PowerKill by John Tynes, wherein at the end of each session of a "normal role-playing game" there is a level of meta-play where the GM takes the role of a psychiatric counselor treating a bunch of normal people whose delusions lead them to believe that they are wizards or vampires or what-have-you and commit horrific crimes that in the delusions are perfectly justified. The idea is that what you thought you were imagining as cool and awesome at the time was in fact psychotic and sad.

    My point is that there has been some exploration of the idea that the consensus of the imaginary that we regard as axiomatically necessary may not in fact be so--but not a lot of exploration. So that's a cool thing to do. I also think Graham is saying something interesting (in the sense of thought-provoking) when he argues that in the tension produced in the interaction between minds imagining things differently--different loci of make-believe, if you like--lies some creative essence of role-playing. That's neat. The point isn't to get to agreement or reach consensus; it's to stage an encounter between dissenting interpretations. To the extent that such differences get "resolved," they stop being interesting. I see a parallel to a paper I wrote where I concluded that the essence of dialogue is a kind of "xenomancy"--reading the signs in the stranger, in other words, and presenting oneself as other as a resource in interaction.
