\n Both as a designer and GM, I keep feeling like combat is the hardest damn thing to make work. Which is sort of weird in that a lot of the games I'm working on (or failing to work on) are very fighty (Thrash, Tokyo Heroes, etc.). I don't have any really specific things to tackle at the moment; I'm just trying to get at some kind of general principles of game design.

What games have done combat (conflicts) particularly well or badly?

How do you incorporate player input/creativity into combat actions in interesting ways?

    \n The big question is..

    What role does combat play in your game?

    You can't judge whether or not combat systems are working correctly or not until you answer this question. Think about the difference between combat in D&D and combat in Dogs in the Vineyard. Both these games have combat systems that work. Both combat systems are completely different. Both games utilize combat for different things. It's the fact that these systems want different things from combat that makes both work despite their vast differences.

    So, what are you wanting combat to do in your game?
    Serve as an escalation for conflict?
    Simulate battlefield conditions accurately?
    Create an arena for competition?

    \n This is a shameless self-plug:

    The manuevers and styles of Savagery (pdf) work really well for combat, we've been through several playtesting sessions, and it keeps pace and does pretty much everything with one roll (hit, damage, and location). Admittedly it's a social / emotional combat system, but it's a solid system, and the basic idea - the unequal dice pairs, extends far beyond social situations.

    On a less self-plug note:

    The problem with your attribute choices for Tokyo Heroes is that you have locked yourself into Attack, Defend, and Initiative, so there's not too much depth for interesting combat decisions - so it shouldn't be used for long combats. But in the source material (sentai and maho shoujo - for those unfamiliar) the combats are extremely short - everything is determined quickly - heroes either defeat their foe at a given power level /technique or must escalate to the next one. Perhaps something like a "let it ride" rule for combat would help, unless one side or the other escalates.

    Come to think of it, that sort of escalation is common in related martial arts genres as well. Fights always go something like: basic technique, better technique, signature technique, signature technique with life on the line, last-ditch succession technique which you've never quite mastered. (Even bringing in allies works this way.) I'm reminded of Ruroni Kenshin and Bleach as typical examples of this sort of technique escalation. In D&D if you hit repeat your action, it might work this turn, in anime and sentai shows a failing attack means that attack won't work unless you change something.

    You know, listen to this more so than about Savagery here...

    \n I think you've got a sort of good point, Simon. Which is that RPG systems do not actually create the cool color of action fights. All of that color is provided by the narration. To that extent, if your only goal is to have your fights be as colorful as, say, a martial arts movie, then you should not have a combat system.
    \n
    \nOr at least none that I've ever seen. And I really doubt that one could be devised that would aid a narrator in this. Because all systems slow things down. Whereas narration speed is the only thing that's going to even potentially create the sort of feel you're looking for.
    \n
    \nIn fact, I think that even sans any system, your desire is doomed to fail. That is, what it sounds like to me is that you want to be able to create a movie in play. No, not action like that which happens in a movie, it sounds to me like you want pictures to erupt before you in real time. Just not going to happen. TTRPGs are a narrative form, and I just don't think that they deliver what you're asking for.
    \n
    \nThat's not to say that they don't deliver other things. See Andrew's (Gaerik) post. There are fun things that a RPG system can provide. Even slow motion color, if you're into that. It'll just never excite you like some cimematic kinetics display.
    \n
    \nEwen, I've played tons of good combat systems. As Andrew says, all depends on what you're in the mood for. If I want to test my actual gamer abilities, in terms of tactics, etc, then The Riddle of Steel is number one (BW is OK, but largely comes down to Roshambo - even more than TROS does). But if I want to understand how my character plugs into the fight that's happening - in more detail than just TROS's spiritual attributes - then I want to play Hero Quest. If I want slow-mo detail, I'd play Phoenix Command...

    Posted By: Mike HolmesI think you've got a sort of good point, Simon. Which is that RPG systems do not actually create the cool color of action fights. All of that color is provided by the narration. To that extent, if your only goal is to have your fights be as colorful as, say, a martial arts movie, then you should not have a combat system.
    \nI guess you're right. RPGs aren't like the movies, they're more like literature. And there's a reason there aren't many "action books" out there. In light of that, it's sort of surprising how important and common combat has become in RPGs, when the medium just isn't suited for it. Although historically, I guess it makes sense. It's also a bit funny how so many try to design combat systems that are "fast", when it simply will never be as fast as in the movies. I think I (and you, too, Ewen) might want to check out some literature that has good combat scenes. It can never be fast-paced in writing, so what makes 'em good there? If you can find that out, and make a mechanic that supports it, I'd say you're on your way to a good combat system.

    My funnest combats have all been about conflict resolution, rather than task resolution. Instead of "I want to hit him", I'd say "I want to kick him out the window". It made for much more interesting scenes, and it gave room for ways that the fight could end other than "they die" or "you die."

    \n The problem with most combat systems is that they focus on combat simulation. But a combat is not so exciting or meaningful by itself. What it is important is what are the characters risking. What is going to be won or lost, what are they fighting for.

    A resolution system which focus on character decisions which are meaningful (force her to risk something, e.g. her love, her parents desires, the people she wants to protect, what she values most) may represent better the thrill of a combat than a good simulation of tactic and strategy effects. All the tactic and strategy may be easily and freely added by colorful narration. A simple escalation mechanism which forces you to risk more and more to win the fight, may produce incredible thrilling combat scenes.

    From this point of view, I think that Polaris, Amber or Trollbabe, may provide more interesting combat scenes than other games with more specific and detailed combat resolution systems.

    \n Fight scenes in Exalted look and feel awesome after the fact. Unfortunately, even in 2nd edition counting successes still lags the system quite a bit.

    Ditto on Iron Heroes - there's so much crunch people have to double-check their character sheets all the time to remember all the awesome their character is capable of. The zones idea from the GM's book is good, though, and they have a much more solid mechanic for stunts than Exalted does (though it's not any faster).

    Shameless plug: In my current project (Sufficiently Advanced) I managed to make combat "scale" very well - you can start some psychohistorical maneuvering (timescale:years), initiate a presidential campaign to help with that (timescale:months), then the other side can launch a smear campaign through the media (timescale:weeks), then you challenge them to a debate (minutes), wherein someone else tries to shoot your candidate (seconds), and the lower ones affects the next higher one when they resolve. It also all feels like the regular game flow - no initiative, no special rules beyond some situational modifiers - so people can go in and out of conflict very easily. I'm proud of it.

    More generally: Incorporating player input can be done the Iron Heroes way (by providing useful scenery and rules to go with it), the Adventure! way (letting Inspiration points change the surroundings), the Amber way (description is everything because you can't pull dirty tricks without it), the Exalted/Wushu way (by giving bonuses for it), and no doubt in other ways as well.
