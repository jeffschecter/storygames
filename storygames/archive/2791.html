\n Hi everyone, after reading Story Games as a guest for a while, I finally asked for my own account. I'm coming here as someone who is pretty new to tabletop RPGs, basically crushed out on story games, and I'm posting this as a long-time mush game player.

On two days later this month, a small group of us, mush players into story games, is hosting a discussion about bringing together ideas of the new RPGs and mush games.

Sunday, April 22nd, 12:00 PM PDT, at M*U*S*H: mush.pennmush.org 4201, page Ide for location

Wednesday, April 25th, 6 PM PDT, at at MPUG: puggy.mushpark.com 9033, @tel to #399.

A few of the ideas we'll discuss: GM-less games, narrative control, player ownership of the game world, scene framing, alternatives to xp systems, abstract resources, relationship and trust mechanics.

We'll start with a short informal presentation and then go to discussion. We want to consider carefully how these ideas can work in the large-scale online setting of a mush. If you like sharing your thoughts about these ideas, we would love to see you there.

    \nBent, this question comes up a lot. From talking to people like the owners of big mud portals, for example TMC, activity at least as measured in overall hits and forum posts hasn't declined at all. However there is some other tangible evidence. Based on a few polls (which are hard to verify totally) new mush players (i.e. those who started playing in the last couple of years) make up about 10% of the total mush player population. This is like the silent majority effect -- again though, hard to verify.

    \n I had/have several issues with MUSHing that it would be neat to have a system to address them.

    1.) The need to constantly wait on Staff to actually do anything important. It just made the pace slow to a crawl. Want to affect anything in the setting or another character? Got to have Staff approval. Hope the appropriate one is online.

    2.) Due to #1, most scenes tended to be superfluous bar scenes where you just sat and gabbed in character. Boooooooooooooooring.

    3.) Scene framing sucks in a MUSH. Because they tend to be stuck on keeping the Mush on some realistic time system, you couldn't just skip all the boring crap and go straight to the heart of a scene/conflict. You actually have to wait until the appropriate time to do what it is you want. Unless, of course, you have Staff there to suspend the time rules.

    4.) Asnynchronous play is hard. You got a great scene you want to do but your necessary ally or your favorite rival isn't online. Sit and wait. Hope they aren't in a far away time zone.

    5.) Social Contract stuff is really hard to nail down on a MUSH. Most of a Staffer's job seems to be slapping someone player for stomping on some SC line that they probably didn't even know existed.

    6.) I'm not even going to get into Creative Agenda problems. The cries of "Twink!", "Powergamer!", "Wimp!" etc etc say enough about that.

    I'd love to see a system that actually handled this stuff. Most of the systems just shoehorn in Storyteller or D&D or something else that really doesn't work all that well given the medium.

    \n I've recently become interested in on line text games and how they are run. I was wondering how Engle Matrix Games might work in that environment?
    \n
    \nRight now we are doing a "Jack the Ripper: the game you play before the game" game on the MatrixGame2 yahoo group. There are six characters associated with the Ripper case (Jack, the Inspector, Lady Coverup, Lord Brutal, Mr Revenge and Pretty Nell). Unlike in a MUSH player don't pose. Instead each turn they write an action they want to have happen next. Actions are not just what their character does but what other characters do as well - so an action tells a whole scene. In the online game one extra player acts as referee. That person decides what has to be rolled for an action to happen. Other players can jump in with counter-arguments ("No that didn't happen - THIS is what happened.") These are resolved in a dice rolling competition. Successful actions/arguments are added to a list of game events. These form the verbal "matrix" of information of the game.
    \n
    \nPlayers engage in discussions out side of arguments so it is very social.
    \n
    \nIn many ways these games sound like MUSHes only without the higherarchy of needing a system administrator to do anything or the need to use any special code. It's all done in English (or Spanish or whatever...)
    \n
    \nDo you think that kind of game would be interesting to MUSH players?

    \n As someone who's still an active MUSHer, I feel disappointed I missed the most recent chat, but hope to be involved in future ones. It seems like I run in an entirely different MUSH circle and that the circles could benefit from cross-pollination.

    Having done both PB e-mail for many years, and MUSH for many years, I find the synchronicity such a huge advantage to MUSH that I never looked back at e-mail or board-based writing. E-mail now just seems too slow for me.

    As to how we handle the issue of players making a difference on the world in my "home" on the net-- We have a very active staff group and there's almost always at least one staffer on that can answer an immediate question. Staffers have a different alt used only for staffing as well as their "IC" characters which are kept seperate. Staff is very approachable and there's much less downtime between an issue brought up by a player and a response from staff. If the issue is complex, we have a system called "Request" that allows you to queue a paragraph of questions for staff reply, usually a request for more information about a plot. Typically you get a response before the week is out even on complex requests; of course this is because our Headwiz is very invested in the game.

    Scene framing is also rarely an issue. If we want to sit and gab, of course, we sit and gab, but so many of the games in my circle have action or cartoon or fandom-based themes and follow the rules of fictional pacing rather than those of how reality is paced.

    I won't deny that having slightly smaller games and communities may also facilitate better pacing and interplayer communication as well.
