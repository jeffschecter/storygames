\n As a huge spotlight hog, I shy away from attempts to justify myself. It's a bad habit, full stop. But the Apocalypse World multi-character thread made me think about this a little bit. So let's talk about spotlight hogging and how to deal with it under different situations, because I'm starting to think that maybe just saying "don't do it!" is not that great.

Here's an example:

A player is taking the spotlight time because their character is boldly coping with dramatic situations, whereas others are being more cautious or uncertain, maybe putting themselves in the "follower" role, or just not engaging that much with the situation of the game.

This is a situation where spotlight hogging can occur "for good reason", in other words, the reason everything is centered around this character (or subset of characters) is because they're the ones actually doing things.

Here are some thoughts on this situation:

Ensure that follower characters are getting a choice to put themselves there, and make that choice have some consequences. In other words, if the team's on a mission, and the spotlight hog is pushing things forward, agonizing over the big decisions, and others are just following their lead, have someone demand to know why the non-spotlight character isn't doing something, or insist that they do something, or compliment them on their support role, or ask them to support other things. This will reward the people that actually like doing that sort of thing, develop consequences for their decisions, and give them opportunities to choose to do other things, but not make things "bad" if they don't.

Here's another example:

A player is taking the spotlight time because they are the out-of-character leader of the group. The group looks up to them, their expertise, their charisma.

This one's more intractable, I think about this one a lot because I have a player like this in my group. (Sometimes I am this player, often at a convention for some reason people don't really like to interact much with strangers? I assume that some people just like throwing money at convention organizers for no reason, given that this is all a convention is?) Because it's at the social level, nothing you do inside the language of the game is really going to change things. The charismatic player might not even be intentionally doing it, and if they try to devolve responsibility on others, the group will certainly resist that. The only thing that I think can really be done here is to embrace it. Be sure that you give everyone a chance to do what they are comfortable doing, but go ahead and have the charismatic player be the one pushing the group forward. Pitch plots and difficulties straight at them and make it clear (like, TELL THEM) that you expect them to delegate tasks and objectives to others in the group.

But maybe someone has a better idea?

Here's another example:

The spotlight hog loves to act - I don't just mean play a role, I mean they love to talk in the voice of their character and interact with NPCs. The ones that prefer to describe conversations or have characters who aren't conversationalists get pushed out of the limited play time of the group because the one player's specialty/preference simply takes more time to adjudicate.

One thing is that I only really noticed this when I began to play with light systems with modest handling time (in the early 90s, though they existed long before that). AD&D's combat system, for example, took up a lot of time, so the players that preferred combat to conversation would undoubtedly have their spotlight time too just by virtue of the time footprint of their preferred action. With streamlined combat systems, but still a "say what your character says!" conversation rule, the conversationalist began to dominate the session far more. Social systems that don't replace "say what your character says!" make this worse, because it adds additional handling time onto the conversation.

The only thing I've been able to do here is to get players who prefer other things (stealth, combat, what have you) to be really detailed in their descriptions. In other words, even if the combat system is highly slimmed down, I have them describe very thoroughly what their character is doing and how.

Other spotlight hogging situations you've encountered, and how to deal with them?

    This is a situation where spotlight hogging can occur "for good reason", in other words, the reason everything is centered around this character (or subset of characters) is because they're the ones actually doing things.
    Not doing things is far more difficult to play, and to give credit. Role-playing games are still infantile in regard to this, compared to theatre and movies. We do not know how to give "screen-time" to the meandering, quiet characters. but it is possible.

    Doing things may be described to great effect ...
    - while not doing anything (being hesitant, bewildered, dumbstruck, etc.) may be described with a lot of affect (that is; descriptions of tiny details that hint on the state of mind, the true position, the inner whirlwind of feelings, and so forth ...).

    I find it a challenge to make space for such characters in role-playing games. In the action-packed games of the mainstream rpgs (fantasy, space opera and horror) the challenge is, of course, even more substantial. But it is a challenge that is fun to meet and play with.

    \n I've tried many, many techniques. Some ok, many failed. Here is what works best for me\u2026
    \n
    \nSTAGE 1:
    \n
    \n1. Hand everyone a poker chip (or any marker that is visually obvious).
    \n
    \n2. When you want the spotlight, pay a poker chip. Anyone can go in any order as long as they have a chip.
    \n
    \n3. Everyone gets a new poker chip after everyone has had the spotlight OR people who still have chips choose to pass. The people who pass now have 2 poker chips! They can store up spotlight for when they feel inspired. Some people need more time and less pressure to participate.
    \n
    \nWhat if people's spotlight scenes are too long?
    \n
    \nWhat is too long depends on your group's preferences. If this becomes a problem, add\u2026
    \n
    \nSTAGE 2:
    \n
    \n1. Grab an index card, and with a bold red marker write "Scene" on it.
    \n
    \n2. Place this card within everyone's reach.
    \n
    \n3. When anyone feels a spotlight scene has gone too long, grab and lift the scene card.
    \n
    \n4. The person currently with the spotlight has 1 minute to wrap up their scene.
    \n
    \nSimple! I've used these techniques a dozen times with friends and strangers to great success.

    \n In "Silver Screen Dreams," having the Spotlight is what the game is all about.

    The game is card-based. The player laying down the highest valued card gets the Spotlight. The Spotlight Player is currently "winning" the game since everything must go their way in the story as long as they possess it. Another player can jerk away the Spotlight by laying down a card in front of them that is of a higher value than the previous player.

    \n I think there might be some hurt feelings, yeah. Any time you take something away from someone who wants to keep it, they will be hurt. But that's not the end of the analysis:

    * Can the hurt be channeled into new and productive things?

    \n There's a line between a dysfunctional player and a dysfunctional person. The later can't adapt to situations, so if you willingly want to play with such a person, you and the group of players will have to find a way to deal with that specific person, make a houserule to give that person as much spotlight as neccesary and/or develope some other way to have fun on your sessions.

    I've been in a campaign (actually just part of the whole campaign) with a player like this. He can actually be reasonable most of the time, but the GM always makes the mistake of keeping the action with him instead of letting other players share spotlight, since this player is the one most aware of the big picture of the setting (space-opera galaxy-wide conspiration)

    Because of the GM reaction, perhaps this isn't exactly the case discussed, however my point about the group looking for other source of fun in the game is this: In this situation, it became a custom among the players to create characters that would conspire against our spotlight hogger; or plainly make his life more difficult. Watching him deal with these challenges became amusing enough for the players, not to the point to turn the game into a GM & NPCs against a single player, but close enough. Neither the group, the GM nor the player have any complain right now, and the campaign is still going on.

    Now, if you're dealing with reasonable people and you happen to have enough tact about adressing with situations that affect everybody in the table, I wouldn't call it "hurt the feelings" to the act of dispensing a little discipline on the table. Everybody wants to have fun, you've agreed to play an RPG, it has written rules, the group has unwritten rules and whoever goes against any of those is as guilty as those who remain silent and resented to such players. Discipline isn't something that kills the fun, it's a tool for sharing the fun with more people.

    Posted By: Evan TornerBut what if it's basically one player, and he/she knows that system is being implemented more-or-less against them in particular?
    \nGood question! I've had this happen. This is what we did...

    Explain that the scene card means:

    - your turn is over temporarily to keep the pace swift and give everyone their fair turn

    But it doesn't mean:

    - your scene is bad, some of the best scenes can be long
    - you can't continue the scene when it is your turn again, just start from where you left off (think of the scene card as a pause button)

    Additionally, I will usually say something like, "I often get so into a scene that I don't realize how much time has passed so the scene card helps me know when to pause and let someone else have a turn. It gets used during my scenes all the time. It's not a bad thing. It's a great tool to keep track of time." I'll even turn a friend aside and ask them to use the scene card on me first as an example and make it clear it isn't personal.

    You could also set a general guideline that scenes get to be at least 5 minutes long (or whatever you want). So don't use the scene card on a scene unless it has been at least 5 minutes.

    If it is still a problem... it's actually a gift in disguise! It might underline some sort of deeper social problem at the table to watch out for.

    \n Tangent\u2026

    We sometimes use an X card. If some content makes you uncomfortable, lift the X card up, explain what content is the issue, and then we remove that content, no questions asked.

    BUT\u2026

    Every time you do this, write your name on the back of the X card. When any one person uses the X card more than 3 times, we stop the game and talk about if there is a problem with the game, a disconnect in expectations, or if we will need to change the game's direction to make it more fun for everyone. If writing someone's name down makes them reluctant to use the X card, have someone else keep track.

    This almost never happens. That said, it makes everyone (in my experience) more cognizant and empathic to the needs of their fellow players. And if someone has a problem with the game, they can use the X card to signal us to take a break and realign what's going on. For many reasons, the ability to simply lift a card makes people much more comfortable in voicing their opinions and interrupting others before they go too far into uncomfortable territory.

    I've thought about implementing a similar rule with the Scene card. If you use it more than 3 times, we take a break and realign. But we've never had to so I haven't tested it out. We've actually had people excited when someone uses the scene card on them! So I've been reluctant to build a solution to a so far non-existent problem. The X card is a different story.

    \n I would question if this is actually a problem. As an extrovert myself, I know that I want to have the spotlight, but I've found that other people absolutely love being the strong silent type.

    I like the suggestions that allow players to request the spotlight, but I think communication with the group is an even better way of handling it. If people are talking about the issue, the hogging player can get a clue. Especially if they are just trying to make the game good for everyone.

    I play a game called "Roses and Thorns" at the end of every session I run. Basically, the players are asked to give the group something they liked (Rose) and something they didn't like (Thorn) about that session. I've found that most of the time when players actually feel left out, that's all the excuse they need to bring it up in a constructive way.

    \n I have a gaming group that does the same thing (we call it Goodies and Gripes). The players each must come up with a good thing about the session, and one troublesome thing about the session.     Posted By: JDCorley
    Yeah, sometimes people are just doing more things, in games where there aren't turns (like PTA or Polaris or D&D combat).
    \nAny engagment of the system takes up a certain amount of time at the table, and the more engagement someone does, the more spotlight they get as a result. If someone's used a half dozen skills in Burning Wheel or D&D or some similar skill based game, and the other players haven't used any, then there's a situation where the spotlight time is becoming uneven, and there should be a switch of spotlight to the other players. I think that it's something that an extroverted player should keep an eye on, and if they see that they've been rolling or engaging the system a bunch, they should take a moment to step back. Again, visible metrics (fate points, checked off experience, or skill checks) provide a tangible, visible example of the amount of game time they're using up.

    Now, that aside, I think that there's a certain amount of responsibility to share spotlight. As someone who is guilty of hogging spotlight, I try to involve one or two other players in the scene I'm attempting to play out. In a combat, I'll call out to another person, engage them in some sort of strategy. In a skill roll or challenge or whatever, I'll ask someone for help. If I'm trying to romance someone in a talky scene, I'll go to one of the other players for advice. I think that the more the spotlight becomes a dialogue between a few players at the table, and importantly a dialogue that shifts from player to player (I've had the two players at the table that do everything together, in full Bromance, excluding others) then this issue becomes lessened.

    There's a mindfulness of others that I think is really important. Those who are extroverts or social leaders really have to remember to be inclusive and helpful in involving your friends at the table. Spotlight hogging is really bad when the player in question is wearing blinders, focusing on their character and ignoring the people around them. I think that if we used our spotlight hogging methods to involve and include other players, it is a good thing for the game in question.
