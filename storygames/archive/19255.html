    \nYou're seriously overthinking the issue. The problem with doing that is that you're actually reducing realism and adventurability of the adventure if you insist that everything in this module you're running has to conform to your preconceptions. You are not that wizard, you are not the ghost, you are not James Raggi. You are merely presenting the facts as they lay on the ground.

    A more useful approach to making sense of other people's adventures is teleological constructive thinking: given that these are the facts, what might have brought the situation about? Of course you adapt an adventure to your setting and whatnot, but these sorts of details - it's just not a very good campaign setting if it can't handle other people's adventure details at all when they clash with your own assumptions.

    Also, about half of the reasons for using an adventure module boil down to not having to do your own prep, which enables you to improvise more naturally. You'll ruin that if your attitude towards an adventure module is to carefully, ever so carefully study it and forensically nit-pick it to death in advance. I'm not saying that it's wrong to prepare well (some people and social situations simply can't handle spontaneity), but it is very easy to have your prep become value-laden as you visualize how given encounters should go in your own personal headspace. Building those firm preconceptions about how encounters will go is unhygienic, because you're in danger of influencing events in play to make them match your preconceptions, or of being disappointed and becoming unstable as a referee if the players don't magically mind-read you to achieve your expected approach.

    With all that being said, there are a huge array of possibilities for how and why the ghost could be there and acting that way at this moment in time, if you go into it with the assumption that it in fact is there and you want to know the reason, instead of assuming that maybe it's not there because it doesn't make sense. For example:
    - This is just how ghosts work in the setting, and the stargazer is tickled pink about having one in his library as a sign of prestige.
    - The ghost is supposed to act differently, but it's broken due to lack of maintenance.
    - The stargazer doesn't know about the ghost, which has appeared recently due to the constant increase of ozone in the tower after the lightning-creation system was left permanently on. Maybe ghosts only show up in ozone-rich environments.
    - The wizard prizes his chess skills and looks down upon the hoi polloi for their lack of sophistication; it amuses his egoistic personality (he's a successful wizard, of course he's full of himself) to have an entrance exam that others can't pass while he can. It's true that it takes some time every time he wants to get into the library, but he enjoys it as a break from his routines. Sort of like somebody who peculiarly insists on having a rope ladder entrance into their apartment because they're just that aesthetic about their lifestyle.
    - The ghost doesn't require the entrance exam from people who have conclusively beat it in the past (maybe it looks at your respective ELO ratings, and anybody with a 1000:1 advantage over it gets a free pass.), and thus the wizard doesn't need to be playing every time he wants to use the library; the ghost will just cover from his superior puissance.

    The most important point, though, is that you're not the wizard, you're not the ghost, so you're not primarily responsible for defending their actions in the court of sensibility. You're merely responsible for judging whether a person with those actions could conceivably exist in your setting, or if everybody always does the rationally sensible armchair theory thing in there. Maybe it's that kind of setting, I don't know - in that case one might swap the ghost for a lock, or assume the wizard doesn't know about the ghost at all. That sort of setting will make it difficult for the players to ever be surprised by the unknown and unexpected, though, so I'd consider it twice before going that way.

What sort of drink goes bad enough for save vs. death, but tastes the same as it did before? Why would the players expect that to be the case?     \nEverything I said about the ghost applies here; as far as I'm considered you're just being a bore if you insist that it's impossible for a difficult-to-detect poisonous spoilage to be present in a wine stock. What's next, are you going to remove the lightning trap because electricity doesn't work that way, too?

    Maybe magic reacts with antioxidants in wine to produce a subtle poison when wine is exposed to the thaumic field for extended periods of time. There, that's your excuse for why a wine bottle has spoiled in such a lethal manner, and it wasn't hard. Now when your players try to nitpick themselves out of death, you can instead offer them an interesting long-term bit of setting lore, one that can easily be turned around to benefit them: they could start producing their own poison now that they have a pretty strong hint about how such might be accomplished, which poison depending on the setting might be almost unique in its potency and useful qualities. (Poison has historically been much more rare, weak and difficult to produce than it is in Gygaxian D&D.)

    Or you could just do a little bit of constructive fixing and assume that the poisoned bottle was put there by the Stargazer. If I were an international man of mystery science like him, I would certainly keep a bottle of wine poisoned with undetectable poison in my hamper for the "special" guests.

    Besides, worrying about the fairness of the danger is extremely unhygienic when you're designing a scenario (which you're clearly doing, considering how you're fixing it instead of working with it). I could be wrong about your thought process here, but to me it reads like your primary issue with this wine bottle is that it causes a poison save vs. death, and death's too harsh. Why do you know this to be the case? Particularly, your arguments about expecting danger do not apply: there is no reason why every peril should be avoidable by cleverly expecting it. To the contrary, this idea that dangers are only fair when they can be expected is another form of GM preconception: you're basically saying that you're the guru of danger-expecting, and know best which dangers the players should be able to anticipate. This is just cruelly insulting upon the players: think of what they'll feel if they fail to mind-read you successfully and step into one of your calibrated traps that were never supposed to go off because you expected "more" from them. By turning every time their characters step into some amusing hazard into a moment of "you should've known that it's coming" you make the game much less fun, psychologically speaking. Besides, it's again utterly unrealistic to have all dangers be avoidable; attrition is the first principle of the art of war, according to Clausewitz.

    However, the problem could well be with your rules instead of the scenario: I personally think that the save vs. death mechanics in old school D&D are a smidgen too lethal, which is why I've revised them to be sort of sadistically semi-lethal instead, so that there's a lot of leeway for midway outcomes between shrugging it off and dying of it. I also tend to use rules akin to 3rd edition D&D, where saving throws are graded like skill checks on their difficulty. Due to these changes to procedure I could easily enough have characters just get violently ill from drinking that wine. Jim Raggi couldn't anticipate my procedural direction in matters like these, of course, so he expresses himself in traditional mechanical terms in describing the scenario; it's up to me to interpret that into whatever my own mechanical framework is. For me it's eminently credible for food to be subtly spoiled in the right conditions (and yes, I am willing to accept it on face value that wine might be one of the foods that could produce e.g. botulin toxins or something of the sort, rather than getting into fact-checking rut against an encyclopedia), but if a character were poisoned, I would have it proceed pretty much like food poisoning: violent illness possibly followed by death over several hours.

    An interesting anecdote from when we were playing this: the adventurers looted the wine and sold it to the bishop's household (our campaign started in the lands of the local bishopric, so they were the temporal lord of the area as well). We had some quite fun times tracking the journey of the bottles, as I revealed to the players after the sale that there was a poisonous bottle in the lot. I tracked the bottle's journey in campaign time as it made its way through the bishop's household, which meant that for the next several sessions we would start each session with a few randomizing checks to find out where the bottle would end up. The stakes were, of course, about whether anybody important would fall ill and die from drinking that wine - could've been the bishop himself, which would certainly be tracked back to the wine-seller and therefore to the PCs. The bishop survived, I think because a lucky roll had an unfaithful servant sample the wines unauthorized a couple of weeks later, to lethal consequence.\n

    \nA "trapdoor" is a door on the floor, not a trap. (Peculiar language, English.) You may assume their depth to be architectonically determined as whatever makes sense for a basement - so e.g. 10 feet from the lip to the bottom could work, or more if you picture the floor as particularly thick for some reason. Trapdoors in ordinary usage (in a normal house, that is) have stairs to facilitate getting up and down, but if they're not indicated (as is the case in the tower), then one might assume that the Stargazer e.g. levitates up and down, or keeps something dangerous down there and doesn't want to provide it easy access out.     \n As Johnstone says, the significance of the differences depends on how much importance you place on flavour vs. mechanics vs. procedure. Personally I consider these basically the same game, because I don't see much of a game in D&D unless we consider the procedures and philosophy, which are basically identical between Mentzer and Raggi. However, if you're mechanically obsessive, you'll find plenty of minor streamlining in LotFP, and if you think that flavour text and illustration are king, then that's also a way in which the games are radically different: Elmore era TSR fantasy vs. grindhouse historical horror fantasy. I certainly know many smart people, including Jim himself, who seem to think that LotFP is an entirely different and new game that just happens to have some Basic D&D among its influences.

    Regarding treasure payouts: PCs do not find and collect all treasure from a single adventure location, and henchmen take e.g. half share in most applications, including the LotFP rules. In addition, characters die (and retire, if you're into that), taking their collected xp out of the calculations. So even if one were to underwrite the prescription of 4 adventures to level up (which I don't; my own ideals on the matter are entirely different), a given adventure should still have significantly more treasure than 1/4 of leveling requires. If I had to really attempt to shoot for this progression speed goal, I'd probably assume that about 20-50% of the xp is never recovered (depends on the nature of the adventure location), and another 20-50% is lost during or soon after the adventure to character death, and a further 20% is lost to henchmen and similar expenses. In practice this means that if I want to have characters level up after e.g. 4 reasonably successful adventures, I'd rather have significantly more than 25% of the 2nd level goal in each of the four adventures. At least double that.

    Obviously all this depends hugely on nuanced campaign practice. For example, if you only run self-contained basic dungeons, and follow reliable treasure placement guidelines, and the players are out to grind, then it's likely that they'll just vacuum the entire dungeon each time to make sure they didn't leave any treasure behind, resulting in a much higher percentage of treasure recovered. "Space-wanking", as we like to call the meticulous searching of all rooms after worst of the monster population has been reduced, in honor of Doom

    In case you're writing your own adventures and are wondering about treasure placement: my own recommendation is that a single adventure intended as a solid opportunity and an endeavour worthy of a name (that is, an adventure module instead of just some piddly random encounter on the road) should provide sufficient xp to level up a 1st level party, modified by all fictional particulars of the situation. So for your typical adventure I'd put about 10,000 xp into the adventure in reasonable reach of a party that is actually achieving goals - this doesn't count all the various red herrings and incidental minor treasure, although I obviously won't necessarily put everything into one neat treasure pile at the end of a dungeon. The logic behind this prescription is that a "complete success" should more or less align with observable progress on the metaphorical bad-assitude track that is your experience level. Of course various other procedural priorities make it so that I'm breaking this parameter all the time for all sorts of reasons, and the aforementioned actual play uncertainties make it so that you almost never see a party coming out of an adventure triumphant. When you do, it's well sensible to have them level up for their success.

    (For calibrating your expectations about the above, I should note that when I run old school D&D, and when the people I've trained run it, it seems that nobody gets to 2nd level without a dozen sessions of play; setting up the paydays takes time, characters die, treasures are missed, and monsters and competing interests secure the rewards unless we're really on the ball, which all means that we adventure quite a bit without seeing a drakhma of treasure. I've personally managed to drag two characters to 2nd level over the last year of pretty regular weekly play, and one of them died the session after the achievement. As this is definitely in the more difficult end of the D&D campaigning style, you might take my opinions about appropriate xp rewards with a grain of salt; if your own campaign features nice and steady achievement, with most sessions ending in at least moderate success, then it likely makes much more sense to be stingy with rewards.)

    Of course treasure payout is a very campaign-specific scheme. Considering Tower of the Stargazer, I can definitely see him being simply conservative in his treasure placement there. As I said earlier, the nature of the adventure (exploration, no big fights) makes it such that it doesn't necessarily require a large party or a large lethality, so I could easily see Jim seeding the adventure intentionally to a relatively low reward standard.

    \n A couple of short comments on the bottle of wine:

    A person who thinks like that already hates the D&D system, for the rules themselves are all about risk and unexpected outcomes that interfere with their clever, ever so clever storytelling; a PC might survive when they don't deserve it, or die accidentally. An easy fight might turn into a slaughter, a hard one end quickly and trivially. The scenario-writer might not account for a clever idea or extraordinary resource the adventurers bring to bear, or they might assume capacities that don't exist. The historical TSR answer to this tension was a total breakdown of the creative agenda, as writers started desiring their own story over the dynamic events of play, which was then followed by undermining the social contract with all the bullshit that we know as illusionistic traditional railroad GMing today.

    As far as I'm concerned all procedural advice in scenarios for how to rule on the various fictional situations is suggestive only, necessarily: you cannot allow some writer to take control of your game away from the group's own higher-order processes. This is a pretty radical theoretical stance, as it means that when I encounter a piece of text like "the wine is spoiled, and those drinking it have to succeed in a save vs. poison or die", the only part that I actually conside authoritative is "the wine is spoiled"; the rest is entirely a suggestion to the referee over how they might opt to resolve this situation with the poisoned wine. Unless this kind of skepticism towards module material is consistently practiced, the process of play is not safe when you utilize foreign scenario material: any idiot who wrote something stupid could manage to break your resolution processes by suggesting something stupid in their scenario material.

    \n That's an interesting point about the admonishment about the undetectability of the poison. I read it as Jim stepping outside the purview of scenario spec and into pedagogical space: he wrote that adventure with referee indoctrination in mind, which is why it has an unusual amount of value-based hedging. Him telling you that the wine's spoilage is undetectable is him trying to get you to let the dice fall where they may so that the conceived danger has a proper chance to occur; he's concerned that the conceit falls flat if the ref makes the obvious interpretation (that the spoilage would be obvious to any consumer before it'd be dangerous). I would read that as a suggestion as to Jim's own preferences, but exactly as Johnstone says, it's at most suggestive: if he doesn't give you causal reasons for why this particular corridor is anti-magical and the trap in it auto-hits any paladin who steps foot in it, I at least would either think up some likely reason myself, or ignore that part because it's not legitimate in my eyes. Of course regarding the wine bottle I happen to side with Jim's conceit: it's a valid conceit that under this dungeonesque sun there might occur an undetectably dangerous bottle of wizard's wine, and that has nothing to do with normal processes of spoilage and everything to do with adventurers potentially getting into trouble for trusting their senses too much when it comes to eldritch locations and foodstuffs procured from such.

    That's not to say that I always agree with the module author, or specifically with Jim - he has a habit for pushing the envelope, seeing what kinds of idiocy he might get other people to run; I'm not even that certain if he's serious about it all the time. I'm used to routinely ignoring the most atrocious brain-farts that aren't compatible with my own procedural paradigm. It's a bit like how I routinely swap the boring AD&D magic items in Expeditious Retreat Press adventures for more interesting and setting-consistent ones; all part of adapting the scenario to my campaign.

    I guess if had to give a heuristic for interpreting adventure module hazards, based on this discussion, I'd craft something like this: as the GM is responsible for representing a consistent setting according to local standards, he has to be able to stand behind the conceits he proffers for the players. At the same time, it is good of him to stretch himself and attempt constructive reading of material, for without such it is impossible to actually run material not written by yourself. Thus a necessary compromise emerges when every element of the proposed adventure is put under the judgement of the GM, who determines case-by-case whether that conceit is legitimate according to the local standards. The legitimate material the GM implicitly backs by introducing it into play, while the unfitting, foolish things that he doesn't understand or appreciate, those he changes to fit. The "constructive" part of the process is that where given a choice, the GM opts to read things in a way that makes sense to him instead of a way where they don't work. In this way he actually achieves a functional adventure instead of having to conclude that all too many adventure modules are impossible to use because of how they differ from his own unique expectations in subtle ways.

    The reason for why I'm so vague with that description of the adventure-adaptation process is that I don't really believe that things like e.g. realistic causality are universal and universally necessary parts of the game - it depends on the local playstyle and standards as to how far one finds it necessary to go in that regard. Everybody in this discussion seems to price things making sense, but there are also people out there who judge under different criteria that I might term "extreme constructivism": whatever has been written in the adventure spec is how things are, and how things are never require any explanations or causes to occur. This is generally the "wizard did it" school of adventure conceptualization

I hope you didn't take my earlier didactic musings in an aggressive tone, I was just carried away in singing the praises of constructive reading attitudes. Of course if you can't stand behind a conceit such as that subtly poisonous wizard wine, then that's ultimatley between you and your setting vision. I guess that my real point in the main was simply that the use of module adventures necessarily implies a pretty liberal setting vision, one where it's better to lean back from minor details and attempt to make them work rather than condemning them on flimsy grounds.

    \n In theory terms I see introduction of new PCs as the same general question of balance between robust SIS and gameability that permeates the entire game. It is useful to realize that we face the same questions in this special case as we do in the most fundamental acts in setting up the game. Consider:

    If your game were a perfectly robust simulation, there would be no room for either challenges or gameability to exist: no character could be a player character, for they would each already have their perfectly imposed motivations and nature, with no room for deviation from determined process of resolution. It would be NPCs vs. NPCs, forever. Players could operate the Sims Adventure scenario, but they could not play with it, or be challenged by it. Win or loss, it is the perfectly justified outcome of logical process.

    On the other hand, if gameability is the only concern, then we are fighting each other: anything ever established in the SIS is a tool of maneuver in achieving victory against the other players; rules lawyering proliferates and all concerns are focused on technical means of conquest. No appreciation is left for the matter of whether your solutions - your very play - are legitimate in the sense of communicating a true thing about fictional tactics and strategy. Victory becomes nothing but bullying and sophistry.

    Because I find neither of those extremes agreeable, I handle introducing new PCs as a balancing act: generally players have a subjective right to play for gameability reasons, which means that they can get a new character into play without too much hassle; we willingly stretch the simulation and assume a new character to be possible here and now. The player is not forced to wait for some extremely narrow simulation gap where it would be perfectly realistic for a new character to appear, and they are not expected to run through a rigamarole maneuver with the other PCs to prove that they have legit social positioning for joining the party. Either of those requirements would be punitive from a gameability viewpoint, we could not play at all if we required such a degree of realism consistently: if new PCs can't be introduced, why can adventurers find adventures to begin with, that's an even more unrealistic supposition.

    However: when a scenario is on-going, challenges have been identified and the pieces are moving, I might limit, forbid or delay bringing in new PCs. The key issue is whether the new character ruins the fundamental challenge of the scenario. An entrapment scenario is a good example: if you only have four friends with you in a gauntlet to the death, and you're dropping one by one, growing weaker by the moment, it makes no sense whatsoever for new PCs to keep popping up to bolster the ranks in the middle of the challenge. It ruins the scenario.

    In real play "you'll get to come back for the next scenario with a new character" is a rare event because it is rare that there would be no role for a player to play in a scenario. Typically the party is in a relatively lax dungeon crawl where it doesn't matter tactically whether "the party had one more member all along", for example. If the situation is slightly more tense (e.g. we know that a goblin war-party is waiting for their moment to strike), the party might have to retreat from the dungeon, rendezvous with a friend who was left to guard the camp, and then go back into the dungeon. Or the player might take over a NPC retainer, if such are still left.

    Last night we had a character die in our face-to-face game. Peitsa just took one of the retainers for his new character. Not a big deal despite the party being stuck on the 2nd level of the megadungeon at the time, as we had about half a dozen retainers with us at that point.

    When a scenario is under tactical tension (so the party can't just get reinforcements) and the social impact on gameability is severe (the night is young and the player keen to play more), we shuffle through a variety of compromise possibilities that best balance gameability with scenario consistency. Here more exotic solutions like "we force our captive to guide us, you can play him" or "the goblins have a PC race prisoner in a cage" come in. The more gameability pressure there is, the more drastic assumptions the GM makes to make room for a new character. I have been known to use "OK, let's do single-roll resolution for the rest of this entire scenario, as it's not that interesting and half of the players are already out".

    Ultimately, though, I am of the mind that asking for a new character mid-scenario is somewhat similar to asking to start a new business in Monopoly when you've gone bankrupt. We allow it because the game is cooperative and thus to a degree the scenario's not an utter failure when one player has been played out, but it is not an absolute subjective right you have. A good player realizes when adding a new character would have a major impact on the established challenge, and won't mind to wait for the others to resolve the outstanding situation before again joining play.

    \n Bold strokes in characterization are good for quick and powerful positioning work. Nuance and realism are good for long-term work that backgrounds those bold strokes. For this reason we want to make simple and gameable short-term tactical decisions ("Today this NPC is ill-tempered.") when within scenarios, but always keep the option of grounding those decisions later ("He is passionate, but strives towards discipline; however, he happened to have a bad day.").

    Thus, Cacidius in a tactical situation and Calcidius in long-term strategic play might be somewhat different considerations. I'm not saying that he cannot be a cartoonish villain, but perhaps a hypothetical scenario helps explain why it might be a good idea to keep every door open for further revision and deeper understanding: say that the PCs dodge his revenge for eight weeks, and then one of the brighter bulbs decides to go meet with the man and apologize for barging in on him like that earlier. Say they'll bring a rare jade statue with them, one that they imagine the wizard would like. So the players are taking a risk off the assumption that the NPC is a human with human psychology, and we're going to reward them with a cartoon villain obsessed with revenge? Sure, it could happen - but if it's going to happen with procedural certainty, then the players should have been made aware of it in advance - they should know that this in this campaign that kind of stratagem will always fail.

    All of the above is not intended to directly comment upon Martin's approach (I haven't even looked at his table yet), it's just to illustrate the questions involved in running important NPCs long-term. I personally generally opt for "infinite nuancing" in the sense that I don't consciously simplify long-term strategic elements such as what a NPC might or might not want or do. (This is a big part in why I don't usually randomize strategic choices, either - randomization loses GM vision and nuance.)

    The way I handle long-term nemesises and rivals like the wizard here, myself, might be of interest: I handle them as special random encounters that might come up later in the ordinary random encounter processes. Once they do, I retroactively figure out what they're about now. So the wizard might come up again a short time later, or a long time later, and I'd decide whether they're out for revenge or it's a change encounter or whatever at that time, rather than having a premapped table of possibilities. I might roll a few dice rolls to make any close calls, but primarily I'd rely on my intuitive understanding of who and what the NPC is - that is, I attempt to foster that deep organic nuance in their depiction.
