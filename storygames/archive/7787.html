\n I want to talk about specific techniques.

When you're roleplaying and your character has its buttons pushed, and you're like, "screw this, my dude totally flips out and goes ballistic now", how do you roleplay that?

Does your voice actually reach a yelling volume? Do you just say "and he yells..." and carry on in a normal conversation volume?
Do you have a "yelling tone" that you adopt?

If a passerby observed you for just that period where you were roleplaying an angry character, would they think that YOU were angry yourself?

Do you play an angry character differently at a con compared to with a closely-knit group? (and is that about respecting boundaries or shyness?)

How believable are you when you roleplay an angry person? What makes you believable? Does believability matter?

How do you react when other players get all loud and roleplay an angry character REALLY intensely?


Please don't give me line-for-line responses. Pick and choose the questions that stand out to you the most and give me well-thought answers to them.

    \n It depends. (Like there's any other possible answer for questions like these.)

    I hardly ever yell for real. I might get a bit louder, or make angry-looking gestures, or speak with more intensity, or explicitly say "I'm furious about this, and so I start yelling...", or describe my character's appearance/demeanor (angry expression, flushed, etc.). Sometimes I might just say something like "I spend the next five minutes angrily cursing the district attorney and using every swear word in every language I know" and move on. Many times I will make out-of-character remarks at some point to make it clear that whatever anger might be showing in my performance is completely fake.

    Because the most important point for me is that the character is angry: it has literally nothing whatsoever to do with me, the guy sitting at the table with his friends playing the game, being angry. So I do enough to demonstrate that the character's pissed off, without letting it touch my actual emotional state. I'm not in this hobby to get some kind of catharsis: I have plenty of things in my real life worth being angry or sad about during the week, I'm not so starved for emotional expression that I need to work it into my leisure time as well.


    As far as how I feel when other people "play angry," it's not something I worry about much these days, since I've been playing exclusively with a group of close friends for several years now; I know that none of them are the type who get genuinely angry during a game, so I know that any signs of anger I might see are only in-character. Not that any of them do anything much different from what I do; we're all pretty laid back in that respect. If we added a new guy who was playing anger to the hilt, it would probably bother me until/unless he demonstrated that it was just acting...and he'd probably be getting the "Seriously, WTF is wrong with you?" look from the rest of us until he dialed it back. We know what angry people look like (most of us see too many of them too often), so we don't put a high value on someone being able to act it out for the game.

    \n I knew a guy who would do really good fuming, angry impersonations of characters. Here's the interesting thing:

    The way he let us know it was the character, and not himself, is that he would have the character screw up, stumble over words, make mistakes, etc, and generally kind of make fun of himself in subtle sort of way. It was effective without being too comical.

    Come to think of it, I do that too, but I have to do it narratively, because I don't have his skills:

    If I'm playing a character who is really pissed off, I might say one or two angry words to express the anger, without going overboard, but afterwards (or in the middle) narrate in some little detail that shows I'm not necessaily siding with the character. For example, I might say, "...and, as he gestures, his arm flies out and knocks over his drink by mistake. Shit!"

    \n In a D&D game once, I was playing a rogue. We came upon a witch. For reasons I can't remember, we wanted to kill this witch. However, she told us the person who killed her would be cursed. Another player, who was playing a noble and was essentially "my boss," promised me a potion to get rid of the curse, which he had come across earlier. So, I killed her. He declined to give me the potion. My character got pissed and I started almost-yelling, and calling him a son-of-a-bitch and things like that. I was later told by the GM that I "needed to calm down" that it was "just a game." We'd just picked up a couple new players, and I guess they were a little uncomfortable as well.

    I found that kind of interesting, as it made perfect sense for my character, who was now walking around with a curse, to be really, really pissed. Sure, real-life me was irritated as well, but not yelling and cursing irritated. That was the character.

    I'd like to be able to do that more in my regular game, but I find it kind of difficult to keep track of so many things (as the GM) that the idea of adding the NPC's emotions as well is a little intimidating. Mostly I just describe an NPC as "being angry" and talk with a pissed off tone.

    \n Kev,

    Not all beasts are the same. Some have two legs. Some four.

    Those 2 questions imply that some games are better suited to certain types of roleplaying than others. If you don't accept that premise, disregard those questions.

    Those 2 questions also imply that certain games are better at facilitating certain types of emotional expression than others. My Life With Master, Shock: Social Science Fiction and Perfect are better at portraying self-pitying underdogs slowly being crushed under the weight of the man than.... Dungeons and Dragons. I'm sure you can play games of D&D that cater to THAT type of play (and THAT type of intense emotion), but it's less supported by the system. If you don't accept that premise, disregard those questions.

    So... assuming you do accept that premise, would you also accept that certain games cater to scenes of intense anger better than others? If so, then my question is... Which ones?
