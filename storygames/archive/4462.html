\n I'm trying to imagine a universalis-like game with less fiddly bits in meatspace. I don't like looking down from the other players or the stuff we're working on or talking about to track or gather dice or fiddle with tokens. I like cards; I like the feel of them and I like how casual they are.

So I'm trying to think of a GM-free game where the players had a good way of sharing the narrative authority, incentive to compete, and didn't write anything down (edit: while playing through a scene). This is a combination of a LOT of different ideas I've had following on my reading of and play experiences with Universalis. There's a lot here but I tried not to bog it down with self-justifying analysis.

So picture this:

4-6 players sitting around a table; they've agreed the basic premise of the story but nobody knows where it's going yet.

One player deals 6 cards to everyone.

Each player chooses 1 card to bid: Highest bid Sets, Lowest bid Directs.

Bid cards are not considered discarded: they can be used by the Director but remain in play.

Player who Sets must establish:
1) Location (Where? Serenity's main hallway)
2) Characters Present (Who? Captain Mal, Jayne, and Vera)
3) Agenda (Why do we care? This is a scene where we remember why we should be afraid of Jayne.)
These cost no cards.

Director narrates the opening of the scene. Director's job is to Put Up conflict whenever a member of the Ensemble Cast goes up against the world or to force the world to Put Up conflict against an Ensemble Cast member.

First Player who suggests a Conflict that has two sides willing to Put Up gets a free face-down card for next round. The Director is often responsible for Putting Up the opposition.

Putting Up: Whenever there's a conflict that at least two players are interested in (i.e. player vs. player or Director vs. player) each side chooses a number of cards to Put Up. Red cards beat black cards. Before the cards are shown, each palyer may add up to 1 card, face-up, drawn from the Deck to up to 1 side, throwing weight behind that side (this is used to show where the weight of facts versus skepticism, but also to indicate what the players think would be awesome). Winner gains an extra 1 card for next round.

Cards can be spent in a few other ways:
One card to change roles: This can include taking over being the Director or (the new Director still has to provide opposition if appropriate). Alternatively this might mean taking the role of something other than a member of the Ensemble cast (i.e. taking the role of a band of space pirates in a scene that your own character is absent in).
One card to End the Scene if there has been a conflict, three cards to end the scene if there hasn't been a conflict.
A card, slid forward face-down indicates a Challenge; this forces negotiation or requires the opposing player to spend a Card or withdraw what they were narrating. If the player withdraws or negotiates to the Challenger's satisfaction the Challenger keeps their card.
One card to Establish a Point - this throws somethign out for the Director and other players to work off of but does not take power away from the Director. Establishing a Point can go back to basically any fact in the world, subject to a Challenge.

During a conflict, cards can also be spent to Establish a Point by anyone, and while these are subject to Challenge, no more conflicts can be resolved (i.e. no more Putting Up) until after the current conflict resolves. For example, the conflict might be "Does Aragorn prove he can protect the hobbits?" but someone might Establish the Point that "Frodo is wounded in the fight." Frodo's player could Challenge that (either to outright stop it or suggesting that this could be a subsequent conflict) but if no one Challenges then the Conflict must be narrated to include the point.

Any cards unspent are tossed at the end of the hand, although some players will have earned extra cards for the next round.

    \n As I sat down to write it I first tried to envision what game they were playing and instead came up with an intro / setup phase dialogue that grew its own mechanics to make sense. The group is called the Whitehall club and I have profiles for them if you care. The members are Travis, Rob, Bim, Andy, Jan (Janice) and Eva.

    Setup Phase (Pitch Meeting / Tenets)

    In the Setup Phase, the group comes up with some Big Ideas and an Ensemble Cast. Big Ideas are like story element Tenets in Universalis, or the kind of things people agree on in a Primetime Adventures Pitch Meeting. The Ensemble Cast is created as a group but each player has to settle on a cast member (and want to play it) before the game starts.

    Players need a piece of paper for the Big Ideas, another for the Ensemble Cast, and a deck of cards. For the setup phase, the whole deck is dealt out to all players.

    For a Big Idea to become part of the game, it needs someone to Propose it, and two to Support it, and no one Objecting. If you can get that, the player who Proposes pays 2 cards, the Supporters each pay 1 card, and the Big Idea is agreed (and recorded).

    \n On "no tracking",

    Just how long could this go one without being written down? Without recording things, eventually there will be arguments/errors etc.

    Universalis brings the burden of recording into play and abolishes all the "writing up character sheets, writing up modules, story maps, relationship maps, conflict webs, writing up our genre rules" that other games "put before play" and via pre-created text chain you down. You have take on the burden to create text as you go as the trade-off for not being bound by pre-created text. Likely less text but the shift in when created does put the burden close into play.

    Ah grim world that demands a price for everything. Recording is Universalis' "price" for freedom, though different media for play and recording likely have some merits to explore.

    \n OK, so I've been thinking about what I wrote and I think this is the important idea in what the example players are doing above:

    Instead of someone proposing a major game-forming idea and seeing if someone will be a jerk and Challenge it, they propose the idea knowing they have to drum up two other peoples' support for it (and have no one Challenge it). To me, that process is more likely to build a cohesive view of what the game is about. If I'm right, then this process will get people on the same page about the game and reduce how many smaller details need to be tracked.

    \n You know, I'm hitting a wall on how to go to the next step (who does what in conflict). Maybe instead of grappling with this from the void if I could start with actual universalis and make Rules Gimmicks towards what I want, see how actual play goes and fix it from there.

    \nLet's forget the cards for a moment (although I REALLY want some kind of cue in the players hands at all times saying "DO THIS TO HIT THE AGREED-ON KEYS/BANGS")
    \n
    \nEstablishing Tenets
    \n
    \n1) Establishing a Tenet requires the proposing player to spend 2 Coins with the support of 2 other players who each spend 1 Coin.
    \n2) Establishing a Tenet cannot overcome a Challenge; all players must agree on the Tenets of a game.
    \n3) Each player must create one member of an Ensemble Cast that is always under their control as long as they are at the game.
    \n4) Creating an Ensemble Cast member is Establishing a Tenet
    \n5) Each player's Ensemble Cast member is a Named Character for free.
    \n6) Each Ensemble Cast member must have an Issue, which is a central, personal conflict that drives their story.
    \n
    \nNarrating a Scene
    \n
    \n1) When setting up a scene, the high bid Frames, but the low bid Directs
    \n2) The Scene Framer MUST establish the scene's Agenda.
    \n3) The Scene Framer names the characters in a scene but only controls his own Ensemble Cast member. The Scene Framer does not need to spend to introduce characters that already exist in the game. The Scene Framer does not need to spend Coins to use a Location that already exists in the game.
    \n4) The Director describes the scene like a GM would in a traditional RPG. The Director has Control of the characters and Location named by the Scene Framer as being part of the scene (except the Ensemble Cast). This is true even if the Scene Framer paid Coins to Create the characters or location.
    \n5) Players do not need to spend Coins to narrate Events or create dialogue for characters under their control. However, a player can Challenge anything in the game even if it is free, and the Challenging side wins ties.
    \n6) The first player who suggests a conflict that is acted on by two sides gets a free Coin.
