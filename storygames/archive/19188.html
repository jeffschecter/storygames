    \n The rules of each game often imply certain things for the story, as well: for instance, in IaWA, a character who is entering contests according to their strengths/desires doesn't make it onto the Owe List. This means they should not recur in future chapters. Following this kind of guideline gives a writer some onus to focus on certain characters over others in future installments of the story.

    Another note: it only works for a particular style of setting, but Burning Wheel helped me flesh out some characters for a story I was writing, simply by going through character burning.
    \nI think Traveller's lifepath could create the same types of backstories, and that example even runs the risk of character death during generation. In the same vein, diaspora has players collab build a miniature universe with stats and Fate Aspects that imply a lot about the political/social relations between systems. They then create characters with Aspects that hook them together at different points in their life.

    I've seen this system stripped out and given a fantasy universe spin, replacing star systems with holdings and cities - so it looks like a flexible plot inspiration tool; each of those backstory episodes could blossom into a story and you've already got an idea how each could fit into the broad context.

    My game (New Best Friend) that got a shout out in the last newsletter is, at its core, a solo creative writing / art gamebook. I don't know if that's a direction you'd be interested in, but one approach I considered: One person writes a very skeleton plot and sends the second the writing assignments, day by day, in a format similar to the game. For a single person, write a few weeks worth of cues and set them aside for a month and then tackle them when you've almost-forgotten them and they can surprise you.

    \n With Breaking the Ice and Shooting the Moon, the character & situation generation are pretty much creative brainstorming exercises. In BtI, you take each of the main characters' favorite color and create a word association web to get some ideas for who they are. In Shooting the Moon, the players pick aspects of the Beloved, and then spin off traits for the Suitors by choosing antonyms and synonyms. And in each you create goals and obstacles to the characters. These could be good jumping off points for story writing.

    At GenCon last year, Corey Reid organized a panel on the overlap between fiction and rpgs. Might be an interesting listen:

    http://blog.scratchfactory.com/2013/10/panel-discussion-stories-out-of-games.html
