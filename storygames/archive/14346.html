\n I came across a blog entry yesterday on Engle Matrix games. It was a person who says they've had a love/hate relationship with the idea for ten years. They always come back to it but then reject it. The reason was simple.

Too much freedom of action quickly dries up the well of creativitiy and quickly gets repetitive or esculates into sillyness.

I'm the Engle of EMGs and I hate to say it but I've seen the same problem. Heck I don't want to make up stuff all the time. Making up stuff seems like it should be easy but it isn't. I'm working on ways to give players a steady stream of outside input to keep games working but that is ducking the issue.

What is it about freedom that is so difficult?

One thing has to be the Czege Principle - where making up your own conflict is unsatisfying.

I can also see that our brains are good at pattern recognition but maybe no pattern creation.

I want to hear other people's ideas. Examples are even more helpful.

Thanks!

Chris Engle

    \n EXAMPLE: A Horror Story

    There are four players sitting down to a game. They are told that it will be a horror story. They have a map and a cast of shifty characters. They are given an opening paragraph about their having to spend the night in the spooky house where someone died years before.

    That's it.

    Beyond that, the system says you can make up actions that happen next. There are some kind of die rolls but bascially people are narrating the story - making it up as they go.

    There is a plot track that suggests what should happen in the game: Find clues, reveal the plot, have a big fight, aftermath.

    That is it.

    Many players stare at this and don't know what to do next. If one player steps up and makes a provocative move (basically becoming the bad guy) the others react against them and crush the first player. They are defeated - a fun game was had but only because a player step up to make it happen. Not because the game presented lead to that.

    This is what I've seen happen with a lot of Matrix Games.

    Chris Engle

    \n In my experience, a lot of games don't really tell you what to "do". They assume that you'll either figure it out by playing with veterans, or you'll just make something up and it's kind of not their problem.
    Don't get me wrong - plenty of these games have lots of suggestions about what to do, but they don't nail anything down. They just kind of fill in the world with possibilities and tell you to have at it (Vampire, I'm looking at you). In my experience, it's a lack of personal connection to anything in the story that lends itself to stereotypically sociopathic play - if I don't have a reason to care about anything or anyone, what's to stop me from just re-enacting Grand Theft Auto in your game? That could be what you want, but it often isn't.

    By contrast, a game like Kagematsu has them doing exactly one kind of story - the players are all women in a Japanese village in the 16th century. Their village is in trouble, and it is beyond the skill of the villagers to solve it. They must woo the traveler, Kagematsu, to ensure the survival of their community. Sociopathic behavior in that game would be overtly disruptive, and essentially outside the bounds of potential play. Being a sociopath is not acceptable - if your character doesn't have human feelings, the slow, delicate growth of intimacy between Kagematsu and the village women cannot happen as the rules describe it.

    \n Ben Robbins calls it the hot-seat in Microscope. "Okay, add something new to this! No, we aren't going to help." He jokes about how it burns but it's worth it.

    Just because being creative on demand isn't everyone's cup of tea doesn't mean it's wrong to design games that way, because some people love it. Like me.

    But sometimes my well runs dry, too. Usually it's because my internal critic is taking over. ("Too cliche! Too repetitive! Too boring!")

    And when I see other people freeze up, I usually think it's their internal critic as well. When I was playing Fiasco with my dad (never did anything creative in his life, afaik) and my brother (fiction writing professor of english) - my brother was the one who was blocked. Because he had this apparatus of self-judgment, ideas of what made a good contribution or a bad contribution. (At the end of the game, I said, "So - that was Fiasco." And my brother said, "It certainly was." I forgive him - it's his internal critic kicking his ass.)

    "Every man has inside himself a parasitic being who is acting not at all to his advantage" - Burroughs.

    A second, less common problem - you *did* have an idea what to add. You were getting all ready to add it. And then things changed so your addition no longer makes sense. That's partly internal critic again - ("My idea doesn't make sense anymore.") - partly something else. Resentment at having your idea ruined.

    \n The blank page problem is pretty common in all aspects of human life. If you ask someone "what do you want to eat?" they have a much easier time picking off a menu than giving you instructions for what to buy at a grocery store. The idea of infinite choice seems to have lots of promise, so giving up that "freedom" for whatever particular thing you actually put forward doesn't seem like a good tradeoff to your subconscious, so you start to find reasons to not say anything. It's even worse if someone says "You can say anything you want!" because then that raises to social pressure -- if what you say ends up being lame then your friends will think "what you want" is a lame story. When you have creative constraints and things to react to it's much easier to offer ideas because it doesn't feel like taking on the burden of "creating" something, it feels more natural and less like you're exposing yourself to potential judgment or criticism.

    In RPGs, I tend to start off slow and need to build momentum in order to connect with my character and engage with the fiction. If there's nothing to hook me in (either interesting mechanics that encourage me to do interesting things, or a compelling situation I need to react to) then things are usually really flat for me and I have trouble contributing. I often have trouble with games that expect characters to be strongly motivated right out of the gate in order to generate the gameplay.

    Posted By: Jim Crocker"The Czege Principle says that when one person is the author of both the character's adversity and its resolution, play isn't fun"
    I beg to differ on that "principle"; it is false. Play may well be fun in a narrative game when one person authors the adversity AND the resolution; it depends on the game.

    In my newest design; ATLAS, the players regularly do this, and still the game is a lot of fun. Of course; they do get to roll the die, and must adhere to it in the resolution. And there's always the danger of failure, which force you to concede the narration of the resolution to one of the other players. That simple trick makes every die-roll exciting, and do a lot for the strong interactive feel of the game. Still; succeeding, and narrating how you succeed, is much of the fun in the game. And why should it not be?

    But: I mention ATLAS mostly due to the fact that the game has a lot of freedom, with a minuscule system laying on top of an empty setting. The main framing mechanism is that you draw a part of the map each time you narrate something in the game, binding your character and its actions to the geography. The drawing of the map works as a mentally focussing element in the game, and makes it easy to play with. I believe it works like this; you get a blank sheet of paper, and don't know nothing about how you should fill it. But then you set pen to the paper, and while the lines are formed, your mind grows, letting your imagination take off and fly, by the fact that you don't focus on it; you focus on drawing the simple lines. Its kind of; when you take your mind off it, the imagination will grow wings.

    My sons (8 and 11) manages the game nicely by themselves, playing for themselves and with friends. We've had silly play with it, and serious play, and have had fun playing either way. It's fast and furious, and have a focussing action at the core of the method, and that makes for a lot of fun!

    \n Chris:

    You might want to look at Matthijs Holter's game "Fuck Youth (Fruit Hangs Low)"

    I started a thread about it a week or so ago. Anyway, it has a nifty mechanic that is decidedly theft-worthy.

    The game has scene cards, a bit over a dozen. Each card has a very short, evocative title suggesting the topic of a scene ( each geared to the core concept of FY, namely life in High School) and a suggested number of main characters/PCs involved).

    The cards are shuffled up at the start of the game, placed face-down and three are drawn and placed face up. The person taking the first scene can choose any of the three revealed cards as the basis of their scene. After the scene is over, a replacement card is drawn and play continues in this fashion.

    (As a wrinkle, some cards say "Play Right Now;" if one or more of those are face up, those have priority over regular scene cards)

    Now, I fully plan to steal some form of that mechanic for some stuff I'm noodling with. At bare minimum, it is a great set of training wheels for for folks who are just getting used to doing scene framing and staying kinda on track. It's also a pretty great idea for scenario based stuff where some direction is desirable, but too much constraint would feel like railroading.

    It also seems like a mechanic that could be adapted a bit in a few simple ways to give a couple other ways to play that wouyld be pretty cool too.

    \n The Czege Principle is distressingly vague about when a player is source of both adversity and resolution. You void the warranty if either of those decisions also involves 1) randomizers, 2) transformations of decisions made in a different context (i.e. chess doesn't violate the Czege principle if you view the main antagonism as the weight of your prior decisions), or 3) strong external goals (this is arguably why Prime Time Adventures needs fanmail).

    In that vein it falls from the theory of RPGs that says enjoying playing an RPG means your experience is both structured and surprising from perspectives you are willing to take. In which case, the Czege principle just says: when you experience adversity and resolution in play if it is not surprising, play will be less fun.

    - Mendel

    \n Good idea Bob

    The game rules I'm playing with this week (I've been going through a lot of total rewrites lately) are similar to this.

    The game is ten turns long - divided into five chapters. Chapters have a built in topic and a default outcome if the players do not prevent it.

    The first chapter is scenario building, which is all done roll on tables.

    The remaining chapters have a set number of turns (3 - 3 - 3 - and 1) so players have a little while to accomplish the task. What mainly happens in turns though is doing things to score victory points. I'm directly rewarding appropriate story actions so people don't have to think to hard - just pick stuff from the menu and build your own shrimp feast (I've been to Red Lobster recently).

    The last play test I did showed that this worked but I haven't repeated it to be certain. It puts the players in the position of filling in the blanks.

    \n Too much freedom kills because creative ideas don't spring wholly formed from your imagination (contrary to the lies that Romantic poets told you). Creativity works by building upon previous ideas. if you say "Do anything", then that does not give the other person a useful starting point. It's like trying to grow sugar crystals in a pot of hot water: you need a seed crystal for other things to start glomming onto, so that they can all crystallize as well. Without a good starting idea that inspires more ideas, people get stuck with the blank page problem/writer's block/analysis paralysis.

    When people are stuck for ideas, they look to the random media crap-soul we all swim in, and pull out the easiest idea. Not the best idea, but the easiest one. The simplest, laziest, most likely to provoke laughter from your friends idea. Silly ideas don't require a lot of thought, but give immediate positive feedback if the idea is silly enough to provoke laughter. So when people are stuck, they go for the simple, goofy answers that don't require much effort, rather than finding the best idea out of the thousands of possible answers.


    If you give them some seed content, then the players have something to build upon, something to crystalize their story around. This organizing principle means that they can start to make decisions based on what is best rather than on what is easiest. Good seed content starts the creative process going. Random die rolls spur on the creativity pretty well, while more evocative seed content like Ganakagok's Tarot deck work even better.

    \n I'm going to just out Chris and his MG mechanics.

    MG mechanics were originally designed for wargaming, as add-on mechanics. The reason that thye're a Big Fucking Deal is that they vastly, vastly improve wargames. Like, a Quantum Leap Forward Level of improving wargames, and they do it really simply.

    Well, at least I think they do. And it seems at least a small devoted audience of wargamers agree.

    Wargames are built for a relatively constrained level of action. It doesn't matter what game you've encountered, whether some kindtoy soldier tabltop game or a board game like Risk, Axis&Allies, or an old hex'n'chit type game, this will hold true. Within that scale that it's designed for, it will allow a certain amount of randomness.

    Now, sometimes, those games will attempt to address out-of-scale events. They tend to do it really horribly. Often their solutions are clunky, rarely used, ill-defined, and very open to misintepretation of intent, willful or otherwise. You'll especially tend to find this stuff in old SPI and Avalon Hill board wargames and sometimes in older historical miniatures wargames that attempt to cover an entire war or period of warfare.

    The thing is, those out-of-scale-for-Game-X mechanics in wargames are there for a reason. Wargamer geeks are also likely to read the hell up on whatever period they're into and learn all sorts of bitty details that tend to affect things.

    They're also likely to read up on specific battles and understand how the Butterfly Effect of events outside of the scale of the actual clash of arms in a given battle or campaign ( be it larger scale of politics, industry, cultural attitudes or related far away battles or a smaller scale of tactical advantages, bad maps, or personality clashes of leading officers) decidely impacted that wartime event.

    And they want that stuff in their game, because that kind of chaos, that For Want of a Nail Butterfly Effect, is interesting as hell. And challenging to deal with.

    MG mechanical concepts reduce all of those older attempts to get that stuff into a wargame to a really simple roll off. More than that, they allow the active participants to, at the moment of interest or conception of a Butterfly Effect initiating event, to attempt to work that into the ongoing game and see if that Butterfly Effect is created at all, and also follow it along to see the impact and where it might go to or how little it might actually matter.

    In this, it is very much like a lot of Narrativist games. It has a chance for something someone came up with Right Now! to impact later events in a way we didn't expect at the start of play.

    And, as with Narr-y kinds of play techniques, there was an evolutionary moment when someone thought, hey, y'know what? We could probably not just use this as an add-on to a system or an informal play-technique, we could really make this thing the core of the game and dump the other cruft, becaus ethis stuff is the stuff that's really getting us fired up.

I'm working on ways to give players a steady stream of outside input to keep games working but that is ducking the issue.

    What is it about freedom that is so difficult?

    One thing has to be the Czege Principle - where making up your own conflict is unsatisfying.

    I can also see that our brains are good at pattern recognition but maybe no pattern creation.

    Chris Engle
    \nThis post has been very topical for me, thanks.

    Right now, Im struggling with scenario or 'module' creation and I realize that its the freedom I have been struggling with. Specifically the scope of the initial situation and goals of the characters. I have been having blank page syndrome because, I think, Ive been sub-consciously setting my sights on grand-scale situation and grand-scale goals for characters. You know, I want this to rawk, I want it to be epic! And I realize that fiction usually dosnt work that way. You cant start epic, and even if I did manage to think of an initial situation and character goals that were epic, the GM and players would struggle with it also.

    So yeah, restraints. Restrict the initial scale is what Im now thinking, and its starting to flow easier. Make it start ff on a personal-scale, and let any epicness play out in time rather than try to design it in from the start. This should make it a lot easier for players and GM to pick up and run with too.

      \n I think Nick is spot on. Seed content is very important. In my gaming circles, we've been talking about "the third narrator" - there's the player, (sometimes) the game master, and then there's the fictional content coming from the system.

      A few examples:
      - Wandering monster tables (ah, okay, we've got five Troglodytes on our hands, let's see...)
      - The scene cards in Fuck Youth (oh, okay, this scene is about getting drunk, let's see...)
      - Fate cards in Archipelago (so, right, your character is getting hosed by something in the geography around her, let me think...)
      - Oracles, oracles, oracles
      - Rolling for your Holding at the start of an AW session (right, so suddenly there's scarcity and your gang goes crazy from lack of drugs, let's see what happens now...)

      So Chris, I'd suggest trying out a random event mechanic or something. Maybe each turn starts by drawing a random influence/event/rolling on a table/whatever so the players have something else than each other to react to (and build on)?
