    \n Those are very different games. What sort of thing do you like, Adam? What sort of group would you be running it for?

    Apocalypse World is in playtest. So don't bring it to the table thinking it's a finished game, which you can bring out and have fun. It's problematic, like all games in playtest. If you play it, you're doing it to give Vincent feedback.

    Capes is complex. Lots of rules to get your head around. It's also GMless, so you don't "run it" for other people, so much as playing with them.

    Dread is pretty traditional. It is the sort of game you run for other people. You play it because you like the setting. Run it for trad gamers or indie gamers wanting a change.

    Ganakagok is an indie narrative game. You, as GM, will be framing scenes for the characters. The characters fight for how they want the world to end (pretty much). You need players who will enjoy narrative challenge, throwing themselves into defeat, narrating the world as well as their character. Don't run it for trad gamers, unless they want to experience something different.

    Graham

    Posted By: skinnyghostPosted By: Brennen ReeceDiaspora, so you can get thefree dice.
    \nGood call - though I've heard a LOT about how it's not really meant to be a one-shot kind of game. Any truth to that?

    I'll speak to that. It is not a good one shot game.

    Why is that?

    First of all, because shared setting creation ("cluster creation") is a really beautiful, elegant process that takes time. It's not something you want to skip or rush because it's what makes the game more than just-another-adventure-game. You want to take your time with cluster creation, and enjoy that process.

    Character creation, like in SotC, is a fun process, but also a time consuming one. This is a game where you enjoy the energy you put into creating characters, and rushing/skipping it would again take something out of the game.

    Brad & Tim & Toph & Byron put a lot of energy into making the game elegant, delivering just the play experience they were looking for, with just the right amount of crunch. That play experience that the game delivers is a multi-session one, where you get to hatch plans, explore a shared setting, get into ship battles between planets, negotiate deals, muck about at a society-affecting level, etc.

    You would short-change the elegance of this system by running it as a one shot. You could do it (speed through cluster creation & character creation, land on a planet, get a mission, play through two sub-systems and wrap it all up), but it would be missing what's good about the game.
