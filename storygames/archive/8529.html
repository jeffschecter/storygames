\n So I'm working on my 'probably won't be ready in time' Indie Game Design Company Challenge project called Midnight Witness, and I realized that its a relationship map based game. You lay down Identity cards, and players connect the ones that are adjacent with Haps (happenings) and you have story cards inserted into the growing map between those cards in response. When I realized what I was doing, I began to wonder how many other games out there do the same thing. Elizabeth Shoemaker's It's Complicated does work on a relationship marker board for instance, which is probably why I like it so much. Out There, the game challenge entry I wrote of company number 2 rolls hooks which give a relationship between the character and the budding mystery too. There are probably a lot more examples, its just I don't see any easy way to search them out. So here I am, asking people to point the ones out that they know and maybe describe them a bit too.

    \n A Taste For Murder is a big relationship map game. You start by drawing the relationships between all the family members ("He was blackmailing her", "They were lovers").

    Then one of the family members dies and everyone has a relationship with the victim.

    Then you investigate everyone's relationship with the victim. Each time you successfully investigate, the relationship gets one step more sinister: "They were lovers" becomes "He used to beat her" becomes "He threatened to kill her" becomes "He threatened to kill her and their illegitimate child".

    Once a relationship has been made more sinister three times, it's a motive for murder.

    \n This is not an RPG, but it is interesting. In Fantasy Flight's Android board game, there is a relationship map showing how major organizations in the game world are connected to the Conspiracy. During play, you can place a puzzle piece that connects organizations to the conspiracy and one another.

    What's weird about it is that there's no narrative importance or impact to mapping the conspiracy. It is merely mechanical, providing a reward for placing a piece, and impacting your victory points at the end. Basically a relationship map is created as a side effect of reward-driven play.

    \n The story stuff seems to be important to the players of Android, though, from what I've observed. They read the flavour texts and savour playing the game slowly, so they don't miss stuff; they insist to others that they read the flavour text in their cards aloud, and when somebody gets a creative flash from something (such as the conspiracy puzzle connecting humans fist activists with the church or whatever), they share it eagerly - and all this happens without disrupting the actual gameplay. It's a lateral value add, just like the bling in all the other recent FFG big box games. Android is just interesting in that it's chock full of story stuff in cards, board, more cards, character sheets, some more cards, game mechanics (the game emulates a police procedural tv show to such an extent that it even includes personal side-plots for the characters) and a couple more different sets of cards for good measure.

    It's like the Descent campaign expansion or Arkham Horror, except even 2-3 times more plot. I love this development, I have no idea where it's going! I probably should go hitch myself on the wagon with some sort of boardgame that goes even farther with the passively ingested story content, if such a thing could be possible. As things stand, the only functional difference between these things and Fighting Fantasy game books already is that FFG turns out better game mechanics, flashier components and multiplayer game play.

    \n As I mentioned in the other relationship map thread, Rats in the Walls makes heavy use of a Relationship Map.
    \n
    \nIn loose terms, the Rel Map isn't necessarily people, it's hatreds. It can be people, ideas, practices, organizations, concepts, etc. Anything you can hate Hate HATE is fair game. Tying these hatreds together begins to connect the players. Perhaps the person you hate knows the person I hate. Maybe the organization you hate proselytizes the idea I hate. Stuff like that.
    \n
    \nAs the game is about changing the present by changing things in the past, the web also acts as a guide for how the ripples of your changes will spread to affect other things. Maybe you'll improve something for yourself, but it will make other things worse for you. Maybe everything for you will be perfect, but it'll fuck over someone else's situation.
    \n
    \nReCoil also uses one, but it's not heavy. It can be just a list, though I think it works better as a web. It helps set up how the NPCs in the scenario may be related, and can help build the situation once play commences.
