    \n An idea for the postapocalyptic genre: take Apocalypse World, which has a setting I'm quite fond of, and translate its crunch into Solar System. The games are methodologically very similar, so you'll find that many of the ideas from AW will translate directly. Moves become Secrets, character classes become Keys and so on.

    WIth a campaign starting from scratch, I'd either make the Keys with the players (essentially asking them what they want the game to be about), or I'd prep just 2-3 "core" Keys and ~1 Key per "character concept" or setting topic (think of how the individual cultures in TSoY also introduce a couple of Keys related to their theming). This would probably be less work and provide a more focused Keying environment than going through the entire genre analytically in advance and making a long list of Keys.

    The aforementioned Apocalypse World is actually a good example of how creative focus works in setting-building: it's a postapocalyptic game, but it doesn't attempt to be a generic postapocalyptic game. It's actually pretty focused thematically on the general level. If I were translating the AW setting into Solar System, I might have just two or three "core" Keys: the setting is about the past being a forgotten and unknown territory, about the postmodern fracture of civilization, and about sexuality as a social force that builds up new human society in the void. On top of these "general" Keys I'd then provide whatever individual character concepts imply. At no point would I go into a long dissection of postapocalyptic fiction in general.

    To say the same thing in simpler terms: first select your focal point (as per the 1st chapter of Solar System), then start developing your crunch. What is the interesting and specific thing about your setting, the thing you tell the players about before character creation? Will the game be set on the Pacific Ocean, where everybody lives on boats and oil platforms? Will it be about the last functioning nuclear power plant on the continent? A random bunch of strangers forced together by the circumstances? Something like that, a focal point the players can relate their characters to.

    \n First of all we have stake setting vs moves and predefined 7-9,10+ list based resolution. In Solar System, you set stakes and are free to change things around completely in the middle of a conflict. You have that stage where you go back and forth until everyone is completely happy about the possible outcomes. In Apocalypse World, you describe what you do and then parse it into one of the moves available to the character. You roll your dice and get either a 6 or less, a 7-9 or a 10+ and then apply some or all of the results found on a list.

    Then there's variable scene framing in Solar System and "what do you do?" based MC description in Apocalypse World. The rules text of Apocalypse World says interestingly little about scenes and instead advocates a more traditional "what do you do?" based way of doing things. I see TSoY and Solar System as "Oh no you fucking don't" games while AW is a "what do you do?" game.

    Apocalypse World has a singular emphasis on actor stance. Take the MC principle of always addressing the characters and not the players, for example. Whereas stake setting and the general character advocate approach makes Solar System have a wider scope for the players. Solar System seems to encourage being the audience and advocate for your own character rather than just sticking with actor stance.

    Then there's the "Make your move, but never speak its name" principle in Apocalypse World where the system option (move) the MC is choosing is intentionally not referred to by its name. In Solar System if a participant calls for a task related ability check or a conflict results or a conflict is escalated and extended, you want to be explicit about exactly what you are doing. I think this is related to the desire to facilitate the players being in actor stance, while Solar System supports a wider character advocacy position.

    The misdirection advice where the MC makes a decision based on real world stuff (cues and participants to use Vincent's blog terms) but then pretends to be making it based on fictional purposes (clouds) strikes me as a bit strange compared to Solar System where you can be far more up front and explicit in terms of the motivation for a given choice. Again, this probably has to do with the commitment to actor stance as drawing attention to decision making unrelated to the fiction can hamper that. The text really emphasis a commitment to making Apocalypse World real to the players.

    Also, compare AW play books with Solar System character creation. The difference is pretty incredible in terms of combinations and customization vs what are pretty much traditional classes. Solar System seems to be a lot more about players choosing what interests them in the moment and the transition of one interest to another as characters lose and gain keys, while AW playbooks are very specific and largely preset. Another person that I'll be playing with has played AW and said the experience reminded them of pre-gens that you rename and adjust a little. I get what they are saying, even if I think they're over stating the case a bit by comparing the playbooks to pregens. I think they're far more like traditional character classes.

    Basically I see AW as being very specific in its support of a single approach to play while Solar System is a lot more open and flexible. I see the lists inside of Moves (and the lists of Moves themselves) as preselected options according to what you call "leverage, propriety and scope." Apocalypse World is actually very supportive of traditional RPG play, but with an emphasis on participant positioning while Solar System's scene framing, conflict resolution, stake setting and character advocacy are supportive of less traditional play.

AW formalizes the character classes a bit by making it an explicit rule that you can't take crunch from another character class, while in TSoY the usual way to play it has always been that you're limited by your culture/niche/concept in character creation, while in play you'll get whatever new stuff the fiction dictates. Thinking about it, this is probably a primary reason for why SS has a skill system while AW doesn't - AW doesn't need that digging tool for determining events from abstract conflicts of interest because it requires players to stay on the bottom all the time.     \n Very interesting discussion here. I have a few thoughts which jump out at me:

    1. Apocalypse World playbooks are not really like character classes. Why not? There are a number of differences:

    * There's only one of each "type" in the world. You're not one of many, you're *the* one. It's much more of an archetype or story role than a class (in the sense of "this is what I do"). By choosing that playbook, you select an archetype and then you tell us how that archetype relates to the world or the story. ("This is what the Hardholder should be in this story. This is what I find challenging about the role of authority and the use of power.")

    * The playbooks are as much about "what kind of trouble should I expect?" as "what can I do/what are my strengths?". If a Hardholder is in play, we know to expect question of authority, mutinies, famines, war, and internal strife. If a Touchstone is in play, we're exploring the nature of hope and how it can spread from a single source outwards through society.

    * The playbooks give a starting point but do not say anything about future potential. There is no "path" laid out for you when you choose a playbook. You could go in any direction from there. More importantly, you *have* to significantly change direction, because pretty soon you will run out of improvement options and have to either retire the character or switch to another class.

    * Character development comes largely from the playbook switch. The character starts out a certain way, and is then transformed in the course of the story to become someone else. In Solar System, Keys are bought off and new Keys purchased. In AW, the carefree Battlebabe accepts responsibility and becomes the Hardholder, wielding power for the needs of others instead for her own desires. The Angel, devoting his life to saving people's lives, snaps and becomes the Gunlugger, only interested in retribution.

    This aspect is not immediately apparent when you read through the rules, but when you start playing, from the first few advances you'll see how much taking new moves can transform the characters (and should, I think). Consider how much it says about your Angel if you take the move "merciless" with your first advance. What if you take "frenzy", instead? ("When you speak the truth to a crowd...") It really pushes the character in different directions.

    It feels very different from "classes" in practice. A starting point, kind of like a pre-generated character role ("You're the captain of the ship, you're in charge; you're the person people go to for support; you're the one nobody trusts;" etc). But from the moment you choose your starting moves, your character's off in his or her own personal direction.

    2. I agree that there a huge advantages to a more "top-down" approach to conflict and story construction: when I play AW, I often really miss explicit conflict resolution. Free-and-clear stakes negotiation allows us to come to consensus on what's happening in much greater clarity. I think this is an important point, because in the last couple of years it's been very fashionable in these circles to look down on formal "stakes" and formalized conflict, probably because designers are exploring other methods for handling conflict in play.

    3. The way we're discussing Keys here (and the way they are discussed in Solar System) often makes me think that there is a little bit of confusion about Creative Agenda-related issues. I'd love to hear from Eero whether this is something intentional on his part (when writing Solar System), a distinction between the roles of the two types of Keys, or something I'm imagining.

    Clinton's Keys (from TSoY) strongly suggest a Narrativist approach to me: the various options suggest difficult moral choices for the protagonist. For example:\n
    Key of Faith
    Your character has a strong religious belief that guides her. Gain 1 XP every time she defends her faith to others. Gain 2 XP whenever this character converts someone to her faith. Gain 5 XP whenever this character defends her faith even though it brings her great harm. Buyoff: Your character renounces her beliefs.
    \nEven without looking at the buyoff, it seems pretty clear that this Key is all about a question of value: "How far will you go to defend your faith?"

    Here's one of Jason's:\n
    Key of the Follower

    You're a natural or disciplined follower, and easily fall in line behind whoever the leader with the most authority might be. 1xp for doing what you're told and backing up the boss; 2xp for following orders even when they seem questionable; 5xp for following orders that cause you harm. Buyoff: refuse to follow orders
    \nSimilarly, this Key challenges the character, encouraging the player to get deeper into trouble (since you get more rewards when you're in conflict with the authority's wishes).

    However, the focus of some Keys in the Solar System booklet are more tied to in-fiction motives ("Talk about vampires, interact with vampires..."). This seems to be what's being discussed in this thread, as well:\n

    On to 3:

    EDIT: Just wanted to add that in terms of communicating, the simple answer is yes, it's a sim thing. My longer answer is about me not accepting the necessity of creative agenda or its utility compared to other approaches:

    My position on the Big Model is that creative agenda only survived as part of it for social reasons and that the model actually works better if you toss creative agenda out and concentrate just on the arrangement of the elements of exploration themselves (and adding challenge and theme as additional elements of exploration).

    Even if I were to keep creative agenda as a thing, I also reject the notion that thematic play is the best indicator that something belongs in the "Story Now" category. Or that tough choices demonstrate theme when they could just as easily be about characterization. Or if it really is about theme, then it's about "getting it right" where "it" is an element of fiction. And thus all the indicators that "Story Now!" is what is happening are better explained inside a "Right to Dream" context when it comes to actual play. "Story Now" play is likely the attempt to get mimetic play that concerns itself with getting thematic elements to appear during play.

    Oh, I also think Lajos Egri was a terrible choice for a source of theory and that the idea that you're only dealing with themes when you are addressing moral issues is a load of crap. I think Ron Edward's use of Egri has damaged people's ability to identify theme without first connecting it to morality. It's alienated them from the much richer source that is contemporary literary theory in favor of an anemic theatre theory from 70+ years ago.

    As for "fighting from moving vehicles" not really being the material for Keys, I was inspired to make that one after reading the word "motif" again in the Solar System PDF section on making keys.

    What's a motif? It's a recurring element in a narrative that helps establish either a theme or a mood. If you give either the first Mad Max or the Road Warrior movies a look, the sounds of engines and the appearance of vehicles massively contributes the mood. In an RPG, it certainly can also be the foundation upon which thematic statements can be made. Like "every piece of technology can be turned to violent ends" for example. And note, it's still a thematic statement whether or not I paint that in a moral or immoral light or make no moral statement about it at all. Even if it's not connected to what a character wants or believes, it's still a thematic statement. Egri was all about loading the thematic content onto the characters, when there's just so many other ways to do it.

    \n My interpretation of the original TSoY text could well be wrong, but my intent in Solar System was not to revise the system much, but rather to merely rephrase and clarify it. Thus I don't think that my interpretation of what Keys are and how they're used differs substantially from Clinton's. My choice of language for the booklet was GNS-free, as I was mostly thinking of a baseline literate audience while writing it - not "lowest common denominator", but people who aren't familiar with Forgean theory discussion.

    My take on Paul's premise question is that Keys are (and have always been) in a loose and utilitarian relationship to possible premises that the game might address. That is, I think that it is not entirely useful to attempt tight coupling between preselected premise and Keys. This is largely because of the specific way Keys are selected and utilized in play: players select Keys on the basis of their incomplete understanding of their own characters and interests, and real play will then reveal its true, actual direction to them. Players are not in a position to select premise reliably at the point of play when Keys are chosen, and the game does not have strong guarantee of any given premise being addressed merely because it relates to a Key that is in play. Lacking these two features, it is impossible to claim that Keys have or should have unity with premise.

    A different game might attempt such reliable connection, but I suspect that it'd stray further away from roleplaying and closer to joint authorship. The key issue is that the fruitful void of an ordinary narrativist roleplaying game resides in large part in discovering the question, as well as the answer. You can see this in games like Zombie Cinema or Fiasco particularly well: in those games it's almost more exciting when you realize what the question (specifically) is, and the answer often flows relatively naturally from there. That discovery element is important, and a game that successfully locks down the premise during character creation would lack it.

    This is not to say that focused prep is not possible in Solar System: the game is specifically loose in this regard, the creative chemistry and goals of the players ultimately determine the balance between exploratory, developmental and resolving types of play. If players give their characters simple Keys that imply strong response to specific sorts of dramatic choices, then everybody at the table is more clear on what that character "is about" from the start. Likewise if you choose Keys that contradict each other. These choices make it easier for the SG to make the obvious connections: my character's all about murder and duty, so the SG has an easy time developing murder and duty content for him to engage. Not nearly all play is so front-loaded, though, and a tradition-embracing game like TSoY leaves plenty of space for less front-loaded play as well.

    Considering all that, what do the dramatic Keys or motif Keys (nice name, although some might confuse that with "motivation Key") do, if they don't imply premise? I'd say that the two main functions of these Keys are to imply plot arcs and direct motif development. Plot arcs are essentially thematic choices for players, a Key asks the player an implied question: will you seek bigger and bigger rewards off this Key? Will you even do the buyoff and therefore "finish" the plot arc implied? Motif development is more of a SG issue: given that this "Key of Vampire Slaying" and its implied plot arc require e.g. vampires to come up regularly, what am I going to do about it?

    (Motivation Keys are similarly somewhat dual in nature: on the one hand they act as pretty clear guidelines for the players in low-level roleplaying interactions, on the other hand they imply thematic choices where the motivation presented is at cross-purposes with other people or other motivations. Motivation and Dramatic Keys don't have any particular analytical connection, though - they just represent somewhat different, alternative ways of structuring and mechanizing drama. As the SS text implies, there's no particular reason why you couldn't have Keys that are structured differently from both of these.)

    Finally: I think that the two postapocalyptic Keys Nathan posted earlier are fine. The "Key of the Road Hog" is pretty specific, so I wouldn't use it in that form unless the campaign was centrally themed around vehicular shenanigans. That's a natural situation when you're coming into the campaign from a very generic angle of approach, though; you'll have lots of material that might prove too widely or too narrowly specced when actual play begins. Not just Keys, either, but Abilities and Secrets as well. A good approach is to calibrate this stuff according to the focal point as it develops. And if the focal point shifts, it's OK to revise the crunch as well; I don't write about this in the SS (it's a basic text, after all), but I've found that it's probably a better idea to revise too generic and too narrow crunch on the fly rather than letting them ride and living with the consequences. The difference is slight, so I'd only do it in a longer campaign, though.

    \n I'm not entirely convinced of the reality of the distinction between player-activated and SG-activated Keys. Of course there are tendencies, but ultimately the entire range of options relies on cooperation: the SG needs to provide a NPC in need for a player to make the choice to help them, and there is only so much that the player can do about ensuring their own Key-fulfillment conditions. If anything, I'd say that completely independent Keys are a problem: a player whose Keys can be triggered without significant creative interaction with other players is probably either intentionally or accidentally cordoning themselves off from the events of the game. For this reason I tend to be critical of Keys that depict incestuous events that the character causes himself, without caring anything about what happens around him.

    Ideally Keys are something the SG looks at hard and often from the viewpoint of a plot-writer who's been given a well-defined, expensive IP to play with: given that I'm writing a Batman story, what elements will the story have? I will be a poor Batman writer if Batman never gets a chance to e.g. shut off people near him due to excess feelings of duty, or decide to not kill a human monster due to commitment to life. Rather, one would assume that the SG guiding for Batman would intentionally work towards highlighting these things: the SG wins when the Keys are activated, or not activated, by player choices.

    From the viewpoint of this two-way dance the difference between the Key of Faith and Key of Road Hog is mainly in the specific sequence of interaction that the players engage in to arrive at a moment where the Key is scored: with the Key of Faith the SG introduces NPCs and the player picks and chooses which to interact with in Faith-related ways (this was the missionary Key, right?). With the Key of the Road Hog the player chooses to have their character own and occupy a vehicle, while the SG chooses to have a chase occur, and that enables the Key to function. In one of these Keys the SG is providing the background and the player is providing the immediate action, while in the other it's the other way around. Is this a significant difference? Maybe.

    \n Postapocalyptica is a lot like western as a genre, that's true. Especially the post Mad Max vision of a barren wasteland.

    That thing about Buyoff caught my eye. As I understand it, you've decided that a non-permanent Buyoff condition is better than one that is clearly permanent. This interests me, as I pretty much encourage the opposite thinking: buying off a Key is a permanent change in the character, and in dramatic terms it usually means putting that topic aside for good. If the game were a TV series, buying off the Key would be the moment when the writers genuinely decide that they've had enough of a given theme, and it will not recur anymore. (Of course it's difficult to find writers with enough integrity to stick to these decisions, but that's another story.)

    Consequently I think that "Give up drinking for good" is a fine Buyoff condition, while "Try to stop drinking" is not as good because it's more ambivalent. Technically these are the exact same thing, as both remove the Key, and neither actually prevents the theme from factually recurring - the player or SG can always take the game towards a direction that leads to the character's alcoholism recurring. There is no binding pre-scripting in the game. Mostly it's a matter of communication and decisive creativity: I like that Keys are only bought or removed as decisive commitments to new direction, rather than mere experiments.

    For characters going back and forth on a topic it's often better to have them declare in-fiction that they're going to give up drinking, but keep the Key: essentially the player is reserving the option of sinking back into alcoholism later if opportunity presents itself. They can always sell the Key later, if they decide to permanently quit this element of their character. If the very act of going back and forth is the important theme for the character, then a Key related to that ("Key of Indecision" or whatever) would be appropriate as well or instead of the original Key.

    \n That all makes sense, so I don't think that we're disagreeing. I definitely think that the coupling of the fictional events and the decision to buy off a Key is very loose - the event indicated in the Buyoff can occur without the player choosing to remove the Key.     \n Thanks for the write-up, I like where you're going here! The car guy'll be easy to play with as SG, you just need to offer opportunities for him to be the big man because he has a car that can take things from point A to point B - and then you need to show him how dependent he is on the community to provide the technical infrastructure that he needs to keep the car going over time.     Using Effects for equipment is entirely kosher. In fact, I suspect that it's the more straightforward and pleasing method for most groups. The SS text is a bit ambivalent on the topic because I wanted to retain backwards compatibility and blow the possibilities wide open. Clinton's equipment system is tricky (you can read the equipment section in World of Near if you want to see what I ultimately ended up doing with it), and a low-crunch campaign might do pretty well by just doing Effects.

    Of course, that leaves the special muscle car, which is definitely Equipment in the strong sense. As you see in the SS booklet, you could do this sort of important equipment in two ways: the classical way with the equipment ratings, or with Effects, in which version the Secret of owning the car simply gives the character a free Effect. The feel in these is pretty different, of course, and if the player likes fiddling with the equipment ratings, then that's a fine choice.

    (Of course if you're using Equipment ratings, I recommend looking at that World of Near chapter on the topic. I specifically wrote that to fix how strong ratings are - they're easily the most overpowered piece of crunch in classical TSoY, for better or worse. That feel might work well in a post-apocalyptic setting, of course, where equipment is sparse and a big deal.)

    Also, a reminder about how to do equipment maintenance when using only Effects: say that a character has an Effect, "My sharp knife 1/I". They were in a knife fight where they expended all but one bonus die out of the Effect. Now they want to "fix" the knife so it's ready for the next situation. In that situation you shouldn't just have the player roll a new Effect next to the old one, so they end up with two Effects (untidy, and mechanically dull), but rather have them roll the old Effect into the maintenance check as bonus dice - one bonus die in this case. This way the dice still left in the old Effect don't go to "waste" when you reroll the Effect, and you maintain the idea that each separate line of crunch on the character sheet corresponds to a different fictional idea. Of course this means that maintaining your equipment might potentially make it worse than it used to be, if you roll really badly. Rolling a failure on a maintenance check breaks the equipment, or establishes that it's too used up to be fixed anymore.\n
    Is it appropriate to have effects created during refresh scenes?
    \nYeah, definitely. We occasionally discuss this topic, and some people seem to think that refresh scenes should be "no dice at all" to count as that, but I think that it's fine to engage mechanics as long as the scene substantially fulfills the dramatic requirements of a refreshment scene. If the scene involves both a refreshment and equipment maintenance, then good for them. Of course it's not refreshment if the scene is just working on equipment, it needs to have that playful social quality that shows the human side of the characters.

    Another typical situation is one where a scene starts as refreshment, but then changes into something else as e.g. guards come in to capture Conan and drag him away. It still counts as refreshment if we've established that refreshment happened. You can basically only cancel a refreshment scene in progress by an explicit character choice to not let their hair down in this situation after all - say the SG frames a refreshment scene with a NPC the PC hates, and the player backs out dramatically at the last moment.
