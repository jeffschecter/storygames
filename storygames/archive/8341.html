\n Background: I've been playing with my group steadily for at least a decade - longer, for two of the players. They are all intelligent, thoughful and creative. We are all good friends away from the table.

Recent experience with my group has me scratching my head with bemusement - imagine a big cartoony \u201c?!\u201d suspended above my cranium. We had spent the better part of two years playing in Jeff\u2019s WFRP game, and after that wrapped up I pitched Burning Wheel to my group because a) I like it a lot and b) I thought it could work for my group. I took great pains to explain the fundamental differences between BW and WFRP, especially the Intent, Let It Ride and BITs/Artha cycle concepts, and they all were enthusiastically on-board....

...until last Wednesday when we all talked and agreed that the game wasn\u2019t working for us. From my perspective, the players didn\u2019t engage their characters\u2019 BITs and therefore did not fuel the Artha cycle. Without that, BW grinds to a halt. My players also didn\u2019t put a lot of effort into understanding the details of Task Resolution and said they found the mechanics clunky (I didn\u2019t). Jeff stated that he felt that the designer of the game devised mechanics specifically to address problems that didn\u2019t exist with our group. I found that to be an interesting observation.

So, having decided to boot the BW game we turned instead to Mongoose Traveller. We turned the evening into a character creation session, and I immediately could see a change come over my group. While playing BW, I sensed an undercurrent of frustration and a lack of genuine fun. In contrast, just making characters for Traveller resulted in obvious enjoyment from everyone, even when they were just observing while another player made rolls for his term advancement. They took special pleasure in the Connection rules, which let two PCs invent a connection between themselves whenever an Event occurs during a career term. By the end of the session they had woven a very interesting and imaginative backstory tapestry for each of their characters. Rock on.

Now, this is not intended to make any value judgements between Burning Wheel and Traveller. However, I\u2019m left wondering exactly what happened to generate such a negative experience in the first case and such a positive experience in the second. In Burning Wheel, the players are explicitly given a tremendous amount of agency with respect to their characters \u2013 using their BITs, the players have great power to push the game in whatever direction they want. The more they push, the more they are rewarded. But for whatever reason in my game the players did NOT push at all, despite the nudging and prodding I gave them. And the game failed.

In Traveller, there is no such narrative mechanic similar to BW\u2019s Beliefs. There is also no mechanic for rewards \u2013 no Artha points, no XP, nothing. There is only in-game achievement. The rules make no effort to provide any special agency to the players with regard to the story \u2013 on the contrary, character generation pretty much subjects the PCs to the fickle winds of fate. So what do the players do? THEY PUSH. Off they go, inventing relationships to one another, fleshing out Enemies and Contacts, dropping ideas of possible story lines, etc. All of the things for which Burning Wheel is specifically built.

I think I know why my group responded this way. Burning Wheel is an example of System In The Fore: the mechanics are right up front and indispensable to the game. Everything one does engages the system, often in very obvious and structured ways (e.g. Duel of Wits). Traveller, on the other hand, is an example of System In The Background: the system is there, but only comes into play when invoked. For some reason, the character-driven play style that I thought would happen with Burning Wheel is instead happening with Traveller, which is not at all what I expected.

Still scratching my head.

    \n It's possible that they were much more enthusiastic about you GM'ing and/or giving a game a try that you were advocating, and not actually invested in the game and its mechanics upon reading it on their own. I had fun playing BW with Luke but I've read it three times and still not felt comfortable enough to try running it myself or even playing in a long to mid-term game.

    And then perhaps their sheer enthusiasm for the Traveller game was what powered their investment in their "background" narratives. Burning Wheel has so many sub-systems it throws a wide net over finding some one thing that any given individual might not care for. Some guys might love Fight and dislike the Artha tracking stuff. Others may just not like Duel of Wits scripting or non-binary negotiated outcomes. Others might just not like the lifepath stuff, or the lack of baked-in balance among the character types.

    As Mark says, it will be interesting to see what carries over from the Traveller pre-play.

    \nSeeing as they've played a long WFRP (Warhammer Fantasy Roleplay) campaign, I don't think this is the reason here.

    As for the original post...

    I've often found, that a system that integrates normal, everyday roleplaying stuff (discussion, describing etc) heavily in the mechanics can be a pretty tasking thing. As a player I don't care much for systems that reward roleplaying mechanics-wise. It sometimes leads to players trying to outplay one another. While this might be fun from a drama point of view, it also has a tendency to kill a lot of the immersion and the comfortable, natural feel that - IMO - make for the best games.

    If you start to concentrate on breathing, it really becomes a major task and gets pretty uncomfortable after a while.

    \nI'm confident that they will pull those events and connections into the game going forward. Another major difference between BW and Traveleller: the former gives one complete control over one's lifepaths, relationships, associations and reputations while the latter gives one very limited control. During character generation in Traveller there is a significant likelihood that your character will be shunted off onto an unexpected path. Case in point: the character that specifically aimed for a Pilot failed to enlist in the Navy and got drafted into the Marines. Another character in the Navy set out to be on the bridge but ended up earning glory in the Engineering section repelling boarders. A third got drafted into the Army, got kicked out, became a criminal, and finally wound up as a Law Enforcement agent. Only one, the Scout, spent his entire career according to plan.

    I think that for my group the randomness of character generation struck a very positive nerve. The table was cheering when the Scout survived his seventh straight term! There was a high degree of engagement with the process, and a lot of creative effort expended to weave an interesting tale of how these four people came to team up. Maybe surrendering to the whims of character generation fate was just a welcome change from the total-control point-buy scene.
