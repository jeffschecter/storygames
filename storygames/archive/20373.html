\n Hello people!

I'd like to introduce a little Dungeon World hack I made on my quest to create a midway point between Dungeon World and Burning Wheel.

You see, I like what DW does and I like what BW does, but I have some gripes about both. DW is excellent for that D&D adventure feel with the smoothness of the AW engine, but I wouldn't use it for grittier fantasy since the archetypal classes, XP & levels and alignments feel too stereotypical and D&Dish.

On the other hand BW is just a bit too crunchy for my taste, especially Fight, but I like the lifepaths, the learn-by-doing advancement and beliefs to drive a dramatic game. So I stripped Dungeon World of the things I didn't like and slotted in the things I like about Burning Wheel, thus: Dungeon Wheel.

Below is a link to the 3 4 7 page PDF. It only shows the changes I've made to DW (Character creation using BW lifepaths, stat advancement without levels, beliefs and "artha" for DW and some miscellaneous changes to moves/stats/how skills from lifepaths work).

Dungeon Wheel

You need to be familiar with both DW and BW to understand it, but I'm hoping there are quite a few people here that meet the criteria. I'm visioning this hack for fast-ish paced adventure drama gaming, less heroic and lower fantasy tone than DW, kind of like medieval Apocalypse World with beliefs to drive play and flag content for the GM. It certainly shouldn't require dungeons to be interesting despite the working name.

Now the question to those that have played said games more extensively, do you think this could work? Would it satisfy your adventuring needs on the DW side, and your desires for drama on the BW side?

    \n I've seen Class Warfare mentioned numerous times and read what I could find online, but I don't have it. I ignored it because it kept the archetypal idea of classes intact, but I never thought to use it as a master index of compatible moves. That's an awesome idea, I'll add a suggestion pointing that way to the PDF!

    In my vision moves from lifepaths are quite rare, I'd make moves for sorcery, faith, armor training and such special mechanics, but it certainly doesn't have to be that way. You could make a move for Ditch-Digging, but I'm not sure how useful that would be. Now that I think about it, some of the mechanical traits could be converted into moves.

    This is one of the things I was considering, does a character need a lot of moves or is it fine to have mechanically very similar characters. My current answer is that mechanically similar characters feel more human and gritty instead of special-snowflake heroes. But it might not be the final answer.

    As for the stats, the stat bonuses from lifepaths are a good guideline combined with the general idea of the lifepath. A mental stat bonus can be social or intelligence related, but the lifepath usually breaks the tie. I'll add some words about this to the PDF as well.

    Another thing I'm still considering is the set of basic moves, do they support the right kind of interactions in the right way, but I guess I'll need to playtest those to get any real data. The set of adventurous DW moves plus a bit more drama oriented support in the form of Read a Person feels generic, but workable.

    I have seen your your descriptive damage hack, yeah, and I'm really torn about it. I like the idea, it's definitely grittier than diminishing HP totals and I like BW's take on damage and injury (easy to get injured, hard to die), but if I'm working with Dungeon World as the base game to hack I'm stuck with numerical damage in numerous places in the system. It wouldn't feel entirely elegant to slot in descriptive damage for PCs, but keep damage dice against monsters.

    HP has its advantages for the fast-paced action the move framework supports, though. It's a guaranteed, transparent plot armor for a hit or two, so the players can take more risky actions without the game stalling for a planning break. Using a riskier injury mechanic would slow down the conversation I fear.

    Another reason I didn't adopt the damage hack was that I like using tried and proven components instead of playtesting too many experimental mechanics at once. Your hack would require more fine-tuning than just lowering maximum HPs and introducing serious injuries via Last Breath. If I decide to make more radical changes to DW I'll reconsider it.

    So in the end I feel DW does descriptive damage well enough if you keep fiction first in mind during combat. Now I started wondering if your hack could be used as an extended combat subsystem though... Likely unnecessary, but a fun thought experiment.

    Did you get to playtest your hack? Did you further fine-tune it, for example in terms of healing and appropriate maximum HPs the PCs should have?

    \nYep, my thoughts are currently along those lines. The idea I'm experimenting with is that the moves you get from lifepaths represent the genre relevant skills you learned during that part of your life, these "skill moves" having individual modifiers that improve when you use the move and there being harsh penalties when using skill moves with a root stat (so unskilled use might mean you can't get a full success result).

    This means there's no big pool of basic moves to rely on, your character just isn't very good at resolving situations they're not used to. Which leads to mechanical character change when they start trying.

    I'm writing this out if someone spots clear mistakes in my design ideas and I can then quickly correct them. Also, 2d12 to make the range of viable modifiers larger, since +2 is the absolute maximum I'd allow in a darker toned game with a 2d6 roll!
