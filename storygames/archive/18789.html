\n So I'm living in Costa Rica with the wife for a while, and I'm totally missing gaming. We used to play on fantasy grounds and we had some epic player interaction.

I'm probably going to use http://roll20.net.

Does anyone have an idea how many players is too many in a situation like that? We usually play traditional systems (BRP) in a narrative style, and I've been running a lot of Dungeon World. I don't know what system I'm going to use yet, but I'd be open to suggestions. It seems like 3 players is the max for a Skype game. Has it been your experience that chat play can handle more or fewer players?

    \n Whatever chat system you use should support multiple, simultaneously viewed chat channels. At a minimum: OOC Rules (dice, rules clarifications/references) and IC Narration (dialog, character actions, GM descriptions).

    Ideally, it would also support replying inline (Google Wave was SO good for doing prep/setup work!!!) or to specific chat lines, so that things stay threaded. Otherwise, you get some odd out-of-sync confusions.

    Go old skool: have a third channel for OOC Player Planning and then only let the Caller enter info into the main narration channel.

    Save ALL channel logs: the narration one is half way to being a story (and a great AP post, if you post long-form AP); and the rules one is a great way to recap for things like XP or skill checks. Pop them onto a wiki to keep track of your whole game's history.

    \n I'm not sure about Skype, but by text, 3-4 + GM seems to be the sweet spot. It'll really help if you play in a venue that tells you when someone is typing.

    \n Skype Chat's "somebody is typing" is certainly invaluable, but you should encourage players not to type out whole paragraphs before hitting enter. It tends to stall the game when there's quiet space of "Typing..."

    I agree that having OOC and IC is a must; I tend to handle dice mechanics in the OOC channel itself. It's a sort of "at the table" thing. Also make sure your players are all on the same page with tense and person. It's challenging when one person is typing 1st person present tense and another is typing 3rd person past tense.

    Finally, get your players to put some thought into it, but make sure they're not taking it too seriously. I had a number of players who took too much time crafting their chat prose, and it really slowed the game down.

    Interesting point: Wave's real-time text transmission got transferred over to Google Documents, so you could open a shared Google Doc and have the same effect.

    \n If you and your group like to try out new stuff, I'd highly recommend the overlooked gem Code of Unaris. It's free, it's designed for chat-play (it has mechanics that allow the players to change words in the GMs sentences to change the situation/challenge), and it has a pretty cool setting with outlaw mathematicians and wormholes through time.

    I ran about four sessions of it years ago (with four players, I believe), and it worked well. Fast typers and quick thinkers will be rewarded.

    \n So... I'm picking a system. I was originally thinking of using an AW variant, since I like Dungeon World so much, but it seems like you need one that was hand-designed for a particular setting, and I'd like to run some kind of modern pulpy horror (like aliens, or World of Darkness, etc).

    My finalists for a generic system are:
    Cthulhu Dark (using conditions for harm, like Lady Blackbird)
    Lady Blackbird

    They seem to have different strengths and weaknesses. I've found that in LB, the characters get too awesome too quickly for a longer campaign, but maybe that's ok. I could keep it shorter. I already miss the 6-, 7-8, 10+ from AW, so I like how LB has you escalate on a failure. But LB always struck me as only being appropriate for a one-shot: that it doesn't scale well into something larger.

    I love that Cthulhu Dark lets the characters be awesome. I'm worried that my players might want it a little more crunchy than this (Hey! I have an automatic weapon, how come I don't get a bonus?), but after playing DW enough, I think I can pull those kind of advantages in the fiction, or give an extra die if appropriate.

    In a chat-based game, a subtle change to the interaction of calling for rolls can change things dramatically. For example, in LB, the GM is supposed to set a difficulty, which is an extra communication step not necessary in Dungeon World or Cthulhu Dark.

    Any thoughts on these, or recommendations? Any of my assumptions wrong?
