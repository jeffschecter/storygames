    \n My first Game Chef was 2006. Until then, I'd hung around the Forge and designed a couple of things, but it hadn't really come together.
    \n
    \nI designed a game called Euthymia, which was all dystopian. I loved doing it. Most importantly, I got more feedback in that week than I'd had for months beforehand. It was a real learning experience for me. It's one reason why I really like the forum-based nature of Game Chef, as it was, because it really helped me.
    \n
    \nThe other thing that was really useful was giving feedback on other people's designs. You spot what works and what doesn't and that all feeds back into your own designs.
    \n
    \nMaking a game in such a short time is easy. You throw around ideas for a couple of days, in your head, then put some mechanics in and start writing. It won't be good, but you'll get it done, and then you can go back and see what was good and what wasn't.
    \n

    \n I don't think Game Chef has helped me improve anything, but the challenge is a wonderful creative constraint, and has spurred me to start (and finish) some things I'm really happy about. It can also be very satisfying and fun to help others - the atmosphere of mutual support has been something I've enjoyed in the past.

    I'm not sure advice on how to be productive and successful can be generalized, and the format is so gauzy and wide open this year advice from past contests seems pointless.

    \n I think I started in 2006 as well, and did pretty well for a first attempt. If I remember rightly I took second place with Liquid Crystal. My 2007 offering wasn't quite so strong, but I liked it anyway. I was ill during 2008 and had to bow out of the competition, though I did enter some stuff for the art part of the contest which received some nice comments.

    I don't think my game writing skills have especially improved as a direct result of Game Chef, though the practice is always welcome. However it usually does provide some interesting inspiration for game concepts that I wouldn't otherwise consider writing, so it's more valuable to me as a source of inspiration.

    I'm happy with the status of my Game Chef games as of the end of the competitions, but I often think about revising, expanding, illustrating and re-releasing my favourite entry from a different contest (Reversed Engineer) because Dream Catcher's mechanics are amongst my favourites. They could use a little tweaking, but I don't have the money for illustrations so there's not much impetus for me to go ahead and rewrite it. Maybe if I win the lottery or something,

    \n My first Game Chef was 2002 and I had joined the Forge just a few months earlier. Before Game Chef, I had never tried writing a playable draft of a game and I don't think I really succeeded that year either, since I picked a concept that was a bit too ambitious to be achieved in a week. This also happened with my second Game Chef in 2003, though the stuff I generated then would later go on to inspire parts of John Harper's Agon. I think that's an important thing, actually. A immense amount of brainstorming and creation happens in Game Chef and it's hard to know who will find stuff that you create interesting or helpful and when the results of that will actuall manifest. Most games are flash in the pan and disappear, but this doesn't mean that they don't have an impact.

    My 2004 game actually got finished -- actually inspired by Tiki Gods from GC 2003, speaking of unexpected influences -- and I still remember 2004 as my favorite of all the contests. Really cool stuff was flying around -- Mountain Witch, Polaris, Ganakagok, Dance and the Dawn, and so many others -- plus, by then, I knew some of the folks (though not in person) involved and it felt like I really want working alongside a group of peers.

    2005 I was a judge and may have been responsible for the Shab Al-Hiri Roach not winning. That was my first encounter with Jason Morningstar, actually. 2006 attempted another ambitious game that was never finished but may come out in some form next year, since I finally know how to write it. 2007 was another ambitious, incomplete game. 2008 I made something playable but not actually complete or really wholly functional. And now, in 2009, I'm running it.

    So I guess my war story is... you don't necessarily have to design something on the edge of your ability, because that will make the contest much harder to finish. I tend to pick a concept and premise that I don't already know how to render as a roleplaying game. For example, 2006 was: a group of bodhisattvas sort through all of human experience to try to find the beings that will create the world to come. And then I try to figure out how to make that game work over the course of the contest. The problem with this approach -- which has led me to learn a great deal about design but only produce 1 finished Game Chef game -- is that it's too much work, I think, to both figure out how to design a completely new type of game and write/playtest your game in the same short period of time (a week or two). It would have been better, I think, to pick a game concept that I mostly know how to write and then be surprised by the unexpected things that inevitably come up when you work on a creative project. In this respect, the designs I've done for smaller contests -- Shreyas' lesbian ninja contest, Jason's Sight & Sound, Graham's Lil' Game Chef last year -- have been much more successful because I didn't treat them as the Big Shindig in which I must prove my EXTREME DESIGN CHOPS.

    My other advice is to write something for your home gaming group, something that they'll get really excited about playing/playtesting and you'll be really excited to play with them. Like I said earlier, the vast majority of Game Chef games are flash-in-the-pan designs that we never hear from again. But if you can go as far as to actually play them, I think the likelihood of them being both 1) successful as games and 2) helpful in your overall development as a designer -- even if you only play them once -- is much greater.

    \n Last year was my first Game Chef. I did Little Game Chef as well...so maybe that was 2 years ago? I can't remember the sequence of events. So anyway.

    Best part was making online friends that I've gotten to know much better the past (couple?) year(s?). I believe this came directly from my offer to trade critiques, one-for-one. I think I turned over 10 detailed critiques and got some really good feedback as well (as well as some nice-but-not-specifically-useful attaboys). The fact that so few (compared to the # of entries) folks took up my offer was enlightening.

    Worst part was, *for me* (so don't get your panties in a wad), a sense of general disconnectedness in my head after the event. There's a lot in that to unpack...lemme see. Like, I totally didn't connect with the Jeep entries, and their presence felt like either a distraction or proselytizing. And watching what did, and did not, gain the general attention of the event usually left me either discouraged or confused. On that note, I thought the anonymous submission system in LGC went a long way in my mind toward softening that second bit.

    The general negativity of some old-time GC participants was also kind of a buzzkill. I now know who to ignore, next time through.

    Mostly it was a chance to "get" the indie lets-make-a-game hobby folks better. It prompted me to start doodling on actual design again, after more than a decade away from it. And I made some friends. So I'll be doing it again this year I think.

    \n Here is what I wrote on the Forge after the first Game Chef:
    "This game is getting published. After judging, I'm going to post a revision / playtesting thread. Are you ready to hash it through with me?"

    I can't really say it better now. The game referenced is Polaris, btw.

    Edit: Okay, I have a lot more to say. For a lot of people, including me, Iron Chef is their first real live-fire experience with game design. I mean, sure, lots of tweaking and twiddling and a game for your friends (I'd written a couple of LARP systems and half of a system for an individual tabletop campaign), but here's a game, and it's yours and you're designing it in front of God and everyone. And you can't fuck around and be coy with tinkering around your social mechanics or some bullshit. Get it done, get it complete, get it out.

    I strongly, strongly suggest designing your game in a week. Take the first three days for concept, basic rules, and basic structure, take the rest of the time to fill out that basic structure with shit like skills or trait lists or spells (if you've written a game that doesn't require it, write examples. Or just drink gin.) Then do playtesting and bullshit or, better yet, don't. Let it sit for six months, reread it, revise it, then do your playtesting.

    A few months ago, someone asked me about my creative process regarding Polaris. At the time, I was living in Beijing, the first time in my life I'd ever lived outside of the US. During the time of the contest, I was studying Chinese around 12 hours a day during the week, going out drinking and dancing all night on the weekends, and swimming in a sea of strange language, which probably comes across in the original text. I would write the game in the middle of the night, alternating between an all-night cafe, where I could concentrate but had no internet access, and a smoky internet bar surrounded by counterstrike addicts, where I had internet access but couldn't concentrate.

    "It is quite possible," I told him, "that I was not in my right mind when I wrote it."

    There's a lesson in that somewhere, I suppose. Oh here's one: write your game tightly, around your own life. Don't take time off to write. Write in the time you had off anyway. Your day-to-day life is where you get your creative spark from.

    \n My first Game Chef was 2006. My submission was called Stained Glass; a game about knights of a mystical order being interviewed by a celestial council to ascertain worthiness for canonization as a saint of the order. The concept was interesting; but being relatively new to hippie games, I struggled to find the truly important elements of the concept that should be supported by the mechanics. In the end I essentially used a stripped down version of the DitV conflict mechanic and the game fell flat. I haven't touched the game since.

    In 2007, I had every intention of participating in the Game Chef event; but on the opening weekend of that Game Chef, on the way to visit my parents, my wife got hit with a really nasty Gall Bladder attack that culminated in surgery to remove the glogged organ. As a result, I was unable to get a game written by the deadline. I decided to continue writing my game unofficially, even though I wouldn't be able to submit. As a result, I developed a game called Keystone Crimewave, a lighthearted competitive game designed for larg-ish groups. The players are split into two 'teams' - the crooks who are planning a heist, and the cops who are trying to catch them. The game was inspired by movies such as Disorganized Crime, the Big Hit, Snatch, Police Academy, A Fish Called Wanda, Hot Fuzz, and many other movies of that vein. I managed to write the majority of the game's rules; but did so old school style, with a pen and paper. I have yet to finish transcribing all those rules into an electronic medium. I think the game, however rough, would be playable as a sort of party game. If I ever manage to find my original notes, I may actually finish transcribing them and try to playtest the game.

    Last year, I entered Game Chef and developed a game called Witch Hunt. It was by far my most complete and most viable game of any of my Game Chef attempts. It's still very rough; but I was lucky enough to have a group playtest it once so far (not a group I was part of). With some hard work I might be able to turn it into a decent game. I recently dusted it off to do some work on a Beta playtest version of it; I don't know how long it'll be before I have such a thing done though.

    I missed out on Graham Walmsley's Little Game Chef earlier this year; but I do hope to participate in the upcoming Game Chef. It should be extra interesting now that Game Chef is under new management, so to speak.

    \n I participated in GC 2003 and ended up getting an honorable mention with Tiki God for most ridiculous or something. It's been awhile, but I remember the contest experience being very enjoyable and friendly. There probably wasn't more than a dozen or so entries back then and that had a lot to do with that feeling I think.
    \n

I had enough time to make a nice collage cover image and throw some friendly taunts at the other contestants. It was a good time and allowed me to blow off some creative steam in a manner that I wouldn't have if left to my own devices.
    \n

Try to design a game that needs somewhere closer to six months of thought and development to really come together, have your contest entry show shadowy but promising possibilites, and if you're still intrigued by the idea after the contest continue to work on it. From what Jonathan wrote above, he is obviously aware of this and I assume it's one of the reasons this coming GC will be a multiple deadline affair.

    \n I entered Game Chef 2008, and found it a wonderfully fertile ground to germinate new ideas, AND bring them forth to fruition. I'd been futzing to myself for some time about like, maybe designing a game, y;know, sometime, about, er, something. . .but GC2008 kicked my but into serious gear with (A) tight time constraints, (B) unanticipated creative prompts and (C) a productive atmosphere of mutual accountability, encouragement and feedback.

    Unpacking that: first, writing a game in a week needed I needed to DO it. Like Ben said, there's no hemming or hawing about halfway functional resolution mechanics or that incomplete magic system or whatever. No buts. Just sit down in the workshop, and make an automobile that cranks over when you turn the key. If you can drive it, sputterking, around the block, so much the better. (I'm currently attempting to kick myself into gear thusly with my growing-pains fairytale game for Jake's Awesome Contest.)

    The factor of outside creative prompts is actually the biggest reason I'm attracted to design contests: "Uh, I don't have any ideas! Someone tell me what to write about!" If you tell someone to 'think of something cool!" or "say something brilliant!" they freeze (Graham talks about this, of course), but if you ask, "what sort of game would you write about Unicorns?" you'll get an interesting response, or provoke interesting thought. It's totally what I need when I'm presented with the wide vista of "anything" that I could be writing a game (or a story, or a song, or. . .) about. The art "ingredients" of the '08 contest provided me with the fodder for a game I'm proud of and enjoy thoroughly, and would never have thought of designing on my own. (Also, the artists, David Bove and Donna K. Fitch have been gracious enough to grant me the full use of that art merely for the courtesy of attribution, which is a great boon for a passionate amateur designer with no budget.)

    The forum atmosphere was pretty cool and helpful for me, as I recall. For starters, I was strongly encouraged by my feedback loop to run with the most audacious and ambitious of my game ideas, which I might have shied away from otherwise. And the general encouragement atmosphere, indeed the very knowledge that others know of my labors and are striving alongside me, helped me push forward to finish the design in time. The only downside of the system was that after that intense week of brainwork, it was hard to muster the energy for mutual feedback. but I believed in the system, and forged ahead with it. And come to think of it, some of the feedback was kind of awkward because folks were confused about what a fresh Game Chef entry is supposed to be. F'rinstance, telling me I need some examples of characters or whatever is a bit disheartening because my week's work went into the raw game mechanisms, examples can come later, right? Then again, such comments DID hel;p inform what needs to go into the more fleshed-out text, so it's all good.


    Now, after the dust has settled, I've found that the experience was creatively rewarding and productive. My game, Spectre of the Beast has gone forward to a fun, playable and maturing design, with an ashcan and everything. I found I had to put it in a drawer and let my brain rest, then come back to it in like 8 or 10 months and dust it off to get it readable and playable. And I found that I had a for-real game on my hands, not just a nice theoretical exercise, and that others could play it and have fun. Which isn't surprising, I suppose, because I've never been able to throw myself into a creative exercise without "meaning it"--this song, however silly, is meant to be sung; this story, however stilted, is meant to be read; this game, however experimental, is meant to be played.

    I'm not entering GC this year, partly because i'm still fine-tuning my LAST entry, AND I've found other contests this year to fill that role. There's Jake's Fantasy Contest, above, and also Joe's 2nd Edition of Irrevocably Broken Games contest, which is past deadline and I'm totally backburnering at this point (in fact, I think it's time to divest myself of the identity of Jopel Kuresaare). But like I said, when I work on a thing I mean it, and whenever I return to the grand revision of Brilliant Aura, it's for real, no matter how joking the contest framing is.

    Peace,
    -Joel
