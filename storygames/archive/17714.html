\n I wrote in another thread "Never give examples. Always ask questions!" when thinking about the readers of my game. I took a step back and realised that what I really meant was that I wanted to make them think. Examples can block the mind. What you want to show is perfectly clear as long as it's within the example itself. We can take the blog entry about fruitful void. Everybody gets it, but as soon as they step outside the example (Dogs in the Vineyard) it's hard to grasp, because the whole explanation in that entry is based on Dogs in the Vineyard. Think about it for a while. What other example can you give of the fruitful void? Examples does almost the same thing as writing a definition about what it's not. OK, it's not an apple but can it than be an orange? A pear? A car?

Games that are played with a group are strange to write, because you need to explain how to explain the game. It's possible to handle any possible situation if you create a really focused game - "This game is about catching a monkey in a scrapyard." - but when your game can create several stories it's more about giving general guidelines that the reader can use for different situations. Something I realized when I read "Always ask questions" is that it's a guideline I use all the time when I'm leading a game. A guideline to create creative players. A guideline to make them think.

But asking questions can't surely be the only solution to make my players think. I've playing roleplaying games for 25 years and asking questions was something I picked up during the last years. So I started to think about how I could make my players think. This is what I've come up with so far. Before you read on, think of some of the tricks that you do and be sure to write them in this thread.

- Ask questions.
- Create a problem.
- Build on established facts. For example, making the players connect two things.
- Give guidelines.
- Draw from their experiences. Make them mix their experience with yours.

If we now go back to writing a game, you can do the same thing to make the reader think. I wonder what else I can use.

    \nThis thread is mostly about how to make players/readers to think in different directions. I'm saying that the writer/reader dependence is the same thing as the GM/player dependence.

    When it comes to examples, I do think it's OK to use examples in text to, as you say, make the abstract to be concrete. But I think it's totally destructive at the table. Perhaps we're thinking about different situations. When I say that you should ask questions rather than give examples, I think of situations where the player is put into a problem that need to be solved with a declared number of tools. Like, breaking into a house, using different skills, or making the guard eat the poison, using the player's verbal ability.

    To give an example of how to achieve the character's goal is taking away that option. "Are you really that lame that you have to use another person's idea?". So instead of giving an example of how to solve the situation - "Put the poison into a biscuit" - you may instead ask "How can you make the guard eat the poison?". Or in some cases even ask a really leading question: "Can you use a biscuit?". Even if you actually told with your question how to solve the situation, you still leave it up to the player to decide what to do.

    And like I said: in written text, examples can be of good use. Just look at this post as an example.

    When I say that you should ask questions rather than give examples, I think of situations where the player is put into a problem that need to be solved with a declared number of tools.
    \nAh, this makes more sense now. I think we were talking about different kinds of games. I was thinking of very open-ended, GM-less story games like Fiasco, Polaris, Dog Eat Dog, etc. where players can always do anything simply by stating that they do it. From what you wrote above, it sounds like you're thinking of games where players' options are more restricted.

    I find that in the kinds of games I'm referring to, new players coming from "traditional" RPG backgrounds in particular can sometimes be overwhelmed by the infinite possibility. For these players, having a handful of wide-ranging and simple examples of "the types of things that you might do in this game" can be really helpful and anxiety-reducing, as long as they understand that those examples are only a starting point.

    In open-ended games, examples are also useful for introducing the themes and flavor of a given game. This is an important part of what the Tilt tables do in Fiasco: they give examples of "the kinds of things that happen in this game," which helps players understand the genre of the story they will be telling. Is this the kind of story where there are frantic chases (hard Tilt), or the kind where there are magical moments in soft focus (soft Tilt)? That's one reason why we need more Tilt tables, so that Fiasco can be used to tell stories in a wider genre range.... but that's another post for later.

    These kinds of examples are probably best introduced while pitching the game, or while introducing a specific rule or mechanic, rather than in response to a creative block during a scene. I often see examples/suggestions used to help with creative blocks in GM-less games, but there's a specific social contract around it. If someone feels stuck for an idea, everyone is usually free to jump in with various suggestions, but everyone understands that these can be taken, left, or modified with no judgement or hard feelings, and that the player making the decision has the final say. I generally think this is fun and keeps the game moving, and I haven't seen it create social difficulty or make someone feel stupid. I think it's just the best when I'm floundering, and a new player trying her first RPG ever comes up with a brilliant new direction for me.

    Maybe examples take on a different tone when playing a more open-ended game where everyone at the table has equal narrative authority?
