\n So I really want to know what's good about these things! I know there have been some discussions here, and there were a bunch at the forge, and if anyone wants to link to them that would be great. I get a lot out of threads like this on SG that I wouldn't think of or would poorly articulate. Clearly loads of people have played using these techniques and enjoyed doing so. I've certainly done a bang-up job using them in the past, but now I have techniques that work way better for me - any game that seems to really require lots of either I'd rather simply not run, and I never really enjoyed being on the playing end of them.

Let me throw out a couple caveats - obviously these techniques are going to be spectrums, and honestly on a 1-to-10 rating I'm probably a 3 or 4 most of the time, where I used to often be a 7 or 8. I've had fun with them and used them pretty well, well enough to have a small set of anecdotes to that effect if I need to employ them. That said, most of the frustration I've experienced as a player has come from playing traditional games where railroading and illusionism were prominent; many of the somewhat-bitter jokes people I know share about gming are essentially variations on characters going off the rails or aiming for goals we didn't want to accommodate. I just got the Tome of Adventure Design from the recent Bundle of Holding; the first principle of adventure design Finch espouses is maximizing meaningful player decisions, which looks great to me but would seem to be the opposite of railroading and badly undermine illusionism.     \n Yeah, but: "Skipping inconsequential decisions" = good; "All decisions are inconsequential" = bad \u2014 they're very different gaming styles.

    Anyway, I think there are two important branches to consider here, Case 1 and Case 2. Both have what I call a Point of No Return, where once you know enough/too much about RPGs on a structural level, they become unacceptable.

    Case 1 is what the Forge referred to as Participationism, which JDCorley references in his post, the "it's ok railroading/illusionism because everyone is at least tacitly on board with it." This happens in a lot of ongoing campaigns in their middle and later stages, where everyone knows that eventually there's going to be a confrontation with the Big Bad and the heroes will triumph and yay.

    The Point of No Return for this style of play is that, well, if you're going to do genre emulation, why aren't we doing it in a system that gives us some real input into the fiction? SotC/FATE, for example, is much better at doing stories in a particular genre without deprotagonizing the PCs than D&D or Exalted (and Monsterhearts is better than Vampire, Dread is better than CoC, etc.).

    Case 2 is where there's actual Illusionism going on. The GM is genuinely succeeding at hiding from the players the true (lack of) consequences of their actions.

    I think the Point of No Return here is simpler: if you GM a lot, have played a lot of games, are good at math, or any combination thereof, you'll eventually notice. At which point you can either vote with your feet, or become a Participationist. But either way, True Illusionism is always fleeting.

    \n Is what Jason is talking about railroading at all? I mean, simply starting in medias res or pre-selecting the subject matter of the session doesn't seem to me like the core property of this thing called railroading. That's just distributing the tasks and authorities of playing the game in a particular manner. For example, I write in the Solar System rules that a central Story Guide responsibility in TSoY is to do "dramatic coordination", which means skipping over inconsequential matters and making constructive choices that lead towards opportunities for the players to make choices instead of away from them. I would not choose to call this "railroading", despite it being about the GM making some choices.

    Rather, if I had to explain why railroading is felt as problematic, I would point at the kind of play where the the GM controls the plot of events that the players are supposedly influencing by their actions. Illusionism is very close to this concept, although there are other kinds of illusionism as well. My understanding is that the concept of railroading has emerged not as a theory term like "illusionism", but rather as a very frank observation about what it feels like to play a traditional adventure roleplaying game when the GM is not playing honesty: you're supposedly enmeshed in this scenario and engaging with it, but in actual fact the GM is pulling the string to ensure that his pre-planned outcomes occur. Play is "on rails" in the sense that your choices are illusionary.

    I should note that we did a pretty thorough investigation of participationist play in 06-09 or so around here, with the intent to see how enjoyable it might be for a post-Forgean crew, when played with intent. The conclusion was pretty much that once the players are on board the railroad and know that their choices and input on the big decisions don't matter, the enjoyment of play will largely rest on the GM being an entertaining storyteller; the players will still participate, but their participation is reduced to providing audience reactions (often via their characters), and controlling the pacing (how quickly we continue on to the next scene) and focus (how much we ask the GM to tell as about detail X) of the storytelling. It's potentially a very functional style of play, although if you're doing it participationist-style, with the players acknowledging that they're just along for the ride, you'll need to make sure that everybody takes their responsibility of "basic roleplaying" seriously despite that: some types of players (myself included, I have to actively restrain myself) will start fooling around once they realize that they're on rails and therefore not responsible for what happens.

    Personally I only find railroading a problem when it is not explicit, and in general it is rarely explicit - I think that many trad roleplayers would feel vaguely foolish if they truly dragged GMing practices into broad daylight and explicitly affirmed with the whole group that yes, this is the extent of preplanning and subtle control that we accept from the GM. I was just reading some Only War adventure material a couple days back, and I felt that it captured the actual attitudes involved pretty well: the GM is egging the players on to immerse and engage in an adventurous military conundrum, planning carefully how they'll accomplish a mission, but on the other hand he's "ensuring that this NPC gets away" and "increasing resistance here if the PCs decide to choose this route" and so on. That's what I understand by railroading, and I personally can't stomach it, I find it a reprehensible and lazy way to construe the possibilities of a roleplaying game. We can be better than just puppets in the GM's play. I can understand the rpg history that brings us to Only War, which so very much wants to be old school D&D from the player viewpoint (I mean, the sort of play that it attempts to offer, we do that every week extremely well in our OSR campaign) while accomplishing it all with control-freak GM micromanagement of setpieces, quantum ogres and other supposedly invisible strings. I can understand the history and psychology that has brought us to this point, I just can't accept it as a roadmap for my own play.

    \n A couple of things that might be of interest. (They might not be!)

    Nine years ago (good lord!) I started a thread on RPG.net called, When People Say "Railroading"... because it occurred to me that conversations about railroading go all over the map as what people are the specifics of railroading is seems to behave like a blob of mercury being attacked by a nail. (Witness Jason dragging in any sort of setting strictures as "Railroading" above!)

    What I learned in that long, 16 page thread is -- as far as I could tell from the anecdotal sample -- "railroading" as a term actually has no specific principles. It is a term that refers to a spectrum of techniques and it becomes railroading when it pisses someone off. I really mean that. The definition game can continue down this thread for five more pages, and I don't think it will ever be found. Because it's only when someone's personal sense of "creative honor" (or something) is triggered that the term is dragged in.

    ***

    A side point, touching on TotallyGuy's story:

    There is, in gaming/nerd/geek/whatever culture, a true lack of trust/enjoyment of spontaneity and creativity.

    There, I said it.

    There is a desire and fetishization for things to be pre-planned and intellectualized. It's "smart" when things are done that way. It's "real."

    Witness, for example, the absolute need to believe in some circles that George Lucas knew exactly where Star Wars was going before the first movie came out. A greater appreciation for the movies is granted because Lucas had some sort of master plot between Luke and Darth and so on. But, pro tip: Lucas was making it up as he was going. But the Grail for lots of smart folks who value being smart (being knowing, being intellectually in control, in knowing more than others, having information others don't have and revealing it to others at the most appropriate times) is exactly this: I knew everything ahead of time.

    I remember discussing Sorcerer on the Internet back in 2001-2002 -- specifically it's lack of location and plot based pre-plotting and prep -- people resounding with real rage at the notion the game could work at all. Now, for the people of this community, raised as we are on all sort of "make it up as you go play" it might be really hard to believe this. But it was true. Over at RPG.net, in once discussion, I was told by people to take my discussions on the matter out of the Tabletop Roleplaying Open forum and move it over to the Game Design & Development forum, because it was not only considered so radical a notion but was pissing people off.

    Now, keep in mind, for years people could say to you with a straight face "I never force my players to do anything," while still talking about their "GM Plot." What I was doing then (not to piss people off, but to talk about my excitement that made no-plot play explicit) was calling people out on their bullshit. As Eero says, if you've going to have a plot, say it out loud so everyone can know it. That way everyone is a) onboard and b) decide if they want to do it. But to be the puppeteer is kind of weird.

    Over at the Play Sorcerer blog, I quote Shawn Ryan as part of this post:\n
    The Interviewer asked, \u201cWhat did you learn while working on Angel with Joss Wheadon?\u201d

    And Shawn Ryan replied:

    The main thing I learned from him is to approach stories from a character point of view, as opposed to a plot point of view. Forget about the plot in the beginning, because if you know what emotional journey you want to take your character on, the rest will follow. We break our crime stories [on The Shield] not in terms of who did this and what\u2019s the clue; it\u2019s what do we want our cops to go through on this particular story. Once we know that, the plot will come later.
    \nI'll posit that lots of gamer's really, really love plot. They want to know it was thought out to make sense. To get the characters from here to there. That it is "smart." That it doesn't have "plot holes." When the fact is, there are plenty of ways of creating that moves forward through the narrative in completely different ways. Stephen King, for example, finds a situation and just writes. Ray Bradbury advised, "Find out what your character wants and follow him." (That's the Sorcerer way.)

    But that isn't, in my view, what many of the people in the RPG community value. Or want to value.

    I'll add that one doesn't have to go back to 2003 to see such reactions of course. I had a discussion with someone on this site about Sorcerer Bangs just a few years ago where he insisted that the entire notion that the GM could introduce new points of narrative pressure on the Player Characters could only lead to utterly random and entirely preposterous events one time after another. Why this had to be the case I have no idea. But the notion of not having all "assets" already built out by the GM seems produce a kind of panic in lots and lots of people in this hobby. Such play, the thinking goes, can only lead to all sorts of illogical craziness. That there is no reason to do things that are either illogical or crazy seems off the table as an argument. To not have all the assets and details of the world already planned out, in this person's logic, will make crazy, illogical things happen.

    When I saw TotallyGuy's I though of all this stuff.

    ***

    I don't think a discussion about railroading can slip past the two above points easily:

    a) people mean different things by it, but have personal tripwires for when it is railroading
    b) the very complex relationship many RPGers have to the value of pre-planning and everything being smart and making sense or it doesn't have value

    Railroading is a difficult topic for exactly the reasons Eero laid out above. If we want to still consider the things JD mention it's easy to see those as the low hanging fruit of railroading where player choice is restricted but it's done to make play functional and/or not completely insane as in the case of the psychic alternate world caveman spy. What those instances of railroading don't necessarily lead to is a play style where player input is virtually not required to tell us how the situation is going to resolve. They set up the situation or maybe move it forward when things get off track but they dont determine the outcomes.

    The most functional form of railroading I've seen is usually one where the GM sets up the situation, knows the approximate likely outcomes and leaves a little wiggle room for player impact. It's typically a situation where the players have to investigate to figure out what is going on. Since the GM knows the whole story he can rule out any attempts by the players to address the situation in a manner that doesnt fit the pre planned scenario, it may even make sense to do so since this is his conception of how the world actually is. No need of using force on his part beyond the initial creation of the idea. Play is locked in to revealing the secrets and then resolving them and how it's resolved can be slightly different. A typical situation would have the characters take part in an investigation that leads them to a result where they need to acquire some sort of Macguffin. The players can decide to steal it back, kill the guards for it or negotiate and buy the item, the end result is the same they end up with the item and there was little to no chance of it happening any other way. The game is on rails and we dont have to worry about it going another way. The players don't question whether acquiring the item is the right thing to do they simply learn it needs to be done and then we set about seeing how they do it.

    The usefulness of this type of play is it limits the amount of improv the GM needs to do. He needs to know what mystery needs to be solved and possible ways to solve it, he can even leave it open and accept what ever reasonable solution the players come up with but we know that eventually their will be a solution.

    \n Great story, Guy. Absolutely great illustration. (And, excellent exegesis, Christopher!)

    I would add that I think there's a reasonable middle ground here: Some things should be develop-in-play and others should be pre-planned. I have a Burning Wheel scenario that I've run three times, The Dwarven Frontier. (You were in it at Burning Con, Guy.) There are a number of elements that are pre-set: the asshole older brother of the Prince PC; the goblins, who are slaves of the spiders, the latter of whom are the *real* threat in the story; the existence of a few other Relationship NPCs. I wouldn't violate any of those things. But there are tons of details to be filled in. The first time I ran it, it turned out that the spiders worshipped an ancient demon god sleeping beneath the mountain (I used the Behema stats from the Monster Burner; the PCs blew it up with dynamite, it was pretty great). That didn't come up the other two times, and I didn't force it.

    I should mention that I deliberately tried to encourage the players to add setting elements, by, for example, giving the Adventurer PC Loot-wise and Tomb-wise. But no matter what, I wouldn't allow anyone, myself included, to violate the basic premises of the adventure.

    And that's what I think a lot of the scaredy cats are missing: the principle that there are *some* things that are inviolate, and that even in a completely improvised game, no one is allowed to introduce elements that contradict previously established material.

    Additionally, in certain kinds of games, I think it's entirely reasonable to want certain things to be known ahead of time by the GM. The obvious example is a murder mystery. Unless it's explicitly develop-in-play like Serial Homicide Unit or Dirty Secrets, I'd be pretty pissed if the GM acted like he knew who-dun-it all along only to find out later he was just improvising.

    I think it's sort of the flip-side of the idea that, if we're going to railroad, we should be upfront about it: if the plot is going to be improvised, you need to warn people of that.

    \n"Didn't really count" is revealing for me here. Things "count" when there are points to be had and a goal to be won.

    Some people enjoy the player-versus-environment aspect of RPGs and if the game ends the way they wanted to, they feel that they personally did well: they as players were clever enough to best the GM's traps. If they discover that the GM's traps were adjusted on the fly to fit the available characters and players, obviously this player's "win" doesn't "count", and therefore they'll feel bitter and cheated.

    This is a bit similar to illusionism. The player had the illusion that their skill and/or choices "counted", that if they chose badly they could get nowhere or fail at the task.

    Other people enjoy discovery/exploration/investigation and are disappointed when they find out there was nothing to discover. Again, it's like illusionism: it doesn't matter whether you go left or right because the GM didn't figure out what'll be there in advance. Wherever you go, that's where the plot will be. Some people like it, others don't.

    \n I think (i.e. my suggestion is that) railroading is where:\n
      \n
    1. Someone in power goes against the social contract
      2. \n+\n
    2. they do it by removing decision-making from someone not in power
      3. \n+\n
    3. and that person either feels disempowered by the action, or would do if they knew about it.
      4. \n
    \nI think all three are needed. So, according to this theory:\n
    • It is railroading if we've all sat down expecting to play WGP according to the rules and the GM stops at four losses.
      It isn't if we've agreed to stop at four losses.
      It isn't if we've agreed the GM can adjust that number according to their judgement to give us all a fun game.
      • \n
    \n
    • It is railroading if we turn up to play exploration mode dragonlance, and the dungeon only let us go left.
      It isn't if we've already agreed that each week we'll face the GM's big monster and that the GM will provide in-game hints to it.
      • \n
    \n
    • It is railroading if someone ignores damage rolls to keep their monster alive.
      It isn't if we've all agreed beforehand that such power is allowed.
      • \n
    \nI think the thing that trips people up is the social contract. If this is fuzzy, we can easily get the position where the GM thinks something isn't railroading but one or more of the players does. Essentially, they were expecting to be playing a different game to each other.

    Finally, and maybe controversially, via this theory, I believe the following:\n
      \n
    • It is railroading if the GM fudges the results to move the story down a particular path and the players never find out - providing they would be unhappy if they did know.
      • \n
    \nSo to answer the first question: what's good about railroading? Nothing, because generally the word is used to mean something universally bad. However, there's nothing bad in itself about the GM taking players in the direction of prepared work, or using fiat to adjust outcomes. It's simply a question of how much they are in harmony with the game everyone sat down thinking they were playing.

    \n Predetermined plot and scenes aren't a problem, per se, until predetermination starts trumping player input. When you have a ton of hidden GM Force ("Force" is another Forge term that basically means using game tools to make things happen), it can invalidate player choices and player actions.

    For example, when a player says, "My guy kills the shopkeeper!" you'd expect that to have some impact on "the plot," whatever that is. If there's a predetermined plot, and murdering an innocent shopkeeper has no impact on further events in the fiction, a player starts to wonder why he makes any decisions in the first place.

    When a player says, "I'm gonna take a big risk here and try to climb this doorless tower. I, uh, make a climb roll and get... hey! natural 20! plus 8 is 28!" and then the GM decides that the door on top the tower won't open no matter what (so as not to derail "the plot," a player has to wonder why she takes any risks or rolls any dice in the first place.


    Here's an anecdote from actual play. I was a pretty experienced AD&D 2E player in my mid-20's. The DM was some 17 year-old kid who'd played a bit but was new to GMing. He invited us to build 10th level characters and show up for his one-shot adventure at the game store. I built some kind of spellcaster with tons of weird earth-related spells, like Stone to Mud and some elemental summoning stuff and whatnot.

    Well, I lucked out and this combo let our party walk over most of the monsters pretty easily. After a while, we chase the Big Bad Guy (a high level mage) to his pyramid lair and I start using spells to just walk straight through the walls to the center instead of dealing with the rest of the monsters and traps. We're beelining right for him. The DM is clearly upset by this, and starts fudging. Suddenly this NPC wizard has special magic items that create antimagic shields and such, and we can't reach him. We say we'll camp out and wait for his spell to end. DM says it never will. We say we'll starve him to death. The game ends in a stalemate.

    Now, I give the DM credit for at least letting the party's creative use of character abilities to short-circuit the adventure to some extent, and that was very satisfying as a player. If the DM had told us we couldn't do that, or if he'd said, "No, these walls have, uh, magic protection against Stone to Mud..." (and hadn't made that a feature of the dungeon before we'd tried it), then I'd feel stymied and shut down. But that ending was very unsatisfying, because the DM just didn't want us to kill the NPC wizard and get the treasure, so he made some stuff up to prevent it from happening. We were clearly in a Step On Up / Gamist situation -- or at least the players seemed to think so -- and the DM cheated.

      \n I would expect the psychological difference between FATE and Dragonlance to lay in the expectations that people have towards the game they're playing. One cannot get around the fact that Dragonlance breaks faith seriously, irrevocably, with the game that the old school AD&D gamer is expecting - if you are to maintain trust and avoid betrayal, you have to explain to the player that this game is not the free adventure sandbox they were expecting, but rather something entirely different. What makes for an experience of railroading in Dragonlance is simply that it encourages the GM to seize powers and use dirty tricks that are not legitimate in the eyes of the players.

      (The above is substantially a pretty nasty characterization of Dragonlance; in fact it is a relatively moderate example of the new school of D&D, compared to what would come later. Still, the core of the issue is as I have written it, as Dragonlance does not hesitate to force the issue when players would desire to leave its at times surprisingly wide plot funnel.)

      In other words: Dragonlance is a bad example of game designer railroading because it is not strictly speaking game designer railroading at all: the game it is designed for repudiates the practices that Dragonlance encourages, and Dragonlance itself is not a rulebook, but rather scenario material for the GM to execute. It is the GM who is responsible for it when he offers Dragonlance to his players, either he gains their acceptance for the methods of the game or risks accusations of railroading. If Dragonlance were genuinely a game of its own, and it was presented to the players with the ordinary procedures of learning and accepting a new game, I see no particular reason for anybody to be angry with it, except perhaps their inflexible assumptions about how roleplaying games should work.

      My own thinking regarding railroading is that it is strictly an experiental phenomenon: it is something we experience as players when the GM abuses us. Thus it would be a mistake to attempt to define it in objective external terms, it all depends on our expectations and the rules and procedures of the game, and how the GM acts. I cannot name an example of a railroading practice that would not be legitimate were it accepted by the players, and all railroading experiences I've ever had have had to do with the players not accepting the kinds of control the GM imposes. We speak of plot control most often when we speak of railroading, but that's not the core of the psychological phenomenon, but rather it merely happens to be the case that GMs most often overstep their bounds by preplotting, rather than in some other way.

      If one desires for an objective definition of railroading, it seems to me that the best you can get is an analysis of cultural history: given the history of roleplaying and how the trad playstyle developed out of early (old school) adventure gaming, it may be said that the vast majority of railroading difficulties have to do with players expecting an adventure game (PC freedom, objective situation and setting, neutral GM) when the GM is in fact proffering trad dramatism (GM plot, illusionist procedure, focus on emotional and aesthetic content instead of consequential player choices). From these general wellsprings flow the multitude of negotiated compromises that we see across game groups, organic oral lawbooks of what is the cricket of GM-ful play and what is beyond the pale. This is just a generalization, though, and as has been said in this thread, it is possible to encounter all sorts of local expectations that may give rise to peculiar local understanding of what is legitimate GM power and what's not.

      \n I think most people play RPGs to make choices and see the consequences of those choices. (That's basically what Creative Agenda is, with the addition of "and which kind of choices are rewarded by your friends?")

      An RPG session is a string of choices. D&D and most traditional games go something like:

      1. GM presents a setting.
      2. Players choose characters to play in that setting.
      3. GM presents a situation.
      4. GM gives details about the situation and setting.
      5. Players choose what their character does.
      6. GM interprets those actions, maybe with dice.
      7. Repeat some or all of the above until done.

      Each player step is a set of choices. Invalidate those choices, and the players wonder why they bother making them. The GM could just read a book to them instead.

      Well, let's look at this with clear eyes and realized these are crazy design parameters. Sometimes a game of chess in eight moves, and sometimes fifty. We get this, right?       But it gets crazier! Watch:

      1) A series of challenges for the Players that can be solved any number of ways that allow the players actual accomplishment by using the rules at hand
      2) The challenges are supposed to last X amount of time
      3) Rises to a narrative climax to a final confrontation with the villain

      Why was design parameter 3) even added? As Adam pointed out, if the focus had been "Collect Loot" the day's session could have kept going. In this case the High Level Mage would only one of the challenges. But by making him "the end" there's a chance the Players can reach "the end" too soon -- and suddenly everything is over.

This is why, if you read Lamentations of the Flame Princess you'll see James Raggi sliced off all the XP cruft that's been building since the days of AD&D where story/simulation/challenges all got conflated and you get a muddle like this:

      1) A series of challenges for the Players that can be solved any number of ways that allow the players actual accomplishment by using the rules at hand
      2) The challenges are supposed to last X amount of time
      3) Rises to a narrative climax to a final confrontation with the villain

      If you derive your enjoyment of play from facing danger, bypassing traps, defeating monsters, and surviving to live another day, then finding out that nothing you did in that 4-6 hours affected the outcome is pretty deflating. That's the hallmark of dysfunctional pro-character illusionism. The GM has a story, and you pantomime your actions but none of them matter: the story will unfold regardless of what you do.

      My young-DM anecdote doesn't address that kind of play. It's more about dysfunctional anti-character railroading. I dare not even call it illusionism, since it was really obvious to everyone that the DM was cheating.

      If we want to talk about good railroading, let's talk about participationism, by which I mean lots of thinly veiled GM force that the players have consented to beforehand (perhaps tacitly).

      My own 1E and 2E games in the late 80's were modeled on the advice from Gygax's "Master of the Game" and other prominent authors' advice to manipulate players like puppets in the interest of fun. A lot of what I did was classic illusionism. Over time, my group and I came to an unspoken understanding that they wanted a certain amount of script immunity, and I provided that through hidden Force (tweaking encounters on-the-fly, mostly, but also a lot of "all roads lead to Romethe adventure") and a fair amount of not-so-hidden Force (allowing rerolls and second chances to escape certain death).

      Certainly, to succeed in those games I ran, you had to know how to work the system. You had to be able to make a convincing argument to get a second chance. That involved arguing a case that convinced me AND the other players without being too annoying. We had rules lawyers, con men, and politicians at the table. How much of that does it take until the social game you're playing is the focus, rather than the D&D game?

      By and large, our table was mostly functional, with some notable exceptions. There was a core group of 7-8 players besides me, plus a handful of others who rotated in now and then. We played mostly every Friday for three years of high school and had a great time. There were some extremely dysfunctional moments, though. Some huge arguments when characters died. One was when the thief thought he could take on a bear by himself, and died quickly, and he wanted a second chance because he felt I was wrong about (or had described poorly) some aspect about the bear's weight and size. Another instance was a fight between characters (and players), and the player of the losing (dead) character never returned.       \n My question is this: is a GM allowed to have any creative input at all?

      Because all this hyperbole about GM's snatching agency and fun out of the players hands suggests that the only viable way to GM is as a kind of menial servant, a human computer, only less than an actual computer, because people don't BLAME computers when they do something they dislike. When a human does exactly the same things, it's seen as personal, vindictive, malicious. And you know what? I don't want to play a game in which I can only be a servant, and one on whose head blame is heaped when the players don't have their every petulant demand met.

      So this is how things go. You're running old school, you're drawing dungeons on graph paper, the players are butchering kobolds and orcs by the mass grave, and you start to think to yourself, actually this is all rather dull. I'm doing a lot of work to arrange this stuff, but I don't get to have much of the kind of fun that players do. I don't get to talk in character, portray interesting people, I'm just a number cruncher. Whats more, none of this masss monster-cide has much relevance to the source material, there's nothing Tolkien or whatever about this, there is in fact... dun dun dah!... no STORY here. So you start to fiddle with things that are a bit more complex, instead of just a random hole in the ground you set up something with some meaning, and like so many stories, you think the players would perhaps like to engage with some sort of personal nemesis. Ands then this happens.

      Which is perhaps not surprising given the prevailing dungeon crawler's ethic is: if it moves, kill it and loot it. But it is neverthless a problem. So you start to take measures to make sure it doesn't happen again, and when that starts getting ridiculously obvious, you start to get cunning, and to mask the setup so that it does not intrude into the fiction. So that it's elegant, naturalistic, in tune with the setting and tone. This of course takes even more thought and work, and yet the thanks you get is to be told that you're spitting in someone's drink.

      Nobody throws a hissy fit when a computer tells them the only direction you can leave town in is towards the dungeon, but when a person does it it's a mortal sin and evidence of your tyrannical character. And yet, practically speaking you cannot have that total freedom, because setting thigs up for play takes time and effort, there is only so much of it you can do, especially if you're working hard to make it thematic and evocative. And yet, every fucker wants to be Luke Skywalker, they all want to decline the call to adventure, and the look in their eyes says "go on, MAKE me have fun, I dare you".

      Now maybe you'll respond to this and say, this problem is solved, by means of GM-"full" games, by distributed authority The creatively frustrated now-ex-GM can set aside the creative responsibility and take part as a player. And if that works for you, great. But it doesn't work for me. I don't think that great works of art are casually improvised in the moment. I think they require thought, and planning, and development. I've read many, many actual play accounts of these improv'd games, and none of them have really struck a chord in me. They lack structure, content. This is of course a preference, and I'm not saying that those of you who enjoy this sort of thing should not; nor am I saying that I really think the stuff I've made actually constitutes "great art". But I certainly think that the application of an artisinal approach, the effort to create something that worked as a form of storyTELLING, the careful construction to canalise play toward deliberately crafted events, has created FOR ME, and indeed for many of my players, something more than we would have achieved with "mere" improv.

      And thus I bitterly resent all this personally vindictive slander that is so casually slung around. I'm not denying that there are many people out there who have tried to do this sort of thing and fumbled it, badly; whose execution has been inept and unsubtle, whose techniques were crude and "dictatorial". But I do NOT think that even you have had this kind of experience, that entitles you to assume malevolent intent. And yet it is that assumption that effectively prevents any further considertion of the form, or ways to improve techniques, or design that facilitates this sort of approach. It's all buried under the dead weight of hysterical opprobium.

      Going by the example of Dread, are we capable of using terminology that clearly separates the issue of creative betrayal from the issue of pre-plotted gaming? (Yes, all three are different things - you can even have illusionism without betrayal as long as the players are willing to be lied at for the sake of the experience.)

      As an initial suggestion, I'd like to posit that "railroading" is when PC freedom is curtailed by GM subterfuge or illegitimate force in an adventure game without explicit player accord. The term should't be applied to a game that does not promise certain types of freedoms in the first place, nor is it applicable when the players agree to make certain choices or whatever. This is the original, historical railroading, whether for good or ill: it's when you think you're playing D&D, but then you see the rails when the GM plays an illegitimate move.

      Furthermore, "illusionism" is when a player (the GM, that is) plays by different rules than others, giving procedural impressions that do not apply. So when the GM uses a quantum ogre in a game where you think that he's using a simulation or heuristic, that's "illusionism". "Participationism" is when similar techniques of railroading and illusionism are used with overt player knowledge.

      Finally, as the final piece of this little terminology clarification/revision tour, I suggest that we should call it "social betrayal" or "creative betrayal" when somebody betrays us in game by cheating in their responsibilities or in executing the rules or whatever. Thus, when a D&D GM fudges the dice, he might be doing "railroading" (if he's doing it to prevent some consequential, legitimate outcome in favour of some other one), "illusionism" (if we think he doesn't do that) or "participationism" (if we know that he does that), and we still don't know if he's actually betraying the group in a way that causes resentment without also knowing a lot about what the other players are expecting of him. If it's the sort of game where the players will just nod along and congratulate the GM for a good game when he reveals later that he really fudged through all combats - well, good for that group, I say!

      Lets split somethings apart here. As some of you will no doubt recall, I hate the causal use of the term "story" because it can mean so many different things. Forge-style Narrativism hones in like a laser on one particular aspect of, or meaning for, that term, and that is the address of premise. This view, not at all ureasonably, argued that the real core purpose of stories, what makes them engaging, is that they present some kind of moral or ethical dilemma for consideration, and then, in the conventional mode of storytelling, the author delivers an opinion, a resolution, of some kind. The appropriate form, therefore, of a multi-participant medium is to eliminate the didactic component, the role of the TELLER, and to present the moral or ethical dilemma as one which the players present solutions to in play.

      This is a wholly credible and internally consistent way of looking at things. But it is not the only way in which story can be considered. Because there are other elements to story, not least of which which being that of "mere" entertainment, in which want you mainly want is to sit back and hear the story of Beowulf for the umpteenth time, or whatever. In this view, the whole premise thing is not so much the point of the exercise, as it is a device. It is a tool for making the story more than just a sequence of events, making it engaging, entertaining, making it worth listening to, putting a bit of juice into the dead dry bones.

It is certainly a different beast from the active address of premise, but it is not "invalid" as a form. It is quite possible that your real interest lies somewhere else; that you choose to engage with stories/play about Japan and samurai because you happen to be a nipponophile, or want to explore this historical context, in which case the whole premise element is USEFUL but still remains SECONDARY. And if, or more accurately when, that is true of a group of players, then it is not in any way trampling on their feelings or expectations for the GM to take that pemise element into their own hands and deliver a calculated, developed experience that delivers what the players really want: to be transported into an alien context. In this appraoch, "story" is just a tool, a sort of lubrication that makes the exercise more approchable.

      Thus, I utterly reject the idea that "railroading", and even less "illusionism" are poor methods. They are perfectly good methods for delivering a certain type of "story", and they can lay every bit as credible a claim to being valid forms of story as Forgite Narrativism can.

      For this reason it's simply wrong to point at some merely fictional detail and say that of course changing that won't hurt anybody. Maybe in one game, maybe not in some other one. As far as I understand, the big railroading issue tends to generally be that players expected neutral fairness, but what they got was directed GMing. It's much less common to hear about players who accept GM plots, yet object to some specific railroading behavior - they're accepting of railroading already, after all.

      Maybe you've encountered a lot of players who get frustrated at railroading without justification: you get the players together, you tell them that this is gonna be helluva railroaded, put on your conductor hat and start play; then the players completely unreasonably get angry at you. Maybe so, and I would agree with you that that's unjustified frustration on their part - they knew what they were getting into. But as you can see, I'm again getting back to the question of player expectations: indignation can only be righteous when you have, in fact, been deceived as to the nature of the game. It all depends on what has been agreed upon, and not, like you suggest, upon whether the things the GM does are large or small. Historical problems with railroading would probably never gotten to be so severe if we didn't have such a bunch of game texts encouraging misleading players about it. "Let them think that they're really struggling, but don't hesitate to fudge a dice roll here or there to make sure everybody ends up happy."

      Of course the entire issue if whether somebody is justified in being angry is somewhat spurious to my mind. We're no longer talking of what you or I think about things, or even about the mechanics of achieving happiness of gaming - we're talking about, I don't know, morality of holding an opinion, I guess? I'm not really convinced about there being a witch hunt going for dastardous auteur GMs anyway, so perhaps this doesn't seem as important to me as it should.

      \n I think it's a historical accidence, frankly: roleplaying matured out of D&D in a way that retained certain promises made to the players about how the authorities are shared; you play and control your character's actions, I respond with the world. "Adventure" as a structural concept specifically posited many other assumptions: a PC party, a mission, dangers preventing accomplishment, player choices upon which everything hangs in balance. When you bring a GM wanting to "do story" into this mix, however else could he approach the matter than by hiding what he's doing from the players? The players are wedded to that "I play my character, you play the world" mindset, they're expecting for the combat mechanics and skill mechanics and whatnot to matter, they're used to the thrill of seeing how everything hangs on one dice roll... how do you explain to them that no, you'd rather want to just tell nice stories together?

      I think this historical context explains it entirely satisfactorily why railroading methodology and other participationist mechanics initially appeared via subterfuge, and have only slowly since come out in the open (largely as a by-product of the crumbling of the cult of the GM: more people switching between the GM and player roles, more critique towards auteuristic genius mythology of GMing, etc.). Illusionism was first because the players were not ready for the kind of play the GMs wanted at first.       \n I have never seen a published game present to players, via text and rules, a role that is truly compatible with "GM runs the show". I have also never seen a game attempt to help the GM run the show without also roping in a bunch of player-facing stuff that gets in the way.

      In my eyes, this is not failed design space, but rather unexplored design space.

      Here's a project I'm working on:

      Mead Hall Tales

      To be played at house parties, perhaps during meals.

      One person is the Bard, composing and delivering a tale to everyone else. The Bard can be the host of the party, or just someone appointed by the host to be the evening's entertainment.

      Before the story-telling begins, the Bard presents the characters in their story, with each person at the party claiming one. Each character has a Talent and a Supremacy.

      The Bard begins the tale, as the party-goers do whatever else they feel like, so long as they're not making it hard to hear the Bard. Coming and going, eating, quiet chats, are all fine.

      The Bard narrates what all of the characters do. However, whenever an audience member wants their character to do something else using their Talent or Supremacy, they interrupt and call for the die. The Bard then walks over to them and hands them a d6 to roll. Talents succeed on a 5-6, Supremacies succeed on a 3-6. On a successful roll, the person who's claimed the character in question gets to narrate an outcome other than what the Bard described. This narration cannot cover anything beyond the immediate action and result.

      If the Bard's story requires the characters to be trapped, and one character's Talent or Supremacy prevents this, then the Bard will simply have to find another way to trap them. The idea is not for the audience to control the story, but rather to stand up for visions of their characters.

      This is highly specific and probably isn't fun in all the ways in which Gareth's game was fun, but I think it works as an example of the possibilities RPGs haven't probed yet.

      Perhaps it isn't commercially viable, but whatever, that goes for plenty of games that are still getting made.

      Definitions and Jargon stuff

      The thread might need to agree on some definitions. I've been using the Forge definition:\n
      Illusionism: A family of Techniques in which a GM, usually in the interests of story creation, exerts Force over player-character decisions, in which he or she has authority over resolution-outcomes, and in which the players do not necessarily recognize these features.
      \nI've been drifting that definition a little to remove the word "necessarily" and assume that players do not recognize the hidden Force. I'm pretty sure the common definition of that term has drifted from that (or never was that in the first place).

      \nThat's a good question: what constitutes a "meaningful choice" in a game? Because there seem to be a few different ways to interpret that.

      If someone asks me whether I want a peanut butter and jelly sandwich or a turkey sandwich, that seems like it could be a meaningful choice (especially if I'm vegetarian), even though in both cases I am eating lunch, and it's going to be a sandwich either way. That I can't choose to eat spaghetti or roast duck or zucchini or a lightbulb instead doesn't make that choice less meaningful. Hell, even if I pick the PB&J and they say, "Sorry, we have no peanut butter, but you can still have turkey," my choice was still meaningful on a personal level, and my lunch experience is now different (tinged with regret, or surprised that I like the turkey so much, or suspicious that they're just hiding the good peanut butter because they're mean).

      Sometimes I think that this thread is using "meaningful" as just another way of saying "effective": that when I say PB&J, I get PB&J and not a substitute. Does that mean I also have to be able to choose what kind of bread gets used, what brand of peanut butter, what flavor of jelly? If I pick the PB&J and get something I strongly dislike but which still fits the definition of a PB&J, was my choice effectively taken away from me? Do I need to know all the details ahead of time before my choice can really be meaningful?

      But meaningful implies some specificity and personal intent with it, so obviously having some details is important; if I don't know what I'm choosing, maybe it can never be a meaningful choice. If I flip a coin to decide between sandwich A and sandwich B, it's a choice, sure, but one I barely participated in. Someone else could have flipped that coin. They could have flipped it weeks or months ago, for all the difference it makes.

      Or is meaningful defined by the larger significance of the choice? If I choose the turkey sandwich and it uses up the last of the turkey so no one else can ever have any, that still makes turkey a very meaningful choice, even if I genuinely don't care what kind of sandwich I have.

      Meaningful might also be a numbers game. It might be a more meaningful choice if I have more specific options available to me, or if I can just choose whatever I want, with or without any context. If I have to choose from someone else's list, maybe that's less meaningful than what I would choose if it was entirely up to me.

      I honestly don't know what the answer is. I suspect that there isn't really an answer at all, that it varies from individual to individual and from choice to choice.


      Speaking only for myself, I think that in general I don't need to know all of the details ahead of time, but I like to know the broad strokes: if the adventure is to the east and there's nothing but monsters to the west, I'd like to know that. And after I've made my choice, I don't like being blindsided with something terrible and inappropriate. I typically don't care if my decision has a larger significance to the story -- I get as much or more pleasure from a choice that only affects how I perceive my own character (does he feel good about this, did he get here by design or by accident, is he being honest or deceptive, etc.).       Ok, maybe I'm too late to contribute here, but here's my two cents:

      When I started GMing neither I or my players knew anything about railroading. I didn't had the chance to read D&D modules until lately but I learned how to GM by playing with a friend who despite having learned probably with such material or WoD stuff, was quite flexible.       I was confused right at the middlepoint between two bad tendencies (at least for me):
      -Prepping too hard for a campaign and then defending my material through tons of illusionism had the effect of stressing me out, even when my players would be quite supportive and go collab if found out and asked them nicely.
      -Prepping too little and winging it resulted in poor scenes and keeping cohesion alone was proving to require such an effort that creating memorable scenes wasn't something I could accomplish anymore as a GM. Not that players couldn't do the work (we had some good moments in that campaign after all) but I felt my portraying of NPCs wasn't doing too much impact, that they become too flat. I ended up reusing NPCs from previous campaigns who already had made an impact in the players, but felt cheap doing so.

      So, after the campaign ended, I talked it out with the players, took a recess from GMing, processed the burn out, read a lot about may other games and experiences I concluded that:

      -Without prep, it's harder to give your players better NPCs, interesting challenges, memorable performances and a more firm frame for them to take meaningful choices.
      -With too much prep, it's easier to fall in the mistake of trying to protect it somehow by railroading, fudging and cheating. On top of that, is easier to burn out.

      So I ended up hacking several games and mechanics that allowed me to improvise better and more creative stuff, setting up a few simple but interesting mechanics for players to add more input to the game, and a whole method to make my prep nicely but with less investment, so I don't fall in love with it and feel better about "looking at it through crosshairs". Because after Trust, for me this is a problem of Investment. Players are invested in their characters and their choices, whatever they are about. When the GM's fiat affect those choices, it affects the players in the same measure of the investment they have done so far.

      OTOH, whenever players choices affect the GMs plot, it affects her in the same measure she put investment on the prep, so the more she improvises, the more she's willing to look through crosshairs, unless she learns to let go for now and recycle that prep for later. But that doesn't make it easier to accept a whole day of work is being thrown trough the window by a move improvised by a player in less than a minute.

      Anyway, I believe the "Investment" part of the problem can be solved with many things:

      -A system that allows the GM to create interesting NPCs, monsters, places, items and even whole situations on the fly. (random tables help a lot here, but my favorite solution so far has been this one.
      -A game where creating interesting characters takes less than 10 minutes (right now I can create a simple one in two minutes and engrane it with the whole setting/PCs in 5)
      -Modular Prep: I prep only the beggining of interesting scenes, and at that, scenes that don't specifically need to be in any order, these are just interesting things that happen or that players find out that happened some or a long time ago, when they get to a particular place or meet a particular NPC. Yet it's easy to move that scene to another different place or link it to a different NPC, and thus recycle it.
      -A wider use of fronts instead of isolated NPCs; say, PCs fight a band of thieves and kill their boss by cleverly bypassing a whole dungeon. It's ok, the whole organization isn't destroyed by that, they just change boss and the new one takes better measures to prevent the use of the same plan. The dungeon can still be recycled and the whole front has become now more interesting since they can be focused on avenging their previous leader.
