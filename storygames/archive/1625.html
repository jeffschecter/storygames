Wag-the-dog design

Matt Snyder
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Story Games
I just posted some thoughts about "wagging the dog" in design in my blog.

But, I'm curious to hear whether anyone else finds themselves doing this.

Here's what it is: Do you ever find yourself getting so caught up in the mechanical tinkering of a design that you lose sight of more fundamental, procedural issues you need to be solving with those mechanics?

I find myself doing this from time to time. It's not always bad -- sometimes it sparks some creative moments and ideas (that was particularly true as I designed Nine Worlds). But, it's also bad because I find it often leads me astray, stalls me out, and basically wastes time and focus on getting design work done.

    Sure, especially when getting into subsystems, like magic or whatnot. I actually like to embrace it while I'm designing since it really tends to expand my understanding of what the underlying mechanics really are or aren't capable of, and when I step back out, those lessons can often clarify my overall vision.

    I mean, yeah, it produces some dead ends too, but I think that's another benefit. I need to know what doesn't work, and this is the way I'm going to find it short of playtesting, which I can't do as freely as I can immerse myself in the mechanics. Problems found on the drilldown can reflect up to the core system, often painfully, but it's something I'd rather discover than have it pointed out by someone reading the game.

    So yeah, I do it, and I encourage it in others.

    -Rob D.

    I tend to do this occasionally, and then rip out the overly-clever rules when I realize what I've done (someday I'll post FLFS's erstwhile naval combat rules -- gah). What I have found that helps, though, is that I write out a page or so blurb that describes what the game is supposed to feel like. I usually do it in first or second person so it's as visceral as possible. I go back and read that blurb many, many times throughout design to keep myself on-target. After these little check-ups, I often realize that the stuff I've been working on isn't in synch with the design goals of the game.
