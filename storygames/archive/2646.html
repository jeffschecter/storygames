    \n Any digital voice recorder (cheeeep) that can upload its recordings to a PC (maybe not as cheep) is good for your stated purpose. The hard part is editing. I recommend Adobe Audition, but the free Audacity is good in a pinch. Then, as stated, you know the rest.

    The OgreCave Audio Report uses a program called HotRecorder to record a live Skype call to a WAV file. It does a nice trick where I am on the left channel of the stereo file, and everyone who's remote from me is on the right. This is why you never hear me cough over Allan or Chris, but you hear their noises over each other sometimes.

    The very best thing in Adobe Audition is a menu item called Dynamics Processing, which lets you amplify different microscopic instances of sound a different amount depending on how loud they are already, automatically. All you have to do is draw a curve saying that the stuff that's yay high already should stay yay high, and the stuff higher should be almost to the top of the VU meter, unless it already is, in which case leave it there. It is pretty intuitive once you are looking at it, and without it the OCAR would sound much shittier than it does. (Note: what I have described is also called "compression" by audio nerds.)

    Then, upload monaural MP3 files (that aren't encoded to a too-nutso-high bitrate - 56k is probably fine) to a host that is okay with your big ol' files being there. The cool kids are using libsyn.com and OCAR may switch there too before long.

    Hope this is more helpful than it is confusing.

    Since the podcast I handle is just a music MP3, by myself or one of the other members of my project, our recording apparatus is the same we would use for ordinary recording and editing. Usually, Audacity, which is free and multiplatform. I recommended it when I was hired to teach someone how to podcast, because you can use it to record directly or to convert a WAV to MP3. Mac users often use Garage Band instead, which works well but is a little pricier, unless you got iLife with a new Mac.

    \n

    If you want to record on the go, there's countless ways to do it. You can use any portable recorder that has an earphone jack, really, and connect it to your mic or aux in jack on your sound card to record the audio on your computer (again, you can use Audacity for this part.) There is also third-party hardware for iPods that turn it into a personal recorder, which makes transfer to a computer easier, since it doesn't have to be in realtime.

    \n

    There are a couple software tools that will do the RSS-side of creating a podcast for you, like EasyPodcast You can even get away with using the blog feature of MySpace to do a podcast, but the podcast URL is not obvious.

    \nMy take here:

    For my sessions now, I have a Behringer MXB1002 mixing board. It can take three 9-volt batteries, so I can cart it with me to conventions and record wherever. I have two mics and can do an interview in my living room, but since most game designers don't live in Sacramento, I also have Hotrecorder which I use with Skype. I was very, very happy when I found that it splits the conversation into two separate channels. It all currently goes into my desktop PC's on-board sound card's line-in via the mixing board's Tape Out ports (as I had some spare RCA cable handy) -- at least one of those two is the culprit of my current line noise problem, so there will be some swapping out in the near future.

    It *can* be expensive, just like any hobby, but the buy-in is ridiculously cheap. A $5 or $10 mic (I highly advise against a headset mic, but if you got it, try it), Audacity or Garage Band, and (if you want) a $5/mo Libsyn account and you're good to go. That didn't stop me from dropping a lot more money on more equipment, but the low buy-in can help you determine if this is something you'd want to pursue and put more money into.

    Oh, and another thing you'll want to ask when it comes to podcasting -- and I got severely burned when I didn't realize this and didn't ask before I bought a bunch of stuff -- is ask where someone podcasts. I basically duplicated half of Paul Tevis's setup where we were talking, but didn't realize that he records in his den and I have to record in my living room (which is a third-story apartment, eye-level to two freeways a block and a half away, with only a pane of glass between me and the sound), so the mics I bought pick up too much to be usable. I'm borrowing some other mics right now that work pretty well, but I can't remember the make offhand.

    I'm still learning about the technicals here, but I'm having a blast doing this podcast.

    \n For The Independent Insurgency, I have been using my Zoom H4n, which seems to hover around $300 right now. For TII, I almost always use the built-in microphones. For Oo! Let's Make a Game! and Ludic Jibber Jabber (no site yet), we use the H4n, but Joshua and I record in his basement and use two $100 mics through the H4n's XLR cable inputs.

    After recording, I:

    * use Levelator on the file(s),
    * drop them all in Audacity,
    * do an equalization with the live recording(s) to remove any sounds above the frequency of human speech,
    * select all the files (live recordings, music, prerecorded intro, etc.) and normalize them so the volume is equal,
    * edit, and
    * upload the file to Libsyn (I use the $12/month account because it gives me stats).

    \n I had someone whisper that I explain what "do an equalization with the live recording(s) to remove any sounds above the frequency of human speech" meant. This was a technique I had to start using because, when Joshua's basement heater is going, it generates all kinds of high-frequency noise that's hard to deal with. So I snip it out.

    In Audacity, you'd click the Effects menu, then choose Equalizer, then take that little line, click where you'd estimate 3400 Hz is, then click again right next to it and drag it to zero.

    You may have to fiddle with this a little to get it right. I'd recommend doing it on a smaller piece of recording to make sure it sounds right for you.

    Posted By: boulet@Robert: does levelator and normalizing remove the need to compress?
    I think he's talking about the effect compression rather than mp3 compression.

    Franck, I'd imagine that levelator is a brickwall limiter, which is a type of high ratio compression that gets as much volume as possible without going over.

    I'm going to suggest that all you guys take a look at CockOs Reaper, which is what I and all of my pro-audio friends are using for recording these days. It's free (kinda, there's a 6-sec nag screen if you go over 30 days, but it's fully functional), and there are lots of really good effects plugins that come with it.

    There are lots of VST (virtual studio technology) plugins out there for free. What I'd suggest is a "channel strip" which combines compression, limiting, noise gate, and equalization into one package. There are usually some decent presets.

    @ Walker: I'm really impressed with those $40 USB mics. Studios have known for years that cheap little condenser mics are a great bang for the buck. On the other hand, there is a very real difference when I record with my Oktava MK-219 large-diaphragm condenser.

    If anyone is interested, I've been working with digital audio recording since Cakewalk 7, around 12 or 13 years ago. I'm a professional musician, and I'm the go-to guy for digital recording at the music store where I teach. I'm new to the whole podcasting thing, but I have the recording technology nailed. I'm happy to (try to) answer any questions.
