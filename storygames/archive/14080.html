    Scenario "plot" here means: GM generated crisis or problem that requires resolving.

    In BOTH the FATE games we've played we're run into tension between crisis in the setting vs. crisis in the character.

    In Dogs in the Vineyard the players travel from location to location solving the town's problems. The GM generates the crisis or problem of the town. It would be weird if a player decided to kill the Steward and put himself in charge. Then after eliminating political opposition go and raid the local territory authority for supplies and weapons. Then finally leading a military assault on Bridal Falls where he declares he's re-organizing the dogs under his personal command. Finally he sends an ultimatum back east demanding the capital pay tribute to him. Right? I mean totally outside the scope of play. Dogs in the Vineyard is not a game about personal ambition.

    Similarly a game like Sorcerer completely sucks when a player doesn't do anything about his Kicker. When he hunts around for clues, tries to find "the bad guy," and treats his demon like some kind of back-talking utility belt. The Kicker is supposed to be the crisis the GM fans the flames of for each character individually.

    So what kind of game is Dresden? Is it a game where the GM presents a problem or crisis the group goes about solving? Or is it a game where each individual player is following his ambitions and the GM is expected to problematize that as much as possible.

    As I said BOTH of the FATE games we've played have been a little wonky because of this confused tension.

    I maintain this is because Aspects are TERRIBLE for generating situation. Aspects routinely confuse content with characterization. "Irritable Drunk" says nothing about what the character is irritable about or for whom his drinking is a problem. There's no substance there. FATE seems to think that as long as the player gets to have a moment where he affects being an "Irritable Drunk" then WHAT he's being irritable about or TO WHOM he's being irritable is largely irrelevant. Or perhaps more charitably that such specifics will enter play through some other means. But those "some other means" have been a bit elusive to me.

    Jesse

    \nYeah, Aspects are not really there to generate situation. In fact the mechanics of Aspects are such that they only color situations. Let's say you have that Aspect and you're a wizzard. We start the game, I'm the GM, and I toss you a chip for a compel. You were drunk casting a spell on your slytherin or whatever and now there's a monster running around. Being a drunk is not the situation - but it does personalize the situation to you, tie you into the situation. The monster running around is the situation.

    Similarly, City Creation inevitably creates a bunch of crazy people who all want to kill each other. Cities in Dresden are glass houses and a pile of rocks. That's the situation. Which ones take which action against each other is supposed to be tied to the players' Aspects. If someone is a good-hearted Sorceress and there's a White Council asswipe in town, then that asswipe is going to decide that Sorcerers are a fucking problem and foreclosure fraud is the solution (or whatever). The situation is the asswipe's evil plan. The player's Aspects tie them to the situation and color the situation according to their character traits.

    You could conceivably run a Dresden game in which the characters are self-starters who want to come out on top, and make the game much more about B than A, and I am pretty sure that this is listed in a sidebar or subsection as a possibility, but no, the game is mostly A.

    \n The Diaspora thing is a little different. I've seen it happen in many systems. We plan to do one thing, then we get into another, and we think we are being signaled by everyone else that something is important to them and we go along even though it's not important to us or related to our plans and before we know it we've successfully buried the game. Sometimes this ends up being okay in the end, other times everyone is disappointed.

    If I misread a player and think they're interested in something when they're not, and I give them more of that thing, they might misread me and think I'm interested in that something when I'm not. It can really happen in any system, since the communication of those interests is a social level thing (though maybe not exclusively, see Keys and Flags and so on).

    \n Here's the Dresden Files scenario-generation process:


    1. Assemble Aspects
    2. Choose Emphasis
    3. Make Connections
    4. Scenario Ideas
    5. Putting It All Together
    6. Scene Hooks
    7. Create Additional NPCs


    And, briefly, the steps:

    1. Assemble Aspects: Make a list of all of the PCs\u2019 aspects, your city\u2019s themes and threats, location aspects (both themes and threats), and the motivations of faces and other key NPCs. (NOTE: in Dresden Files, all the players contribute to creating the city, the locations, and the NPCs.)

    2. Choose Emphasis: Choose which of these aspects you\u2019d like to emphasize for this scenario. [...] To generate a shorter scenario, pick just two or three from among the PCs\u2019 aspects and one or two from the city. For really long scenarios, stretching over several game sessions, pick up to two for each PC and two or three from the city.

    3. Make Connections: Take aspects related to different PCs, or a PC and an NPC, and start pairing them up. Look for strong connections that leap out at you.

    [and so forth; that's probably enough to get my point across.]


    My point here is that, if you follow the DF System as written, you're building the scenario out of PC Aspects. The plot of the scenario is driven by the characters' agendas. I don't really see how it could be otherwise, if you follow this procedure.



    Cheers,
    Roger

    \n Well, let's take a look at the example from the manual and see if it sheds any light on the process; this is from the section on #3: Make Connections, as listed above:


    One example of this is Harry Dresden\u2019s "Warden of the White Council" Aspect, and Thomas Raith\u2019s "Fallen Prince of the White Court" Aspect. The potential for tension is obvious -- Harry is supposed to act in the interest of the White Council at all times, but his own brother is a Prince of the White Court. Given the war, that\u2019s a pretty big potential conflict of interest.

    Other examples of Aspect pairs that create tension might be Fix\u2019s "Geased by Titania" and Harry\u2019s "Faerie Debts". Harry\u2019s "Warden of the White Council" and Murphy\u2019s "SI Lead Detective" frequently put them at odds on how to deal with supernatural crimes in Chicago.

    Harry provides yet another great example here. His Aspect "Wizard Private Eye" and Murphy\u2019s "SI Lead Detective" put them at the same crime scene, working together to try to figure out how -- and why -- Jennifer Stanton died.


    Based on that excerpt, it seems to me that the authors of DF are of the opinion that Aspects do (or can, or should) say quite a bit about a character's goals. I mean, sure, it's possible that you drop "Wizard Private Eye" and "SI Lead Detective" into a crime scene and they decide, ennhh screw solving the crime, let's go out and get mojitos. But it doesn't seem immediately problematic to operate on the default premise that the reason the players are playing characters with those Aspects is because they want characters with goals like investigating crime scenes and catching bad guys.

    Posted By: JesseBut those "some other means" have been a bit elusive to me.
    \nCity creation and the aspects related to the locations, threats and faces.

    Those are the tools the GM has to bang away at stuff. But by the same token, those tools only work if the players are playing in that sandbox. If the player decides to go somewhere else, either the GM can make it still about the City and use the stuff the group made, or they've got to make new stuff.

    Our game has been having similar issues as yours with regard to aspects. We play a lot of Burning Wheel (and SotC), so we approached Dresden like SotC, but with Beliefs in the form of Aspects. The trouble is that Aspects don't direct play that way, precisely as Will points out. There's no goal. Aspects are very abstract and they're fun for color, but they only sometimes function like Sorcerer's Kickers or Burning Wheel's Beliefs. We're fixing things by discussing the issue amongst ourselves, deciding on agendas and going for them. The GM also has had to grab the NPCs he had at his disposal, give them clear agendas that are in opposition to our goals (not just highlighting an aspect).
