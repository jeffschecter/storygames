    \n Ditto everyone else. There should be a really great place for design discussions these days, but there isn't, at least as far as I know. Praxis could potentially be such a place, but there are few incentives to check-in regularly. Honestly, in my experience, high-traffic isn't what you want in a design discussion and neither is high visibility. What you want is high visibility from a specific group of constructive and supportive people. Blogs, I think, are still the best way to go, but it's difficult if you don't already have a decent following, which is something you have to build up over years. Some folks have had success with local or regional forums.

    \n For me, G+ has become yet another thing I have to check and, like Facebook and Praxis, I just don't find time to check it. The traffic is already so high, even among just the limited number of people I'd want to follow, that it's too much to keep up with.     Honestly, the best conversations I have are still always face-to-face over beers, when I happen to see Sage or John or Jackson or Lukas or Jamie in Seattle. Or, lately, they've been happening through collaborative projects I'm working on with folks -- They Became Flesh or Stage One -- where we have a reason and commitment to working and talking with each other about games.

    I wish I had the time to run a hardcore game design discussion thing year-round, but Game Chef is the place I give back to the community right now and I'm not sure I can take on something else. I mean, I can imaging running the Game Chef blog as a more active thing, maybe, to talk about best practices for game design and start some discussion in the comments, maybe to engage in ongoing discussions about Game Chef alumni games that are moving forward in development, but that would mainly be using the tools already in place, not starting something new.

    \n Personally, I think part of the problem with game design forums is that the way people use them isn't effective. The most benefit I ever got from a forum was from Joshua A.C. Newman's Game Designer Studio, where there were specific expectations about how you'd use the process. I'd like to see more people attempt something like that, or possibly something analogous to an online writers' group where designers read each others texts and give each other honest reactions. A while back on Praxis I tried some "be the change you want to see" stuff where I tried to give in-depth attention to as many games as I could, but it gets tiring being the only person doing that, especially when it turns out that a lot of people aren't actually serious about developing their games further.

    I also think the big missing link in the design community is a way for similarly situated designers to find and interact with each other so that they can have deeper conversations, even if they don't happen via forums. The biggest benefit I ever got from Praxis was finding some other designers who were willing to Skype with me so we could playtest each others' games.
