\n It seems like most future settings are either far-future space travelly, post apocalyptic, or Cyber-Punkesque. I'm trying to define a future about 100 years out.

I have some ideas of what I think the world will be like but I'm not an expert in any of these areas. If anyone has any thoughts on any area that would evolve over the next 100 years (I.E. Transportation, Medicine, Health & Nutrition, Economics, Wars & Conflicts, Industry, Ecology, Politics, Technology, Religion, Science, etc.) I'd be interested to hear them. I don't need a ton of detail, just some flavor to add some atmosphere to a setting.

    Nuclear weapons will be considered less important for defence, but more countries will have them. A nuclear weapon will have been used in war, but the popular backlash and horrible side-effects has made it unlikely to be used again.

    People will have a single identity for government, Internet, banking purposes with a single pin code.

    All computing will take place in the cloud. Every computer can become your PC.

    I'm not sure if this is too optimistic a vision of the future to be much use. Not all of it I believe will happen, and not all of it I want to happen, but it's what I got from a bit of brainstorming.

    \n Data fog

    As the amount of information we produce increases, so will our technological ability to handle that much information, but there's one part of the system that isn't adapting fast enough, our ability to label all that information. Using only 26 letters and numbers, how do we give each and every piece and collection of data its own name to make it recognizeable, findable, and discrete from every other piece of data (don't think Chinese will solve this, because non-alphabetic systems hit the same problem, possibly quicker)? Even worse, what do you do about those who would attempt to cheat the system, or just make mistakes in labelling their data? This isn't science fiction, it's happening already, but it'll get worse as time passes. That's data fog, a big, amorphous mass of data that makes it difficult to find whatever you really want.

    And now we have people pushing the idea of spimes, data produced by every individual consumer good as it moves through the world over its lifetime. What makes me laugh is that this idea will create a collossal amount of data fog, something overlooked by Bruce Sterling who came up with the name "spime" to be a term that was rare enough to be findable through the data pollution that already fogs up search engines. The archivist/cataloguer/metadata researcher in me doesn't know whether to point a finger and laugh at the absurdity of it or cry at the shortsightedness of techno-utopianism, so I'll create a game instead.

    \n Some further ideas...

    Medicine

    Cloning will be fully viable, but with some ramifications we hadn't considered.

    In order to prove certain scientific theories, exact genetic matches of historical figures are cloned from their DNA.

    Contraband DNA is taken from mummies, instilling a new belief in afterlife concepts. Scandinavian Valhalla cultists exhume the corpses of great warriors, or steal the DNA from museum specimens, bringing about a self fulfilling prophecy about a great eternal war. Tragically, these experiments are taken to finally prove the end of the "nature versus nurture" debate, the cloned individuals are rarely the historical equal of their originals.

    The catholic church decries the "science", claiming that a failure to reproduce the historical figures of the past merely reinforces the notion of unique souls. Buddhists claim that this proves nothing about souls and actually verifies their beliefs in reincarnation, they believe that the clones truly are reincarnated versions of their historic originals, but in this incarnation they have new lessons to learn and thus develop differently.

    Computing

    Moore's Law still proves unstoppable, despite computing "experts" continually saying that "it will end any day now".

    Combining Medicine and Computing

    Nanotechnology becomes a reality, in the form of bio-organic quantum computing cells that network as an effective hive mind. A single pill literally sets loose a swarm of robotic bacteria which reconstruct cells from a tiny level. A single pill can be used to cure all ills...for a price.

    On the down side, computer viruses become more akin to biological viruses. Hackers can literally program viruses that damage people, or manipulate their DNA to catastrophic ends.

    Certain companies offer immortality in the form of uploading a persons entire memory into a data storage cell. The descendants of these individuals relate to the memory patterns through a digital avatar in the form of the original person. While these digital avatars seem incredibly lifelike and have all the memories of their originals, they lack certain mannerisms and other traits. Once again, religious groups weigh in on the debate, claiming that this is due to the soul passing on, and the remaining memories merely being a reflection of the truth. As the technology improves, this becomes harder to argue.

    The aforementioned nanotechnology swarms eventually become capable of housing the memory matrix of an individual, and thus is heralded the dawning of a truly immortal age.

    \n What is your game about?

    Figure out what's important in the world to your game before trying to predict the future of your world. One is easy, one is very difficult.

    For example: is your game a romantic comedy? Then unless you make clones a major aspect of the romcom, cloning tech in the year 2110 probably won't be important.

    You don't have to say when the game takes place. This removes a lot of pressure to make things "realistic" (whatever the fuck that means).

    Here's how to do it. Do some research and find some cool technology that you want to have your game focus on. Make an assertion ("In the future, intelligent machines will be prevalent and provide a cheap form of slave labor."). Okay, now what does that mean? People have more time to spend with their families so social bonds become tighter but more clannish because people spend more time at home and do work remotely? Or do people get emotionally attached to machines -- can one become loyal to a machine? (Macintosh users anyone?).

    And if you put a timeline in your game I will bite your head off.

    \n Anti-Globalization:

    Because industry succeeded in quashing fuel source research until after the oil wells were already dry, travel has become something reserved for the
    super-elite.

    Globalization largely collapses in on itself as the price of moving goods becomes prohibitively expensive. No longer is it cost effective to produce items
    in China or Bangladesh and ship to its destination.

    Because offshore production is no longer an option, the corporations strip the rights and wages of the American worker to recoup their losses.

    The collapse of globalization reduces America to a Chinese vassal State. The two dominant superpowers are China and the European Union.


    Global Warming:

    Seasonal conflicts over water are commonplace and rarely make the news.

    The sea level has risen by several feet, resulting in roughly half of the world's population becoming refugees.

    The sheer number of global warming refugees overwhelms the UN's food aid agencies, causing their systems to collapse. Two billion people starve to death in
    just five years.

    China and Russia invade the Canadian North and wind up rendering the North inhabitable by humans after they nuke each other's outposts in a conflict over
    newly revealed resources in the Canadian artic.


    Society:

    The United States becomes a feudalistic theocracy comprised of a tiny celebrity class and a vast serf/worker class while still paying lip service to
    democracy.

    Americans still do not have national healthcare.

    With the rise of global warming refugees, the European Union closes its borders and turns away 99% of all immigrants. Tens of thousands of refugees fleeing
    water conflicts are killed each year by EU forces.

    \n Much as the primary differentiator of "class" shifted in the 20th century from being about bloodline to being instead more about who had money and who did not, by 2100 the main determinant of "class" will be who has the ability to alter their own genetics and who does not. This might turn out to be the same thing as "who has money and who does not", but also might not.

    The ability of a percentage of the population to control their own DNA will create a class of people functionally immune to death by natural causes. This will change the world in ways that no one expects.

    At least once, a single disease will kill over a billion people within a year.

    Either enviromentalism or warfare will lead to the construction of an organism that can digest plastic. This will create a problem several orders of magnitude worse than whatever issue led to its construction.

    Several years after a quiet advance in basic chemistry (probably in the area of artificial photosynthesis) solar energy will suddenly, and surprisingly, become more cost effective than any other power source. Within a decade, "the west" more or less abandons the Middle East, which descends into an orgy of clan violence and religious idiocy, but no one gives a shit. (Essentially, it will be like central Africa through most of the 20th century).

    A nuclear weapon without any clear country of origin will be detonated in a major city. Many will claim responsibility, but few will be believed, the public instead making up vastly complicated conspiracy theories that fit their particular ideology.

    At least one technology will upend the world, making all current speculation pointless.

    \n "Either environmentalism or warfare will lead to the construction of an organism that can digest plastic."

    We have those. For some time now. My friend in Biochem, at the undergraduate level, is working on making little guys that can eat the really nasty stuff. The reason we don't just let those critters out of the lab is that well there is simply too much risk and no profit. The second I imagine, is that they probably turn plastic into something worse in the process of breaking it down. There is some really wield and wild stuff going on in artificial biology right now but I suspect it a problem of funding and risk. The future started to be the present sometime back in the nineties.

    A hundred years a very long time and no time at all. It's licence to get weird and far out; pretty much do what you want. If you're short of ideas, read some SF. Any really.
