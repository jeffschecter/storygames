\n So I'm thinking up this idea about scene framing for Galactic where you make sure that you're setting up a really meaningful conflict. Like, instead of a Trollbabe-like request: "I want a scene in the marketplace with Jill," you build the conflict into that, like you might in improv: "this is the scene where Alex is trying to stop Chris from detonating the bomb." Don't wander around looking for a conflict to emerge. Go into the scene knowing that the stakes are high, and that walking away from the conflict isn't an easy option.

What I'm wondering about is how to divide up that job so that the Czege Principle doesn't creep up on it. I'd like to keep the idea in there that the results of a previous conflict give the player more or less control over setting up that scene, but I don't think it'd be a good idea for the player to control too much of it.

Thoughts?

    \n This is also how I play PTA in Play-by-Post gaming, with an occasional break for more free-form RP'ing, especially at the beginning when everybody's establishing their characters. Most of the time, it seems to work best to hit conflicts hard, one after the other. It's a bit like an episode of "Law and Order", where every scene builds character or plot, there's a Da-DUNG sound effect, and the next scene follows with a quick payoff. This is fun, although I think it's actually drifting PTA quite a bit.

    From my experience, the biggest risk with this playstyle is that it can get so Meta that people can find it uninvolving -- in other words, the OOC discussion that goes into very aggressive scene framing becomes a major part of the game, and the "playing your guy" part of the game shrinks even further -- which can make the game uninvolving for some players. This seems C.P. - related, but it's not quite the same thing, it feels more like a "playing before you play" problem.

    I've found that one thing that really helps with the Meta problem is to use shows with very strong genre references. If the structure of the show obviously calls for a scene where the hero confronts the villain in a public area where neither one can start a fight, then nobody minds jumping right to that scene. More open-ended scenes are the kiss of death for this technique.

    I hope this is understandable, I've used a lot more scare quotes than I usually resort to.

    \n Hmm, in Polaris when I set a scene as the heart I:

    1. Decide on who the opposition is.
    2. Decide on what the protagonist is trying to accomplish

    What I don't know is:

    1. What the antagonists are trying to accomplish
    2. What the antagonists will do, if anything, to stop the protagonist from achieving her goal.

    Sometimes the mistaken will yield the goal of the protagonist, but have some other counterforce or price in mind to refocus the scene.

    When mistaken I:

    1. Decide on the antagonists
    2. Know what the antagonists are trying to do
    3. Have a sense of what price or hardship I'd like to inflict on the protagonist

    What I don't know is:

    1. Will the protagonist be willing to pay the price to stop my antagonists
