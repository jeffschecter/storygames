    \n In SHORT, it went awkwardly, if eventually enthusiastically once we got later in the day.

    There could be less of the same 5 people playing Dresden files in every single slot with a GM uninterested in running anything else.

    It definitely needs to be more tightly organized; the mustering was pretty much a free-for-all cluster fuck. I ended up facilitating the one game I knew how to do THREE times because there was so little else on offer. The lack of NEW stuff to try was a big disappointment, but a necessarily annoying consequence of the authors wanting to be in the dealer's room to hand-sell their games during the EXACT hours that GoD was otherwise running.

    So, it was much diminished as compared to last year's experience. I suspect part of the blame for that may laid at the door of the new venue, the Crowne Plaza, with tiny, loud, crowded rooms compared to last year's much mire spacious digs that were given over to Pathfinder this year.

    A half-hearted open call for volunteers is clearly an ineffective approach. someone needs to make sure that there are facilitators/GMs registered and committed to each slot... The book lists GoD as a FORTY-EIGHT capacity event. Leaving aside that we only had tables reserved for 36, a much more robust selection of games, especially including some of the newer titles folks actively want to play would be a huge improvement. There was plenty of decent gaming, but a lot of it was in games people 'settled' for, not necessarily what they actually wanted. I love Fiasco, but would much rather have played in 3 different games than had to step in to facilitate it when we had such a lack of on-time GMs ready to go. I made the most of the InSpectres game I was in, but it also was far down the list from my first choice, as it was for everyone else at the table.

    The Oddest thing that happened was getting chewed out by the Dresden Files guy when I arrived at 10 AM on Friday and asked what was up because there was NO ONE else in the room except his Dresden players, eager to drag him away from any sort of oversight role so that he could run the game. I was sure I was in the wrong place to start out... No signage, no games, no facilitators. Eventually enough people wandered in that we got a game going, and by the afternoon slot it resembled the great event from last year a bit more, but the process definitely needs a fluffier to show up early and set it up...

    -Jim

    \n (edited on review)

    Say what you want about the "Dresden guy", but Scott (that's his name) was one of the very few people trying to organize GoD at all, and he's been at it for months. He was there ready to run a dozen games, but ran Dresden because there was a constant demand for it and he was the only one around who would do it.

    I spent Friday and Saturday morning over there and while we generally had more players than folks who could run games, it stayed busy. We had six tables each seated for 8 players (of course most games in our world don't accomodate a crowd like that). The only time I saw them all full was when one was being used as a waiting area. And some folks elected to take their game to the lobby for peace and quiet.

    In the hours I was there I saw games of Dread House, Mecha, fiasco, Zombie Cinema, Spirit of the Century, Chronica Feudalis, and others get played. I know that there was also a bit of Blowback. The Design Matters guys did a great job of keeping GMs on hand and pulling people into fun games.

    In the end, the venue wasn't great- the Crowne is out of the way, the room loud and hard to find. And it would have been nice to have GoD run on Thursday and Sunday as well. However the fault is ours- not having enough volunteer hosts and committed GMs upfront (months ago) means fewer sessions. Not gathering enough tickets in years past meant a diminished presence and worse location from Gen Con. These are problems we can fix.

    \n This was the first time I played any sort of RPG at a con, so I don't have anything to compare it to, but I had a lot of fun in the two games I got into at GoD. Both days I came in around 2:00 and was able to slip into a game quickly.

    Thursday's was Shock; the game was just getting started and they were kind enough to let me join in. Previously I'd been a little ambivalent towards the game (I'd read the rules but never played), but I came out of it wishing we'd had more time to dig into the various stories.

    Friday's start was a little bumpier: there was a Fiasco game forming but it quickly became clear that there were too many people, and the other tables appeared to be in full swing already. I plopped down at the table by the door to wait for something to open up. There were a couple people there waiting for the MC to their Apocalypse World session, who never showed. One of the folks waiting said that he'd never played the game but had read the rules, and would have been willing to try running the session himself, if he only had the book, which I just happened to have in my bag. Go, team! The guy who was watching over the room and collecting tickets joined in, a fifth person joined while we were setting up chars, and away we rolled. The MC was really great, the players were into it, and I had a blast. I simultaneously really want to start a regular AW game, and feel like there's no way I could run it as well as the dude who ran our session.

    I had come with a couple games that I knew fairly well (Fiasco and Zombie Cinema), just in case there was a void that needed filling. Someone borrowed the Zombie Cinema game while I was playing AW. (My unhealthy urge to overstuff my backpack got a lot of reinforcement this trip.)

    The room was crowded and a little claustrophobic, but that fell away when the games got going.

    So one data point: this one participant had a great time, and I'm very grateful to the organizers.

    \n Scott-

    Thanks very much for the explanation, I appreciate it. I was just really taken aback to see a setup that looked so completely different than last year. I was just honestly wondering if I was in the right place, or had misunderstood the format.

    By the time the later slots rolled around, there was certainly quite a bit more activity, certainly a testament to the community's ability to improvise and adapt. I'd just love to see the whole thing START that way, rather than lurch towards it as the Con progresses.

    To be fair, the same sort of general disorganization and difficulty with getting people seated ALSO plagued the WoTC events I (tried to) attend, so it's not exclusive to GoD, I think it's more a symptom of the general lack of a specific protocol everyone agrees on in advance to make things run smoothly (a pretty common occurrence in all aspects of our hobby, to be sure, with its institutionalized Asperger's and premature presumption of shared familiarity).

    At the risk of being pilloried, I have to say that as an outsider and a 'civilian' interested in PLAYING these games, that one of the barriers seems like it might be creators more interested in being in the dealer's hall to hand-sell their games and meet-n-greet than in actually facilitating play. I can't help but imagine that most of the authors would be far better served from a sales and marketing standpoint in making sure there's someone there to RUN their games (especially if that someone is them!) while leaving the F2F sales to someone practiced in that skill. At the very least, putting as much effort into making sure there's play of their game(s) happening as they do making sure their books arrive. I don't know whether the Blowback games that were run were the result of Elizabeth making sure there was someone there to run them, but that was one of the bright spots: a game debuting at the show that actually had several slots run. Eppy showing up later in the day to run Dread House was a good save for some of the late arrivals, too. It was a crushing disappointment to me that I didn't get to play either, but I didn't want to be a dick and go play and leave 4 people standing there with an empty slot, either.

    @Steve: The cheat sheet and playsets were a huge help, I can't thank you enough for providing them, they made it possible for me to facilitate (I didn't have my copy on site with me...)!

    @Adam: The 'change I want' is to see debut games, and interesting stuff I read about here, but haven't picked up for whatever reason run in multiple slots so more than just 4 or 5 people get to play it for the whole show... to give those of us who don't have playtest/limited pre-release access to all the stuff you well-connected folks consider old hat a chance to see what the fuss is about. It may be a Quixotic hope, but the lack of any opportunity to play, oh... Apocalypse World, Annalise, etc, felt like a big lost opportunity (at least for me, who showed up with a specific interest in those games and walked away empty-handed).

    I feel like I can (hopefully constructively) comment on this year because I agreed on the spot to facilitate 2 full-slot sessions of the same game I had already played because it was the only one I knew how to run, and hadn't prepped anything at all. Part of my frustration was walking in excited to play, but ending up stymied by the lack of GMs and poor selection of NEW games. One of the other difficulties was a bit of... elasticity in the start times, which resulted in a confusing mess of games going off at odd hours. When participants, particularly GM/facilitators, walked in at 20 mins past the hour but we were already started, that was something of a frustration, to be sure.

    Last year's practice of having GMs registered in advance for slots they were committed to, with sign-up sheets on the table, clear labeling outside the room, plenty of space, and a very wide variety of games was the thing that made me so excited about this year. I had NO idea how it all got organized, but now that I have some insight, I hope that I can be a help in making it work well at Origins. I know you guys are very much a punk rock/DIY community here, but this may be one of those times when we can help with the mission of spreading the love with a bit more specific advance planning.

    Jeez, I sound ungrateful, but I really did enjoy myself, once we managed to get it going. I just think there's some ways to reduce the stress, really ratchet up the enjoyment and buzz factor of the whole thing, and make it into something that doesn't just run, but runs smoothly, and, not coincidentally, sells games.

    And FWIW, I REALLY like the idea of going all the way and making it a truly collective event... I was going to suggest going that route at Origins, with an 'Indie RPG' ribbon ala the board game room or Loony Labs Experiment that bought an admission to the continuous GoD/Story Game(s) session going on in a dedicated room for the whole show. It's good to see I'm not way out on a limb on that.

    -Jim C.

    \n I'm torn on how it went this year.

    I think it was great that a huge flood of gamers who missed out on scheduled Dresden could get a game at Games on Demand. On the other hand it swamped out two tables pretty constantly and tied down volunteers to just one thing, 4 hours at a time. I personally would like to see more variety, but Dresden had huge demand. If it was always the same people I don't how happy I am with that. I wish they'd try something else after getting a game of Dresden, or let other people play Dresden.

    At Design Matters we had scheduled times to be on the booth, at Games on Demand, and off the booth. This meant that there was always two people from Design Matters at GoD. But, crucially, that does not mean that all our games were available all the time. I'm cool with that. Annalise was represented at GoD but not all the time. Same with Mecha, Remember Tomorrow, Dread House, Blowback and so on. It sounds like Jim was there when Blowback was one of the games we could run. Awesome.

    I was there for one Design Matters "slot" (Fri 4-6). Clint Krause had been doing Mecha before I got there and we then got some Dread House on for three people who'd wandered in "off slot" (i.e. they'd missed the 2-6 slot but we caught them just after 4).

    We had a lot of fun and it was a nice pick up game that they'd otherwise have missed out on. It was fun for me and Clint too.

    Down sides were the lack of white board to write stuff on (Hey, be here at 4 for Dread House, Annalise, etc.) or signage. I wandered around the Crown Plaza trying to find it and ended up in Union Station with room after room of Pathfinder. I may be rubbish at directions but this was clearly a step beyond my ability (or my willingness to ask the staff where to go)!

    Eventually I did ask staff after making my way back to the Crown Plaza and the GenCon volunteer told me where to go, but added "there's no point though as they've already started."

    I'd like games to be more than just the 10-2, 2-6 slots, and if we are offering two-hourly slots or something (hey, some games don't last 4 hours) then we need to communicate that with GenCon, so the volunteers can direct traffic our way. A bit like how the boardgames are come and play. So, ideally the GenCon volunteer would have said "hey, you've missed Pathfinder but try the Games on Demand, they have games starting every two hours with generics".

    Scott and his wife were awesome in making sure we got any books and stuff that had been left lying around (especially Eppy's black Jenga set THANK YOU!) and giving us leaflets to direct people from the Exhibition Hall to GoD. Steve's leaflet was awesome. I also gave all my generics to Scott and the other guy watching the stuff at GoD.

    So, for me there was a lot of good stuff and a lot to improve on.

    Jen, Jay and I arranged a game in the Embassy Suites on Sunday during the day as we were without GoD. On reflection it was easier to find, quieter and closer to the con. Huh.

    \n Wouldn't GoD profit from having a definite section of the resources dedicated to pick-up gaming intended to tide over the slot openings and give people something to do immediately as they get to the area? Like, have one big table off to the side constantly running Action Castle, D&D or similar low-commitment, easy-to-play games. This way the host would always have something to offer no matter when somebody came in, and the pick-up game could absorb extra people, iddle hanger-ons, people waiting for their session to start and people who didn't fit into a session. And when you need players for something else to get a full group, the pick-up table acts as a reservoir to strongarm people from into a "real" game.

    The above has been my philosophy when I've done similar things here in Finland; you need to have something going on all the time, it's not nearly enough to tell people coming by that the next game maybe starts in two hours. Then again, I don't use scheduled slots when hosting the thing either, so it's a bit different in that regard as well - games just start whenever a sufficient number of players has been attracted. Games with easy procedures like Zombie Cinema, Under the Bed or such are valuable in managing the crowd and making sure that everybody gets to play, too, as those are games where you can show the group how to play and leave them to finish the session between themselves while you go guide another group or do other host duties.

    Sounds to me like the problems indicated are nothing fundamental, just a lack of staff (always a problem in something like this) and a lack of dynamism that doesn't fit well with spontaneous group dynamics of a convention crowd. Prepare more games that can be entered and left "in the middle" and more games that can be taught to a clueless group in half an hour so they can play autonomously, and you'll find that the crowd can be controlled better. This in turn tends to translate into a larger crowd, which means better fungibility overall - more games starting and ending all the time, which means that the waiting times are smaller and the selection of games is better at any one time, resulting in more satisfied customers. For example, take it as a target to have a full third of the game activity at GoD be "dynamic" in the sense of not requiring a committed staff GM or being low-commitment pick-up gaming outside slot structures. That'll help in getting the time-slotted traditional sessions going better, too.

    \n Some of the problems at GoD arise out of a lack of dedicated volunteers, and work being put in to schedule times and provide materials. I think only one person who had committed to being a room host was actually at the con, and that was Scott. Many others made an effort to come ready to run games, but I don't think most of those (including myself) were committed to specific times. Again, this can be improved in the future.

    I think a bigger issue comes from different ideas about what purpose Games on Demand serves. For some folks I think it's simply a free-play space (not unlike the Embassy at night) where you can get together with some friends and play a selection of indie games during the day. Others- particularly those selling games at booths in the vendor hall- see Games on Demand as a place where potential customers can go to try before they buy. It can serve both of those ends, but maybe not perfectly for either one.

    If what we want is just a quiet place to play with friends, that can be arranged entirely outside Gen Con restrictions; we take over a hotel lobby or set of conference rooms during the day, tell everyone we know, and show up to play games. I'd imagine the Embassy would work for that even better during the day than it does at night. It would probably be good to make arrangements with that location first.

    In order for GoD to be useful as a sales tool, it has to be an official Gen Con event, listed in the schedule where folks can see it and find it. It has to have enough space and dedicated GMs that people showing up to play a specific game can be accommodated. And ideally, once the folks have played that game, their excitement can be redirected at some other games on offer as well.

    Ideally, for both purposes, the event should be near the convention hall, easy to find, comfortable and well equipped with signs, whiteboards, etc. The best way I can see to make that happen is to get Gen Con on board and show them it's worth supporting.

I'd like some more public way to declare willingness to run/desire to play - like the whiteboard in previous years, or whatever - but it's still a fine alternative to hook up with random players when all your other buddies are playing or manning booths during the day.

    Oh yes, and at the end of the weekend, I basically donate all my surplus generic tickets to Games on Demand. Some years have been better than others, but every bit helps, right? I urge others to do the same, if you enjoy it.

    \n Hey, gang. I was way too busy at the IPR booth to ever get over to GoD, but that will likely be different next year.

    A couple of things: it is far more useful for a game designer to "hand-sell" his games at a booth, you can reach many more people that way. Game designers should seriously consider getting GMs they know to come and run their new games at the con, however. GoD is a great venue for that, but scheduled games are also a good way to do that. Game designers, think about your strategy as you go to the con. How are you going to get people that play experience that will really sell your game?

    GoD is a great thing, and I would be really sad if it went away. It has its kinks, it seems, this year and years before, but I really appreciate the effort of those who have spent so much time and effort trying to make it work. Dresden definitely swamped it this year. Dresden sales were the single biggest at the IPR booth as well.

    Speaking of IPR, I spoke with Darren on Monday before we left the con and he expressed a lot of interest in GoD as a complement to the IPR booth. If you are looking for sponsorship or help organizing, IPR would be a great place to go. If GMs are running games, IPR will clearly benefit, and they would definitely be interested in making GoD better.

      \n Here is a single datapoint, not intended to be indicative of anything but one random dude's idiosyncratic experience failing to play at Games On Demand this year. I wasn't gonna mention it but y'all seem to be interested in how it went this year so hey, one extra datapoint might be useful to somebody.\n
      "I'm gonna do some Games On Demand this year cause I want to play more games than I buy. Unlikely but a worthy goal. I'm not gonna just wander around and buy things and get the occasional demo, I'm gonna PLAY! YEAH!

      "Hmm... only offered two days, and on each of those two days, there are exactly two slots. I can work with that I guess.

      "Friday morning, ready to -- Oh crap. Slept too late to get there by 10 AM. Missed that slot I guess. I'll hit the two o'clock...

      "During the ensuing four hours I get kind of excited about Freemarket, and hear that Luke's running a game at 2, and people have failed to show up at such games before, leaving open slots. I decide to show up at that one. Hmm.. nope, everybody showed up, no room. Games on Demand it is!

      "Ok, it's super hot outside so I'll try and find my way there through the mall, which is mislabeled on the GenCon map for some reason... Eventually... I find my way... with all the speed I can muster, all things considered.... Eventually I make it there.... Twenty minutes late! Well, it's only twenty minutes, and this is all pretty casual, right? Hopefully not too bad...

      "The nice fellow in the tiny faraway narrow room crowded with people explains that all the tables are full, though there are a couple people waiting around at the far table to see if they can get another GM -- they're plumb out of GMs. I wander back and forth past the tables in the tiny jam-packed narrow room, don't recognize the games being played except for one or two (House of Dread, I think). Rather than wait around I decide to head back to the dealer's room and shoot the shit with people at the booths.

      "Saturday! OK, this is it. I'm really going to -- oh crap. Overslept again. The ten o'clock slot is out. Will I hit the two o'clock?

      "I have to think really hard about this one. Cause my group leaves fairly early on Sunday, so Saturday afternoon is my last chance to have a nice unhurried time hanging out in the dealer room. And I'd talked to somebody at one of the booths about a possible very short pick-up/demo game at one of the booths that I was really interested in. It wasn't a guarantee, but.... did I want to sacrifice my last full afternoon at the con for whatever happened to be available in the jam-packed tiny room at Games On Demand?

      "Nah. I guess I'm not doing Games on Demand this year. Bummer. But I'm apparently too lazy to make the ten o'clocks, I blew one of the two o'clocks by showing up twenty minutes late because I wanted to sneak into a Freemarket game, and the other two o'clock, well... apparently I don't value play versus wandering around the dealer room quite as much as I thought, at least not when it's down to my last chance for an extended wander round the dealer room."
      \nThere ya go. I don't think there's probably anybody else at the con who had exactly the same combination of laziness and competing priorities that I did that hosed my plans to play at Games on Demand, but who knows.
