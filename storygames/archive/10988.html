\n I've got a lot of games, and some of them are games I'd love to run, but my poor head isn't in a learning-happy state right now.

When you've got a new game you want to run/play, and there's nobody there to teach it to you, how do you go about learning it? Do you just read through the whole text, for example? Or do you generate characters and roll some dice? Or what?

    \n I sit down with the game and a pad of paper and make a diagram of how play proceeds so that I understand the flow. For most games, the dice roll goes in the middle. Stuff that sets up the dice roll goes on the right, with arrows into the roll; for example, things that modify the dice, positioning, and so on. Results of the roll go on the left; like damage, fallout, etc. If the game includes some kind of feedback loop, arrows go from the results back to the influencers.

    This does a bit better job, IMHO, of illustrating what happens at the table, than you get from simply reading the rules. I really cannot run a game or even play very well without these notes.

    \n I read the book through once, constantly running a critical department in my head. Ideally I understand each paragraph of text not only as a passive student, but as a critic and designer. Therefore I understand not only how to do something, but also why. This helps immensely in putting together a complete picture of how the game's procedures should go, and consequently I don't usually have any significant problem in running the new game after one read-through. I still need to have the book there as reference for anything more complex than, say, Nine Worlds, but that's just because I don't memorize the text when I read it, I only analyze it.

    A negative facet to this active reading (as we might call the technique) is that sometimes I project too much and end up inventing my own procedures that do not in fact reflect the game text so much as my internal sense for how the game "has to" work. The effect is usually not very pronounced, but it does mean that I sometimes have great reluctance to play a game exactly according to its own rules after I've figured out how the rules "should" go. Probably the worst experience is when I read game texts from schools of thought that I don't appreciate or understand - Nordic immersionism springs to mind. It's like I have to turn my brains off and skip as much of the text as I can to get to the end. I guess that happens to you whatever way you read, though, as long as you allow yourself to evaluate whether the game sounds fun at all.

    I can usually play a game reasonably well if I've read the text in the above manner within a month or so before the session. More than that, it starts to fade. The same goes for when I read many games in succession (such as when going through the Gencon haul). It also depends on how interesting I found the game, of course. To give you an idea of how this goes, I'm pretty confident that I could play Time & Temp (read 3 weeks ago) or Dance & the Dawn (2 months) right away, while Annalise (2 months) I'd have to read through carefully a second time, because it didn't excite me that much on the first read-through.

    This is not to say that I can't have trouble with game texts, there certainly are some games that are written in such convoluted ways that I have to reread them several times or even ask for help from the author. Tony LB's games spring to mind, as does the first edition of Nine Worlds.

    \n I read the rules cover-to-cover, straight through (if the book says to use a different reading order, I follow that; if the book says to stop and do something else, I ignore it). Then I read the rules again, maybe cover-to-cover, maybe selectively. Repeat as needed. (This reading pattern comes from how I read computer books.) Then, if appropriate and they don't exist, I create cheat sheets for the game, particularly character creation cheat sheets. This both helps people I play with and makes me think about the game. I usually only create a character in my head, unless the process seems particularly fun or complex.

    Posted By: whiteknifeI read through the book more or less, skipping around and only actually reading the parts that interest me.
    \nSame here.

    If I like what I see and think that I might want to play it, I try to read through the rule section in order. I find examples useful here (if they're well done, naturally). Then I'll do a "test run" of the rules, where I sit down and make up a situation for various rules situations (a combat scene, a bartering scene, etc.), roll some dice, and satisfy myself that I know how it works.

    Then I write up a cheat sheet, something I pass around to everyone else that lets them know the basic rules and any finicky stuff (combat actions, how healing is handled, how a particular innate magical ability works, etc.); while this is for other people to use, it also cements my understanding of the game (because I learn best by teaching other people).

    So all in all, it's kind of a labor-intensive process if I want to do it right. I can cut corners here and there (usually giving the players a caveat of "I'm still learning this game, so this is an experiment"), but the cheat sheet is practically essential to me at this point. It's just so useful to everyone that I'm reluctant to skip it.

    \n I skip most of the flavour text and almost all of the information about the setting. Then I read the character creation rules once and then read the rules on how to handle tasks/conflicts, misunderstand them, read them again and move on to the rules about magic/space travel/cyberware, get bored or confused or both and give up on magic/space travel/cyberware. After that I put the book down and realise that I can't remember how to make a character, so I look at that chapter again, but by now I can't be bothered to read it thoroughly, so I just scan it quickly and convince myself that I'll remember when we start playing. By this time my friends are ready to play and I hand them the book, mumble something about rolling some dice while handing the book over to them opened at the character creation chapter. I veto any players attempts to create a character which might use magic/space travel/cyberware, making up some shoddy excuse about keeping the game simple for the first few sessions. Then when my players try to do something that I'm not equipped to deal with I either make up the rules and hope they won't notice or try to find the relevant rules in the book while they talk to each other about non gaming related stuff.

    \n I read the rules from cover to cover, then re-read the complex parts (normally the section on conflicts). I try a few conflicts myself, maybe roll a character if the character creation seems awkward, and then I hit the net.

    I haven't read a game that I've been able to digest properly without web help since I started down the story games path. There's a massive paradigm I'm trying to digest, and it's never explained in enough detail for me - in any rulebook I've read. I end up trawling the net for podcasts, listen to those and then I read the Actual Play reports. Once I'm happy with that, I try to run the game, fuck it up, and return to the actual play reports. At this point I rarely use the book anymore. I try another session, tweak how I think I should run it for my group, ask some questions online, and then finally, in the third session, I normally get things on track.

    Phew!

    \n I find I usually need a deadline. So after skimming the book very quickly, and checking out some buzz on the internet (which I usually do before even buying the book), if the game grabs me enough, I contact some friends and find a date to play.

    Then I start reading in stops and starts, often with days between each read. Mostly I read a chunk right away, then a few paragraphs here and there in the coming days/weeks, and then as much as I can just before we start playing. Sometimes I get through the whole thing, sometimes not.

    I usually create a few characters just to get the feel of how that works.

    If the game is weird enough, I study it in detail. Lacuna probably got one of the most thorough readings I've ever given a game.

    On a few occasions I've set up little flowcharts to understand the rules better. I did this with PTA, to see where fan mail comes from and where it goes.

    During the first session, there's some leafing through the rules, because there are always points that seem obvious during reading, but suddenly raise questions during play.

    \n I read the whole book straight through if I can; being that it's fun to read and enjoyable that is. If I end up struggling through more of the reference stuff like lists of things, I skip them or cherry pick interesting items.

    After reading it, if it's long I usually take a break from it entirely as I've had enough of that world or setting for a while. Then if I get excited about actually playing it with people, I'll reread pretty much the whole thing again. I'll need to do this because I'm reading it for functionality now. That's fun, but it's a different part of the brain for me.
    The first time I read a longer game my problem-solving mind simply skims over mechanics. I'm looking for more of a "feel" of the game. The second time I read it, I'm actually trying to figure out how to play.

    If it's a short text, I try to run it as soon as I can after reading it the first time since shorter games have less color and setting, while being mostly mechanics and rules. If I don't play them quick, the info will ooze out of my head fast!

    All this is assuming I find the games awesome. If I dislike it for any reason, I'll stop then and there.
