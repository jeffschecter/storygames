OK, over in the Least Favorite Jargon thread, I said that the Creative Agendas are mutually exclusive. Brand pointed out the CA Hybrid:

Hybrid

Play which combines two or more Creative Agendas. Observed functional hybrids to date include only two rather than all three, and one of the agendas is apparently primary or dominant, with the other playing a supportive role. See my review of The Riddle of Steel.

Now, I haven't played Riddle of Steel, though I've owned it for a good long time. But this is what Ron says in his review of RoS:

The Riddle of Steel includes multiple text pieces regarding the thematic drive of the game, which I have paraphrased to the Premise: "What is worth killing for?" It also includes a tremendously detailed, in-game-causal combat system. My call is that we are looking at Narrativist-Simulationist hybrid design, with the latter in a distinctly subordinate/supportive role. This is a scary and difficult thing to do.

The first game to try it was RuneQuest. [It didn't work.]

Now, I can see where Sim play might be marginally compatible with Narr play if I really stretch my mind. But also notice that the presence of two Agendas means that one of them is of much reduced importance. That means that, if I'm going for Narr play and am uninterested in the Sim aspects of the system, I'll be a little annoyed, and maybe it's little enough that I'm still having a good time doing what I want to do. But if I'm looking for a Sim experience, I'll be really frustrated by this other stuff on my sheet that matters a lot. I want Renaissance tropes, not challenging philosophical stances. Because, as Ron says,

In The Riddle of Steel, these elements of design aim unerringly toward one thing: the character as a philosophical statement...

Obviously, if I want Renaissance combat tropes and philosophical statements about violence, I'll be happiest with this game. But I think it's important to note that the presence of a particular CA at the very least reduces another CA in importance dramatically.

I think it's also important to note that TRoS doesn't go about this lightly. Those CAs aren't mashed together lightheartedly. I have enough faith in the game that I wager that where the CAs would clash in their design, the technique favoring the relevant CA is the only one remaining in the design.

It can still work, if the Gam external challenge mirrors the Nar moral challenge in some way. If it doesn't, the Gam players are aiming for something completely different than the Nar players, and there's a conflict.

    Of course, even */Sim hybrids can have agenda conflicts, depending on player tolerance levels for personal interaction. If you have a Nar player who also likes Sim alongside a straight Sim player who really just wants to daydream about Star Trek or LotR and doesn't want to hear interjected moral commentary, or even a player who occasionally likes Nar, but doesn't feel like it tonight, that Nar/Sim hybrid is going to lead to a conflict.

    A gaggle of bullet points, which you may or may not agree with, of course:

    * You can't really identify CA except over a long period of observation. Just like you can't really tell why anyone does any activity, or what they get out of any activity, from moment-to-moment observation.

    * I envision CA as a continuum, rather than as individual states. The states are more like Platonic ideals. Therefore, hybrid play isn't really hybrid at all, so much as accomodating of a wider range of points on the continuum.

    * Talking about CA in an individual context without taking into account a group context is almost entirely useless. I bet you I could tell the difference between you playing Capes purely to support your Gamist desires at a table where everyone else has a strong Narrativist agenda.

    * Because they have most to do with the real person's motives and desires, Gamist and Narrativist play actually look very similar to each other most of the time. The trick to spotting either is in the payoff, which will affect the kinds of choices made. Gamist payoffs have to do with self-esteem and acknowledgement, and so will generally revolve around making difficult choices less difficult through the application of system strategy (and I'm using system in a Lumpley sense, so not just textual rules). Narrativist payoffs have to do with investment in the themes generated by play, and so will generally revolve in creating a situation that's somehow arresting to the player regardless of its "wisdom" from a strategic perspective.

    * Games that tie in good rules strategy with theme-making can make it slightly harder to suss out the difference. I'd put Capes in that category. I believe I've observed Gamist play of Fate, where the player created a self-goal of always having more fate points than everyone else, and eventually started pointing that fact out when no one really acknowledged him as being a rockstar for the achievement.     Over the past few months, reading various descriptions of Creative Agendas (CA), I've come to the conclusion that they are the gestalt aesthetic of the play group. The rest of this post will be based on that conclusion, so please take it with a grain of salt.

    That means that, you cannot have multiple CAs in a group, because the CAs are a property of the group's actions. And it means, as a gestalt property the CA of a group is always in the process of becoming.

    Congruence is not a permanent state, as decisions occurs, the CA clarifies further. Hybrid play presupposes that two (assumedly) conflicting CAs can existing in a single group, as though they were a single CA. But since the CA comes from what you all do, not what you believe, there ultimately is at most one CA.

    Oddly, enough, this has nothing to do with any specific creative agendas. Time and time again, when I play with various groups I find ourselves building a shared sense of what is valuable, and what is not inside the game. Sometimes this does not come together, sometimes the aesthetic doesn't emerge. But the game is always better when it does.

    Now the claim of GNS then is that only three classes of CAs are observed, namely: G, N, and S. But can there be others? Could you have a single CA, resembling both G and N? Quite possibly. But for it to be a gestalt aesthetic, it needs to be self-consistent, allowing the group to reliably value different aspects of play in the same way. From the sounds of things, that may describe what Tony does, although it would need to also be true of his group.

    Perhaps, a game like Capes has different CAs than more traditional games. Many things become possible when you free Creative Agendas from GNS.

    The reason the theory fora got shut down was discussions like this. I like Andy's explanation of his actual play. Skip the G and the N and the S in the discussion. You'll find that the idea of a creative agenda makes sense then.

    Just a bit of history, GNS modes were claimed to be mutually exclusive, because when people started saying that they weren't, suddenly it became pretty provable that it was impossible to identify mode of play, and the theory became useless. So the definition came to be that they were mutually exclusive, simply so that they could exist as a framework for discussion. Nobody claims that any CA is composed of activity that can 100% be attributed to one mode or another. Simply that, taken as a whole, the agenda can be identified as X or Y overall.

    That is, it's quite possible to say that agendas differ by the specifics, as Andy has done. Also, remember that mode is but one way of classifying a creative agenda, and the most gross. It's also suitable to talk about Creative Agendas in terms of the lower level elements like techniques used, and ephemera (again as Andy does).

    Basically if modes don't seem indicative of a problem or phenomenon, they probably aren't, and it may be something intra-modal, or at another level entirely.

    Anyhow, a lot of this can be circumvented by pointing out that a text like TROS can only be said to be seen as promoting a particular CA. And that different players will probably interperet it differently. That is, there's not likely any one correct answer as to what TROS supports. At best you get like Ron's assertions that it seems to him to best support narrativism. Meaning, more than anything else, that when he plays it he sees narrativism play produced.

    Only actual play has an agenda. And nobody says that identifying the mode used there is always possible, either (Ron claims that it's usually discernable, but never says that it is 100%). Mostly it's important merely in discovering when there is a CA difference of some sort.

    Yeah, there are positive aspects of study of modes, too, like figuring out things to do better if you understand the preferred mode. But where its unidentified, the theory does nothing, and doesn't claim to do anything.

    If that makes it seem somewhat unuseful to you, then you may see why the theory fora are closed at the Forge.

    Another concept that people are forgetting is that of mode shift. Though an "instance of play" can be identified with a CA, that doesn't mean that the next will always have the same mode. That's different from both hybrid play and congruence.
