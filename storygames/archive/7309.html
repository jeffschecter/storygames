    \n It's a pity that this thread is dropping fast in the usual internet cesspool of personal attacks and defensiveness (with two different "tribes" that jump to "attack back" everybody seen as "attacking one or our own") because for a while, after Christopher calmly replied to the initial post (that, frankly, WAS a flamebait), it seemed as it could be possible to talk here about the nucleus of Kamil's and Philip's reaction to a game about their country history, without dismissing it as trolling.

    By the way, It's rather ironic seeing this dismissive behaviors in a forum that goes up in flames every time someone touch with a game some hot point of American history (like the old flamefest about Steal Away Jordan, for example).

    Trying to return to the point: I think that it's perfectly normal that somebody who see some part of history as his own, for better of worse, would be disturbed by the idea that someone, somewhere is playing a game about it. What I think is internet silliness at it's finest is the idea that this mean that someone, the people who are disturbed or the people who play, is "wrong" for doing so.

    We turn history and facts into fiction, to be able to understand them. It's the way our brain works. To take the most disturbing example: people who did not experience the nazist death camps can only hope to get even a glimpse of what it was only by experiencing these facts as stories. These are only a weak and diluted manner of exchanging experiences, and it's perfectly normal that they are dismissed by people who experienced that in person or by a close friend or family. But without these stories there would be no way to communicate, at least weakly, that experience.

    By the other hand, turning facts into stories mean distancing yourself from them. It's only from a distance that you can create fiction from history (and this not limited to history's horrors: people too near something can't create good stories from them. Think about when you saw last time the holiday's pictures of somebody, with him or her telling you what every photo shows...)

    For example. I am Italian. Renato already talked about some recent polemics here about the partigian's role in the war. Politics. From a day to the next heroes can turn into war criminals and then turn again to heroes, depending on who write the scripts for the news. But let's say that somebody do a game about the WWII in Italy. Could I play it? Sure. If I could play a freedom fighter killing nazis and fascists by the dozens as in a old hollywood movie. Or even in a more realistic setting, but still using the tone of the story as a distancing device. (it's what most spy games do, anyway: in most of them you don't play someone who plant bomb to kill civilians, or capture students to rape and torture them, right? That's unreality for you. Or Even Westerns, or most games of D&D that don't give a rat's ass about how people really lived in the middle ages). If you remove that distancing device, if you start talking about what really happened to the civilian population... I could not really play it. It's my parents life that I would play about.

    I have no problems whatsoever in playing a game of Carry, instead. It's more distant, even if it's closer to our time. I could create a story with it. And it wouldn't have to suck (players inspirations notwithstanding...), because it would be a story not about the specific experience of someone who was in the Vietnam (an experience I can't have) but it would be a story about my human reaction about what I saw about it from movies, TV and books. That distance would help me metabolize and use that historic material in a way that I couldn't use with events closer to me.

    I don't think that Jason's games aren't "for normal people", because they work in this "normal" way. Normal even for old rpgs that people play every day without thinking about it. How many WWII games have you played? How many spy games? How many westerns? How many martial arts games? How many games where you investigated a murder? How could you? Think about people who got someone close murdered. How can you play a GAME about it? How can you even see FICTION on TV about it?

    It's very normal doing it, it's very normal never notice it, it's very normal seeing it only when, after all this time, it touches you from a lesser distance, and think "this game is not for normal people", even if you have always played in your life games that did the same thing with other people's heritage, without even thinking about it.

    Jason's games only turn the flashlight over to some other target, and not the usual ones that people are accustomed to and are "safe" because "everybody is doing it". And for this I applaud him. Even if some of these I could not play myself. Stories are the way we learn, and with no stories, history would fade into oblivion.
