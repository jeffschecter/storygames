So... when do you deserve the label "Game Designer"?

Andy
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Story Games
So, I was thinking about the "What is your Go-To-Game (for design reference)" game, which I added to.

Currently, I've written a single joke RPG that is approx 1 page long.
I've participated in Game Chef and have a 40-page currently-unplayable game (which I intend to fix up in the next two years).
I'm translating a game from Japanese into English, but I'm certainly not designing it. I'm adding to it, and editing it, but certainly not designing.

Other than that, I've got some ideas for games that will take up a couple dozen pages, that are more-or-less in the works now (that is, I throw a little bit more words at the document every few days or so).

I don't really feel like a designer, though I think a lot about the process of designng games. And if you look online or to RPG creation groups, you'll see a lot of people who introduce themselves as "game designers" even though they have never released anything, and are perpetually working on/talking about a game that is probably unwritten...

So what is that dividing line for you? For you, what does a person have to do/accomplish/publish/etc to be an "RPG designer"?

Do they have to have published their games for money? Does for free count?
Do they have to work for an RPG company?
Are you a designer if you cranked out a 24 Hour RPG, Ronnie or Game Chef game?

-----------------------

For me, I've got kind of a blend, or "range" of "How much I think someone is an RPG Designer".

If someone has themselves put together a game, no matter how shitty it was, and sold it either in hard or soft copy, and sold, say, like 50 copies, then I consider them a "True RPG Designer". Exceptions are sometimes made for folks who publish free a fuckload of their work, like Simon Washborne or Marco Chacon.

If someone has only published stuff online from their website/wiki/blog/etc and the like for free, they kinda fall into a second category for me, like an "enthusiastic hobbyist". But that's at the same level to me as someone who publishes some excellent house rules for D&D online.

If someone hasn't released anything complete of their own (ie folks who call themselves "RPG Designers" who haven't actually published yet, and haven't done any side-works and the like), I don't consider them an RPG Designer. This includes folks who have done the Game Chef, 24 Hour RPG or Ronnie or whatever, but haven't gone back to finish and publish their title. Like Me!

If someone writes for a 'large' or 'established' RPG company and have their name in the credits to a large release, then I'm mixed: If someone can say, "This system here, I designed with my own hands and playtested it", then I consider them an "RPG Designer". Otherwise, they mostly fall into the "RPG Writer" category (writing world fiction, etc: A noble and difficult craft, don't get me wrong, but it's not designing).

You?

    I don't really feel the need to argue with other people's definitions, because I think y'all are saying smart, reasonable things, even if you're coming from a few different points of view.

    For me, personally, I make a distinction between being a game designer and being a professional game designer. You can totally be a game designer even if you've never formally published anything or worked on a publication-oriented project (which caveat I add because of all the great designers I know in the computer game industry who've had their projects killed), and for what it takes to "be a game designer" I'd agree with the consensus that seems to be emerging.

    To be a professional game designer, though, I think is a bit harder. Either you have to make money off your work, or you have to be doing your work with an expectation of making money (either now or someday), or you have to in some other way treat it professionally - otherwise it's not your profession. A lawyer who volunteers as a paramedic is still a lawyer, professionally, and I think the same argument goes with game design. Of course, most of us can't afford to make our whole living off of making games, but it's an attitude/expectation thing.

    To bring this back to specifics, I am a professional game designer. I used to support myself full-time making and consulting about mostly-non-roleplaying games, and even now that I'm back in grad school I still do a fair bit of paid, professional work. While I don't intend to make a living directly off my role-playing games (because, well, ha!), I do expect that it will contribute materially to my game design skills, and to helping me better understand and perform the research that will make me an academic success and therefore lead to my becoming a professor, etc. etc. This may sound cynical, but I have to think about how I want to be working in this field in five years, and that means taking my design practice very, very seriously, and thinking about how it can help me professionally in the long term. I also love designing role-playing games - but when I think about it in a professional context, loving it is not the point.

    I'm also a game design volunteer, with the various work I do with kids and women and all that. That's not something I have an expectation of helping me professionally; I do it because it matters to me. I suspect for many people, the design they do falls into this category - avocation, not vocation - not because the work is less professional, but because there's no real expectation about the outcome.

    So, on to the sage head-nodding, or the ritual kicking of the ritual butt.

    Here's some sage headnodding:

    I think Jess is right about the specificity of "professionalism". It's another fact: do you make money doing something? Enough that you'd miss it if it wasn't there? You're professional. That's where I'm at.

    Mearls is obviously a professional game designer (and a decent person, to boot), as is Jared. Where the question gets interesting is in cases like Ben's and Vincent's, where they both make a significant percentage of their incomes from games, but do other things, too.

    I think I draw the line at effectiveness. If you've made a game all the way: from conception to public acceptance, dealing with playtesting, printing issues, getting a PDF workflow that functions, and so one, then you've designed a game. (Of course, you don't have to do all these things yourself; you just have to confront the issues)

    You can't say you're biking if you're just pumping up your tires. You could say that you're biking over to Northampton while you're on your bike, but unless you know what's involved from experience, that's an optimistic appraisal. It could turn out to be much harder than you thought.
