\n I'm interested in hearing if anyone has written up a sort of mission (or vision) statement before they've started work on a new RPG design project. Sort of a "here's who our audience is, here's what the game should be about, here's why it's cool." It would be a combination of a wish-list, writer's spec, and elevator pitch.
\n
\nIf you have experience with this or have ideas, please share them! I'm hoping to do this for my next project and would like to hear from those who've tried it before.
\n
\nCheers,

    \n The Big Three Questions are definitely about making a prospective game designer focus on these sorts of issues. They don't worry about marketing issues ("Who's the target audience?" "What are your plans for publishing?" etc.). But they do make a game designer say "This is where I think the game design is going." And having some sort of vision for a work helps guide the design... and makes it easier for others to give advice on achieving the project's goals.

    \n I can't speak to other game companies, but I know that at my previous employer, making one of these mission statements would have been a waste of time, because marketing would come in at the 11th hour and finally get involve with what you were doing. So retrofitting and ret-conning were the prescribed methods of getting things done. That said, I always start with one myself, but I'm never afraid to deviate from it in the course of making something fun.

    \n I've written something akin to a mission or vision statement, as a designer (not specific to any one game).

    Taken from this page, here are my design principles:\n
    My designs are meaningful and thoughtful.
    Even the simplest and smallest among them are designed to provoke meaning.

    My designs are simple and shareable.
    They are easy to teach and can be played within one or two sessions.

    My designs are near and dear to my heart.
    You can discern my secret self through them.
    \nNot all of my designs have held to all of these principles, but Ribbon Drive and Perfect Unrevised and The Quiet Year all do.

    \n As I worked on ApocaLARP, I started out with an inspiration about the setting - "what if ALL the ways that the world ends in different apocalypse settings happened at the same time, and the players were in the middle of it, and many of them are at fault for one part of it or another." (I pare it down from there for the elevator-speech version, but that was pretty much the starting thought.)

    Then I developed a set of design principles that I wanted to work toward. I had a few mechanics ideas in mind, but I forced myself not to get too attached to any of them before developing the principles first.

    To relate it to software development, I forced myself to write up functional requirements before I started on implementation.

    I wanted everything in the design to be there for a really good reason.

    And that included identifying who the audience was and what the game was about and why it was cool.

    It's also part of why the game only ran a few times. Because one of my principles was that I wanted the game to be a supplement to the other games going on in the area, not a replacement. I was specifically planning to recruit players from other games, but I didn't want those players to run out of time and money to play the game I recruited them from. So I refused to schedule it at times when there were plenty of other games happening. And after I ran it a few times, some other games started up, and the schedule has been pretty dang packed ever since.
