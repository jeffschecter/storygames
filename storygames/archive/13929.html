Smallville Abilities: when do you have to spend a Plot Point?

\n
\n
\n
\n\njenskot \n\n\n

\n\nMarch 2011 \n edited March 2011 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n I'm being shot at and I want to disarm my opponent.
\n
\nI grab dice for my appropriate Relationships and Value. Additionally, I figure since I'm being shot at my Super Speed d10 would be useful for dodging and reaching my opponent quickly.
\n
\nQuestion:
\n
\nWhen do I spend a Plot Point to use Super Speed?
\n
\nI can use Super Speed when it is relevant for free. I don't spend a Plot Point to activate it. I only have to spend a Plot Point when I want to use certain effects...
\n
\n1. Join a scene or switch to a new scene regardless of distance.
\n2. Catch bullets or other missiles out of the air.
\n3. Run fast enough to cross water as if it were solid ground.
\n
\n#1 and #3 seems straight forward. Spending a Plot Point lets me traverse a non-mechanical obstacle that is still a problem fictionally.
\n
\nBut lets take #2... If I'm always moving fast for free, why would I spend a Plot Point to catch a bullet? If I'm super fast, wouldn't I just get out of the way or move any potential targets to safety? I could definitely see doing it for the cool factor. But am I missing anything?

    \n Nope, you're not missing anything. Special Effects are "the cool factor", they let you do things you normally wouldn't be able to, like use a Control power to attack or an Attack power to do something else. Maybe it's a Kryptonite bullet and you don't want just any old cop looking at it with a microscope? Or if you're Jack Burton and it's all in the reflexes. As a practical matter, if your goal can be achieved without using the Special Effect, you don't have to use a plot point.

    Edit: Another situation where catching the bullet would be better is maybe you're trying to make the shooter more Insecure ("holy shit, that's badass, he didn't even have to dodge") instead of Angry ("ARGH FUCK ALL SPEEDSTERS")

    \n A lot of Abilities discussion I've seen assumes that the Plot Point expenditure happens outside of Contests, and sometimes takes the place of a Contest roll. So if yo have Super-Strength with "pay a Plot Point to throw or knock another character out of the scene" or "catch a flying car or heavy object", that just happens, without a Contest roll. Cam has stated on the Cortex boards that it only becomes a Contest if someone else wants to counter it with a related Ability, or if two people want to activate conflicting Special Effects at the same time. So in those cases it's a Contest to see which character gets to use the SFX, and the winner then spends a Plot Point and the SFX, once again, just happens, without its own Contest to determine the effect. I got the feeling that you can still, while rolling an Ability die within a Contest, narrate like, whatever, with your power (try to catch bullets? Fine! T ry to knock a foe away? great!), but it just doesn't have the automatic, decisive effect that a Plot Point SFX does.

    Now, the above has seemed a little wonky to me, but not without a certain logic. And it's about the only way having SFX at all makes sense to me...otherwise it's an unwelcome, simulation-y intrusion in an otherwise smoothly narrative conflict system.

    \n It's not really a Contest if you're not picking up dice, yeah. It could be seen as a kind of cheat, but then again, there are many circumstances that would make it prudent not to be invoking the SFX.
    \n
    \nDon't forget of course that you can always roll that d10 into your pool if you want, for free, unless you've got another Asset in there already. Most of the time, speedsters are doing that. It's how Flash somehow keeps getting smacked, tripped, clobbered, etc even though he should technically be faster than anything on the planet. He just doesn't spend the PP all the time, he's rolling the die into his Contests instead.
    \n
    \nThat said, the whole point of SFX is to handwave stuff that would be stupidly annoying to roll dice for and which are for the most part massive fiction edits in your favor. Hence Super-Strength's throwing a truck into next week for a PP, or Flight's joining a scene. The game doesn't really care how many tons you can lift if you're Super-Strong, only how dramatically important it is at any given point that you're stronger than normal people.

    \nYeah, basically it's a "nope, you have to achieve what you want another way".

    Example from a Marvel Movie Trailerverse game I ran:

    Mystique wanted to "obtain" a mutant kid who was under the protection of a former superhero, now private eye (basically a Jessica Jones-alike).

    The private eye realizes Mystique is about to grab the kid. I'm literally reaching for my dice.

    "I punch her out of the scene?" he asks, ponying up the Plot Point.

    "Okay." I say, and poof! Mystique is gone, just as if they'd battled and she lost, with one exception: she has not suffered any Stress.

    She came (almost) right back at him two scenes later.

    Using Plot Points/Special FX to bypass confrontations is not tactically a good idea, because both getting and inflicting Stress is good. But as noted it can be good for other, non-tactical reasons.
