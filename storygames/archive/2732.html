\n So, in my experiences with Misery Bubblegum, I've found this interesting (at least to me) thing. The game gives mechanical support for expressing your opinion of others, and for seeking their opinions in return. So I don't have that "outside the system" feeling when I'm doing that kind of thing for the story.
\n
\nBut here's the thing: Expressing things about yourself proves to be pretty bad strategy in those terms. Expressing your dreams for the future does not, in fact, change people's opinions about you. I've tried it, and ... it just doesn't. People make up their mind about you based on the things you do that aren't aimed at them. You can't change that by expressing yourself to them.
\n
\nSo why do we do it?
\n
\nBecause we really must do it ... I'm finding that I'm taking time and effort to make up secrets and then reveal them, even though it's getting me nothing in mechanical terms. My instincts for a good story absolutely demand it, and now I'm trying to deconstruct my instincts.
\n
\nOne obvious thing is exposition: Characters share truths with other characters because it helps the audience be in the know. But the same thing can be achieved with an internal monologue, so I don't think that's the necessary factor here.
\n
\nNo, I think the thing is this: Telling someone else a secret is a great big honking flag about a change in your relationship with that person. By revealing something that you wouldn't reveal to just anyone, you show that a person is not "just anyone."
\n
\nKare Kano time: Arima tells Miyazawa that he is adopted ... and goes on to tell her that his birth parents were scum-bags, and that he has spent his life desperately terrified that if he isn't perfect every damn second he'll turn out to be just like them, and hurt the adoptive parents that he loves so very much.
\n
\n'scuse me ... Give me a second. >sniffle<
\n
\nOkay, I'm back.
\n
\nMy point (before I got all teary) is this: Yes, that's a sad, sad story ... but if I learn it all through flashbacks and internal monologues and stuff then, frankly, my response is only a fraction of what it could be. What grips me is that Arima tells someone. What must it cost him to say those words to another human being? And, therefore, how much must he love her to make her the only person he ever reveals that part of himself to?
\n
\nYou can totally play a character that never does that. In fact, I would argue that every PC I can think of tells their story without flagging their relationships this way ... because we, as roleplayers, are horrifically, abyssmally bad at using this kind of revelation properly. I cannot remember ever having seen it done well. We rely almost entirely on other flagging techniques (for instance, showing that a relationship has evolved by the choices that a character makes).
\n
\nWhen I realized that, I said to myself "Okay, so what kind of character do you get when you choose never to single a person out to confide in?" My response: "A lonely one." We play very, very lonely characters ... even in the midst of proven family and friends. Isn't that strange.
\n
\nI genuinely do think that it's just a technique thing. Certainly, before I started thinking hard about this, I would never have thought to use a revelation in precisely that way. I wonder if all that's needed is to make a little mechanic that points out to people "Hey, if you're feeling strongly toward this person then you probably want to confide something difficult about yourself to them."

    Perhaps something like this...

    Only the player revealing the secret can decide to do so. He doesn't gain any mechanical advantage in doing so. The Player who is told the secret gets a some sort of advantage in an immediate conflict they are having. I'm thinking something along the lines of Bob is reallys struggling with his relationship with his parents, so Fred opens up to him and tells him about his abusive parents and how he understands Bob's position. Bob then gets a bonus in dealing with his parental issues because he feels someone understands and cares. However, Bob also gets a token of some sort that can be used as a bonus only against Fred in a future conflict. This represents Fred's vulnerability to Bob since he shared a secret.

    Thus, sharing a secret is of little value to the person sharing the secret except it helps his friend out but it is of some value to the person being told the secret. The person getting the real value just doesn't ever get to make the call on whether or not the secret gets revealed.

    Admittedly this would need to be tweeked. I doubt I came up with something brilliant off the top of my head.

    \n The thing is ... I think that revealing a secret to somebody often does have a self-oriented motive. I don't think it's selfless. I mean, look at the thread title ... Let me tell you a secret. I think we mostly reveal secrets that matter to us. It isn't (necessarily) the secret that matters to the other person, but the act of revelation itself.

    \n Why do generals get drunk and boast of their secret plans in the hearing of spies, or criminals confess to prosecutors? Neither of them is trying to build trust. In the generals' case, the secret is "positive": Revealing the secret makes you sound well-connected, in the know, and powerful, so there's a strong desire to get it out there to impress other people. In the criminals' case, the secret is (usually) "negative": Revealing the secret will probably make people think less of you, but concealing it is outright painful, and there's a strong desire to get it out there so you can stop worrying about other people finding out and just get it over with, and maybe get some sympathy as well. The "you can't handle the truth!" scene from A Few Good Men is a classic example that hybridizes both of these: The protagonist's crucial insight is that the officer he's prosecuting wants to say he committed the crime in question because he's proud of having done it.

    The relationship between the person telling the secret and the person hearing it is utterly secondary to the internal psychological tension within the person struggling over whether to keep the secret or tell it. In the sentence, "let me tell you a secret," the "tell you" aspect is far less important than the "let me." The primary reason people tell secrets is to relieve internal psychological pressure.

    \n Sydney, you are not talking about the same thing. You examples aren't secret sharing, they are secret exposing. Sharing a secret is done with the intention it will stay a secret while secret exposing is done for the express purpose of eliminating the secret by revealing it to the general populace. Everything Tony has said thus far seems to indicate that it is secret sharing he is talking about. If I am wrong, I am sure he will correct me.

    That said, maybe a mechanic for secrets will need to handle both sorts of events, since a betrayal of the trust will often (if not always) manifest as the exposure of the secret. You'd want small continuous mechanical benefits for sharing a secret and keeping someone else's secret while offering a larger one-time payoff for exposing the secret and a slapdown for the sharer if it is exposed... and possibly a way to mitigate the slapdown if you expose your own secret.

    \n I think, though, that Sydney correctly expresses some of the emotional freight of what I'm talking about here: People have this (seemingly insane) desire to share even the darkest of secrets. Keeping a secret ... even one that nobody is asking about or has any clue about ... is hard emotional work. It ties people up in knots. Revealing the secret (whether to one person or to the world) is a relief. People want to do it.
    \n
    \nBut Sydney, I think that you're wrong that the relationship is secondary. You're deliberately picking some extreme "any port in a storm" situations ... criminals confessing their crimes, etc. Those are people who can no longer be discriminate, no longer choose who they reveal themselves to. In most cases, people are under less intense (but usually longer-lasting) pressure ... they will wait years (or a lifetime) to find the one person they can share a secret with. They will bear the burden of the secret far beyond any reasonable limits. That's why the right relationship can be so powerful ... it can let you release a tension you've held so long that it's become a part of you, as unconscious as breathing.
    \n
    \nI think, as Eric says, that a secret shared with the world ceases to be a secret (and should cease to have any lasting mechanical effects). It's the secrets shared with just one or two people that make the difference ... those secrets show that you view those people as importantly different from everyone else. It shows that they are special to you.
    \n
    \nConsider this situation:\n
    \n
    Amy tells Brett that she' wants to be a police officer, even though she's small and wiry.
    \nBrett feels valued and special, being in her confidence this way.
    \nBrett supports Amy as she secretly takes steps toward her dream.
    \nAmy wins a Judo tournament, surprising everyone (except Brett)
    \nPeople think of Amy as a tough girl now. She reveals her dream of being a police officer, and now many people support her.
    \nBrett feels lessened now that everybody knows Amy's dreams. He shares no secret with her. He's no longer special.
    \n
    \nGoing public with a secret doesn't have to hurt people because of the contents of the secret. Sometimes the contents of the secret are, actually, pretty good ... "I love you" is a classic secret, for instance. But going public is always going to hurt in some sense, because it destroys the private little t\xc3\xaate-a-t\xc3\xaate of a secret shared.

    \n I wish I had a better sense of which piece of this phenomenon is the right place to "stick the wedge in", rules-wise.
    \n
    \nYou could have rules where a secret that you're hiding from everyone is a sort of two-sided Key ... one which lets you do seemingly nonsensical things (motivated by the secret) and simultaneously gain both power in the story and more tension driving you to reveal the secret.
    \n
    \nOr, you could have rules where a secret that you've shared with someone else is the fundamental thing: maybe a source of income that you profit from whenever the person you confided in acts in a certain way.
    \n
    \nOr, you could have the threat of public revelation be the major mechanical factor, so that the act of revealing a secret is a second-order mechanical effect ... one which changes the landscape by giving people new options and responsibilities.
    \n
    \nRight now I feel like I'm not giving anyone enough directon to make the thread a productive one ... nobody's called me on the whole "What the heck are you looking for in this thread?" thing, but I'm very conscious of the fact that they could.
    \n
    \nI guess what I'm looking for in the thread is a good sense of what I should be looking for in the thread :-) The right questions are so much harder to come by than the right answers.
