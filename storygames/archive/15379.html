\n Warning: this is even more half-baked that my usual posts, and what I'm after isn't quite gelled in my head...

A standard trope of fantasy gaming is the "go retrieve the McGuffin from the 'dungeon'" adventure. By this point, everyone (particularly the players) knows that this dungeon probably has around 40 trap- and creature-infested rooms in it, and you can be pretty damn sure that the McGuffin is in room 39 or 40 (an inevitability of entering a directed graph).

So, I'm wondering about such a dungeon where the McGuffin is in room 2 or 3. That is, the party finds the McGuffin almost right off the bat.

There are some obvious reactions you can expect:
  • "We got the McGuffin already? Sweet! Let's get out of here."
  • "We got the McGuffin already? That sucks! Your game sucks! Let's get out of here."
  • "Well, we got what we came for, but there is more here to check out. Let's go kill things and take their stuff."
  • "Well, we found the McGuffin, but check out this other thing here. Doesn't that kind of remind you of that legend we heard a few months back about the Whogiwaggle? Maybe that's here, too. Let's go find it."
\nI'm mostly interested in this last reaction. It seems like encouraging this reaction would require legwork from the GM, but the adventure itself could probably do things to help out a lot.

Given the huge number of adventures generated for the d20 system, I'm wondering if any one knows of examples of published adventures that did this kind of "bait and switch" well. And if so, what did they do that helped make it fun?

    \n Probably the key is that finding the MacGuffin is some kind of dramatic reveal. They find it... only to realize that the story is much bigger than what they thought/not what it seemed.

    Some kind of surprise twist would probably be the thing here. It could be as simple as finding the treasure, only to see signs that this desired object is only a small fraction of the total wealth accumulated here... but to find the rest, they need to travel deeper into the dungeon. Complex conspiracies based on the past or loved ones of the characters are somewhere at the other end of the spectrum, I suppose.

    \n I have done something similar to this when I ran DnD in the past.
    Town A is about to attack town B because town A is running low resources.
    Town A pays party well and pleads their hearts to reclaim artifact X which will help their (crops, water..etc) so they will not have to fight town B.
    Party finds Artifact X, if removed it will destroy the crops of the entire eastern half of the continent.
    Yes, it will make Town A prosper if they have it. But everyone else will suffer greatly.

    Do you help Town A and get your money, even though it will likely lead to the destruction of everything else. It is even better if you also know that Town B will destroy Town A once they realize what has been done.

    And for DnD it is also necessary to make it a relic in the cave of orc tribe or something.

    \n I don't think this would require a lot of legwork on the GMs part. I could go into more detail but basically have an interesting environment with multiply meaningful choices. A lot of the "what's over here, let's go" is about GM technique such as a West Marches game or most Traveller games.

    Modules:

    Death Frost Doom does this pretty well but requires the PCs the fiddle with everything in the dungeon to set stuff in motion.

    No Dignity in Death does a great job of setting up a crazy environment and social situation for the PCs to follow through on.
