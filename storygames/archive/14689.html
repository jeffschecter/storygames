    \n I can't speak about Leverage, but Smallville does something that I've not seen before. It's great for doing soap operas. Character generation produces the wonderfully twisted little knot of people who all have strong feelings for each other. The play practically writes itself from there. Yes, other games have lifepaths and R-maps, but they don't reliably and easily produce something as great as what you get from Smallville.

    In play, the inter-PC relationships and values are always front-and-centre, as they're the core of the resolution. Even before you get to the dice, you're thinking about why your PC will be doing something, and who they're doing it for. And the challenge/rewrite cycle means you're constantly looking to bring in the melodrama as you change your character's beliefs and relationships. Plus, the fact that you get rewarded for taking damage and having it healed means that you want to plow into tough challenges, even if they're tough.

    Oh, and it's got some superpowers as well, but you can quite happily ignore them.

    We're playing a fantastically low-key, very English game of Littleton at the moment. I'm thinking about using Smallville to run a game of HBO's Rome soon. It'll be ideal.

    \n I've read and run Leverage.

    It does a great job of emulating the show's particular sub-type of the heist/caper genre: flashbacks, on-the-fly complications, sudden revelations that cause the plot to turn 90 degrees on a dime, emphasis on teamwork and the extreme competency of the player characters, and making failed die rolls interesting instead of disappointing.

    In a more broad sense, what it does well is take a system that -looks- traditional and retool it in a focused way that evokes it's source material beautifully.

    As a side note, I really like Leverage (the show), and I despise Smallville (the show). I was not going to buy or read either game because of my past experiences with other MWP/Cortex games, but a friend loaned me his copy of Leverage because he wanted me to run it for him for his birthday. I was maybe a third of the way through reading it when I decided I needed to buy my own copy. Once I'd finished it, I decided I needed to own Smallville too.

    \n A little background: I've headed up all of MWP's RPG projects since 2009. Prior to that I was managing editor and a contributing designer, as well as line developer for the last few Dragonlance d20 projects. My first big project as lead was the Big Damn Heroes Handbook for Serenity (at the end of the product line), for which I specifically directed writers to go a little indie and play with new narrative ideas.
    \n
    \nWhen we acquired the Smallville and Leverage licenses I decided to use the opportunity to update the basic Cortex rules and embrace the whole "system matters" philosophy I'd been seeing in the small press and indie community. I figured we should be designing the games from the ground up for each license or property. I was aware of criticisms of our older games, and I also wanted to put some distance between Cortex and Savage Worlds (which is doing just fine all by itself).
    \n
    \nEven before I started work with my design teams on the projects I settled on some changes that are shared between the Cortex Plus games. I threw out the d2, eliminated "Life Points," increased the potency and importance of Plot Points, removed fixed target numbers, and settled on the "roll a bunch of dice but keep the best two added together" mechanic. I didn't want the advantages/disadvantages model which so many other games had (like Unisystem and Storyteller), and wanted to cap dice ratings to d12 instead of going to the d12 + d2 and d12 + d4 thing.
    \n
    \nFor Leverage, I had Fred, Rob, Ryan, and Clark tossing great ideas into the mix to develop how the capers and heists of that property would work, how characters would be modeled, all of that. The Quickstart Job was our initial design and even that had numerous iterations. For the rulebook, there were more layers of revision and changes that drew on how things were going over on Smallville. The end result resembles Smallville in many ways but flips some expectations completely, which I like.
    \n
    \nSmallville benefited from Josh Roby going completely bananas with cool story and narrative ideas which I would then drag back toward mainstream or, upon further reflection, restored to their hippy status. It was a great collaboration. We're really nothing alike in design but it helped tremendously. Even better was what happened when it was heavily playtested by various groups. One group actually fed us the idea of ditching Attributes and Skills completely. Neither Josh nor I had entertained that idea, really, but once it was put into practice we were huge fans of it.
    \n
    \nFor each game that we produce now, I start with the Cortex Plus skeleton and the language we use to describe it, and push it in the direction of expected play. Dragon Brigade, our first real fantasy setting (albeit with airships and muskets) is a very swashbuckling romantic intrigue kind of thing, so it's got different requirements than either Smallville or Leverage had. And so it goes.
    \n
    \nCortex Plus games are tied to licensed properties but they have great potential beyond those licenses. People have been using Smallville to run games inspired or even directly modeled on True Blood, Deadwood, various Smallville-style DC or Marvel takes, the Tudors, Zelazny's Nine Princes of Amber, Glee, Roswell, House MD, and many other homebrews. Rob Donoghue has hacked Leverage in all manner of ways as seen on his blog. And the upcoming Cortex Plus Hacker's Guide is an anthology of hacks of all kinds, including a Roman hack for Smallville by Shreyas Sampat, which is awesome.
    \n
    \nSo that's why I think our games are awesome.

    Anyway.

    So, i'm a Smallville Survivor. I checked out the show for two seasons, and when it was good it was good (mostly some season 2 eps), but when it was not-good it was very not-good. I think, like so many modern dramas (only moreso) the creators have a killer concept on their hands but don't know how to handle it. ANy time I see a show like that, it triggers the thought in the back of my head: "Man, I'd love to play this as a PTA game so it would be AWESOME."

    Well, the Smallville RPG does that. If you like Smallville, awesome, it lets you do more of that. If you've ever cringed at Smallville, awesomer still, this game will allow you to surpass it. Sure, you could p-lay PTA instead. And for some groups that would be the right call. I switched my Smallville New Mutants group to PTA after two sessions b ecause 3.5 of the players were allergic to the rules details and dicehandling that Smallville requires. But for a group that doesn't mind some crunch with their drama Smallville has a lot going for it.

    Most of its strong points have already been hit on. The lifepath development plus relationship mapping is solid gold, and even my crunch-leery group loved that. It really puts you on a solid footing with who your character is, and how they relate to the other cast and the world. And as JD said, each and every single action yo take in the game has why you're doing it baked right into it. All the statements you write, for your six values and every relationship on your sheet, are like Burning Wheel beliefs in the way they give you and the rest of the table a handy guide to what your char is about, but with a really neat system for challenging and changing those statements. It MAY even be better than Key buysoffs. Maybe.

    And the contest system is intriguing. In addition to the In a Wicked Age back and forth that Jamie brought up, it also carries echoes of Dogs in the Vineyard: Escalate or Give, Esacalate or Give, until someone either backs down or suffers fallout. And the "fallout" is Stress in the form of Angry, Insecure, Injured, etc. SO you argue or fight, until someone either convinces the other, or else loses their temper/gets punched out/flees the room in tears.

    This last bit makes it particularly apt for soap opera, and is the biggest "TV Drama" thing you don't get from PTA. You're not just roleplaying and drawing for Stakes (or to answer a question," as in the PTA3 playtest rules)...you're speficically driven toward getting up in everyone's grill--your friends, family, enemies and frenemies--and convincing, cajoling, browbeating and pleading. Basically, you exacerbate tensions in a bunch of brittle relationships, to try to get what you want. And at least half of these confrontations will end without the central goal resolved, but with the relationship escalated, beliefs challenged, and important things said and done that can't be taken back. Perfect soap opera.

    Posted By: ccreitzPosted By: amnesiackIt does a great job of emulating the show's particular sub-type of the heist/caper genre: flashbacks, on-the-fly complications, sudden revelations that cause the plot to turn 90 degrees on a dime, emphasis on teamwork and the extreme competency of the player characters, and making failed die rolls interesting instead of disappointing.
    This is 90% of a sale to me. Could someone explainhowtheLeveragerules help the group get to this kind of fictional position and drive it home?

    It shoots for this on a number of levels. Rolling a 1 on your dice invites the Fixer (GM) to hand you a Plot Point and create a Complication, which is a temporary Trait he gets to roll in against you. The Complication has a name (like "Suspicious Guards") and a rating (starts at d6). The table can acquire a number of these as the game goes on. Plot Points can be spent on Assets, which are a player's version of the same thing, representing favorable circumstances, improvised tools, lampshaded objects, etc.

    Flashbacks are invoked by other players or can be activated by Plot Points, too. They let the players fill in the blanks and establish heretofore unrevealed truths about the timeline. At the end of the Job, the Mastermind player may also conduct a wrap-up flashback which involves all of the other players narrating flashback scenes, rolling dice to see if their flashbacks can generate bonus dice for the Mastermind, which he often needs to oppose the Mark's big pile o' dice.

    Many of the Crew's Talents are specifically team-oriented, like being able to create bonus Assets for other Crewmembers, pass around Plot Points, trigger cool events, and so forth. Crew competence is reflected by a general assumption that no roll leads to a dead-end failure, merely a disruption in the plan. And so forth.

    Leverage is, above all things, a game about being part of a team of bad-asses who face mounting complications and trials but will, in all likelihood, prevail. It's how that happens, and where the narrative goes, that is often the most fun part of the game.

    \n The only shared narrative control elements are:

    * When a complication comes up by someone rolling a 1 (and this can be in both Smallville and Leverage), the table is supposed to quickly brainstorm to figure out what it is. The GM has the final say and if nobody can come up with one there doesn't have to be a complication.

    * Players can create minor useful details in both games with in-game currency, similar to Adventure!'s dramatic editing. It's optional, you never have to do it if you don't want to.

    The BIGGEST shared narrative control thing (by the way, shame on you for this terrible piece of jargon even though I knew what you meant) is in Leverage, at the end of the caper, each player has a flashback showing "what REALLY happened" - planting a bug on a bad guy during a fistfight or putting something in a safe rather than taking something out.
