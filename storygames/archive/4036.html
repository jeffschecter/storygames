\n OK, folks -- time to open the can of worms.

At GenCon, I had a few things happen that bothered me, and I've been waiting for them to percolate in my brainpan long enough to come up with the appropriate way to discuss them.

What this all gets down to is the rather insular nature of the Indie games crowd -- which appears, at times, to border upon hostility to perceived outsiders. This sort of badge of self-identification that sets the "Indie" folks seperate from everyone else.

As some of you know, I was once told on The Forge, that I wasn't "Indie", mostly because I work with d20 in addition to my original designs. Sadly, I heard this said in a discussion at GenCon as well, at the IPR booth, in reference to another small-press publisher....specifically saying that his company would not be welcome at the booth, because they "weren't Indie enough."

I also had the misfortune of having someone relate a conversation back to me, wherein a third party was apparently critizing me for being a "carpetbagger", because I was coming in, taking the FATE system, and using it for a "commercial" product (Far West). Like I am somehow "The Man", cherry-picking from the Indie movement, just so I can sell it to the masses. I was told by the relator that I should expect a cold shoulder from "Indie" fans when FAR WEST is released, because they know that "I'm not one of them."

In another instance, I was on a shuttle bus, talking about my plans for Adamant with someone I had just met, who self-identifies as part of the "Indie" community. He took the opportunity to lecture me on the fact that "The Supplement Treadmill Is DEATH." And, when I pointed out that I was aware of this bit of Ron-Edwards opinion, he shook his head and told me that I obviously "just don't get it, because I'm in the industry."

What the fuck?

Even a positive event, like the ENNies, I've heard described (by Indie folks) as "being allowed to sit at the Cool Kids table" or "being asked to play with the Big Kids." Come on.

This is a false division, kids. We're all small-time. The entire games industry is a pimple on the ass of the entertainment industry as a whole. We're not nearly big enough to be divided into "Corporate Whores" and "Punk Kids." Not to mention that ALL of the companies in this business, even the largest, started out as basement-press efforts by gamers.

Enough with the attitude. That's all I'm trying to say.

    \n "Naming names" and even, really, the specifics of what was said (the merits of supplements, for example), are secondary to the point that I'm trying to bring across -- namely, that there is an exclusionary vibe to the Indie movement, that most posters here have acknowledged to one extent or another.....and, human nature or not, I feel hurts not only the movement itself (insularity is never good for a vibrant form), and the wider field as a whole (dividing into "us" and "them", which can prevent both "sides" from learning from eachother).

    I'm not interested in saying that "publisher X said this bullshit thing" -- it solves nothing. My main interest is in trying to communicate that "Indie" is, really, a false distinction -- that we're all part of the same field, and that exclusionary attitudes really need to go away (and to be fair -- they need to go away on both sides of the non-existant divide).

      \n Of course there is a perception of elitism and exclusion regarding 'indie' games. That perception is the learned response that society has developed to all things that have the audacity to label themselves as 'indie'.
      \n
      \n- 'indie' music that eschews the popularist beats and styles of chart music and instead embraces guitars and 'meaningful' lyrics
      \n- 'indie' comics that deny four-colour spandex heroes in exchange for deep analysis of the life of the teenager goat-herder. In monotone.
      \n- 'indie' wrestling where fireworks and soap-opera storylines are tagged out for stiff bouts, spot monkeys and 'proper' wrasslin. In school gyms
      \n
      \nEach of these creations have said ' we see the mainstream product and we want something different. We see the apparent barriers between us and success and we can overcome them. Rather than being consumers of a hobby, we are becoming participants of a hobby. And then producers of the hobby.'
      \n
      \nThats a very empowering statement but for many people who buy into the mainstream version of that product, it comes with some different assumptions. It says that there is something 'wrong' with the mainstream product. And when you have invested emotional and real capital into something, you don't want to be told that you are wrong. It says that you, the fan, are elevating yourself to a different level, moving up a perceived ladder. But it also says that you will not be part of that mainstream and somehow know how to be part of the machine but not be the machine. You clever bastard! Its very easy to generate some very negative elitist vibes from a pretty standard indie statement of intent.
      \n
      \nWhat I have also seen in other 'indie' sectors is a degree of zealotry from the fans of these new products. They have made a decision to seperate themselves from the mainstream but they have become participants in a far smaller section that runs under some very vulnerable basics. So everyone else's music simply isn't cool enough and they are 'sheep' to the record companies. Everyone's comics are purile and not exploring the artform. Everyone's wrestling is neutered and facile and soap-opera. If you aren't 'indie' then you aren't getting the true way that things can be. And you are therefore a zombie sheep of the corps.
      \n
      \nAnd what makes this even worse is that the learned knowledge is that 'indie' creators will tend towards the mainstream in a search for commercial success. The bands will 'sell out' and get a record label and end up on MTV. The comic writers and artists will get picked up by DC/Marvel and end up working on titles that have been around for 50 years with characters that appear on lunchboxes. The wrestlers will eventually make the leap and work for Vince, usually with the truncated WWE style and never fly again. Sure, there are the Enter Shikari, Dave Sim and A J Styles of this world, but they are few and far between.
      \n
      \nIf you apply these lessons to 'indie' games what can we see?
      \n
      \nDo we have people who have said that they have viewed the mainstream product but want something similar, but different? Oh yes.
      \nDo we have people who have lifted the curtain on the means of production and said 'Actually, I can do that!'? Oh yes.
      \nDo we have people who have moved from 'fan' to 'creator' to 'producer'? Oh yes.
      \nDo we have instances of 'indie game fan zealotry'? Oh God yes!*
      \nDo we have instances of language, understanding and preference being used to make value judgements on people? Oh yes.
      \n
      \nIt's pretty much standard for all things labelled 'indie'.
