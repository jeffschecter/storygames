Shadows Over Camelot

Jason_Morningstar
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Story Games
We just played a seven-person game of Shadows Over Camelot and holy crap, it is distilled rock and roll.

Production values: A+. Beautiful art, beautiful sculpts, beautiful cards and boards. It takes up a lot of room but all the fiddly bits work well.

Mechanics: A+. Brilliant. A cooperative game that scales seamlessly with player count, ratchets up tension effectively, and has a built-in, elegant end game mechanic - when you finish quests, cards that would otherwise hinder those quests instead become resources that press the game toward a (failed) conclusion for the knights.

Pacing: A+. Fast - your turn is constrained to a handful of choices and the game moves fast. You are engaged and involved throughout. Plus it doesn't take four hours to play - we rocked out in an hour and a half and only one guy had ever played before, and that was a seven person game.

If you play board games, go in with your pals and buy a copy of SoC. My mind is already racing over the cooperative mechanics and how to apply them, or some distillation, to current projects.

    I keep forgetting how relatively unknown this game is. It is in my mind one of the most important board games of the past couple of years.

    Andy, to answer your question: The big thing I learned from my first game of SOC was that the game itself can act as a fit form of opposition (at least in some cases). That is, while in RPGs we have traditionally assigned the role of opposition to a player (the GM), it may well be possible for the game mechanics to take care of this (or at least a large part of it). This opens up interesting possibilities for solo play, and it (along with Breaking the Ice) point the way toward some interesting two-player cooperative stuff. (Though I will note, because it is important here, that I don't think SOC would be nearly as compelling without the traitor mechanics. Every game you play, it is possible that one of the players is out to sabotage your effort. This adds a sense of human adversary, even if there isn't one.)

    Daniel, yeah. It's very much in line with Mafia, but with some very nice (what I consider to be) refinements. 1. The question of a traitor is not answered before play. So instead of trying to simply figure out who it is, you have to wonder if there even is one. 2. More importantly, it provides a proactive goal for the non-traitors. You win if you accomplish the victory conditions, not just because you outsmarted the traitor. This puts a shifting time pressure on the game as a whole: it's possible that you can pull a rush to victory before the traitor can fully hamstring you.

    Thomas

    Everyone is harping on the traitor mechanic. While I found it interesting and useful, I found it didn't affect play terribly much. It's hard to be a really good traitor. The first game I played, I was the traitor. I played along with everyone else, cooperated as much as possible, but made sure my choices were the weakest possible without arousing suspicion. I won, but mostly because the other players lost.

    The first thing I really took away from this game is the fact that players can join or drop from the game at any time. Since every turn includes Bad Stuff and then the player's go, there's not a big impact on the player count going up or down as play progress. Yes, you gain or lose some special abilities of the knights in question, but one of them might have been the traitor anyway. I do wonder if it might not be an easier game to win with fewer players (say, three or four) than the full complement.

    The last thing I really took away from this game, and I'm trying to apply it to my current RPG design, is that you bring your own Bad to the game. Since every turn includes Bad Stuff happening and then your go, things remain balanced. This ties in to my first point, but what I like about this part is the idea of a game where players can pool their efforts on something, but in doing so, more things might go wrong. In an RPG, it might work something like this: if one player participates in a conflict, the GM rolls X dice in opposition, and for every additional player who participates, the GM gets +X dice to his pool. It still might prove worthwhile for other players to get involved, as they might have abilities or expertise that might really help the conflict, but it dissuades players from dogpiling just to get a bunch of rolling done. There's an element of this in Dogs, as it is, in that anyone participating in a conflict is going to have to See and has the potential to get Fallout from a conflict. It really adds a choice to things. At the game level, this mechanic is fantastic for forcing the players to cooperate, as every bit of Bad Stuff brings all the players closer to losing the whole game, so there's more social pressure and table talk to have an agree-on group strategy.

    Just my two cents' worth.

    I love SOC, and for months it was our go-to game on Tuesday nights. But I'm... gonna step out here and say that I think the mechanics have some faults.

    Chief among them is that the only player with any real choices is the traitor. Once you're an informed player with a little experience (and once you understand the ratio of, say, Grail cards to Despairs), your choices come down to "go immediately to the quest where your cards will be most effective" and "lose." Sometimes there's a little leeway here, like if you've got a full house and neither the Black Knight nor Lancelot is occupied--but even then, Lancelot has such better rewards that it's just a calculated risk based on the black cards already played.

    Another problem, to me, is the table-talk rule. I understand the purpose: the game would be lose a lot of atmosphere (and choices for the traitor) if you could say "I have a run but I'm missing a two, somebody want to back me up at the Saxons?" In practice, though, it just serves to punish inexperienced groups. The Tuesday-night group I mentioned above can, at this point, communicate volumes without ever naming a card or a quest ("I'm going home soon unless somebody can back me up with a heavy hitter? I mean, not too heavy, but..."). That wasn't possible when we started playing, and it wouldn't likely be possible in a pickup game.

    Finally, as has been mentioned elsewhere, the pressure to win almost always comes from the siege engines, not the Table. The pace of engine-placement increases dramatically as the game continues, whereas the pace of black-sword placement remains the same or even slows. This leads to a lot of games where you're hoping to "win by losing"--everybody sits at home and draws cards to fight catapults, hoping that they'll draw enough Black Knights to fill the table (which already has a majority of white swords). The anticlimax can be remedied by houseruling that you have to win a quest to end the game, but that doesn't fix the pacing issue.
