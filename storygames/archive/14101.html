\n Here's a die mechanic.

Roll a number of d6s (usually between 3 and 10, can be higher or lower, but that's a good spread)
4+ is a success (so, 50% success per die) (successes are spent for all kinds of stuff, not relevant here)
If you wish, you can use your Drive to reroll all of the dice. Drive is a number between 1 and 10.
If you get more successes on the reroll than your Drive, your Drive increases by 1 (in addition to spending them on things)
If you get no successes on the reroll, your Drive lowers by 1.

The intended effect is that using your Drive makes good sense at lower levels, and it'll increase pretty quickly until it hits 5-6ish, and then diminishing returns kicks in and it's pretty difficult to get to the highest levels of Drive. Obviously, once you have a higher Drive than the die pool you are rolling, it's pretty hard to increase Drive (there are other ways, but not significant to this question).

However, this started to remind me of oWoD die pools, where larger pools rolled against higher numbers meant you succeeded less often. So I figured I'd see if any mathlete's could assure me that there isn't super statistical wierdness at the extremes (like, rerolling 10 dice with a Drive of 8, that kind of stuff)

Thanks!

    \n What you use successes for totally matters in this sort of calculation. For instance, if you only really need one success, then using the Drive only makes sense success-wise if you rolled no successes initially. Or if you always want as many successes as possible and nothing else matters, then you should use the Drive when you roll less than half of your dice as successes and never otherwise.

    If you're trying to increase your Drive and not so much simply succeed, that's a bit more complicated, but also instructive. Let's say your Drive is valued at D and your pool at P (assume P>D), then your probability of worsening your Drive (not getting any successes) is 1/(2^P) and your probability of improving your Drive is the cumulative binomial function F(D, P, .5) = Sum[i = 1 to D](P over i)p^i*(1-p)^(P-i) (simple in concept, but hard to write neatly without math symbols). Now that we know how probably a given reroll will increase or decrease Drive we can calculate the expected value of the Drive after the reroll: let's say that the probability of the Drive worsening is P[-] and of the Drive improving P[+]; now the expected value of Drive after the reroll is of course D-P[-]+P[+], as both outcomes add or deduct one point from Drive. Therefore you should only reroll (insofar as improving your Drive goes) if and when 1/(2^P) < F(D, P, .5). As it happens, the very worst case here is when D=P, in which case you need to get all successes to improve your Drive, the probability of which happens to be 1/(2^P). However, this is merely the same probability you have of fumbling - the probability of gaining all successes is the same as all failures for coins flips. Therefore we can conclude that you should always reroll insofar as improving your Drive goes as long as you have at least as many dice as Drive, or at least rerolling is never any worse than not rerolling.

    I'll add that if the above conclusion (that it's always beneficial for Drive to reroll) bothers you, you might use the solution I used in a similar situation: in my game when you fumble, you lose half of your Drive score instead of merely losing one point. This compromises the expected development of the Drive value drastically and makes for more dynamic scores in general, as going up is slow but coming down is quick.

    \nJust pointing out some things that might make the results easier to see in your head:

    You can replace these dice with coin flips.

    For an exactly average roll, you will get one success for every two dice.

    The more dice you roll, the more likely the result will be close to the average. (That is, the "bell" part of the curve narrows as the number of dice increases, even though the endpoints get further apart).

    You can never get fewer successes than zero.

    You can never get more successes than the number of dice rolled.

    Someone with Drive 2 rolling a pool of four dice is actually in the almost same situation as someone with Drive 6 rolling a pool of twelve dice. The only difference is that the latter is much less likely to get zero successes.

    If the only outcomes that change your Drive score are "more successes than Drive" (giving +1) and "no successes at all" (giving -1), then you should re-roll any time the number of dice exceeds your current Drive, no matter what your Drive score is. For example, say your Drive is 10 and you are rolling 12 dice. Your chance to gain drive in this case is only 0.31%; however, the chance to loose Drive is only 0.02%. So, since you are over fifteen times more likely to gain Drive than loose it, you should re-roll. (This analysis assumes that "re-roll" means "roll again and take the best result". If it means something other than that, there may be other reasons not to re-roll.)

    Hitting the "intended effect" that you mention, depends a lot on how you control the sizes of your pools. This means you will hit your desired effect if, as Drive increases (linearly) at the rate of x, guarantee that pool sizes increase no faster than linearly at the rate of 2x.

    \n One thing that is worth pointing out is that if player's pools increase in size over time the chances of drive decreasing is low. What this means is that unless players choose to risk their drive on those 3 and 4 die rolls the drive is going to trend upwards over time. Depends on number of rolls, etc but to get from 6 to 7 on 10 dice is more than 15%, while reserving drive for rolls of 5 or more dice gives me less than a 4% chance of losing drive. There are similar numbers for any other die pool size. If I'm rolling 6 I actually have a pretty reasonable shot at hitting drive 5 from drive 4 in ten rolls.

    Is drive used for anything else? If having a high drive is valuable to me for other reasons I might be less likely to risk it.

    How often do you roll? Will waiting around for that 15% chance pay off or will the game be over by the time I get ten rolls?
