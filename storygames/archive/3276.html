\n Interestingly, there seems to be a vague assumption among the SG crowd that conflict resolution is preferable to task resolution. I base this (admitted) generalization on both the language used to discuss ideas on these boards and the design choices of the games being released or talked about.

So, my question is simple: Which resolution scale do you prefer and why? In particular, I'd like to hear both pros and cons for both methods and see if we can map out the strengths and limitations of each, e.g., if you prefer one, do you think the other might be better suited for a particular type of game or particular genre?

    \n I much prefer mechanical conflict resolution where each individual "bit" of narration encompasses the actions a single character takes to achieve a simple, specific goal (i.e. the Intent).

    Larger scale (i.e. on a scene-by-scene basis) narration is a bit less to my taste; it feels like a more significant job, and you usually have less mechanical results to guide you.

    My favourite is rather like DitV; smaller actions build to a significant outcome; one way or another.

    And also, I find it better (both as a GM and player) that the Intent of every character involved in the conflict is clear to everybody at the table before the dice are thrown (or whatever) and anything happens ingame. It lets me get a feeling for the risk/reward trade-off before I commit to it (in the same way that I wouldn't want the croupier putting my money down on the craps table without me knowing what's going on. -- As an aside I wouldn't really want that same croupier to take less money than I owed if I rolled badly).

    \n I prefer conflict resolution for two reasons:

    1) PRACTICAL - As a general rule, my time for gaming is limited and for us, conflict resolution is much less granular and thus speeds up play. (For me, an extreme example of task resolution is d20 or Hero System combat - I love the principle, but don't have the time to lose a session to combat any more.)

    2) PSYCHOLOGICAL - It helps me as a Referee or a Player to understand what is important to the other players and their characters. Yes, you and should (and we do) discuss such thing before the game and before the sessions, but "forcing the subject" around each roll keeps it front of mind and can help proactively flag situations where the player or character's internal motivations shift mid-play.

    \n I have a pretty strong preference for conflict resolution, or intent-relevant resolution, as Fred's chart puts it. I don't really have much of a preference between small-scale vs. large-scale resolution (i.e., whether the mechanics resolve things blow-by-blow or resolve the entire fight with one roll) - some games work better with small-scale, some games work better with large-scale, and some games (*cough* TSoY) ingeniously incorporate both. Why do I prefer conflict resolution?

    The biggest reason is that all roleplaying games involve the resolution of conflicts. The problem with task resolution (or, more specifically, intent-opaque resolution) is that the mechanics don't resolve the conflicts for you. You can roll for all the tasks you want, but ultimately, succeed or fail, whether you achieve your goal is entirely up in the air, left to all that handwavey social stuff - which 99% of the time, means it's up to the GM. As far as I'm concerned, that's pretty shaky game design right there. I honestly believe that if task resolution didn't have the historical impetus that it does, no one would even consider the task- vs. conflict-resolution question - pretty much everyone would agree that conflict resolution is just better.

    \n I've been interested in understanding task and conflict resolution to see where my games fit into all of this, given that my stuff was developed long before these ideas were discussed.
    \n
    \nI see task resolution as 1. Player says what they want to do. 2. GM says what skill they have to roll to do it. 3. Player rolls and succeeds of fails. 4. GM narrates what happens after the roll.
    \n
    \nConflict resolution on the other hand seems to say 1. The player says what they want to do. 2. The GM helps them define what the stakes are. 3. The player role plays to build up their dice pool. 4. The player rolls and succeeds or fails. 5. If the player succeeds, they narrate how they win, if they lose the GM narrates how they fail.
    \n
    \nWhat my games do is neither. 1. The player says what they want to have happen next - (Action - Result and Reasons) right up front. 2. A referee (another player in the game or a set referee) sets a "to happen" roll based off of their judgement of the argument. 3. The player rolls and succeeds or fails. 4. Successful argument become facts as written, failed argument just don't happen at all. At no time does the referee have narration powers.
    \n
    \nSo... with a tear in my eye, I walk away knowing that my stuff doesn't fit in the catagories.
    \n
    \nWhen I do role play I frequently use task resolution because that is how the commercial games I own are written but I take a poetic reading the rules.

    \n It's never black and white-- I'm much more used to Task Resolution systems, and I think they can be quite elegant. But I do sometimes yearn for Conflict Resolution systems when a simple conflict degenerates into an endless series of rolls (Combat, fast-talking, and espionage ("have they seen me yet?" N"o, but roll stealth again") seem to be the biggest problem areas).
    \n
    \nThat said, I've recently had more exposure to games with only conflict resolution, and I find it very limiting, especially in multi-player scenes. The really cool unexpected action taken by a fellow player often doesn't have the ability in conflict-framed resolution to just change the conflict entirely, where if it was a series of tasks, their action could veer the scene off into a new, interesting direction.
    \n
    \nIdeally, I think systems that scale are the answer-- For instance, HeroQuest, where you could have a full extended conflict with twists and turns, or reduce it to a single roll if it's appropriate.

Encounters have a fairly clear unitary win/loss outcome. Individual actions are all directed at the goal of defeating the encounter and progressing to the next one. Every action expends resources, and may incrementally advance you toward resolving the encounter.

    In contrast, D&D flounders in situations where the intent is not clearly connectable to component tasks. If you're trying to subvert the king's advisors, it's not like they have "loyalty hit points" you can wear down through use of social maneuvers. You have to either just roll a simple skill check and lose all the fun, unexpected details and tangents of actually playing through the conflict, or you have to "roleplay it out" with a bunch of tasks that may or may not have any meaningful impact on the outcome.

    \n Anecdote time!

    Anecdote the First
    So in Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles, Mutants of Avalon supplement, I played a druid (he was a coypu, not that that's relevant). Druids pick up psionic powers with levels, and start off with Body Manipulation: Itch. Yes, that's right: you can use your mind to make people itchy. It gives them a -1 to strike, parry, or dodge if they fail their resist roll (which you only get if your ME is above 15, I think). So that's the 'task-scale resolution' part.

    In play, I used to do this trick: I'd walk up to some NPC in my druid robes. I'd wave my hands and tell them that they were GEASED to do my bidding, or else a dire CURSE would befall them. As I spoke, I'd use my druid powers to make them itchy, so they got a sensation that could be the magic spell falling on them. They might resist the roll and therefore not be itchy, but there was no mechanical support on whether or not the hapless NPC actually did my bidding. My brother, who was GMing, basically made a fiat decision. He let the trick work a handful of times, and then had an NPC call my bluff. At the time, I was perfectly happy with that state of affairs. It was totally GM recognition and acknowledgement of "Hey, you were clever there, but don't go crazy with it."

    Anecdote the Second
    Flash-forward to playing Nobilis a couple months ago. We decide to go stir up trouble at the Locust Court. The court officers enjoy torturing the poor souls brought before them for ridiculous, crazy-ass Nobilis crimes. We step in and directly interfere with their enjoyment of the process without actually touching the process itself. Then the court officers get all huffy and call us to the stand in an inquiry. So I'm up on the stand, giving the court lip but not actually doing anything that's really and truly illegal. This is all, it should be pointed out, a set-up to intimidate the Locust Court for the case coming up the next day: we're basically saying, "Rule in our favor or we'll come back and do this all the time."

    So I'm sitting there playing, and I'm totally, totally rudderless. I have nothing on my character sheet, I have no resources, I have absolutely no formal way of making it more likely that our gambit will work out. All I'm doing is sitting there wondering if Judson, who is Hollyhocking, is going to think my roleplay is sufficient, and I hated that feeling. Even after we got the ruling that we wanted in the next session, the experience of play right then was very uncomfortable for me. It was, in rough form, the same social set up as my fourteen-years-ago TMNT game: the HG recognizes cool contributions but doesn't let them be perfectly effective, because then there'd be no need for new cool contributions. But this time, I really didn't like it. It wasn't Judson; it was me. Teenager me was okay, perhaps even eager, to garner that sort of approval for creativity. Adult me is profoundly disinterested in having improv creativity judged; I'm way more interested in building something cool with other players, and here I felt completely naked and toolless; I wasn't collaborating with the other players, I was submitting content to the HG-judge.

    Now, as was pointed out before, neither of these examples is without conflict resolution -- it's just in the hands of the GM/HG to decide if the plan works out, and it's totally up to his discretion. In fact, the 'task resolution' in both examples (a psionics resist roll or spending miracle points) is on the weak end of non-existence. In terms of Fred's awesome table, I was in that middle column -- my intent was clear, and was merely advisory. Which drove me up the wall.

    So beyond conflict-versus-task, it's important to look at the social relationships that the rules create. I'd actually prefer opaque intent over clear-but-irrelevant intent, because in that opaque column, I'm interacting with the other players, which is what I want to do, rather than making a suggestion and waiting to hear whether my suggestion is any good, which sucks ass.

    \n I think that the dichotomy might be at once stronger and the boundaries blurrier than we think. The core difference between the two seems to be the difference between "discovery" and "invention", and I think we can agree that in general both have their place.

    Task resolution is well tied to (and by saying this I'm trying to answer a question previously asked of me, because despite some of the argument above I will stand by my idea that one form might be intrinsically better suited to some settings, games, or interfaces than the other) discovery. That is, when the philosophy behind the game design is such that a priveleged individual creates the true Story and, using the mechanism of the game to provide mediation of the truth about the Physics (and I'm capitalising these at once to be snooty but also to indicate that I want more out of them than their dictionary, GNS, or whatever meanings), we want a system that unveils the result. The task resolution at its polar extreme takes our decisions as inputs and tests them against the truth machine, may mix in some random for variety (or verisimilitude) and churns out a Fact. Discovery.

    Conflict resolution, however, is (in its polar extreme) about Invention. Rather than create truths, the system distributes narrative authority and generates direction for its application but does not state any Facts. The player instead states the Facts and uses the result of the mechanism (including, usually, all of the inputs) to create a story consistent with the results -- the intermediate steps do not need resolution because we are not uncovering the Facts, we are authorising people to create them withing constraints of the results. But she is empowered to elaborate a great deal -- there is less (or no) priveleged Story that has to be addressed and there is not really a secret being revealed. The Facts are created by the system rather than revealed by them.

    As with any pair of poles, the vast majority of geography is between them and in this case can be complicated as we have systems that encapsulate one within another or provide multiple mechanisms for use in different circumstances, but I think that where the interface of a game (or the setting proper, but obviously not the system as this generally *is* the system) meshes with the ideals of Discovery or Invention, we get a smoothly functioning game.

    Dogs, for example, is about Invention. The players create the details of the conflict and are not placing themselves in the hands of the Physics of the world (and I include here any attempts to create a Physics of communication that might act as a machine to churn out results in that vein) at all.

    Dungeons and Dragons is about Discovery. We are revealing the world of an authoritative GM as mediated through the Physics of the world, which are a simple and predictable machine whether for falling, striking, jesting, or knowing.

    Thus, "it depends".

If I tell my players "there's really no way to tell where the thing you're looking for is just by randomly looking around" (most identified-as-task-resolution systems I've seen have sections on 'impossible tasks'), they know to go another route - trickery, magic, bribery, etc. You've created an impossible task in this example, but assumed that nobody would tell a player that it's impossible. That is contrary to most identified-as-task-res systems I've played with.

      It sounds like the problem is the pixel hunt, or the equivalent thereof, not task resolution per se?

      But I maybe still don't get it.

      \n I go with 2-1-3.

      If the info is so important that the scenario fails if the players don't find it, then even if you use CR, you've either constucted a bad scenario or agreed to bad stakes when you posed the question "Do the PCs get the dirt?"

      If the scenario doesn't require dirt-getting, then the question is resolved via so-called TR in two steps: (1) Do the players search the right place? (2) Do they make their safecracking skill?

      Assuming a prewritten scenario, that is, not GM manipulation behind the scenes.

      Pixel-bitching is a side issue, depending on player interest and other factors. In some games, keeping track of game time used for TR makes pixel-bitching moot. (If it were a dungeon in the old days, repeated searches would have meant eventual wandering monster rolls and/or using up resources.)
