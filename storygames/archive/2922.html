    \n Yeah, sorry, that's totally what I meant.

    It seems to me that the cool thing about the moment where the hero empties his clip into the monster to no avail is that feeling of "Oh, shit, the usual stuff doesn't work anymore!" You see it all the time in action movies: the hero's shooting bad guys left and right and the all of a sudden--click!--he's out of ammo! Now he's gotta start punching dudes left and right.

    What makes those moments effective is that they don't happen at a machine-gun pace. They happen once in a while, at dramatically appropriate intervals. The hero's got a gun and you see him kicking ass with it for a while. The gun's effectiveness is established. And then suddenly, that resource is taken away from him. Now he's got to rely on something else, and the tension is kicked up a notch.

    Now that's a fine thing to expect your game to deliver.

    Here's an idea I've had for an alternate system for CoC. Instead of relying on Fortune (dice rolls) to resolve actions, use a Karma-based sanity system. Like, each player would have a pool of Sanity points (bag of marbles?) and if he* wants to do one of those cool little moments that CoC is known for, he just pays a price in X marbles. So, if there's a clue in the Necronomicon that the players have to find before the plot can move forward, they just find it... as long as somebody is willing to pay a point of Sanity first. If you want to unload your Glock into a beastie, it happens, no roll needed... but it'll cost you some Sanity if it doesn't take the thing down.

    The point is, it replaces all of that rolling -- which is often pointless anyway, since the GM already has an outcome in mind -- with a basic currency of those moments that really make the game.

    Perhaps one could combine this with the bid mechanic from Greg Stoltze's ...In Spaace! to good effect.


    *We're talking Lovecraft, and I shall be chauvinist.

    It's not a whole system yet, just a core mechanic. So, who's the "mystery author"? I was kinda assuming a pretty traditional GM here (backwards compatible with existing CoC scenarios!) but there's certainly the possibly of having new-fangled player-authored mysteries and such.

    As for your second question, that's why I brought up the bid mechanic. It might be pretty dull if the GM has assigned a soak value of 30 to "Shambling Walker of Splugfrlorgoflop", checks it against a pre-determined killing power value of 8 for a full burst from a Glock, and says, "Oh, yeah, he sort of staggers a bit. Got anything bigger?" I suppose you could do all sorts of things. I guess there's sort of an assumption there that the thing that's really squicking you out is not that the thing looks gross, but the realization that you just dumped a massive amount of firepower into the thing and it barely scratches it. So... maybe as the power of your attempted attack increases, so too must an amount of Sanity you'll willing to wager. Say your pistol has a minimum bid of 1 and your shotgun has a minimum bid of 5 and that stick of dynamite has a minimum bid of 10. Because, if it survives each of these things, it has just demonstrated its true danger to you. Maybe you'd be better off running away before you learn more.

    And there's an assumption here that successfully killing a critter is somehow non-threatening to one's Sanity. If you blast that ghoul into a lump of cold flesh, well, at least you know you can kill the bastards now. But you could certainly also make a statement about how even resorting to violence erodes one's Sanity, and have a cost for even attempting.

    Oh, we can certainly trick out a bunch of nifty cases for a combat system, but I think we'd be better off talking about a unified mechanic that works equally well in combat as in researching Things Man Was Not Meant To Know as in exploring the lost city of Tchtchziknix'a. If you want to succeed, you have to wager Sanity. This also creates a player-controlled consequence for preserving Sanity. Instead of failing your SAN check and being stricken by (random roll) Tourette's Syndrome, you simply declare how you choose to protect your mind and lose the conflict.
