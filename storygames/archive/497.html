I began to wonder this myself. I can happily say I know a lot of gamers personally. All but two I know, are married. To women. (Just putting that out there for the record!) :D

(I feel like I should be wearing a tiara and a sash that says Gamer Wife whenever I walk into our local game store.) *end snarkiness*

When I ask why won't she I usually get the "It's not her thing" or "she doesn't think it's fun" or "she feels like she just won't get it."

*blink*

I'm beginning to wonder if my friends are going about it all wrong. I personally have managed to get two of their wives to play in games and each time, both men nearly fell over at the fact that their wives played and even enjoyed it. When I tell them "see, she'll play" they inform me it's because I was there. Could it be that maybe I explained the game differently? Or coudl it truly be that they saw another woman enjoying what the men in their lives enjoyed and realized they could too?

I know some people have said it's the artwork in some of the books that turns women off.     I always try to recruit female gamers using a simple four-step process.

    1. Get really excited about some story from a game . . . without telling them it's a role-playing game. Usually I'll make analogies to popular culture or classic books that I know they like, so they get a sense of what the story feels like.
    2. Drop the, "Yes, you can make up stories like that, too" bomb.
    3. Bring them into a rules-light, story-heavy one-shot.
    4. . . . PROFIT!

    The same process goes for non-gamer men, by the way, but I find that men are less likely to be initially puzzled by the idea that gaming goes beyond D&D. Introducing people to a rules-light first session is really important, though, We as experienced gamers - both men and women - sometimes forget just how much skill and hidden expertise go into being able to navigate a session. First-time players have enough to do just figuring out when they can speak up and what kinds of things they can say. They don't have to also be worrying about stats, dice, etc.

    (My boyfriend decided to introduce a group of newbies to gaming using Ars Magica. We had words afterwards. Those words included "what," "were," "you," and "thinking." And as for the gamers - only two of them came back.)

    I'm a woman, and of my female friends, I'd say about 40% are gamers and another 40% have played in one or more games at my invitation. I wear my sparkly "GAMER" bracelet everywhere and carry a d10 in my purse. I try not to talk about gaming in the Interminable Gaming Stories way, but rather in a "OMG AND I GET TO MAKE THIS STUFF UP AND ISN'T IT NEAT" way, and that's been really successful in getting most of my non-gamer friends (male and female) to at least understand why I care about this so much.

    I'm also an observant Jew, and I find it much easier to get my female friends to try gaming than to introduce it into the religious community. Judaism doesn't really have the gaming = Satan thing going on, but there's a sense of, "Why would you waste your time on that?" Being a gamer and being Jewish pretty much places you on the fringe of the community.

    As for what I think turns women off? A lot of little decisions - yes, the artwork, yes, the places where the books are sold, yes, etc. etc. - but I think the biggest factor is that there are very few popular ways to 'get your feet wet'. I can't really think of any systems that are specifically built to support new players (the gaming equivalent of Dick and Jane), and most GMs have no idea how much expertise they already have, turning new players off. Male players are more likely to stick through that initial period for social reasons, including that the culture welcomes them more and that they are more likely to have a tight peer group who are already expert players.

    Here's the other thing, and this is definitely me blowing smoke out my ass: I think women in our culture are taught not to play. Certainly most women have less time than men for play (for example, the average woman works one month a year more than the average man, if you include both housework and paid work), but I also have found it hard to get women to admit that they like to play. We teach women to be caretakers, to be 'selfless' and think of other people - and there are very few things less overtly productive and apparently selfish than play. It doesn't produce anything, and it doesn't take care of anyone else (unless, of course, you're playing with your kids, and that's an entirely different discussion). Every woman gamer I know has either had an eccentric upbringing or a seminal encounter with a strong-willed woman.

    This is why, as an academic studying play, part of what I try to do is to show why play matters. We won't get women, and observant Jews, and the elderly, and all the other minimally-represented demographics to take gaming seriously without changing both the culture of role-playing and the culture we live in.

    'Cause, you know, everyone needs a grandiose dream.

    3. For all the people that feel they are minorities, do you try to get any of your non gamer friends/partners/spouses to game? How did you do it? Did you ask? Did you whine? Did you pester?

    I got a lot of non-gamer friends to play, with very little effort at all. All of use happened to be into similar things- comic books, HK movies, anime, etc. so there was very little work involved to get folks interested in playing Feng Shui, since it purports to be a game about HK action.

    Since then, I've found little trouble in recruiting minority gamers mostly because my groups tend to be mostly or completely people of color.

    If anything I have found keeps people from interest, is if they face marginalization in the groups they play with. Roleplaying suffers more in this regard because often groups are based on weird power dynamics that many people can sense if not articulate that makes them feel uncomfortable. Even if that issue happens to be the normal sort of thing "the new guy" has to go through, it still tends to push buttons and triggers and be the sort of things people don't want to have to deal with when they're trying to relax and have fun.

    For this reason, I often find gamers of color tend to either stick together or not play at all.

    Telepresence: YES. Building on people's existing interests is very smart.

    Here's one other thing I've tried with excellent success for non-gamers: playing one-on-one games in non-traditional settings. For example, I'll take someone out to dinner and run a free-form two-hour one-shot in a setting they're already familiar with (a la telepresence's suggestions). I've made converts top gaming on airplanes, on long car rides, and while waiting in line at the DMV. The analogy to knitting is really good, I think: people are more willing to experiment if they see it as something they can pick up and put down at will.

    Also, calling a game session an "episode" is terminology that has really helped people understand that they can just come and play once, instead of committing to a long-term campaign. They'd watch just one episode of a TV show; they can try just one session of a game, too.

    kleenestar, you raise a great point. It can be hard for many non-gamers to wrap their brain around a typical roleplaying situation, but "what if" stuff at parties or over dinner is very relatable

    "Which celebrities would you sleep with/which celebrities would you die before sleeping with?"

    "What music/books/people would you take to a desert island."

    "Describe in lurid detail what you'd do to your horrible ex-lover/clueless boss/nasty in-laws, if you were guaranteed to get away with it."

    Nearly everyone plays such conversational games with their friends or siblings, or did so in high school or college, and the line between them and "role playing" is trivial.
