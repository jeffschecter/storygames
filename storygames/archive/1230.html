    Good idea, Mark.

    Mine's from Ashok Desai. It's all squishy with biology, which I'm going to take as coded metaphor. Who would have cells and use a biology metaphor? War-time academics. Who would use Ashok's old-timey font? People in the ninteenth century. Viola! Red String peace activists at Richmond College, 1862.

    FREE ENTRY FIELDS
    Name: The name of your character, I think. Maybe your code name?
    Flavor: Your character's demeanor - sweet, salty, sour, or bitter.

    Membrane: Your character's protective social layer.
    Vacuole: People who can and will aid your character, subsidiary to the membrane.

    TRAITS RANKED ONE TO SIX
    The four traits are rated between one and six. These are weird but workable. Higher is more resistant and generally better.

    Pliability refers to a character's susceptibility to pressure and overt intimidation.
    Viscosity refers to a character's susceptibility to charm and persuasion.
    Cunning refers to a character's susceptibility to trickery and deception.
    Sense refers to a character's susceptibility to debate and reason.

    FREE TEXT WITH RATINGS
    Tendrils: Potential cell recruits. Each has a method of approach (P, V, C, or S see above) and a value (1-6). There are six you select and write down from a pre-set list of 20.

    The plasm diagram: Your character's cell, with your character in the center seven position. One other player character fills one position, forming a chain around the table. The remaining five get filled in as you succeed in recruiting.

    18 plasm markers for cells you've successfully started. Write the value of your recruit in the symbol.

    You'll be selecting NPCs to try to recruit. Each recruiting attempt will be a scene. Failure means suspicion or detection, and you'll have to burn vacuoles or even membranes to avoid imprisonment. Success means filling out your plasm and building new red string cells.

    Something with trust and betrayal and burning off vacuoles and membranes, counter-spies, getting your people ratted out and hung as traitors. 20 codified NPCs to pick from with varying risk and reward.

    My sheet is from Jeff Moore.

    At first, the broken and solid lines are reminiscent of the I Ching, but a little bit of research shows only a loose connection to the accepted meaning of the various I Ching trigrams, so I'm not sure how much I can use them (if at all).

    The attributes given are cascading - I imagine only the full trigram ones on the right are generally used. Strength, Endurance, Grace and Accuracy on the Body side, versus Reason, Willpower, Perception and Charm on the Mind side. Interestingly enough, with the equations given on the sheet (which I must use), three pairs of equations will be equal: Endurance and Grace; Willpower and Perception; Accuracy and Charm. This makes me think those equations are just a starting point, and after the game begins, the values can change separately.

    Considering the space on the sheet devoted to the attributes vs. the space devoted to skills and abilities, obviously this is a nature over nurture kind of game. The attributes become even more obviously the focus when you look at the Key Aspect chart (where I'm now obligated to use the Taint Of The Wolf, the ten-sided die). Musing on that Key Aspect chart some more, I'm not sure what a 'turn' is in the game yet, but obviously it has them. Nor am I sure exactly what it means to have a Key Aspect on your turn (or not). Maybe you get a bonus of some sort if you manage to use the Key Aspect? Maybe it's a mandate?

    The use of a ten-sided die, plus the compounding nature of the attributes (where a Body of 1 implies a Strength of 4, 2 gives 8, and so on), suggest to me a percentile-type system. Interestingly, characters also have a "level" value. The "easy way" of course is to treat it like levels in D&D or D&F, especially since the Level sits right next to an Experience area.

    I'll have to keep musing.

    So I got Jason Sims' violent gem. Already I'm fascinated.

    Maybe I've just been playing too many of Mark's tyrant games, but I'm seeing the gun violence as a metaphor for an unfriendly examination of another person. The FBI pursuing a criminal, the mob hunting a stoolie, a stalker after a pretty co-ed. As play progresses, they get closer and closer to the bullseye: finding out about the character.

    My ideal for this design: you have a character in mind, but you write down nothing about him. Your character sheet describes the challenges other players face as they try to get to know him. My vision of endgame includes the phrase "I know everything about you..."

    So, I'm toying with some mechanics to tie that idea to this character sheet. Some thoughts:
    • I really like the idea of little yellow numbered evidence flags, a la CSI, etc. that would get scattered over character sheets.
    • I'm drawn to the idea that at some Colossal Arena style moment, the dice would get rolled and someone would get a "hit."
    • I'm trying to decide where on the continuum between one sheet per player and one sheet per table this game falls.
    • Everything I consider runs up against the problem of who the Angel is. It's possible that the Bullseye, the Shooter and the Angel are all roles a player takes on. But in those roles, do you use the stats on your sheet, or someone else's?
    • Maybe the Angel is ambiguous in play. Could be a guardian angel, an angel of mercy, or and angel of death or righteous anger.
    And I guess I recognize that I'm being a little perverse. I think I read the idea of a character who's self and world are taken apart over the course of the game. It'd be easy to say "all your rings get hit points, and every resolution you take a blow determined by the shooter's aim , and your avoid and ammo." But! There's no place on the sheet to talk about your possessions, or your friends, or your self. And where's the fun in getting your anonymous "loved ones" destroyed?

    Which is really where the unfriendly examination comes in.

    Mendel Schmiedekamp's Sheet for me to work with.

    The sheet suggests to me:
    1) A clock. 12 section. 12 scenes? Let's try that.
    2) 4 places for character entry, each with "Form" being a major attribute. 2 upside down, 2 right side up. 2 light, 2 dark.
    3) You play 4 different entities throughout the game. You start with 2, and then at some point, the sheet is inverted and you play the other 2.

    SO:
    Dark old gothic house. Ancient grandfather clock. The people here tonight have secrets of evil deeds past. And the clock judges them.
    We get 12 scenes as it ticks toward midnight, then midnight strikes and it's time for Judgement.

    In early scenes, we will have low-level supernatural strangeness, and will establish what the past deeds of the characters are. Then, the clock will give them a chance for redemption. As the scenes progress, we get more and more surreal, deeper and deeper into the nightmare world of their past deeds. Everything transforms, they will be confronted with the evil they wrought, and will be tested to see what fate they deserve.

    THE SHEET
    Right-side up light is your main character, the human person who has commited evil in the past, and is striving for redemption.
    Right-side up dark is the minor shadow of SOME OTHER PLAYER'S main character. You will torment him with this shadow.
    Upside-down light is your nightmare world character. Transformed into a icon of your evil, and stripped of your humanity.
    Upside-down dark is the terror monster of SOME OTHER PLAYER'S main character. You will terrorize him with this creature.

    The players use their POWER throughout to control the facts of the world and the outcomes, but at the expense of their HUMANITY, which is ultimately their only defense against the horrors of the shadow world.

    That's as much as I got right now.

I'll offer a possible ingredient direction: In the board game Kremlin, there are a set array of "NPC" apparatchiks who can do things according to what office they hold, and can get promoted, demoted, and purged. The players have secretly allocated influence over many of these apparatchiks, and will reveal some of their influence in order to make them do things.     Nowhere's Edge

    There is a small realm where the soon to be dead wait to cross over. A space on the outskirts of reality where the subconcious bleeds out and shapes reality itself. Those at the border of Oblivion are given a chance to reflect on their lives. The lucky ones, those who find a deeper purpose in their existence, are given a chance to find a way back to the living and complete their mission.

    The characters are all hanging out in the Antechamber of the Great Beyond, they have all been involved in an accident of some sort and are chilling together. Each character periodically gets sucked into warped versions of significant events from of his/her past and pulls the other characters along for the ride. The point is for a character to discover his/her reason to live (purpose, drive, what have you) and to then use that as a handhold to claw back to the land of the living.

    The various keywords are used during the flashbacks. I'm not sure how yet as they are totally obtuse.

    The boxes to the left side are your "health" use them all and you're on the short bus to oblivion.

    The 3d boxes are used to store "fetters" (to steal from Wraith: The Oblivion) or things that tie you to the world of the living. You fill these through the flashbacks.

    "S", "M" and "H": No freak'n clue.

    The cut off bits at the bottom: the box is to store your purpose. You actually write another person's purpose and hand it to them, then you need to orchestrate their stories so that they can discover it.

    The boxes are used for attempts to revive oneself. This is the only mechanic I've put any thought into. The player writes a number from 1-(max size of whatever dice is being used) in the box and keeps it face down. Then when the player has a shot at waking up they can pick a box (still face down) and try and roll under or equal to the number they have written. However if someone else guesses the number they have written then it's all over. There is some sort of trust/bonus to showing people one of your numbers, thereby allowing them greater odds at screwing you, if they want.

    Upon further reflection this morning, these other points caught my attention.

    ~The only labeled slots are "Name", "Role" and "Wounds."
    ~The "Name" slot is really long...like longer than it probably needs to be to accomodate the average player or character name. Gotta figure out what goes in the rest of that space.
    ~There's that "Wounds" slot that seems made for tracking hit points or the like. That means combat, maybe? Could they be wounds YOU inflict rather than wounds you receive? Couldn't they just be metaphorical wounds?
    ~That unlabeled box to the right of "Role" should probably be labeled with a symbol or a number.
