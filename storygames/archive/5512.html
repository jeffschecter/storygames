\n If you intend to publish a roleplaying game for sale, why are you going to do this? If there are several reasons, if possible, rank them. Try to be gut-deep honest. If any part of it is for status or other possibly-controversial reasons, I'd love to know that.

    \n Over the years I have designed four alpha stage games and have ideas for a few more. Currently I have serious designs on publishing two of them. Here's the thing, I don't consider myself to be a game designer. I don't WANT to be a game designer. All these designs have been written against my will...

    ...Because every once in a while an idea for a game I want to PLAY creeps up into my brain and simply nothing on the market or in my collection will suffice. Sure, I could play the idea with The Pool or PtA but none of those have the foundational structures or mechanics for creating the particular social and creative dynamics that I'm imagining in my head. So I'm forced to design it myself.

    And if I'm going to be compelled by the muses who have enslaved me to their bidding to design then I might as well share them and make some pocket money on the side.

    Jesse

    \n Jonathan: I think that'd be interesting but perhaps as a parallel thread? I'm not just saying that as a passive-aggressive, "no thanks," because I think it would genuinely be interesting.

    My answers, in order of strongest to weakest:

    I've got an idea: I have had fun ideas for games, and I want other people to try them. I do not believe many people will try them if they're not investing (literally) in a book that they can carry around with them. I want many people to try them.

    The game doesn't exist yet: My motivation for the game I'm working on right now has to do with the fact that there's no game out there that's designed precisely to do what I want my game to do. Since becoming friends with people who design games, I've come to feel that the proper thing to do when confronted with that situation is to make a game. See #1 for why, for this need, I'm publishing for $ rather than for free.

    Socialization: It's not that I want to be in the designer's crowd. It's that by designing a game, I have become much more social. Not that I was a shy and retiring flower before, but now I am required to ask people to help me, to give me input, to care about their enjoyment at a level I never have before. Designing for profit magnifies this, pushes me to be more mutual with other designers and players, to talk to more artists and layout people and editors, to help out more and to ask for help more.

    Self-fund my hobby: It's expensive to go to cons. I'd like to not have to shell out so much money to go.

    To get money: If there's a sliver of profit left after the prior requirement is met, it would be nice to have some more money to pay bills and get my kid birthday and Christmas presents and go out with my friends.

    (PS: As far as I'm concerned, for the purposes of this thread, questioning people's answers is fine. Ideally I'd like to see people reserve their questions to genuine questions, not rhetorical inquiries designed to elicit a specific response or prove a point of view one might hold.)

    \n So that people will actually play my games.     \n Honestly, prestige does not enter into it for me. I don't even put my name on the cover. That said, here are my reasons:

    1. I really love the act of creating. That is more than just writing. Laying out the book, placing art, deciding on the art, figuring out ways to market, ways to distribute, how to use what exists and how to build that which does not.

    Edit: 1.1: I truly love learning. This is a great way to learn a lot of disparate skills.

    2. I really like running my own business.

    3. I like working with others. I love working with the artists to help realize my ideas. I like working with editors especially when they offer up great ideas of their own or make my ideas better. I like working with retailers to come up with new ways to get my books selling in their store. I like working with fans to help expand what I wrote initially.

    4. I like to share my ideas. Some folks think playing squirrels is weird, some think a Roman Empire that never fell due to magic kitchy but others think it is neat. I listen to both sides but I would be lying if I did not say I prefer the folks who think my ideas are neat.

    5. Financial. Yes, you can make money doing this and I like that aspect.

    6. I like games. I like making games. Publishing for profit allows me to do that and continue to do that. I can;t do it alone so it is nice to reward people for helping me.

    Those are the ones on the top of my head but I am sure there are more.

    Bill

    \n So many good answers here. I'll try to add my own, though they're going to overlap with a lot of the others. These are sort of in order, but not really.

    1. I have to create. When I think about gaming, I think about rules, and I create rules to do things I think would be interesting. When I think about books, I have to create pictures, borders, font/layout combinations, charts. When I think about stories, I have to creates rules that make that story happen, pictures that illustrate that story, and words to tell that story.

    2. I want a game that does certain things I want to play, all of those things, together. I want a game that focuses on the things I want to focus on. I want a game that plays like a traditional game, but puts story power in the hands of the players. I don't want to keep playing games that are ALMOST perfect for me. I want to play a game that IS perfect for me.

    3. I want to ACCOMPLISH SOMETHING. I have to confess to a bit of a self-esteem issue. I've never really set out on an ambitious project and worked all the way through it to completion. It's not that it's too hard and I quit, it's that I lose interest, or I start to think no one else is interested, so why bother? I want to change that, not just with a "look at this, isn't it neat?" online publish. I want something I can hold in my hands, show off to other people, and know that I made that thing.

    4. I believe there are people out there who want the same thing in number 2 that I want. I want to give those people what they want. I want to know that other people are enjoying something I produced. This is connected to 3 as well, in that it will make the accomplishment more complete.

    5. I'd like to earn something as a creator. I want to take my creative obsession and make it work for me, make it my profession. It doesn't have to be my tabletop RPG design that does it for me, either. That's why I'm learning to code, as well.

    6. It's... fun. It's frustrating. It's educational.

    \n After reading Fred's post over on LJ about publishing, I found myself tripped up a little. I've got a number of reasons for wanting to publishing Criminal Element, most of which are very personal rather than a "sound business idea". I want to publish Criminal Element because...

    ...there isn't another game like it out there right now.

    ...I love the way a book feels in my hands.

    ...no one else is going to publish this.

    ...I won't let anyone else have my baby.

    ...I'm a "visually-minded" person and when I write I also think of layout, art direction, and ultimately what my creation would look like as a book.

    ...I want to create something that is an artifact containing my ideas, my writing, my artistic skills, all together.

    ...I want to share this book about something I love with other people that love the same thing.

    And since we're on the subject of personal... I want to publish because someone I love more than I could ever say would have loved to see my book published. Now I see my book as another way to tell this woman how much I love her.

    But, in the end I want to publish CE because I need to. Because not publishing it and taking a risk on something I believe in would be a betrayal to myself. Because I'm proud of this work that I've created and I want to see it all the way through.
