\n Bear in mind that indie games have only been on my radar for less than two years. But in that time I have seen what appears to be a real inflating of game prices. Last year I used to see a lot of content at $12-17, with the occasional $20-22 product. My first indie purchase was Burning Wheel Revised, which was (and still is) two fat-assed books for $25. That was a lot of value for the money to me.

This year I've been seeing a lot of games in the $20-30 range, and some very small (12-32 page) games for $8-15.

I'm not posting this to be an ass. I am just curious whether: A) Printing costs have increased considerably, The indie gaming market will suddenly pay 20-50% more, C) There's more content either print/artwork-wise that I'm missing, or D) I'm an idiot and don't know what I'm talking about.

Some of you seem to have done a bit of purchasing at Gen Con. So tell me...am I wrong here?

    \n As others have described, I think you're seeing a result of more games being sold through fulfillment houses like IPR, and even occasionally moving into distro.

    There was a time when the only one stop shops for a lot of indie RPGs were the Burning Wheel crew flogging our colleagues' games at all the cons we attended, and also the Forge booth at Gen Con. Other than that, you pretty much had to order directly from each individual producer.

    There is a great deal of convenience to the consumer in being able to go to a place like IPR or Key20 and pick up multiple games they want. But the producers must pay a percentage of their revenue to the fulfillment houses in return (and pick up shipping costs, in the case of IPR). If the book goes into distro you could be selling twice as many books while seeing greatly reduced overall profit.

    We can get away with selling games at cut-rate prices when we do everything direct and we still see the majority of the revenue as profit. But as the games become more popular and we shift to organizations like IPR to handle our fulfillment, it starts to become unrealistic.

    \n Indie games are hitting a huge cost/value discrepancy. And in my estimation quite a few authors seem to be trying to fix this discrepancy (using various meanings of the word fix) by increasing the "production value" of the books rather than increasing the quality of the product in ways that might actually enhance gameplay. This leads to an observation/principle: An increase in the production value of a book, on average, decreases the price to gameplay ratio of the book, detrimentally to the consumer.. This to me is a BAD reason for price increases, and is leading to me buying less games (the all-important brain surgery victim market). But it is a factor in the trend, and sweeping it under the rug will probably only encourage the trend to continue (it is also one that our consumers, for some reason, are encouraging; I think the "ooo pretty" factor leads people to say "yes, more art, please" regardless of the detrimental effect on the play/cost ratio).

    That said, there are some good reasons being mentioned here as well. I doubt the cost of printing has gone up $5+ a book, but the increases in the costs of printing do need to be addressed and considered. There's also general cost of living inflation, and the (perceived, at least) belief that many designers were underpricing in the first place.

    The use of fulfillment houses such as IPR seems to be an empty argument - as a justification for an increase in prices, it falls completely flat. I didn't increase my game's price because of IPR, and won't - what they are doing is getting Fastlane to more people than I ever could at home by myself. So what they charge, I make up by an increase in sales. That balances out, and usually comes out ahead in my experience (the extra sales, I mean). People using this as a justification seem to fall into 3 categories: (1) People who haven't really thought it through, (2) people who seriously think that they could distribute their games just as well as IPR/etc could, or possibly (3) people who are thinking with their wallets instead of their heads or hearts.

    And for those people in the #2 category, if you really think you could get the exact same sales numbers without IPR or whoever, why are you choosing instead to jack up your price and use them? That doesn't seem very nice.

    Part of this, too, is not rational for me. I don't mind the prices going from a standard $12-17 range to a $15-20 range. But those books over $20? I really start to wonder about something if it needs more than one standard ATM-bill to pay for it ($20s are the standard for ATM withdrawals, so they're how I view purchases, even though most of the time I use a card). It doesn't help for me, on a personal level, that I feel my biggest regret of the 2006 was also the one over-$20 book I purchased (or at least the only book whose over-$20-ness I noticed even at the time, and am still kind of bitter about).

    But there is an inflation strong enough that my purchases per year are going down, despite the # of new products per year going UP. And a lot of this seems to go back to my first point: people are bloating their prices through production values that do not have any impact on my gameplay (hardcover, art, color interiors...). I am not a part of the 'ooo shiny' crowd.

    I think the cost of printing books has increased $1-2/books, at least (for short-run printing).

If you're willing to cough up the money, you can get 100-200 copies of B+W book printed at $3-4 (maybe cheaper). But that same book will cost $6-8 at Lulu. The point is that printing though Lulu probably *is* $5-6 more expensive than the average book 5 years ago.

    Also, the price scheme for selling books is a percentage. IPR takes a percentage, and retailers take a percentage. Retailers are more significant. They take 40-50% of the cover. That means that any increase in printing costs will actually get doubled by the time the consumer sees the book (given that the publisher wants to keep the same net profit). Example---the price of printing a book is raised from $4 to $5. The old retailer price was $8, now its $10. So $1-3 increase in production prices makes the new price $2-6 higher.

    And retailers are buying our books more and more. Isn't that one of IPR goals? So publishers *do* have to start worrying about fitting into the traditional price scheme. In the past, retailers weren't an issue, so people could price their books how ever they wanted.

    3) Fulfillment Houses
    I, personally, don't buy that the argument that increase the number of sales enough to off-set lowering your net profit. There's no guarrantee of that. And even if they did, why should I care? The promise of increased sales is really the promise of increased profit. If I'm not going to make more money, why should I worry how many copies of are sold? I could post it for free and get thousands of downloads.

    My point is that it's cool you don't mind lowering your profit in exchange for increased sales, I respect that. But I don't feel that way. I want to maintain a certain net profit per book. That means I have to find ways to off-set the price of the fulfillment house, the easiest way being to raise the cost of my book.

    And it's unfair to say people are necessarily "thinking with their wallets". I fully understand that there are people out there who have reasons OTHER THAN the game to purchase a gamebook (note the comments consumers have made about wanting a nice looking product, a gamebook to put on the shelf, being willing to put out extra money for extra art, etc. - the "ooh, pretty" factor, as I'm calling it). I do not begrudge anyone making the choice to appeal to consumers for whom these are positive factors.

    However, despite these other reasons one might have for getting a book (reasons that turn these factors into positive factors), they do not usually change the price/gameplay ratio in a way that improves the 'gameplay' side of the equation, and almost never change it in a way that increases the gameplay anywhere near the amount that price goes up. In other words, the prices are going up for reasons other than the gameplay.

    Valid reasons, assuredly; certainly not reasons that amount to "gouging" or any other nefarious behavior. Reasons, in fact, encouraged in this community by many more voices than my one discouraging voice. But these are explicitly reasons other than gameplay and should be acknowledged as such.

    And as the ratio slides more away from the gameplay side, me personally (and perhaps only me) am less likely to purchase the resulting product. For me, what I'm buying is the system and other material that I would use regularly at the table to produce my awesome gameplay (and/or cannibalize for my own designs, which ideally also provide awesome gameplay). A hard cover, a lot of art, glossy color interiors - these are things that provide other services to the consumer besides awesome gameplay, several of these which have been noted above.

    If we want to take this any further, it's likely best to make a new thread that links to this one. I'm hoping my points are finally clear, and that people stop reacting defensively. There is no accusation of "gouging" or otherwise unethical behavior in my statements. There is just an acknowledgement of what I feel is a fact: that there are choices in game book production other than ones involving providing better gameplay, and these choices often impact the price.

    And currently, to bring the topic back around, the trend I'm observing is that the price increases in indie products are in part because they are making more choices (and feel they have more room to make choices) that involve production in ways other than providing better gameplay (choices that more "industry standard" products make all the time).

    \n I don't want to derail this thread but.... what the heck, let's derail it. Because I think that the direction it's going will never arrive anywhere. We, all of us, have brought a lot of games that we will never play, or that we played once. When one game like this cost to you a little amount, you can forget about it or even be happy anyway to have the object "game" to collect. But when that game cost a lot of money, you begin to be much more careful with your purchases.
    \n
    \nMany had said that this would be a good thing. I agree. But ONLY in presence or a hard, rigorous and honest critical environment that would inform the purchasers about the games he could buy. If not, it will not be quality that will win, but name-recognition.
    \n
    \nBecause if I go to gen-con with 300 dollars in my wallet to buy games, and I find $600 of new games at the forge booth, it doesn't mean that you have a 50% chance to sell me something. FIRST, I will have to buy the new Vincent Baker game. The new Ron Edwards game. The new Matt Snyder game, the new Paul Czege game, the new Mike Holmes game, and the new games of the other half-dozen game designers that I buy everything of. Because I already trust them. THEN, If I have still 20 dollars left in my wallet, I will look at the other 320 dollars of games on the shelves, and I will choose one.
    \n
    \nOr not. At that time, with so much new games to choose from, and a backsack already full and heavy with new games to try, I think I will simply leave with that $20. If I don't recognize a game that I read about and I decided that I wanted absolutely.
