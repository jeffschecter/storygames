    \n Rafu, I read IAWA and I loved it and it somehow looked perfect for GMless playing, starting from world creation and the big number of NPCs, to the spotlight mechanism of scene rotation. When we tried it, we realized immediately that there was too much stuff that was not regulated, especially if we wanted to play it with some gumption.

    JDFristrom rightly points out a good number of the issues that didn't work.. who frames scenes for whom? can NPCs be introduced at any time? the lack of dice refresh rules for IAWA means that after a few scenes everyone is down to small dice.. but you can just introduce a new NPC with full dice.

    What about narrative authority? We thought about Polaris-like mechanics but it's not clear how to mix that with IAWA rules.

    So.. to answer to Rafu: IAWA hints to the wonders of GMless play with its co-creation mechanisms.. but when you try to do it, it falls flat. I would love to keep that co-creation spirit going during the game, while also allowing for full-blown IAWA aggressiveness.

    \n We almost always play one-shots, so reduced dice isn't much of an issue. You lose a few dice or you just give in and go looking for a different conflict to try and win, and after a few hours you might have only one stat you can win with.

    For scene framing, somebody suggests a scene they'd like to play and I think one of three things happens: everybody says okay do it, or we make a few suggestions and negotiate, or somebody has an idea for a scene that should happen before that one. If somebody wasn't in a particular scene, it's sort-of "their turn" to have a scene. Mostly whoever is in the scene gets final word on what it is. NPCs get introduced as we need them, which is occasionally, but certainly not for every scene.

    Essentially, our characters are antagonistic to each other and probably have shifting alliances throughout the game, but as players we work together to make something that entertains everybody. Occasionally somebody will feel ganged-up on, like if we have 3 players, and there's a lot of 2-on-1 or one player fails a bunch of conflicts and feels like their ideas get shut down, so we do have to watch out for that.

    \n I have played IAWA GMless, and in fact the times I've played it with a GM they have basically just ended up playing one NPC more or less as though they were a PC, because there was really no need for external conflict.

    I am surprised to hear your comments about PCs running out of dice, ivan. In my experience, most conflicts end without somebody choosing to damage the other person's stats -- the negotiation rules are used frequently, in an attempt to extract fictional concessions, with stat damage being a secondary option. It still happens, of course, and over time PCs will definitely get worn down, but at least in my play (note: my play and Johnstone's play overlaps a fair bit, since we game together) this has never felt like an issue. It usually takes more than 'a couple' of conflicts before PCs start to feel the hurt, in any case.

    Similarly, there are usually very few NPCs introduced after the initial situation among the PCs has been established -- generally, the web of Best Interests creates such a tightly-woven situation that only one or two additional NPCs seem necessary, and I can't really remember ever introducing a brand new, statted-up NPC very late in the session, because usually the focus becomes narrower as play proceeds and the central intra-PC conflict comes to a head. It would have never occured to me to bring in an NPC specifically to hose over other PCs, for example, or to indirectly take advantage of their lowered stats.

    In terms of controlling NPCs, I seem to remember that usually one player has primary control, at least at first, though this may then switch around if the NPC seems to be coming into conflict with or interacting with that player's PC -- nobody likes to roleplay both sides of a conversation. Generally the NPCs' motives (or more accurately, their role within the story/situation) are pretty transparent, and might be discussed a little bit at the table when the NPC is first introduced -- I think if there was a situation where the NPC's actions are both a) uncertain and b) decisive to a conflict between PCs, I would probably start a conflict specifically regarding the question of how the NPC acts (as each PC tries to get her on their side, for example.)

    \n Yeah, that sounds about right. A couple things you could try:

    1. Take turns framing scenes. And remember, the start of a scene is no big deal, really. You can still have characters move around and stuff.

    2. If you're used to being the GM and asking "what do you do now?" you need to ditch that. Frame a scene as a GM, then start paying your character in it. Have your character just come in and get what he wants unless the other PCs stop him. And make sure what he wants is what they don't want.

    3. Going for the throat is perfectly fine when you're in that conflict, because you're rolling dice and making demands. Outside of a conflict, you can take your time, throw in suggestions. One thing to note is we tend to play with people who suggest ideas at any and all points during the game, even when they're not involved as either a player or a character. Players in most rpgs tend to have a sense of ownership over their character, and you can extend that to the character's sphere of influence, so you're portraying more than just your character as a physical body walking around. You are portraying the full character, with background, culture, ideas, and themes as well.

    Posted By: ivanIs it correct to say that you guys played IAWA fully freeform, aiming to converge on conflicts where two mutually exclusive, undecidable events could happen.. and only then you went for the dice?
    \nThis just seems like a very strange definition of 'fully freeform' to me, though you certainly are accurately describing our play. What I don't understand is the sense I get that you think we are discarding some enormous edifice of rules; we are after all still using the core conflict-resolution mechanic, as well as the core situation-generation mechanics (oracles, best interests.) That's like 95% of the game, from my point of view.

    The only things we're changing in our play is the rules surrounding who frames scenes, and who controls NPCs. The way you describe us using the conflict rules, for example, is how the conflict rules work in the version of the game with a GM. You go to a conflict when one person says 'my character does THIS' and somebody else says 'no, you don't.' There's no special GM version of this, that I am aware. That's always (and only) how conflicts start: with character action opposed by other character action.

    From your description I get this feeling you think we are having to constantly negotiate a large number of rules; from my point of view, we are using the vast majority of the game's rules as-written, and changing a very small number of rules which, importantly, only really affect before- and between-scene play. In A Wicked Age is such a character-driven game that, in my experience, things like scene framing and NPC ownership are relatively minor in terms of overall play.\n
    Posted By: ivanGuys, what would you do if you cannot easily reach a negotiation on the meta-level, between the players? would you make up a rule on the spot?
    \nJohnstone has kind of already described what rules we have in play. For example, the rule for scene framing is: once a scene is done, the next scene is framed by a) someone other than whoever just framed a scene, b) whoever is the most excited about their idea, and c) if several people are excited about ideas then preference goes to whoever has framed a scene less recently, or both ideas are put forward and the group reaches a consensus.

    Given the catch-all nature of the last rule, there's really no case that falls under your example. I am guessing that if we had two players who were absolutely adamant that their scene had to come next, and no consensus could be reached among the other players, and the scenes they were proposing were mutually exclusive in the fiction -- then we would probably just have them roll a die to see who gets to go next. So yes, we'd make up a rule, but the rule would be completely arbitrary, because we'd only ever get to that point after we'd somehow exhausted all social negotiation.
