\n Hey,

I have mentioned this game before on story games and got some great feedback on how to proceed. Now I drafted the rules of a 2 page "white sheet edition" and would love to hear what you think about the game.

The original idea for the game came to me when me and my high-school friends were reminiscing about our old Warhammer and Earthdawn sessions. And how everybody remembered slightly different details about the adventures. We where talking in a pub over some beers. So an idea for a drinking game was born.

I am aiming for this version to be laid out on a single sheet (2 pages) for a small game you can easily print and use in a pub. Front will have majority of the rules, the back will have a "magic circle" inspired character sheet (where you write down your aspects and put down your beer) and the Moves explained.

As the game is now, it has a lot of unexplained quirks that come out during play (kinda took this feature from board games - you start seeing strategies and mechanics during play, instead of having them explained in the rules). The biggest one is the beer mechanic and how the gameplay will sometimes force you to use a Move in a particular way. If you have any questions about those quirks I will be happy to explain them. Once I get the white sheet edition done I will work on a pdf with examples of play, rules explanation and extra add-ons (I am toying with the idea of using an optional writer's die and a alcohol free version based on pretzels...I mean tokens).

Mostly, I want to know if the game is understandable enough in the form as it is. I am limited for space and I want to both explain the rules and show the game's mood - is what I have good enough? How would you improve it?

Read on the rules below and let me know.

Thanks in advance.

    \n Tales & Tankards

    You're all sitting in a tavern. Surprisingly no Mysterious Stranger\u2122, troubled noble or wounded soldier shows up to ask for your help. There is nothing else to do, but to order some quality ale from this fine establishment and reminisce about your past adventures.

    What is this?
    Tales and Tankards is a story driven drinking game. Yes \u2013 a drinking game that is also a kinda-sorta RPG. No dice or GM though, just you, your friends, storytelling and beer!
    You and your fellow players take the roles of a veteran adventurers enjoying a bit of downtime at a local tavern. You will drink beer and tell tales of your party's glorious past exploits...or at least the version of the story YOU remember. After all, you want to make yourself sound like the greatest hero this land has ever seen, so don't be afraid to color the the story in your favor... gods know other players will do the same.

    What will you need?
    Few friends
    A character sheet for each friend
    A tankard (or glass) of beer for each friend
    ...and a pitcher of beer for the table
    pen or pencil will come in handy
    pretzels are optional (sad pretzel picture)

    Before you start.
    Each player should get themselves a beer in a see-trough glass. Someone should also get a pitcher of beer for the table. Players sit down around a table, with their beers and character sheets in front of them. Everyone should be within an arms reach of the pitcher.
    Discuss the setting. Don't be afraid of using a setting you are all familiar with. If you can't agree on one, just use a staple fantasy setting. You are all heroic adventurers in this world. You are now ready to...

    Create the characters.
    Describe your character in few sentences to the other players. Feel free to use a character you have played in the past. Bear in mind that you are now an experienced adventurer \u2013 so don't be afraid to boast about your strengths. Other players are free to add to your description what they \u201cknow\u201d about you. While you can see yourself as a great warrior of immerse strength, others might see you as a drunk who drags around his oversized sword everywhere.
    Now turn this sheet around and write your name above the fancy looking magic circle. Next, inscribe one important aspect of your character in one of the circles of your newly discovered character sheet. Give your sheet to any player of your choosing. They will add a second aspect to another circle and pass it to a player of their choosing to do the same. Once each player has 3 aspects it's time to start.

    Starting the tale.
    One player (the person who bought the drinks perhaps?) starts by introducing the topic of the tale. They start the narration with \u201cRemember when...?\u201d and follow it up with a feat the party has achieved in the past. Other players should respond with \u201cAye!\u201d if they want to explore this story, or stay silent if they want someone else to suggest one. If majority of players stayed silent, the person to the left of the player who just spoke introduces their tale.
    Once you all agreed on the tale, the person who suggested it becomes the current narrator, takes a big quaff of beer and starts telling the story \u2013 hopefully from the beginning.

    Telling a Story
    Narrator continues to tell the tale of your party's adventure, setting scenes and describing events as he remembers. As soon as anyone disagrees with his version of the tale, they can Add to the story or Challenge the narrator (moves described on the character sheet). Narrator can Pass the tale to any player, if he can't recall what happened next.

    Heroic Beer Drinking
    Heroes NEVER pour themselves more beer from the pitcher!

    When a move tells you to drink, you are expected to at least take a proper quaff, worthy of a hero! We want to hear you gulp!

    Outside of moves, you can drink as much or as little beer at any time as you want \u2013 no gulps necessary.

    If you ever end up with less than 2 fingers worth of beer in your glass, something bad or simply embarrassing happened to your character during the tale. Not only other players tell you what happened (and ridicule you in the process), but you can't become the narrator until you get more beer (if you are a narrator at the time, the narration rights pass to the person on your left).

    When you drink without using a move, you can change on which aspect your beer sits. If you do, narrate how you used that aspect to add color to the tale. When a beer sits on an aspect, this aspect can't be used in any moves.

    Challenge MOVE
    The narrator has it all wrong and you can't stand it! Use one of yours and one of the narrator's available aspects to explain why the narrator is wrong. You and the narrator start drinking. Each can stop at any time by putting their beer on the aspect used in the challenge. Whoever has less in his mug becomes the current narrator. If any player has less than 2 fingers of beer they automatically loose the challenge.

    Add MOVE
    The narrator forgot some details, but you are here to remind him! Use one of yours or one of the narrator's available aspects to add detail to the story. The narrator will pour you some beer from the pitcher as a thank you for reminding him and use the new detail in his story. If he thinks you are wrong, he needs to drink until he has less beer than you do to prove his point.

    Pass MOVE
    Only the narrator can pass. The narrator has forgotten what happens next. He passes the story rights to a player of his choosing. This player will pour some beer into narrator's mug as a thank you and will continue the story where the last narrator left off.

    \n I really like drinking to move your glass around and change aspects.

    It seems like some of the rules are unnecessarily complex. Like a stumble due to having 2 fingers or less in your glass. And then the chug-chicken of taking over as narrator if you have more than 2 fingers in your glass, but less than your opponent. I'm not against that idea, but why not have stumbles simply come from emptying your glass? If you keep the 2 fingers rule for Challenges, that actually creates an interesting situation because you can outright choose to finish your beer (faster) to win the challenge - BUT, in addition to taking over narration because the previous narrator was wrong, you have to narrate your own stumble.

    Instead of two fingers, consider placing something in your glasses. If it breaks the surface, that creates whatever situation you want (stumble, challenge loss, etc.). If there are multiple objects of different sizes, you could add another simple layer of play that includes putting those in each others' glasses. There's a kind of playful, unsanitary intimacy in that. Plus, if each person is responsible for one item, it's fun to see what they bring.

    Due to the basic nature of drinking games, I think I'd have to play it to really get a feel. Seems appropriately simple for when you're pretty drunk or easily hackable for when you're not.
    It's an immediate candidate for adding Elbows, which means that during the game, you cannot point with fingers. You must use your elbows to point. If you point with fingers, you drink. Wire in some kind of narrative hiccup that occurs when you point with fingers and you're well on your way to some comical spills.

    Not rule-related...My first reaction, really, was that the bars I know that are welcoming and atmospheric enough to do this don't serve pitchers. And, for that matter, it's a waste to pound good beer served by those places. That's not a game criticism exactly, but, honestly, the only time events might align to make this a public game for me is Oktoberfest. Obviously, you could play this at a local dive, but would you actually want to do that? Mostly off-topic, but it just makes me wonder how narrow my own experience must be not to have a cheap-but-not-utterly-commercial bar for this kind of thing.

    \n I like the theme, but I wonder if it wouldn't be more powerful if people actually took real characters from previous games. You probably don't need to have this, but I wonder if the incentive to take over the narration would be stronger if there were a win condition. Are you deliberately playing around with the right to brag about the character, and creating an investment through that? That you want to defend your character and make it shine brighter than the others? If you told stories about characters that the players has played, the incentive to brag could possibly be greater.

    I really like the mechanic that even the things out. If someone is three gulps before anyone else, that means that the challenger must drink at least four gulps. I do wonder what happens if two people have about the same amount of beer. I also wonder why someone would like to add beer before they reach the two finger mark, because that only means that they have to drink more (or others drink less, if they are "gulps" before).

    I don't mind the two finger mark rule, and I think it sounds good as it is. I'm also seeing a bluff mechanic, where you can take one gulp and then pretend continuing drinking, even though this will probably only work once.

    It's hard to understand some parts of the rules, but I guess that's because you will have pictures in the rules that will make the rules more clear. Some things I don't like is the "do what you like" rules, because they send a signal that the creator doesn't know how to play the game. Tell me that the buyer of the pitch gets to start the narration. Tell me that my paper is going to be passed to the left, instead of any person of my choosing. That will ease up the learning of a new game, and bring more confidence in wanting to play it.

    In the same way, have a slight structure for how the story is being told. Right now, you pretty much ask to "narrate whatever you want". I know this is a drinking game and that the narration is not the important part, but structure brings comfort.

    I like how you create characters together and the Add Move mechanic. I would possibly add a notion that the character should brag and in the same way describe or insinuate how bad the others are, possibly by describing how their traits are useless or turned against them, so the story will put more pressure on the others to want to take over the narration.

    \n ...few days and another playtest later, I yet again need your help, Story Gamers!

    Creating the keywords/details before the game seemed like a good idea, but it seems it needs some proper system to handle it.

    During the 1st playtest people (experienced story gamers) just took inspiration from it and it worked fine.
    In the recent games I took few people who are only familiar with traditional RPGs (and one who is completely new) and they seemed lost.
    I think I might need some rules on how to use those details, or some other approach to story structure entirely.

    Here's an exempt from the rewrite about creating the details:
    How do we create those tales?
    Choose a starting player who will introduce the tale. They start with \u201cRemember when we...\u201d and follow with some heroic feat the party has achieved in the past. Now, all the heroes should now try to remember details of this adventure. Things like \u201cIs that the one with the airship?\u201d and \u201cI remember something about goblin alchemist\u201d are good details, but try to come up with your own. Get inspired (or simply steal) details from fantasy adventures you played in the past. You know, the ones with dungeons, dragons, tombs of horrors etc.
    Create interesting locales, NPCs or obstacles you want to see in the story, but keep them vague enough so others can use them. Each player should come up with 3 details and write them down on their character sheet. Once this is done, the starting player says: \u201cSo, we were sitting in a tavern, when...\u201d and starts narrating the adventure hook.

    First playtest, people only looked around the table and incorporated a detail in their narration. It took some power away from the ADD move, as people concentrated on on the details on the table (Is that a bad thing?). In the second playtest, people seemed lost and told me they needed more structure.

    Any ideas how to handle this guys?

    I am toying with the idea of using post-its (or scraps of paper like in doctor magnethands) to write those details, and maybe use them as cards? Something similar to Aye, Dark Overlord. Or maybe I should create Adventure Modules akin to fiasco playsets? Both require the players have more stuff prepared (bring post-its or module). This takes away from the chance for spontaneous play. But now that I am thinking about it more and more, I don't think spontaneous play is something people will do that much :P

    On the other hand, the re-write is going forward, but is severely slowed down by my job. You can check out the progress HERE!
    The Brag move was something I had in mind for using the details, you can skip that - it's not finished, and probably not even a good idea :P

    \n So you need something to stimulate the players' imagination? Maybe provocative questions to guide them (where, why, who's chasing you...) Ask each player one question. It's easier to invent "Hey, remember that time at the marketplace in Iscandria" rather than an entire story. Once someone has started (or picked from a list) the others can associate.

    Or some generative heuristic, with moves that confuse "No, you're got it wrong again, that was Numijkad, the story in Iscandria was the one with the Sultan!" or elaborate "That fat sultan with his enchanted carpet collection?"...

    I think random association works well to kindle the imagination. By focussing on just one tangible aspect or question, you can let the players start thinking. Give them something first, rather than "now figure out everything!" Inventing entire stories, even shaky ones, isn't easy!
    Also, by sharing the brainstorm (or even deliberately create confusion) you make it easier to associate.

    Another suggestion is to remove the Pass move (and fixed turns, if you have that). And change the Add or Challenge into someting more tangible, perhaps formulaic, like the "remember when we..."

    "You were totally ... then, this is what really happened..." instead of Challenge.
