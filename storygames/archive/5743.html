\n So the NC Game Day (link) had me thinking. How hard would it be to organize a mini-con in the Triangle area? Assuming, of course, that we haven't already had one and I missed it.

I know there's a lot of game designers in the area. Is there any interest in pursuing this?

I can think of a few ways that this event might manifest itself:

First, we could find a sizable venue, preferably well-lit and sheltered from the harsh and inclement elements. This might cost some money. Or not -- if the group includes someone who's a student at a local university, for example, we might be able to secure a meeting area on campus.

Second, we could meet at someone's home, but unless your place was recently featured on MTV's Cribs, I'm thinking it might get a little crowded (depending on how many people get involved).

Third, we could convince a local game store to host us. All Fun & Games (Highway 64) has a decent gaming area. And they've let me run demo games there before.

Feedback? Interest? Ideas?

    \n So I spoke with Vivienne at All Fun And Games. As usual, she was great to talk to. Very nice person.

    Their game room can hold between 50 and 60 people. On Saturdays, they host Magic: The Gathering enthusiasts. However, if we wanted to schedule a mini-con in advance, AFNG would let us have the whole place to ourselves. This would probably need to be timed to coincide with a lull in MtG gaming -- apparently, there's a major Magic release in July or August, so right before then would be the best time for the Triangle Mini-Con.

    Vivienne also noted that if we ran sessions of our own games at the event, she would be happy to find a way to make these available to her patrons. For example, consignment sales, or maybe IPR (I told her a little bit about how it works, and she said that sounded good -- though I'm sure she'd want to learn a little bit more about it from someone more knowledgeable than myself).

    At any rate, if there's enough interest, I can make the arrangements. The upshot will be that in order to pull off a successful event (and thereby improve the chances of a second such opportunity), we'll need to get forty or fifty people in the store on the day of the event.

    Alternately, we could just gather in smaller numbers, and that would bring the date of the event forward a little bit (if there were only a dozen or two people, then this would not require six months of planning and scheduling on AFNG's part).

    What do you guys think? I'm meeting with her next week to discuss the possibility in more detail. Input welcome. I'm just gathering info and presenting it to the collective hive-mind of SG, here.

    -- Rafael

    Posted By: Quintin StoneWhat kind of con is under discussion here? Rafael mentioned game designers specifically in his original post, so I wasn't sure if this was a general kind of gaming convention.
    \nDefinitely a general kind of general gaming convention -- but on a much smaller scale. I'm thinking it's going to be a bit like NC Game Day, but focused on independently-published games. Though I see no reason to exclude anyone, so if someone wants to run a game of Rifts, hell, I got a third level Juicer with a vibro-katana, so count me in.

    I mentioned the game designer angle because I think that part of the appeal of the event is that local gamers will have an opportunity to do the following:
    1. Enjoy a well-organized con without having to pay for it;
    2. Play new games, and get exposed to new play styles; and
    3. Meet the designers of the games in question.

    NC Game Day does all of the above, to a degree, but it's only a few times a year, so I think another mini-con would be a good thing.

    Having thought it over, I'm leaning towards the following structure for CollectiveCon: There are 9 tables, and we have 12 hours, so 3 slots per table seems pretty logical (but this means that each session can be no longer than 3 hours, to allow time for meals). This adds up to 27 three-hour slots. I doubt we have 27 game designers in the area, so no matter what, everyone should have an opportunity to run at least one session of his/her game. In addition, designers will be able to hand out promotional materials (hell, we might even collect them in a bag or package of some kind, and give one to each person who plays a game).

    What do you guys think?
