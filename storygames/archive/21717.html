\n
\n

\n I'm all about exploration in my D&D. Climbing cliffs, squeezing through caverns, traversing nigh-impassable terrain, oh, and a handful of random encounters: that's the good stuff. The journey is half of the adventure.

The problem is that D&D doesn't do a particularly good job of making that exploration interesting. There aren't many choices to make in modern editions when these tasks arise. Make a [skill] check. Roll high, you're good. Roll low, you're not so good. If you roll a nat 1, you fall or something, depending on the GM's capriciousness and internalized principles.

I don't particularly like this style of exploration, but by that same token, I don't much care for PbtA moves for exploration or Mouseguard/Torchbearer's take on the issue (either the twists/conditions or the conflict system).

    \n Ditch the skill checks.
    I don't charge successes for my exploration. They want to search they can search.
    I charge time. ("OK, you spend 20 minutes searching the room.")
    And there are wandering monsters. Oh so many wandering monsters. And life is brief.

    Also the clarity zooming thing helps keep engagement. When there is lack of clarity, zoom in time. When everything's clear, zoom out ("Searching the street takes... does four days sound right to you?").

    ValyrianSteelKatana said:

    The problem is that D&D doesn't do a particularly good job of making that exploration interesting. There aren't many choices to make in modern editions when these tasks arise. Make a [skill] check. Roll high, you're good. Roll low, you're not so good. If you roll a nat 1, you fall or something, depending on the GM's capriciousness and internalized principles.

    \nSo what makes D&D combat interesting, and these types of checks not interesting?

    It seems to me that combat has significant stakes, multiple rolls, with significant chances of failure, but no one failure is catastrophic, so there is space to recover, by luck or by adapting your approach.

    On the other hand, "roll Climb to climb the mountain" is often treated as a single check, so either the stakes are be neutered to make failure less than catastrophic, or the chances of failure are reduced (making the occasional catastrophic failure all the more jarring).

    So maybe all you need is to make break down the tasks from a single roll into multiple with meaningful choices? Still well within the toolkit of standard D&D, just zoom in a bit more on the things that interest you?

    So stuff like: take the reasonable route, or the riskier shortcut (more difficult, but same check gets you further along the way)? Fail and expend resources: maybe you get hurt, maybe you watch your sword clatter down the cliff. Partway through, reassess your odds: at this rate, will you make it, or would it be wiser to take double back and take another route?

    \n If you got the resource management and descriptions right, there's a few simple things you could add:

    For every exploration scene, after you have decided on your prep how would the place look like (a.k.a what the players would see when they get there), prep these:

    1-3 Things to be noticed. Like, details that no one could notice until it's too late. You will tell the players about them only if they ask about them, if their characters look or are near enough to notice, etc. You can also give one of them out on really good rolls if things go that way, but try not to. The idea is that this whole thing should be interactive.

    These things could be the slippery moss growing on what looks like an otherwise firm climbable surface. A crack on the ceiling of that room you're about to cross. The faint glowing mushrooms that seem to go brighter as you approach. Players will only get this info and it would be up to them to decide if it means anything or not. If it your prep it means there's a hazard, spring the trap when they don't take precautions, ask for a saving roll if these are not enough. Have them cross these things safely, no rolls required, if they play it right.

    These details can also lead to opportunities like recovering one resource, finding a shortcut, learning lore from the place, etc. Or you can just put some things to get them paranoid

    1-3 Things that can be picked up and/or used for something. Like cover, or to store or hide things, to create a hazard against enemies. A thick layer of dust. Pebbles or dryed leaves that make noise when stepped upon. Flammable things, shallow water, places to hide and ambush. Furniture in a house or a bar, or even a dungeon. Statues that can be pushed over. Things that make thick smoke when burnt or have a terrible smell.

    You better make these found on plain sight, at least most of them. Put these among the things to be noticed only when it makes sense. Enemies could also use these against the PCs!


    Also, whenever you describe a place, think of an emotion and let it fill the place. Hatred places will show signs of violence, past or possible. Sadness can instill a forest, specially in certain hours of the day, etc.

    \n So what we do is to focus on the lack of clarity. Like, when they have a cleric willing to expend slots, water is no problem so we skip past that with one sentence. When they have lesser restoration, hygenie and disease is less scary. When they have a ranger in their favored terrain, we skip the nav roll. Usually they haven't covered all the bases. Encumbrance has been a major focus in this campaign, in a good way (after a while, it became more "hmm, what do I carry and what do I leave?" than "aaargh, I can't grok this stupid encumbrance system"). If Jen, their kurbits-painted horse, dies, they're in trouble since the horse carries so much.

    When there is a lack of clarity of outcome, emotional engagement will follow. Last session a PC caught serious illness and the exhaustion levels were racking up. So every morning, the disease saving throw was the focus of the day. Zooming through all the navigation and skeleton bashing with the minimum of engagement to resolve those things, then when it's time for the disease save, everyone goes quiet, chips in their insp, cheers, etc.

    \n For detailed exploration to compete with quick exploration, such as immediately throwing dice and gaining information for a find traps, secret passages, navigation etc. roll there should be fun player choices and experiences, such as the dilemma of going left or right depending on the GM's description of two trails in a forest (imagine the GM grinning) or an abnormally detailed description of the strange spider you find in a hole in the wall.

    On the other hand for voluntary exploration to compete with minimum required exploration (e.g. having to exit a locked room) there has to be an obvious reward for getting into trouble and spending time and other resources that the player knows about (e.g. search a house for a stolen letter). I wouldn't expect rules to play a major role in this kind of motivation, it's an adventure design concern (more exploration, less fighting).

    \n You're talking mostly wilderness exploration. You can treat city as a biome too. From Reve, Ryuutama's inspiration : make characters experts, make players experts handing out examples for their areas of expertise, then let the players invent resources as their characters gather them from the milieu with skill rolls. Resources : time, safety, bonuses, etc. The GM does the same, only providing mostly harmful stuff. The various biome yield resources proportionally to their dangerousity (known success threshold number).
    Watching players soaring from reading their basic biome examples to producing their own is the reward.

    \n I like a lot of these suggestions. Would it be too hokey to have something like...\n
    The River
    HP: 10.
    Abilities:

    Crashing Rapids. On a failure, suffer 1d6 damage. In the summer, in the dry season, or after a dry spell, this does only 1d4 damage. In the spring, rainy season, or after a heavy rainfall, this inflicts 1d8 damage.

    Swell of Waters. Only available in the spring, rainy season, or after a heavy rainfall. The river immediately restores 1d4 of its hit points.

    Sharp Rocks: Anyone swimming suffers +1 damage from the river.

    Skills: Test Swimming to attack the river, and test Swimming to defend against its attacks.

    Victory: Once the river's hit points are reduced to 0, you have arrived at your destination.

    Failure: If you fail, you drown.

    At any point, you may permanently lose a significant item to reroll a Swim test or reduce any hit point loss by half.
    \nI realize this is somewhat reinventing Mouseguard's wheel, but Mouseguard's conflict system does not privilege individual actors enough, in my opinion. I want the potential for the entire party to drown in the river, and I want the potential for no one to drown in the river, and I want the potential for one person to force the whole party to start to drown and let the whole party devote themselves to saving him (and potentially drown in the process).

    This might even be over-complicating things. You could even do something like:\n
    The River
    Tests: The river requires three Swim tests to cross.

    Failure: Every failed roll inflicts 1d6 damage on the swimmer. If a swimmer is reduced to 0 HP, he falls unconscious and begins to drown.
