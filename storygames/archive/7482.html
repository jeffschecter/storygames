\n This is, as advertised, a request for help.

I was recently a little stumped by a situation in a game, and would like to have some advice on it.

(Disclosure up-front: Odds are good I will never get to continue this game. But I figure I might learn something from it... and, after all, who knows?)

So:

Playtesting a game of my own design ("Land of Nodd"), I had a player create a character I had no idea how to challenge--at least not on the spot. For the purposes of this post, just assume that I was the GM, and the player the sole PC. The system is a light one with very few obvious Flags.

The setting was a near-future cyberpunk kind of situation. The character in question was an old, frail, but very intelligent man, who had become, in his old age, the CEO and owner of a huge megacorporation based in California (Silicon Valley). His goal in life at this point is to poison millions of people in China by feeding nasty stuff into the water supply. The way this particular game is set up, the story should revolve around this goal of his, and whether he manages to achieve it or not. The story begins with him living comfortably in California, surrounded by bodyguards, friends, etc. No important family members or connections exist at this point (it's a very pick-up-and-play sort of game).

What kinds of compelling situations can I present him with, throw at him? Remember, they must be related to his overall goal of poisoning huge numbers of people in China. Assume a Narrativist bent in terms of the game itself, if that helps at all. More precisely, I'm looking for stuff the player cares about, and there's not much in the way of mechanics/system that can be gamed or used simply to "explore" stuff (as in, say, d20).

Any ideas? What, as the GM, can I throw at him, given this little information? Or is this setup doomed to failure and suckiness?

    Yeah, it's not about "whether he succeeds or not". It's about choices he makes. Give him the choice about whether to do it or not, and, along the way, figure out the things the character cares about, asking if he's willing to lose them to accomplish his goal.

    \n

    "I'm an old man and I want to poison China" isn't a premise that lends itself to Narrativist play because there aren't any choices implicit in the situation. Make the situation dynamic \u2014\xa0other people who want other stuff, and their desires and actions impact the old man \u2014 and stuff will start to move.

    Posted By: Paul T.The way this particular game is set up, the story should revolve around this goal of his, whether he manages to achieve it or not.
    \nThis does not usually work for me as a set up of compelling moral questions play. Because it's not a moral question in and of itself. That's not a question of whether they the person inside can do it, it's a question of whether they the person outside can do it. Does he possess the capabilities to succeed at his goal?

    To turn this into a moral question I'd go with, yes, he can do it, that's never in doubt, and then the question would become, does he want to do it, and how much is it worth for him. So him doing it would provide the framework for the scenes to follow. But those scenes will only superficially be about doing the actions required to commit genocide. In reality, they would be an opportunity to tilt his world.

    His daughter showing up to say that she's going there, or marrying someone from there. A long time friend with family there, whatever. A connection telling him that the only person with the necessary knowledge of toxicology is the assassin who killed his brother. Tell him that certain people he requires are in prison and they are demanding that he do X if they are to help him. Have him find out about a reporter who knows too much and is about to expose him. Introduce elements to ask him, "are you still sure you want to do this?" What those elements are will depend a lot on the character and what has bothered him the most already.

    \n Very good points, across the board. Maybe it makes sense to separate the two approaches--success at a goal vs. exploring a premise. I'd like to focus on the "success" angle for a moment. So:

    Let's say we drop the "moral decision" angle for a second and focus on "can he do it". The problem here is that most of the action is being done by people in his employ--his workers, often halfway around the world. While insubordination, rebellion, etc, as you have advised, is a good option, it's also going to get stale real fast if overused. I find it hard to imagine any tense, active scenes for this old man.

    How can his progress be challenged in meaningful ways? For instance, I can envision scenes where a lieutenant comes to him with a tough decision: "Our operations have been spotted. If we continue in Beijing, we'll be investigated by the Chinese Air Force." This requires him to make a choice--and, often, this is where the moral/ethical angle can come back into play. That can create _internal_ conflict. But how does one create _external_ conflict in such a scene--a situation where we're really unsure how things will turn out and who will prevail? All the scenes about such characters that I am familiar with from movies, books, etc, come down to a conversation in an office, ending with a decision and an order given by the chief. All the action and actual conflict happens off-screen or involves other characters. How do we create some variety in these scenes, some different types of scenarios?

    (I think I'm going to start another thread about internal conflict, so let's leave that be for now.)

    The suggestion to send him into the thick of it is good, of course. It seems to me that trying to convince the player that his character needs to go there would be a bit
    of unfun, so I would probably just ask the player how they feel about it and then frame to the action there ("you land on a private helipad in a small Chinese town..."). But what would he be doing there?

    You can not address both of these questions simultaneously in a meaningful way. This is the problem.

    \n

    "Can I kill China?" is a Gamist agenda.

    \n

    "What does this mean to me? What am I willing to sacrifice to do it?" is a Narratavist agenda.

    \n

    Either way, you want some buyin on the player's part. You want him to say why he wants to do this, what will happen if he succeeds, what will happen if he fails.

    Well, if it were me, I'd lift a page from Terra Incognita: Play out the fight scene in flash-back.

    Your evil billionaire gets to say things like "You idiot! I send you out armed with the best in military grade weaponry, and you still fail me?"

    Then the guy playing the hero gets to say things like "But boss, he had flashing fists of kung fu fury ... what good are armor piercing rounds when we can never draw a bead on the guy?"

    The fact of (at least temporary) foilage is established by the mooks coming back in defeat ... so the stakes become what the mastermind gained during the setback.

    "Ahhhh ... so you say that he appeared when spunky girl reporter Li Tzen Chu was threatened? Innnnnnnteresting. Leave me. I must consider what to do with this information."

    Which implies, of course, that the hero can only be defeated when the mastermind himself makes a personal appearance, and the scene is played out in real-time. Flashback scenes are always flashbacks of partial-to-total defeat.
