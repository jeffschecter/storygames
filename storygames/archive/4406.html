    \n Welcome! I definitely wouldn't worry about offending anyone or anything like that. Everyone here is seriously cool with games of any stripe. (And I have a great love for oMage and nMage.) I agree with you that "working towards enlightenment/power", for me at least, is one of the more core threads of the what nMage is about.

    Firstly, there's the simplest option of using the WW system and paring out / simplifying the rules you don't find necessary. (For example: just keep Spheres, Quint, Gnosis, Willpower, and the base Attributes, strip out any real damage system, and keep Skills / Merits / Flaws as freeform and supply some bonus dice at most. You'll need to modify some difficulty numbers to make basic tasks easier, but it should work out.) This might be the easiest if you have players already used to WW, but you may want something more.

    Beyond that, I think there are some rules you can reuse wholesale, regardless of system. For example, the definition of what spheres do, how many spheres you need, etc. could be kept exactly intact and don't depend on system. Similarly, it's clear that vulgar magic should be harder than coincidental magic and more prone to complication (regardless of system). You could plug this stuff into most any light system, like PDQ or the Pool.

    As for what system to use: Shadow of Yesterday (and it's setting-less version, The Solar System) have cool, easily hackable setting-bits, and mechanisms for reflecting character change and evolution.

    I'm not super-experienced with Sorceror, but given that it's focused on dysfunctional relationships and getting What You Want (i.e. Power) it could work. There's nothing like a freeform magic system built into Sorceror as is, and I don't think that'd be the right way to go. Instead, I'd just use the normal Lore stat to act like Gnosis (i.e. a stat to pump up as you pursue knowledge) and for performing most magical acts (constrained by the rules of Spheres, etc.), and use Demons as things that your character is effected by or uses in order to become stronger. So I think an ideal scenario is the Mages seeking out powerful artifacts (i.e. demons with Wants and Needs) that will strengthen their Lore or Will as they seek to increase their power. However, using Sorceror will gear things towards a certain kind of Mage story.

    \n1) Strip attributes down to Physical/Mental/Social. Select one descriptor with a -1 and one with a +1 to add variation in Power/Finesse/Resistance.
    2) Select 5 dots of additional descriptors that are broad. You will add these like skills, but they're very broad and can also represent a special passion or emotion, etc.
    3) Willpower is Mental+Social. Health is Physical + 5. Defence = lowest of Mental or Physical and applies to ranged attacks as well. Gnosis starts at 1.
    4) You do not buy rotes. You're just better at descriptor-based tasks when you use magic (add your Arcanum)
    5) Other magic is Gnosis + Arcanum.
    6) Combat is quick n' dirty. To hurt someone enough to impose a -1 penalty on dice rolls for the scene score 1 success. To knock somebody out of acting for the scene score successes= to Physical. To kill somebody, score successes = to Health. You can spend a Willpower to knock a condition up one step.
    7) Anything that affects people uses a similar scale, except that "death" can stand for imposing your will by whatever means. Subtract the applicable attribute from "attacking" dice pools. Against the environment simply pick a number between 1 and 5 as the penalty.
    8) A magical effect only works on one person or thing at sensory range unless a mage employs Style. Mages have a Style Capacity equal to Gnosis + *lowest* Arcanum. You recover these points every game session. A point of Style can affect an additional PC/central NPC target, a variable group or area (1 point=5 guys/a backyard, 10= a whole city), but you must back that point up with an equivalent expression of Style. Same for intensified range, duration and so on. Style takes time, resources, sacrifice or compels the play group proportional to the expenditure in points, rated on a 1-10 scale. A 1 point sacrifice is 50 bucks or a rabbit. A 10 point sacrifice is the kind of thing a bad guy does to summon Cthulhu on big gory altars.
    9) Style always impacts Wisdom, based on what it is and how it is used in equal proportion -- literally. Style increases and/or lowers Wisdom, as follows:

    *Magic is foolish (magic spell punches a baby): subtract Style x.1 (10=1 Wisdom
    *Magic is wise(magic spell heals a baby): add Style x .1
    *Style is foolish(punches a baby to boost magic): subtract Style x .1
    *Style is wise(kisses a baby to boost magic): add Style x .1

    Apply *all* circumstances. Doing good with a dark ritual may balance out. You can add a 1-5 point wiggle to represent the moral differences between ritual and result.
    10) Pick 6 dots of Arcana as usual. Orders are a descriptor that adds a die to spells that fit its mission/ethos.

    That should do it. This system has several "handles" for theme-based systems, but doesn't make them necessary, so you don't have to make someone talk about his/her Sooper Special Story Mojo if they don't care or don't want to define it that way, but another character in the *same game* can define things by passion or story circumstance.

    (This is supposed to be impossible, though, and may give you cancer. Or something.)
