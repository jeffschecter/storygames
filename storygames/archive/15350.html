\n Thadrine, made a good point on his thread about samples characters (http://story-games.com/forums/?CommentID=348510): why don't designers put out demo kits?

I agree with this whole heartedly, I have played some games with total confusion because I didn't "get" it solely by reading the rules. What I would like to see are demo kits that contain the following:

- Sample adventure
- Sample characters
- Example AP
- Rules cheat sheet
- What materials are recommended/required for play
- A gold star sticker for demoing the game (not really)

    \n I suppose there's a fine line between "wanting to make it easy for GMs/players to get started playing" and "wanting to get the person who bought your game so fired up about how awesome your game is that coming up with a demo session is fun work".

    It's possible that there's a fear that some GMs will be insulted if you do their work for them, because they like doing that part and are used to it.

    Or perhaps it's because it's not seen as a substantial added value enough to justify the effort of doing it well. I mean, you could theoretically throw it together in a super simple format. But if you want to polish it up so that it's up to the standard of the rest of your game text, that takes time and energy.

    That being said, I'm a recent convert to the "give me predeveloped content" camp. These days, I'm quite excited about finding useable scenarios, character concepts, etc.

    \n In the good old days, you'd buy a game it would be 200 pages or so, and then you'd have supplemental books to actually give the dry mechanisms some flavour. So the publisahers would give you a sample demo game to show how it could all be put together in a palatable fashion. The sample/demo game would be a few dozen pages in length.

    These days (especially in the indie sphere), it seems that if you write a game longer than a few dozen pages you get shot down for adding in too much crap.

    Case in point:

    Lady Blackbird IS a demo game.

    It just doesn't have a full game to go with it.

    \n Yes! Demo kits are awesome, and I wish more games had them. These days some games don't even include character sheets, why would someone intentionally put up obstacles for people for enjoying their games? Especially in the small scale indie circuit, where the games don't put the food on the table, and all things considered probably cost more than they make. I get 100x the happiness from seeing an AP online than I do from seeing the sales count increase by one on Lulu.

    Jeffrey's list is good, but even if you only provide some of the stuff he lists, it will help getting your game into actual play.

    The Nordnordost collective has built demo kits for many games (Polaris, Panty Explosion, Love in the Time of Seid and Montsegur 1244 are the ones that come to mind now). But if the game already has everything prepared, that gives us a lower threshold for offering the game in the Indie Gaming Lounge. We only have four hours to run a game for a group of visitors. And many of our hosts will not know the games they demo by heart, they are greatly helped by the demo kits.

    Lady Blackbird is a good example of a game that thanks to it's presentation has seen much play. Zombie Cinema is another (except for that horrible black on dark grey rules sheet, but since it's only a single page we'll overlook that), A Taste for Murder doesn't even have character sheets and the necessary game board for free download - but since the rules are very clearly laid out in the order they are needed, the game acts as it's own cheat sheet, and it was our most played game last S\xe4vCon.

    \n Some more grist for this particular mill:

    The Guided Play Scenarios for Annalise are basically demos, though someone at the table needs to know the rules of the game to play (as opposed to being some kind of "intro" or "lite" thing). But they give you pre-generated characters and setting (basically skipping the first phase/session of standard play), so you can pick one up and go.

    I haven't gotten much feedback from them. I know a couple have been played at conventions. They have wildly variable star ratings on RPGnow. That's about all the feedback I've ever gotten on them. I like them, so they continue, but I dunno how useful they are to people.

    I used to have a demo kit for carry available, but it was for running 10-15 minute demos (forge booth style), and it got lost in the shuffle at some point. Since the game itself is pretty much pick-up-and-play, I haven't felt the need to generate any additional material for it.

    I do tend to try and make examples fairly AP-like, including in- and out-of character conversation and context leading up to the core example, but writing up a full AP for your own game is like making a pumpkin pie from scratch - it's about 90% more work for about 10% more reward over other people's AP.

    I dunno, I've come to accept that the first time I play a game it will be halting and weird and curious as we try to figure out how it works and what parts mesh with the people playing and which skills need to be trained. Other people's experiences with the game don't really help me until I've had a chance to try it out first my own way. So it's interesting to hear these other perspectives.

    \n In terms of my writing for Ars Magica, it's because comittment to learning the game actually does have a play enjoyment reward. That is: system matters.

    In Ars, one of the most fun mechanisms of the game is Spontaneous magic. That being said, it's not eay to remember these mechanics in your head: you need to look them up.
