    \n Play-by-post has always had a very particular and somewhat stately pace of call and response. In that sense, Storium if anything is faster paced by default, in part because it has an extremely robust system for keeping people on the site itself.

    That said, if you find yourself reloading any sort of play-by-post game every hour \u2013 you've missed the point. And without a question, and without commenting on the quality of the system or the intent, it's very clear that the creators took as their inspiration the already extant (and even "long extant" if I want to feel very old) play-by-post community that already exists in multiple interactive media.

    Why is the "constant refresh" approach to PbP wrong? Because even in the days back before there was a web, there was notifications. Notifications were not pioneered by mobile applications, they were pioneered long before then by people who eventually went on to create "you've got mail!" Email was the original notification, and it was the first readily available medium by which PbP could exist. When a story or game updated, you were then notified and either took your turn or waited until the next person made theirs, committed it, and then you were notified. The next great movement of play-by-post gaming was to some of the earliest "online journal" sites (LiveJournal in particular), in particular subcommunities within the number of people that you followed. Like today, the usage pattern for a social network is that you monitor/follow/friend/Circle/whatever-the-verb-of-the-week is, and then simply use the site as you would normally, keeping an eye out for notifications, which would then tell you what the activity in your community was and then you could react appropriately.

    Remember, this is still the PbP workflow that a far greater number of people are engaging in, right now, than the sum total of people who are actively engaged in what we think of as traditional role-playing games and their structure. Without question. They interact, they do other things on the site or in their life until a notification that their input is necessary comes up, then they reenter the flow to interact as required.

    Storium takes that as understood to be the standard means of interaction with a game. While the side chat is wonderful and can take the place of IRC or other high-speed/immediate assumptive communicative channels, that doesn't fit with the workflow that they've created and that they've inherited. The asynchrony is a feature, a huge feature, of what they're trying to do or, at least, of what they're trying to emulate.

    PbP exists largely so that people with other demands on their time (old-school: literal school, new school: getting the kids off to school) can go about their business until the call goes out, and then it's their turn to immerse themselves back into the story, help build an extended portion of it which should interest everybody involved, and then once again shift to waiting for notification.

    If you know anything about different methods of message passing and the coordination of multiple live objects in modern distributed computing platforms, play-by-post is almost wholly event driven. The locks on process between actions are long-term (comparatively), so like a watched pot, checking it repeatedly will make sure that it won't boil. You're waiting for the event, the pot to boil, so instead you wait for the whistle. Now you're signaled, you act, you do what you do without worrying about the time pressure because no one is waiting for you to put down the pen \u2013 they're waiting for you to bring things to a boil so that you can now signal the whistle to trigger the next person.

    If you're pointlessly refreshing the page every hour for three days, yes \u2013 it's a total waste of your time. If you're going about the rest of your social network engagement, across multiple platforms, and you notice a bleep, a boop, or other such notification come in, you service it. And then you go back to doing what you do.

    That's the intent. That's the workflow. If that is not getting communicated to players in GM's on the sites \u2013 that's a whole different point of failure that I can't address.

    And if Storium is not emailing anticipations when conditions are such in their particular story that it's their turn to inject content, they're failing to do the second most important job of the entire system, right after actually recording what people say.

    It's not like running a game on IRC. It's like nothing so immediate. That is the part that will stick in the craw of a certain portion of the online community, but luckily we have a multitude of other methods to feed their hunger for life role-playing that does not require multi-day pauses in order to get minimal invested responses.

    It's a good thing we have tons of diversity in online rpgs, no?

    \n The default activity for Storium games is 2-3 scenes per week, ad the expected activity is clearly stated when you sign up to a game. The fastest setting is 1 scene per day. I narrate one story and play in four, and all of them have used the default setting, but have at least initially played a lot faster. Then the narrator go missing for a few days and you're back to slightly above the stated pace. I feel that if the game says 2-3 scenes per week and players sign up for that, there should be no expectation of more frequent posting from anyone. I don't care how enthusiastic you are; you should join a 1 scene/day game if you want that speed. I think that the risk for narrator or player burnout is higher in faster paced games, at least if they're faster than expected, and that it can be good to play more leisurely from the start, even though everyone are eager to engage a new story.

    The centralized narrator role makes it more important that the narrator is present to close the current scene and set up the next. But even if the narrator doesn't do that the players can go on playing. You can make moves without playing cards, and in two of my stories there are players who set up mini-scenes within the scene where their characters interact without playing any cards, in a say ten-move back-and-forth. The nice thing is that the stuff they create feel like it has less impact on the overall story, because they're not playing cards and winning control of challenges, so high-volume posters don't steer the story to an unproportional amount.

    The narrator can affect how many players are needed to conclude the scene by how many cards they assign for challenges in a scene. In a 5 player game there's a maximum of 15 cards, if every player plays all their 3 cards. If the narrator only spends 9 cards, three players can conclude all the challenges even if two players don't move for a couple of days. The narrator can also end the scene with open challenges, but that's not fun for anyone, so I think it's good to limit the number of cards. On the other hand, in the above scenario three players can address all challenges before the last two players even have time to check the game, so too few cards is not good either. It's possible for the narrator to continue a scene, and you can always add more challenges if you want to give the last players something to do as well.

    SquidLord said above that a centralized narrator may not be the best way to handle a shared story, and I agree. There's been talk about adding co-narrators to Storium, and if you can have any number of narrators it would be possible for all players to also be narrators, and to set up challenges and hand out assets and goals to each other. That sounds like a lot of fun to me, and in such a game you could easily rotate the narrator role between scenes and have characters that are in the same story, but rarely share a scene.

    \nThat's what I do as a narrator and what I want to see as a player. It's also good to have a big challenge, say 6 cards in a 5 player game, which means that at least three, but probably more, players have to address the challenge, while at the same time have a number of smaller 1-2 card challenges. That way the faster players can attack the big obvious challenge, while slower players still have something to do when they show up. Fix example, you can have:

    6 cards: Fight the fire
    2 cards: Rescue survivors
    2 cards: Find out how the fire started

    That's 10 cards out of 15 in our example, so you can have three players playing all their 3 cards and the fourth player who mops up one challenge, or all five players play 2 cards each. That's a fairly big scene, though. You can remove "Rescue survivors" and it will be a smaller and quicker scene, with the risk that three players finish all the challenges. It's not always the players want to play three cards either, so it's not good to force them to do it in every scene. It depends on how many players the narrator thinks should be required to end the scene.

    One thing I've noticed is that in one game me and the narrator are in Sweden, while the rest of the players are in the US. The narrator usually starts a new scene before she goes to bed, so I can't address it until I wake up in the morning, or actually around lunch time. By that time the Americans have usually either finished all challenges or left one of them for me to address, so I can't pick which one I like. This doesn't really bother me, and I'm sure we could fix it if I asked the others in a comment, but the dynamic is interesting.

    One nice thing is 1 point challenges. They're basically an offer for one player to decide a specific part of the story. The narrator suggests a Strong and a Weak outcome as usual, and the player plays one card to pick which version they like (or let the narrator decide, if the player plays a neutral card). In my film noir game The Dead Don't Speak, scene 2, I had a number of "Get a Guy/Girl a Drink" challenges for 1 point:

    Get a Guy a Drink
    The drinks are free, but the gesture may get people talking.

    Strong: He accepts, and during the conversation gives you a piece of information that you need. What is it?
    Weak: He refuses, and leaves you standing alone. How do you react?

    That gives the players a chance to show of what their character is like, and introduce new information into the story. I ended the scene with one of these challenges still open, because they weren't central to the story and I wanted to move on.

    Me, when I look at that dynamic "in action" (such as it is) I'm left feeling very high and dry. Disconnected, suspended, alone. I want direct P2P interaction. I want realtime. I want immersion. I want some depth. I want to roleplay, not cardplay. I want to play a character, not myself. I can't spend 24 hours a day in character, and I can't just jump into an immersed state like a Pavlovian dog whenever I get a ping. Yet if I don't, I'm being rude to someone else who wants her immersion, and she wants it right now too, but then woops, we both have to wait for s
    ome other guy.
    \nAnd this is where I get to bust out one of my favorite, get most painful, truths about the gaming industry that sometimes gets forgotten, tossed aside, or maybe just intentionally ignored \u2013 not just by everyone else but by me, which is why I have to remind myself of it so often:

    You are not the audience for every game. You are not the audience for every popular game. Sometimes, you are not the audience for a game that you would really, really like to enjoy. Sometimes it's the play style; it just doesn't gel with the kind of fun you want to have in your games. Sometimes it's the physical architecture that just doesn't work with how you want to play your games. Sometimes \u2013 as in this case \u2013 it's the temporal architecture that doesn't work for you in a given game (and this wouldn't even be the first one that was true of). Sometimes, it's just not for you.

    And here's the hard part: that's okay. It's absolutely fine if the most popular thing in the room is complete crap for you. If people were honest, that would be true far more often. There are games which are extremely popular in what passes for "mainstream" RPG development that I find hideous \u2013 complete wastes of time, effort, and ink \u2013 but they have an immense following. I'll even name names: Apocalypse World leaves me utterly cold, even though it has all the aspects you would think would look right into my hindbrain. The GM-fullness of AW strikes me as a horrendous waste and there's just something about the way that the playbooks are constructed that I find gaming-distasteful. Burning Wheel \u2013 I find it a hodgepodge of mismatch mechanics, deliberately obtuse design, and a couple of really clever gemlike structures which don't make it worth putting up with the rest of the system to enjoy. Both Savage Worlds and FATE fall into that needs for me as well \u2013 I just don't like them because of certain basic failures, as I see it, in their design for the things that I want to achieve in my gaming world.

    They are not for me. They are not designed for me. They do quite well without me.

    And I'm cool with that.

    Storium, in particular because it has some extremely specific mechanical assumptions which go along with the very architecture of the game, maybe one of those games that I simply don't like because it just doesn't work for me, most likely in particular because it makes certain assumptions about how much and how varied and impact a given character can have in a given scene that appears to just feel wrong to me. As far as I can tell now, they've made some significant choices about centralizing power with the Narrator which I am totally not on board with. I do a serious Spock arched brow at the mechanical connection of possession of certain cards and being able to trade them for narrative/mechanical effect \u2013 not that I'm against it, but in connection with the diversity question above, I fear it might have a tendency to become a little specifically stale, the "I hit it with my sword" problem from a different direction.

    The actual temporal pace of Storium might be one of those things for you that just make it not a game for you. And it's cool if that happens. It's all right to talk about that happening. Despite the feeling that you might get from some other inhabitants of these forums, it's okay to say that the game just doesn't work for you and that you have serious concerns about whether it actually does as well as its profligate posters would have you believe. It is even, and here comes the true heresy, okay to say, "if that's the sort of thing you want to do, this other thing might actually work better for you," and then talk about the thing that works for you instead.

    But at some point you'll have to accept that you're a rebel and join a few of us out here in the outer dark.

    You'll get used to it. But sometimes there's a lot of bailing when we're all on the same boat together.
