    \n Well, without knowing Brickquest in detail, I can't suggest what an advancement would look like when you got one.

    However, as to when to advance, are you wanting advancement to incentivize some player behaviour (as gold for XP does in old school D&D), or because the process of growing more powerful / capable is a part of the fun of the game?

    If the latter, then I would say, given the shortness of the campaign, just give an advancement at the end of each session. Maybe grant a whole new power each time, rather than changing any numbers: Fighter gains ability to run twice as fast or leap large ogres in a single bound. Thief gains ability to move completely silently. Wizard gains two new spells. etc.

    If the former, then consider giving very small bonus (e.g. a benny or whatever for a reroll) many times per session. Whether a player met the conditions for this (e.g. "Leap into danger", "Defeat a foe", "Protect a friend", "Grab some loot") would be adjudicated by the table at large, not the GM.

    \n What is your goal in having an advancement system?

    With a short campaign, you can do fine without anything like that. So what do you want to have happen? Are you hoping to encourage specific player behavior? If so, what behavior?

    Don't simply latch on an advancement system because other games have one. Add one in only if it helps advance your goals for the game.

    \n You're making a custom short campaign with advancement out of an advancement-free one-shot built from legos? Clearly the advancement system should be physically within the dungeon, eclectic and not generic, built of parts, and a one-off. Normally I would not advocate this since it involves a bunch of throwaway prep, but you're already building an entire dungeon out of legos each session, you'll have plenty of time to brainstorm.

    Include within the dungeon certain Feats one may accomplish to gain stats/abilities/etc. For example, acquiring the Stone of Peace before killing any monsters in the room, and subsequently killing one of the monsters in that room, will let you learn the special ability Backstab if you don't already have it. Or exiting the dungeon carrying at least 8 Rocks you've carried the whole time (16 available in starting room) will increase your Move by 1 and give a +1 bonus to Jump but during this dungeon it also makes other players have to make a skill check to get anything out of your swag bin and gives you a -1 to Jump.

    \n I'd do this:

    Make all advancement Devil's Bargain-type advancement. Advancement is really *change*, right?

    Everything is 'do this, and that happens to your character'.

    1. Some will be, "Do this, and something will change - but it's random or unexpected." (Jump in the pool and get a random +1 or -1 to one of your scores/A random limb turns blue.)

    2. Some will be, "Attempt this difficult thing, which is scary. If you manage, you get THIS benefit or advantage!" (Jump off the waterfall, and if you land in the carnivorous plant AND survive, you get immunity to poisons! Who's game?)

    3. Some will be, "Do this, and you get THAT, but it costs you something else." (Sleep with the Witch and you learn a spell. But she takes one of your most treasured possessions to keep for herself. Cut of your own hand you can replace it with this magical item/weapon. Etc.)

    Over time, characters will change and the players will have some say in how they change - advancement.

    \n For my D&D 5e Towerlands campaign setting, I'm using the following advancement system:

    To advance a level, you must check all boxes for your level:

    At 1st level, to advance:
    [ ] Engage in a meaningful scene about your Personality Trait, Ideal, Bond, or Flaw
    [ ] Engage in a meaningful scene with your Mentor
    [ ] Perform a deed that would impress the appropriate spirit for your character class

    At 2nd level, to advance:
    [ ] Engage in a meaningful scene about your Personality Trait, Ideal, Bond, or Flaw (one not used in the last advancement)
    [ ] Engage in a meaningful scene with your Mentor
    [ ] [ ] Perform a deed that would impress the appropriate spirit for your character class

    At 3rd level, to advance:
    [ ] Engage in a meaningful scene about your Personality Trait, Ideal, Bond, or Flaw (one not used in the last two advancements)
    [ ] Engage in a meaningful scene with your Mentor
    [ ] [ ] [ ] Perform a deed that would impress the appropriate spirit for your character class

    At 4th level and beyond, to advance:
    [ ] Engage in a meaningful scene about your Personality Trait
    [ ] Engage in a meaningful scene about your Ideal
    [ ] Engage in a meaningful scene about your Bond
    [ ] Engage in a meaningful scene about your Flaw
    [ ] Engage in a meaningful scene with your Mentor
    [ ] Complete a downtime project
    [ ] [ ] [ ] [ ] Perform a deed that would impress the appropriate spirit for your character class; add one box for each level beyond 4th.



    On advice of a friend, I'm treating the first three advancements as "training levels," since they're intended as such in 5e (at least to 3rd level) and characters usually move through them quickly. My system lets players discover their BIFTs in play, and at least three of them will be defined by the time they reach 4th level.

    The "impress a spirit" thing is specific to my setting. Levels represent binding with spirits who are impressed by your deeds. To multi-class, you need to somehow impress a spirit of the new class you want to diversify into.

    \n My Magic and Steel has an unique and original advancement system that is based on the token spent by players and master.
    Assuming that:
    - the token spent by players mean how much they wanted to make easy the quest and
    - the token spent by the Master mean how much he/she made hard for them

    The relationship between them (lesser or higher than a given number) allows the character to evolve or not.
