\n
\n

\n Seriously; we now very little of what the future brings, but we know one thing; people need to relate to each other. There's no other way to become a real human being. And that insight may pave the road for some interesting perspectives ...

What if verbal role-playing games is growing into a position where they may connect to this truth; that people need each other to become human, and that rpgs are a great way of exploring and developing our humanity.

I believe we have reasons to be patient about this. If we continue to develop verbal role-playing into increasingly subtle/strong/effective games and methods, I expect our kind of games to grow in importance. It will be interesting to see how new ways of connecting to humanity will evolve, in role-playing games, in the future.

We have some pretty strong games today.
And it's only the beginning ...

    Do you count using things like Skype or Google Hang Out as verbal rpgs?
    Yes, I do see that as verbal role-playing, but I am of the opinion that the layer of "virtuality" between the players makes it a lesser form of verbal role-playing. Still fun, of course, but the potential is less.

    There are obvious reasons why the potential of screen based verbal interaction is less than that of real face-to-face interaction. You need to understand that human beings communicate in lots of subtle ways, when physically present to each other. That is part of the "magic" of role-playing games.

    By recognizing the strength of these "subtle communications", you may tap into the real power of what you do by the table, when engaging in a game of role-playing. These communications are so intuitive we all do it, whenever we meet someone and talk to them, as we do in role-playing games. But we may also do it as a conscious part of a role-playing game. The method for making these subtle communications a conscious part of your game-play is deceptively simple; eye-contact, shaking hands, affirmative words and touching, smiling, opening your heart, emanating peacefulness ...
    - every normal thing you do to strengthen the emotional field between you and another human being.

    And these are but a small facet of what verbal role-playing let us play at, with each other. The strength and versatility of our interactions are almost endless, as we really engage and make use of the whole human being (from the deeper sensual, through the wider social and into the higher spiritual layers), when playing our games.

    I firmly believe that we, as designers, may tap into these things more and more, making them powerful parts of our new methods. We are already doing it, in the best of our new games. As we continue to develop this form, and reveal the true strength of it in our methods, the verbal role-playing games will grow in potential and importance.

    Humans are suckers for effective means of strengthening themselves, and our games will do that like no other games* (or other form of art) will ever do.

    :-)

    * With one exception; Live Action role-playing games does it too. LARP is closely related to RPGs, and have the same psycho-social potential.

    \nWait, wouldn't that mean that past trends are just as unlikely to come out as the future? They were yesterday's current trends, after all... :-)

    Seriously, though, I think it's obvious that face-to-face RPGs aren't going to overtake online RPGs in market share anytime soon for simple reasons of convenience for consumers, the amount of money to be made by publishers/studios, and lingering stereotypes limiting the audience. (Video games get around this stereotype to some extent because they don't resemble a children's game of "let's play pretend" as much, and their audience is so broad nowadays that it's seen as a more "normal" activity.)

    I do think that the audience for face-to-face RPGs has the potential to expand in the near future (and I wouldn't mind if it did, as it'd be nice to have more people I could play with). The normalization of RPG, fantasy, and science-fiction trappings in bigger-budget media (video games and film) can play a part in that by at least helping mitigate stereotypes limiting growth. And actually, digital media have done a lot to make it easier to nurture this hobby, giving a space to keep discussion alive between all-too-infrequent game nights and fan conventions. (Think about what you're doing right now, on this forum...)

    Still, as nice as it is to hope that we'll to return to some golden age of oral communication, people have been saying that for many, many years \u2013 after the telephone, film, television, and hell, even the printed word. Face-to-face communication will always be important to us as humans, and technology will both help enable it and get in the way. The "trend" toward mediated communication isn't just a short-term fad, though.

    \n Although I agree that humanity doesn't change (although, society does, and quite significantly too), I think there is an element here that has not been mentioned. Most of history has not enjoyed the massive quantity of entertainment media we do, and as a consequence, people entertained themselves: they sang, and danced, and played instruments, and performed plays, not for a paying public, but for their friends, families, neighbours and acquaintances. And convenience isn't the only reason that we largely no longer do that; it's also the case that amateur productions of this kind struggle to compete with professionally produced entertainment that is available at the click of a mouse.

    I think the draw for a lot of people is the opportunity to be personally creative in that way, with an audience whose responses are more personally meaningful than simply a commercial transaction. To be able to do something of your own, rather than to passively consume the creations of others. Even, to enjoy the shared sense of purpose that prior eras might have gained from a barn raising or some other sort of collective endeavour. And RPG is very well suited to all of this, because it's accessible, and basically easy, and requires little in the way of tools or long periods of training (to paint or play an instrument, say)

    So I agree with Thomas that there is indeed a credible possibility that RPG could become a major, widely recognised, cultural art form in some future society. That society would have to be somewhat different from ours, in that we seldom live near our friends and family, and working hours are tending to lengthen, which again provides motivation to just flip on the TV rather than get together with people to play a game. But it would be possible to solve those problems, and then yes perhaps RPG could become pretty widespread.

    \n Humankind (and nature) goes the path of less resistance. To have, say, 70% of the world population playing verbal games, you'd need games that:
    - Are free.
    - Have really simple rules
    - Require less conditions to be enjoyed (like players already familiar with the system, a quiet place and a table, an specific attitude towards roleplaying, GM prep, players who enjoy mental activity more than phisical activity, etc)
    - Have no need for props or perhaps just one, and better if players can make it themselves.
    - Take little time but can be extended as long as players want withouth repeating itself.
    - Produce content almost by itself instead of relying heavily in the players to provide it.

    And the greatest PR campaign on the whole history of humankind. Then you could make them trendy and have them change society in a substantial way. Sounds more possible now? Less? Well, games could certaily improve if us designers weren't also following the path of less resistance (ok, not that all of us do, I'm particularily interested in developing games with the conditions quoted above and probably most people in SG too). We need to roll a 7-9 in weird+cool and play it unsafe from there.

    There are two really important points here, that I want to address;

    1) Being personally creative; expressing yourself is a great way of learning, and developing yourself. As our games grows steadily more refined in method, and nuanced in themes, the opportunity for personal growth by playing RPGs increases. I've seen this in play amongst my hundreds of young players through the years, and I've seen it in play within the broader Nordic community for LARP/RPGs; players and designers of role-playing games develop admirable social, mental and moral skills; their great developments sets them apart, in positive ways.

    2) Engaging friends and family; it is highly meaningful for groups of friends and family to take part in meaningful interactions, producing fiction that challenge our ethics, and help us refine them:
    - in fictional conflicts; speculation -> action -> reaction -> solution -> consequence
    To be an active participant in such an ethical praxis, is laden with large benefits, both in the short and long term perspective:
    - We enjoy it as a game.
    - We grow closer as a group.
    - Each one of us strengthen our identity.

    The ethical praxis of RPGs makes us more sure of our place in society; it helps us develop the ethical apparatus necessary to function well in society. High ethics is a way of experiencing our life as meaningful. We grow more easily into a positive identity by playing RPGs.\n
