\n I just oredered a new MacBook, and I'm all excited about the fact that it has built-in iSight. I've been having the damndest time getting a regular group going, and thought this might be a good opportunity to do some telegaming.

I know that a few people here play regularly over Skype. I was wondering if when they do this, if they're using video. Is video conferencing an easy thing to set up on Skype?

I'm also wondering if anyone has tried other video chat/conferencing solutions and what the over all experience was like.

I recently discovered MeBeam, and I *think* it would work great for telegaming. It combines video, audio, and text; so, really, anyone could join in (although, there doesn't seem to be any way to create user names, so you have to identify text chatters by a short string of letters and numbers). It also doesn't matter if people are on Mac or Windows or Linux (I'd actually love to do iChat Video Conferencing because of all the cool features, but you're limited to 4 people total, and I *think* they all have to be using iChat). Has anyone tried MeBeam specifically?

Finally, if you've played in/ran a telegaming session, what was you're experience like? I doubt it's *as* fun as playing at a real table, obviously, but was it a satisfying and fun time for you? What are some ways to make it better?

    \nI just ran a game of Evolved Grottoes & Gryphons using my PS3 Eye. It was terrific (I'll post an AP as soon as I finish typing it up). The main thing, oddly enough, is camera placement. You want to be sure that your entire torso is visible, because if you talk with your hands the way that I do, it's good for the other players to see that. It's also good to add at least 15 minutes of padding before the game begins, to account for connection issues, microphone adjustment, and so on.

    \n I have had good luck with using Skype (we play the Skype game every other Thursday). It's a bit slower than face-to-face and people's attention may wander. We have video cameras but have not figured out how to do group-video. We use online dice rollers.

    Online play has some big advantages. One of the GM's made flash utilities for a specific game and displayed the graphics for us at the appropriate time to our shock and awe

    We have also had good luck with desktop sharing software (GoTo Meeting is what we use--it's not free) and using, for example, web pages or graphics imported into Power Point as maps.

    Expect it to go slower. Expect the odd "drop." Let the guy with the best computer and internet connection host the call. Leverage the on-line experience if you can. Google Documents is good for sharing characters and so on.

    -Marco
