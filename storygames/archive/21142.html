    \n Hi. I was directed by a member to answer your valid questions, Rafu. I am the one who designed the rules of the game, but The Creativity Hub, makers of Rory's Story Cubes, is currently developing the physical product. (NOTE: To be clear, I'm honestly not the OP in disguise to get attention. The game is already funded on KS, but a fellow NYer told me to jump in.)

    Untold: Adventures Await is the customizable storytelling game powered by Rory's Story Cubes that makes the players the heroes. Is it a roleplaying game? Traditionally speaking, not quite, and even I wouldn't try to sell it that way.

    How does it differ from Fate and Primetime Adventures?
    Untold (which I didn't name) features a board upon which five (out of 30) tiles will be progressively placed, each representing the 5 movements of popular media plot lines. There are over 7,000 combinations of plot lines.

    The "customized" part of the game is rooted in the 9 Story Cubes chosen at the beginning of play to "direct" the action. One (it's solo, too) or more players will experience a very different game if they go with the original Cubes included in the box than if they used, say, Dr. Who Story Cubes.

    The game utilizes Inspiration and Capturing Strategies to create the unique experience of making players be BOTH storyteller and audience simultaneously as they play the game. The "heavy lifting" of storytelling - that is, all the left brain stuff including structuring, tie-backs, revelations, etc. - is supported by the Scene Cards (tiles) and their deliberate color patterns to allow more freedom for the stimulation of right brain creativity.

    This is a NO-prep game - the emergent narrative takes place as the game proceeds with questions answered by Story Cubes and actions resolved by Reaction Cards (to be explicit: resolved without math. No numbers in this game).

    The game plays in an hour; players are actors and spectators of the episode. Again, NO prep.

    Is this game for everyone? Definitely not. Many story game veterans may find it unappealing for a variety of reasons.

    Paul_T said:

    Interesting! Is the majority of play spent taking turns telling the story?

    \nNot exactly, but close. Taking turns in the traditional sense isn't necessary as group collaboration runs smoothly, first via base simple sentences (made from tile and cube icons) that are then contextually built upon with shared questioning allowances. (Everyone agrees upon what they want to know about, then the group asks a question to the Story Cubes, up to the per-scene maximum.) This is followed by actions taken - as the group sees fit - during the Danger, Confrontation, and Showdown scenes.

    A complete game (episode) takes just 60 minutes.

    Rafu said:

    Hey there! @Virginian_John I have also looked at the KS page, and @johnfiore I really appreciate the explanation. It's interesting how this uses boardgame-like components and comes in a box. I'm not clear how much player:character identification is involved in the game --- in terms of, do I have "my guy", or is it more like "we're all film directors", and I know like a billion shades in between are possible.

    \nEach player is a defined and unique character in the adventure, either created from pure imagination or with inspiration from rolled Story Cubes. There is a "Hero Sheet" that is filled out after the first game has already started and the DANGER Scene has been established. In that way, players can tailor their Hero to face the opening threats (but don't worry - the following INTRIGUE Scene could confound those tailored choices quite amusingly).

    A Hero's characteristics include the usual RPG-ish stuff - name, role or job, personal aim, home, plus two of the following: an amazing ability or power, a helpful item, or an area of expertise (each of which may be impaired, damaged, lost, or stolen during play). There are no stats; players go along with the spirit of the world they are in and must agree with one another's descriptive choices. These characteristics provide the justifications for actions that Heroes can take later. Playing more games with the same Hero will grant new attributes later, some of which can be taken from things recorded on the Adventure Journal, like a found artifact or a scientific discovery or Jerry the Rat (a sidekick from a 9 year-old playtester's super-heroic animals game). \n

    \n Yes, there's a free PnP on the KS page under one of the updates. Sadly, it's not very printer-friendly.

    WARNING: The PnP is NOT the complete game. Also, the instructions included are a draft derived from my original (not written by me) and are missing key pieces of information, including these two crucial bits:

    1. The PnP Danger card with the "attacking" symbol has two small arrowheads above and below it which means "attacking each other."

    2. The color rule is tragically missing from this document. The game will play as intended if you replace the entire CONFRONTATION section of the rules with the following:

    In the Confrontation Scene, your heroes travel to a new location to deal with the Intrigue that was unveiled in the last scene. Here, they either meet a new threat or are challenged by a previous foe who directly opposes their mission. The tension increases as the action focuses on the heroes themselves.

    As you can see, you play through each scene in a similar fashion:
    REVEAL the new Scene Card, then ROLL the remaining StoryCubes.
    PLACE StoryCubes on the Scene Card, then DESCRIBE the scene in a simple sentence.
    ASK questions to add more details, then PERFORM actions to resolve the conflict.

    Along with choosing StoryCubes from those that have been rolled, you now get a second option for how to PLACE StoryCubes on a Scene Card: You may reintroduce StoryCubes from a previous scene into the new scene, keeping their original meaning and creating dramatic plot connections. To do this, remove any StoryCube from its square on a previous Scene Card and, without changing its icon, place it on the current Scene Card on a square that contains a colour matching any colour from the original square.
