\n So, Jesse posted a bunch of threads about Sorcerer. (I don't have time to post all the links at this moment. I'm just trying to get this thread up.)

Josh has a bunch of questions across several threads, but it seems to me there's a commonality to the issue at hand.

I believe this topic has a broader application than to just the Sorcerer rules, but we're going to be focusing on Sorcerer for now. Other games can be brought in as examples for points, of course.

So, in one thread, Josh writes:\n
Posted By: Josh Ballyhoo RobyOkay, so the system gives me this sturm-und-drang dice mechanic that basically gives me a pile of dice in front of me to tell me how much advantage I have in the particular situation, and it's up to me, working together with my fellow players in groupthink, to figure out what that advantage means. Does the game give me any tools to help negotiate differences of opinion in the group? Why exactly do I need this die mechanic?
\nIn another thread he writes, in response to a quote from Jesse:\n
Posted By: Josh Ballyhoo RobyPosted By: JesseEven more so there may be more than one way to legally resolve that ambiguity and which of the options is more appropriate depends entirely on the greater creative context of the narrative.
\nWho is the arbiter on what is appropriate to the greater creative context of the narrative?


Now, at this point I get the impression Josh is after something that is still unstated, and I neither want to put words in his mouth nor make assumptions about answering questions that aren't the question he's asking.

With that caveat in mind, I'm going to quote a response Jesse made.

Jesse wrote:\n
Posted By: Jesse

The answer to all your questions is always going to be the same: It's the music metaphor. It's about call and response among an ALREADY creatively synergistic group. Sorcerer wasn't designed with the idea that total strangers would play together.

If it helps, the GM very much has a leadership role, which is different from "control." For example, Sorcerer strongly recommends that the GM comes to the group with a strong aesthetic vision that the other plays buy into, such as the outlines for my Gothic Fantasy setting I carry around with me. This is analogous to someone saying, "I'm starting a Rock 'n' Roll band."

If someone suddenly busts out The Blues in the middle of my Rock 'n' Roll band, it's an obvious and easily corrected social blunder.

Jesse
\n
From here, if Joshua could explicate what's up, that'd be great, because right now I'm somewhat confused. In the example he gives of (an admittedly) lame exchange between the GM and a Player, he asks, "How does the mechanics prevent this from happening? And if it doesn't, why do we have the mechanics?"

This almost makes sense as a question if you think that the purpose of rules to prevent lameness. And maybe some games work that way.

However, I see the purpose of Sorcerer's rules as to engage risk -- creative risk. They are a tool or instrument used to facilitate social story telling. To get a die bonus, you add color and detail. When results come up during multi-character conflicts, Players must come up with creative justifications on the fly. It's about responding to the next beat of conflict that leads toward the Resolution of the Kicker. It's about the GM tossing out creative and compelling Bangs that provoke more cool responses from the Players. It's about coming up with details about relationships and more that demand -- as the dice are rolled, cool, evocative color. It's a focusing tool to elicit details of story.

And you might fail. That's the point. You might toss out a crappy Bang, or come up with a bit of detail that will get you a die bonus that gets no reaction from the table and so falls flat. The risk is there. It is required. Because the expectation of the game is that everyone will be doing the best to be at the top of their creative game.

In Josh's example of the lame exchange, I don't see either the Player or the GM trying anything interesting creatively. And so they're missing the whole point of the game.


Now, what is interesting is the question, "Why the rules, then? Why not just sit down and write a story as a group?"

Well, as someone who is sitting around writing stories most of the day I can only say because it's a fun experience to make things up, moment by moment, with a group of fun people. That's all I got. And the Sorcerer rules -- and I'm including the dice mechanics here -- focus creative thinking here: "Okay, I won the roll, but she's backing away. She's scared because something wrong... What is the detail of fiction, color or human behavior we haven't tried yet that will close the deal between these two characters?"

That's it. That's what I got.

The game isn't there to prevent lameness. It's there to create the risk of it, and the success of the game is when you and your fellow players are creating something great. Because just as it offers the risk of lameness, it offers the opportunity for great, synergistic creative excellence.

So, Josh, does that start us in the right direction? Or are you talking about something else?

    Focusing tool -- That's a good term and what I was trying to get at in my System & Narrative. The rules do produce creative and social limits but not in a vacuum. In Sorcerer, if we come to an impasse, the game fails, and fails dead. And I'm sorry, but no amount of allusions to a jazz band is going to help me out of that mess.

    So, to sum up: I get the sense of the gameplay that is being attributed to Sorcerer. I'm not seeing how the rules actually make that happen.

    \nIf you'll forgive me, Josh, I think maybe you're getting a little to attached to your point. I think the contrast there is between a quite hard and a very very soft limit. Yes: in Sorcerer you could play out a conflict ad infinitum, even just counting on your ability to narrate new stuff indefinitely (and possibly interestingly) rather than just blocking and accepting increasing penalties. But all those successes do mean something: they have a concrete advantage going forward, and so long as we're considering that in the narration, further tacks in a lopsided conflict are increasingly doomed to failure. You come to a point where you can mechanically prolong the conflict, but can't ever expect to overcome. I think the fruitful way to look at that (be it explicit in the text or not - it's been quite a while) is as the increasingly pointed question about how you lose, or what more desperate thing you attempt, underscored with the idea that your answer is on balance with your group's social contract.

    The contrast is: In a Wicked Age presents the choice: do you lose effectiveness, or something else - crunchy mechanics or fiction + mechanics? Sorcerer asks: is trying one more thing worth the amount your friends will think you're a dick - fiction or social? I can certainly see a reasonable criticism being brought against either game on those bases, but there's something a little bombastic about saying that the Sorcerer conflict dicing doesn't do anything meaningful.

    \n Others can certainly speak with more authority, but that's how I read the band metaphor, at least. There's a pressure in a band to "play along." If you've got a groove you're into, there's only so long you can doggedly pursue it without the implicit agreement from your band mates (who agree by picking it up and riffing with it) before you're just being discordant. There's not really a hard line about where that is, and the difference between your groove being awesome and you being a dick is whether the rest of the band picks up on the line, or if you stick with it past that illusive point where it's no longer cool.

    To be honest, as I've started to grok that it's a fiction v. social contract tension (and I think and hope that I grok rightly), it's kind of a fascinating dynamic. On a par, say, with Turku manifesto stuff. Like seat-of-pants high-voltage stuff: you can make some really cool, really brilliant stuff happen, and you can also get really really hurt if you don't see what's happening.

    (Possibly related: Bjarne Stroustrap on C++: "In C, it's really easy to shoot yourself in the foot. In C++ is much harder, but when you do, you blow off your entire leg.")

    \n Hi David,

    I saw this kind of behavior all the time in HERO system and I think GURPS is very similar in this respect.

    In GURPS, if I recall correctly, dice rolls determine whether a task can be completed. Failure of a dice roll generally means the character wasn't able to do something. Also, the more dramatic or wild the action is, the higher the chance they will fail. This means that anytime a player starts something that will require a dice roll, they risk having their character look uncool, despite all the points they spent making the character competent and setting up an image.

    Also, failures result in reducing the character's ability to act, either by wounds or putting them in mechanical disadvantages. In GURPS, the character is pretty much a player's only way to affect what's going on, so disabling the character disables the player.

    Does the reward of playing a disadvantage balance the risk of rolling dice? I recall that a player can earn 1 or 2 XP at the end of the session by playing a disad. I'm guessing that many players don't feel this is worthwhile.

    The nature of dice roll results in GURPS may also be why your group is happy waving rolls aside in some cases.

    Part of the recent wave of new game design is about addressing these problems -- making "disadvantages" elements that actually give the player more influence over the game or designing dice resolution so no character looks uncool unless their player wants them to. I've played many of these games and I don't see any of the "blocking" or resistance I used to see in Champions or D&D.
