\n I've been playing around with Dogs with one of my co-workers over lunch, getting ready to run it, and we came up with an interesting situation which forced us to look closely at demons and demonic influence. I'd like to hear what folks here think.

So imagine lucky Brother Chance, a Dog with some theological issues. He has come to believe (False Doctrine, of course) that it's OK to gamble when you feed a hungry Faithful with a profligate atheist's stake. If any of the recipients of his Robin Hood gains question this, maybe their full stomachs quiet their consciences (False Worship). In fact, when he gambles with this noble aim in mind, he's unusually lucky \u2014 if you asked him, he'd say blessedly lucky.

But we, the players, know differently. Brother Chance is a Sith Dog; his success in gambling is Sorcery.

Now, mechanically - can he have the Demonic Influence dice? Or does he only get dice from a Relationship with a demon? Can he have both?

    \n Brother Chance does not get Demonic Influence dice unless the GM decides to give him some. Brother Chance's player cannot demand those dice (although, given the specifics of your table, he might suggest that he should get them). Also, Brother Chance's player has no more right to getting the Demonic Influence dice than any other Dog in the game; he might position himself narratively so that he is more likely to get those dice, but that's a choice that he makes that any of the other players can also make.

    Brother Chance can also take a relationship with Gambling or whatever, in which case he gets those dice whenever he wants to bring them in.

    Also note (and I suspect this is what Jason is asking after), whether or not gambling is false doctrine is entirely irrelevant to what dice he rolls. What is or is not false doctrine is a judgment made by the players, never the game mechanics or procedures.

    \n Okay, I missed that this was a PC we were talking about. PC Sorcery works just like NPC Sorcery.

    1) You need a relationship with the Demon. No relationship, no Sorcery. You get those relationship dice under two conditions. The first is as per the normal rules if the relationship is helping you during a conflict or is the thing in jeopardy in the conflict. The second way is if you let the demon possess you. In that case you gain all the benefits of a possessed person per those rules.

    2) Once you have the relationship you are now a Sorcerer and can use Demonic Influence just like any NPC Sorcerer by narrating in sorcerous effects. Josh is wrong that the GM has veto power over this. If you're a Sorcerer, you're a Sorcerer and get those dice like any NPC Sorcerer would.

    \n This is something I haven't had to face before. If a Dog breaches a tenet of the faith, they have the authority to do so with impunity. If Chance says, "Gambling is okay is you give your winnings to charity," then it explicitly is okay. There's no false doctrine, no sorcery.

    This is part of what makes Dog's so cool. The temptation of the absolute power. So... how do you make it into a conflict?

    What if the Town Steward thinks Chance is spouting madness and the two face down over it?
    What if the Dog comes back to Bridal Falls City and the... what are they called? They take him to task over it.

    Or what if... despite what Chance says aloud, he *knows* it's wrong. This seems to be where you're headed. This could be a conflict similar to the one you get when you're dying. You play you, the GM plays Death.

    In this case, the GM plays the demons, trying to take hold of Chance's soul. The stakes are "Does Chance Stave off Corruption?" Or something like that. Any demonic influence would go to the GM's dice pool. If Chance loses, he doesn't get to be a Dog no more. Something like that.

    \n I think you're misunderstanding the Demonic Influence dice.

    The Demonic Influence dice only come into play under two circumstances.

    1) A sorcerer using sorcery.
    2) Situations where there is no discernible character opposing the player. For example, in one game an unmarried girl was giving birth out in the desert. A player wanted to race to get the father to her in time to marry them so the baby wouldn't be born out of wedlock. So I rolled Demonic Influence to "play" the distance and time with raises like, "Your horse gives out half way", "The trail ends and you're suddenly lost", "a bit of rock face collapses and pins you" That kind of stuff.

    Note: That the healing rules are simply an application of this principle.

    So in this case The Dentist is a character, and he's not a sorcerer using sorcery, so no Demonic Influence. Even possessed people don't get Demonic Influence just demon relationship dice within the scope of their possessed powers.

    Also considering what Josh said, I'm not sure at what point you would ever play Demonic Influence on the player's side.

    I'm clearly not Vincent so maybe there's something I've missed but this has always been my understanding of how Demonic Influence, Sorcerers and Possessed people work.

    Jesse

    Edited Note: There is ONE case I can think of where you *might* roll Demonic Influence in on the side of a non-Sorcerous character and that's if there's a environmental factor SO compelling it can't be ignored *and* it's narrated to be taking what can be perceived as a proactive action. For example imagine a Dog is in a fist fight with someone in a burning building. The burning building has become a REALLY BIG DEAL beyond just being a cool back drop and you narrate, "And that's when the floor gives out..." Then you could roll demonic influence in.

    I still can't think of a reason you would ever roll it in favor of a player though.
