\n Hello,

I had some money to burn so I started crawling through IPR and LuLu looking for small press/indie RPG publishers and their games to buy*, and I noticed for the first time that with few exceptions, almost all small press RPGs concern fantasy or the supernatural. It's the rare ones that deal with science fiction. Note: I'm excluding the plethora of Super Hero RPGs (because I don't rightly know where to classify those).

So the question becomes, is this simply a matter of taste, of coincidence, or is it something inherent in the nature of small press that sci-fi is excluded? Is magic easier essentially easier to handwave, because it's 'make believe' and we can all pretend? Whereas, science is harder to do because it requires plausible explanation, and we all don't have degrees in quantum physics (or whatever) or the space involved in a book to explain?

Just pondering.

    \n Brent,

    To answer the question completely literally, neither fantasy nor sci-fi are inherent to small press. That is, frankly, a silly way to state the question.

    As far as a trend goes, there is a trend among all role-playing games, in general, towards fantasy. There's nothing inherent to "small press" that would require it either to follow that trend or to buck that trend. So if there is a trend towards fantasy titles, there would be no reason to assume it has anything to do one way or another with their "small press"-ness. Occam's Razor says you should attribute this to their "role-playing game"-ness.

    Also, as pointed out by others, there are a pile of small press science fiction games, so this provides negative evidence for your claim that small press somehow excludes sci-fi.

    So basically... we can talk about why role-playing games in general might favor fantasy, but a discussion about about it as a phenomenon unique to "small press" doesn't really have any legs to stand on.

    You follow?

    \n Sometimes, when I hear the objection that -this- or -that- movie/book/game/whatever isn't real science fiction;
    \nbut rather space opera/ or science fantasy/ or whatever... I disagree.
    \n
    \nFor one thing, SF purists often seem to want their SF to explicitly state its thesis, and address that topic in a no-nonsense way.
    \nSuch as explicitly presenting 3 laws of robotics up front, and then offering concise vignettes which each highlight a specific implication of the robotics speculation.
    \n
    \nHowever, such a definition denies the genre access to the full variety of powerful storytelling techniques available.
    \nSuch as simply presenting droids as a fact of life, with certain coherent parameters to their behavior and status depicted, without making the story
    \nmainly -about- the fact that Holy Crap What If Droids Existed!?!
    \n
