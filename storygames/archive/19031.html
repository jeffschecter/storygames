\n Some friends asked me to have them try Cthulhu Dark, and I should be the GM.
In the past few years I have literally become "allergic to heavy prep".
(and my own idea of what "heavy prep" actually is keeps getting closer and closer to "anything more than zero" :P )
I've read around that it is possible to play CD in a GM-Less or at least Prep-Less way and I was wondering how exactly to do it.

I've read Stealing Cthulhu (amazing work) but if feels very much like an incredibly interesting lecture to inspire the GM in prepping a coll/original/lovecraftian scenario using his own devices; there are no ready-to-use procedures, or at least, they have to be extrapolated from the broather lecture.

Then I found this unfinished document ... are there any updates?
Anyway, it may be a solution to my problem, or it may not be.
Reading THIS thread I see a nice example of scenario... but it looks like exactly the kind of prep work I don't want to do!
I mean... the author came up with lots of background stuff and also expanded on the basic advice of the original booklet, for example devising a list of targeted questions.

Is such prep necessary?
I can't help but feel that if I "just improvise" with nothing underneat the result will be both tiring and/or lame
Any suggestions or first hand experiences?

    \n If you've thought of some kind of creepy situation, then sharing some of this with the players before character generation often generates interesting situation for me. I started one game in which the PCs had all had some kind of scary dream set in London. They didn't have the same dreams, but I tied them together into a coherent threat and we started from there. Players can do a lot of the creative heavy lifting for you, the essential I think, with Mythos games, is to preserve some kind of mystery.

    \n Hasimir,

    I would do this.

    1. Read an HP Lovecraft story. A short one.
    2. Change the setting.
    3. Maybe change the creature.
    4. Run that.

    This is potent stuff: it's amazing how far you can get by transplanting, say, The Colour Out Of Space to a seaside town.

    Alongside that, I'd do this:

    1. Start with them arriving somewhere weird. Let them wander around for a bit.
    2. Scatter some weird things around.
    3. Give them somewhere to explore, where things are weirder.
    5. Let them descend into some underground place, where things are really weird.
    6. Let them encounter the creature and run.

    I think that this is the best thing I have written on scenario preparation.
