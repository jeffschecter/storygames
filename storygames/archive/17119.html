    \n Sharing more cool Monsterhearts stuff - in my games, especially one-offs, I've found that players tend to focus on making moves and forget to spend their strings or take advantage of conditions. So I put together a one-page 'mechanics summary' that tries to demonstrate the different mechanical ways of achieving a particular goal (so, trying to influence a PC you can make one of a couple of moves or you can spend a string in different ways).

    It's not as comprehensive as the MH player summary (it skips over experience and gangs and other things I didn't think I'd need for a one-off) but I hope it provokes players to make a greater use of the game mechanics.

    I'm MCing Monsterhearts at the Concrete Cow games day - most likely for first-time MH players - and wanted to add to the game's accessibility and 'on-table' visual signature. I'm also using cards, but I'm using them for 'portraits' of the PCs (the players can either pick from a bunch of photos or draw their own). I'm hoping that the first-timers will latch onto a portrait and that will help guide them as to their skin choice and character decisions. Once they pick one, they'll have it in front of them and stick the coloured tags on it to denote strings (again, to remind people to use them) - with each PC having their own 'string colour'. Not final on how to treat conditions yet. As they're written on the player's own playbook, other players don't generally see them to use them. Writing them on sticky notes and adding them to the portraits would be more consistent, but I quite like the idea of putting them on larger stickers and have the players stick them on themselves.

    I also found that first-time players don't use Dark very much, so I'm bringing colour coded dice to roll for specific attributes (red for hot, blue for cold, yellow for volatile, black for dark). Again, I'm hoping the fact that there's a pair of black dice on the table that no one's rolled yet will encourage someone to gaze into the abyss.

    Here's what I'm taking so far. I'll need to add the lacrosse pitch and squads for the big game.

    Again, I'm hoping the fact that there's a pair of black dice on the table that no one's rolled yet will encourage someone to gaze into the abyss.
    \nLet's talk about that for a second!

    I think that of all the basic moves in the game, "gaze into the abyss" is the one that most demands some gentle nudging - the same is true of "open your brain" in AW to a lesser extent. It takes one or two demonstrations before people see the abyss-gazing possibilities ripe in almost every scene. Nudge them toward the possibility of "gazing," and it may snowball into one of the most reached-for moves by session's end.

    It's as simple as: Becky's got a hot date with Lucia. She's getting ready for the date. You nudge by saying, "So, Becky... are you a decisive person? You've got a critical choice to make right now. Your wardrobe holds the key to Lucia's heart, and the validation that comes with it, but it also holds a number of fashion pitfalls that could sever the relationship before it even starts. How are you narrowing down your options? What kind of outfit are you thinking about wearing?" If Becky's player responds with a cool and collected decision, then c'est la vie. But if Becky's player responds with a wellspring of Becky's anxieties and teen-life-problems, you can just nod sympathetically and say, "It sounds like you're gazing into the abyss about this, aren't you?"

    Once people see the move in action, once it's been demonstrated in play a couple times, I think people are much quicker to seek it out in scenes.

    This is in support of the "Make the PCs feel unaccepted" Agenda. It puts into action some Principles and Hard Moves: Blanket the world in darkness; Accept people, but only conditionally; Leap to the worst possible conclusion; Herald the abyss.

    \n We've played with a few variations of a dominion-style RPG (unrelated to Monsterhearts). It makes for a wonderful creative exercise in reincorporation and constrained options. At times it's not fiction first but creates lots of inspiration for fictional events.

    My favorite part of our experiments is we printed all the cards on index cards so that we could also include blank index cards to write on. Instead of taking Harm, you would take the equivalent of dominion's curse cards. Your deck would get filled with wounds that you would write down on a blank card, place in your deck, and reincorporate later. It felt great in the sense that wounds would randomly pop back up to hinder you. Very satisfying.

    We also incorporated elements from Penny Arcade's Gamers vs. Evil where you bosses were replaced with goals.

    \n When we experimented with this I was GMing and I would create random cards on the fly that players could buy into.

    It was like Lore Sheets from Weapons of the Gods.

    I could seed the table with ideas and then let players decide to buy whatever they were interested in. We even stole Fiasco's Dark and Light dice end game mechanic with Dark and Light Victory Points. I would seed cards and put a number of Colored Victory Points on the cards to entice people to buy and reincorporate them.

    Then at the end of the game, it wasn't about winning like in Dominion but angling for the kind of ending you want.

    To strengthen the appeal of a "good" ending, we made it so that players with the lowest victory points can seed bad endings with terrible fates for you to reincorporate in your epilogue. This gave "losing" players a fun advantage at the end of the game.

    It was ridiculously fun.

    Edit: We also played this GMless. On each turn you can add a card to the table that other players can buy (but not you, we initialed the cards to keep track). We made it so that you also scored victory points for people buying cards you created so you were incentivized to create worthwhile cards.

      \n I decided to print up some of these cards for my own Monsterhearts, and while I haven't had a chance to use them yet, I have a few observations, questions, and a few ideas:

      (This is mostly for @jenskot.)

      1. Errors:

      * The NPC Advantages card is missing some "s"es in the last two lines (they should be in the third person instead of in the second person, in other words - e.g. "leaves" instead of "leave"). Not a very serious error.

      * The "lash out physically" card has a more serious error. Under the 7-9 options, "if they want to" is placed on the wrong line, making it look like it's your opponent's choice whether you turn into your Darkest Self or not. This needs to be fixed!

      2. Wording

      All the basic moves are reworded, with the original title of the move at the top of the card.

      They all make it sound like the outcome of the move is a foregone conclusion as a result. Why did you choose to do it this way?

      For example:

      Original version: "When you lash out physically at someone..."
      New version: "When you physically harm someone..."

      The second way is a little odd, because, after all, you could roll a miss and the move could have a different outcome (which does not involve you hurting anyone physically). This is case with all the basic moves.

      I decided to print them a little differently, too. Details in the next post!

      \n The way things are set up now, you get 20 different cards (which is a lot of material, and, as some people mentioned, too much to hold in one hand).

      I decided to make my own set of cards, by printing them on regular paper, gluing two cards back-to-back, and laminating them. Doing two cards back-to-back makes it harder to spot something visually (since you can only see one side at a time), but I have a feeling that with a smaller set, if organized properly, you'd learn very quickly which card was for what purpose.

      Here's what I came up with as my personal set:



      As you can see, there are seven different cards in my set. Each has a front and a back. Here's how they work out:

      1. A basic rules card: "Rolling" and "Experience".

      2. A card for things which affect your character: "Harm" and "Conditions".

      These first two are basics, for everyone to reference. The third one is for the MC to hold:

      3. An "MC" card, which is twice as large. One side has all the NPC Advantage/Disadvantage rules. The other has "Hard Moves" on the left, and "NPC Strings" on the right, with a few Principles which might be relevant in play pasted in the blank space.

      I also added "How does that make you feel?" as a second basic question after "What do you do?" to the Moves section.

      This, I feel, is all I need at my fingertips to actually run the game: it's a card-sized "MC screen".

      The other four cards are mainly for the players' use during the game:

      4. "Manipulate an NPC" and "Strings". Need to get someone to do what you want? This is the card for you.

      5. "Lash Out Physically" and "Run Away". This is for all your Volatile needs. Doing something physical? Grab this card.

      The last two are less obvious, perhaps, but I think they still work:

      6. "Turn Someone On" and "Shut Someone Down". These are the two basic moves which allow you to influence another person: pull them towards you or push them away. @Daldno described "shut someone down" as follows in the original text:

      this move is intimately connected with the turn someone on move, thematically. you don\u2019t get to control what turns you on or who you fall in love with. what you do get to control is who you drive off,and how you keep people at arm\u2019s length distance. this move will often set up future conflicts for you.

      7. "Hold Steady" and "Gaze Into the Abyss". These are the two basic moves which allow you influence over your own self.


      The connection of the last two cards is perhaps the weakest, but I feel like it would take all of 20 minutes for everyone to get used to it and reach for the correct card when necessary. One is targeted at other people "someone...", the other is targeted at your own self.

      There are only three 'basic move' cards, so you won't be looking forever even if you do get it wrong.
