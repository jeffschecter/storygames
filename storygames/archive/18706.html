\n Okay Paul, since you DID invite me to your g+ community for this game, I can only assume you care what I think. So even though I was reluctant to throw my bullshit around, here it is anyway:

I've been checking in with Undying now and then since the beginning, and it looks sharp -- clever, focused design, innovative use of the AW base to create something really new, blah blah blah deserving of praise etc. But then I see this rule:

When you wake for the evening, spend 1 Blood.

And I'm like, "fuck this game." I can't help it!

Because I hate this rule. I hate the shit out of this rule. Maybe I don't hate it more than any other rule ever, but if we exclude the rules that I would never even consider using in play and would make me leave a game if someone else did, it's probably pretty close. Yes, the "fix" in Undying is better than the one in Vampire. I still have issues.

I used to run Vampire a whole lot, many years ago, so I have a lot of experience telling people "another day is over, you wake up, lose a blood point." And what do they do? They respond by pixelbitching. I guess because there's a resource loss there, but possibly only because I describe time in terms of days, they always have to try and cram as much game-important activity into each and every night. If you try to tell them a week passes, it's like you are saing the apocalypse has arrived. Maye they were supposed to be playing ancient, immortal vampires, replete with cunning patience, but they always ended up playing undead speed freaks.

Because this rule isn't just about blood, it's also about time: It demarcates days as the basic measurement of time in the game. This is appropriate for vampires, sure, but rather superficially and only during times of extreme action or danger. In fact, I would say it is far more appropriate as a measurement of time for the victims of vampire attacks than it is for the vampires themselves. If these creatures are supposed to be ancient, then they're going to have to find routines that will support them over the course of years and decades, where they can wait for annoyances, threats, and enemies to grow old and die. Or where they can wait for advantages to grow over the course of time. None of this kind of behaviour is encouraged by measuring time in days, regardless of the vampire's nocturnal lifestyle. Instead it makes you want to get the most out of your blood point loss, because time is always your enemy. And why should time be your enemy? Are you not immortal?

Finally, there's another issue. One blood per day is a thing in the setting. It's not just an abstract game mechanic that works at the speed of plot, it's an empirical fact that vampires lose the equivalent of 36.5 people worth of blood every year just by existing.* Aside from custom moves, that doesn't leave a lot of room to customize the fiction of each individual vampire's bloodlust. It's hard to turn that measurement of blood loss per day into an emotional state. It makes it easier to consider it merely a strategic concern and nothing more.

* Fun fact quiz: If vampires existed in the US in the same numbers White Wolf assumes, how many people would they have to kill every year to exceed the actual homicide rate? For 1993, it's just 9 people each. Now try to imagine vampires living in non-homicidal countries!

Right, now the "fix." Back in the day, the Storyteller's Handbook introduced a rule for time passing: when we come back to you, roll a d10 for how many blood points you have! So basically, you have at least a 10 per cent chance of automatically murdering the shit out of whomever the Storyteller frames you into a scene with. The thing about BOTH versions, the d10 and the start-at-full, is that after you've been working towards your goals and managing your resources, this rule comes along and arbitrarily resets things. So even though you are back at full, which is good, if you play out a hunting scene to get more blood and you ended up making certain sacrifices or causing a certain amount of trouble, and then th GM calls for time passing, the gains you made, in blood, become meaningless. It's like, why did you end up playing that scene in the first place? And I don't mean to imply any maliciousness on the GM's part here -- the order of scenes and events can make sense to everybody, and then still leave the player feeling a bit cheated. They worked for something and then everybody else gets it for free, and they could have too.

On the other hand, I CAN see it encouraging people to go all-in during what is obviously a climax, spending most of their blood pretty secure in knowing that time will pass right after this event goes down BUT (and maybe it's just me) that also feels a little to desperate a ploy for ancient, immortal vampires to be throwing down on a regular basis. At least it DOES encourage players to lobby for time passing, so they can pass GO and collect their $200, so it's got that going for it over Vampire's awful d10.

Now, just as a comparison point, if I mentally re-skin Undying into a game about the violent upward social mobility of cannibal gang-lords in a Necromunda-style hive city, the rules as-is actually make a lot of sense. A LOT of sense. Because it's fast-paced, constantly violent, every day matters, you're preying on the weak to increase your social status and influence. You could also swap out the danger of sunlight for something else, depending on the world you want to portray. Maybe if the gang-lords don't meet manufacturing quotas, the Imperial overlords flush the whole sector and repopulate it with new convict-workers. Also, with this setting, I wouldn't be asking the question: "If it's so easy to get killed in this game, why is it called Undying? Is it supposed to be ironic?"

So yeah, anyway, those are the various problems I had (and still have) with using that rule in play. It really did annoy the shit out of me after I figured out how it was influencing the games I ran. I can probably think of a few alternatives, but it really depends on your goals for the game, and this could very well just be my problem to get over.

    (This goes back to Johnstone\u2019s post) There are two time scales in Undying: tactical and strategic \u2013 I\u2019m borrowing some Regiment syntax here because that\u2019s how I think. The tactical time scale is measured in nights (or, as in most of my experiences \u2013 a night). The strategic time scale is measured in weeks, months, years, decades, or centuries.

    In the tactical situation, a vampire\u2019s immortality is called into question and we care about each and every choice made and the tally of blood in and blood out. More importantly, we care about what shortcuts a vampire takes to get through that night. That\u2019s the blood-humanity cycle. The tactical scale is where plots are acted on and, win or lose, the status quo changes. This is where a character\u2019s immortality is not so certain and we see if they survive to see another moonrise.

    The strategic scale is addressed by the passage of time move, where the plots surface and fester and the details of night-to-night blood maintenance are not relevant. We know vampires are immortal and that they can establish long term routines to preserve that immortality, so there\u2019s no need to micromanage. The GM has a part of this move and so do the players.

    \n Yeah, your strategic and tactical split is pretty much how I see the two competing vampire tropes, too: the vampire as ravenous beast, addict, or parasite vs. the vampire as immortal, shadowy manipulator. You can go back and forth between them, but you can't have both at the same time. Howard Hughes the junkie isn't the same as the dudes in Trainspotting, for instance. He has a system set up to get his fix, so he can focus on strategy instead of tactics, and his decline is much more gradual than your average street addict, even if he gets to the same place at the end.

    But Vampire's switch between the two was pretty much the d10. It was a crap shoot, and players never had much incentive to push for it. Maybe there were more rules later on, I had a bunch of books but nowhere near all, even though I played it for years on end. If there actually WAS a system for setting up a power base, security, and a routine so that the blood-loss rules no longer applied and you could get some long-term goals accomplished in Vampire, I'd have been a lot happier running it. And if the tactical aspect of play was clearly defined, both situationally (say, when war breaks out, either because you begin hunting another vampire or they come after you; or when you lose control of all your assets, servants, routines and whatnot and you need to forcibly carve new ones out of the mortal world before opportunistic vampires take you out) and fictionally (as a heightened state of awareness and/or the hunting instinct, a "blood in the water brings on the frenzy" type thing), then I might not even dislike that blood points are lost per day.

    But the Undying text kinda gives strategy the brush-off. It says it's there for storytelling purposes, to put distance between the action scenes basically. It's a bit like window dressing that allows us to say these characters are immortal vampires, instead of jacked-up hive gangers (and I'm not dissing that concept either, it would be pretty rad). Part of the fun of a political vampire game for me would be setting up plans and pawns during those stretches, actually building things, so I could bring them to bear in tactical engagements. Perhaps I'm just reading the text uncharitably and your intention is that I should play it the way I would play it. I do feel the game text isn't encouraging me, though. And maybe that's the way you want it! It IS wrong of me to complain about which aspects of the vampire mythos you chose to focus the game on.

    However, I think chalking it up to vampires as make-believe is an easy out. On the one hand, sure, we want to play vampires-as-dramatic-story and not vampires-as-literal-disease (or any other exercise in masturbatory verisimilitude). But on the other hand, the very nature of rpgs involves creating fictional structures and then imagining the implications. Using a measurement of time as it passes for the characters establishes something in the fiction, at the level of things that we are supposed to be imagining as "true." White Wolf literally asked their customers to imagine a world where America is home to 3,000 vampires that each require the equivalent of one person's blood every 10 days just in order to be awake, with the only way out being torpor. Of course the implications of this are idiotic, and yet this was still the fiction they presented and we were expected to engage with. Even if we decide that vampires ignore science and whatnot, that just ignores the why, not the what. If blood loss is measured in character-time, it's kind of hard to just ignore that fact when I'm taking my character's perspective. At least it is for me, anyway -- I'm not thinking "okay I have this lust for blood, how do I deal with it?" I'm always thinking "okay, I lose 1 blood per day, how do I deal with that?"

    So yeah, it's not the fact that it's days that I hate, either. If there's a difference between strategic and tactical in the fiction, with regular blood loss being a feature of the tactical, then days is the logical unit to choose. That's why it's days in Vampire. If that's not the angle you want to take, if it's more about the vampire-as-story-arc (which is the impression I get from the text), then I think you should swap out blood-loss-per-day for something else that uses a game measurement (scenes, sessions, certain actions by characters) instead. Also, it kind of feels like getting off on the wrong foot if as soon as we sit down to play, I'm saying "we're going to take this rule out," as opposed to: "there's a rule for losing blood points to the ravenous hunger inside in here, but we can define what that actually is for our campaign," you know? I hope all that makes my issue pretty clear and my other baggage isn't getting in the way of my point.

    As for suggesting an alternate measurement to use, I wish I had a perfect fix, but I don't think I do. I would assume that 1 per session would make it almost meaningless as a loss. But then, 1 per scene seems like a pretty damn monstrous hunger. Also discourages you from being loose with your scene-framing. So I'm curious to see what John was thinking.

    \n@Clinton, that captures my experience with the mechanic in play.

    @wyrmwood, yes the core theme for undying is vampires are predators.

    @Johnstone, I'm going to try to sum up what I think you're saying into a couple bite sized pieces and try to answer them from my point of view. Let me know if I'm still not hearing you correctly.

    Point 1 - The nightly blood thing, if extrapolated across the fictional vampire population of an expected size, would result in an unrealistic number of human deaths and that break with the fiction where I say "Everything's cool don't worry about it" is a big turn off.

    Point 2 - The nightly blood thing, emphasizes tactical play and forces a strategic-minded character to break from their careful plotting to join the fray, when they may not actually do so.

    Point 3 - The strategic play element of Undying, as written, is primarily an interlude between tactical events.

    To the first point, I view the blood thing as a fictional pacing mechanism and a manifestation of hunger in mechanical terms. It's not an attempt, as @Paul_T put it, to simulate the vampire parasite. As you point out, scene-by-scene is too much to be workable and week-by-week is too small to be meaningful. To not have a spend blood to exist move at all would mean you only need blood to fuel your superpowers, and that seems like a bad message to me. Maybe there's some clever way to enforce the hunger principle with a different mechanization? I'll have a solid four hours on an airplane today, so I'll give this some more thought.

    To the second and third points, Undying is about the upset of things. To clarify, in Undying, tactical play is not about the development of long-term strategy, it's about resolving a breakdown of the status quo (i.e. the fulfillment of a stratagem). Maybe that's a fundamental difference in the objectives of tactical play between Undying and Vampire: where, in Vampire, tactical play is the only framework for developing a long term strategy. In Undying, the tactical phase is about the resolution and the strategic phase (passage of time move) is about the setup of future conflict.

    As you point out, there's not a lot of depth to the passage of time move (strategic phase). The GM makes some new hard moves and each character gets to choose a number of fictional "advances" for their schemes. It is my design intention to quickly resolve the scheming phase to return to the action phase. That said, there's no count-down clock, so folks can take as much time as they want going through it. However, the idea is to focus the play experience on doing rather than preparations for doing.

    If you are keen to play Undying and you want more of the scheming, a thing you might do is daisy-chain passage of time moves end to end. The GM decides what the time jump is and you can play it on the weeks or months scale if you want to. You can keep making passage of time moves back-to-back until something comes up that demands resolution. If that doesn't cut it, then what you want may be beyond what Undying is meant to deliver.

    \n Side note from a person who is still dying (har har) to run/play this game: I've been wondering about how that move would work since I first read the rules, so this conversation is super interesting to me.

    I was a little worried about this leading to every session starting with my players going in search of blood, so I thought up a sort of fiction-based contingency plan in case that ever became an issue: Predators can get up to 1 blood a day from refrigerated blood (and no more, no matter how much of it they drink), so long as that blood is no more than 3 days old. It's just enough to offset concerns about the skyrocketing murder rate, and it gives some obvious starting dependencies with NPCs (whoever controls the blood bank) right off the bat. Of course, any setting could also have "blood bars" or "prey brothels" with willing victims, charging Debt to enter, so there are other ways to deal with this in the fiction, too.

    To be honest, though, I kind of like that the game is vague/bare-bones enough that I felt encouraged to come up with ways to explain in the setting ways to address this hard-coded dependency. I saw it like the note in Apocalypse World that the players lose 1 Barter a month for basic material needs (and I could see adding just a line \u2013 not to the moves, but to the GM's section \u2013\xa0along the lines of, "Do members of the community have any standing arrangements to offset nightly blood loss?"). It's a mechanic that encourages PCs to keep hustling, and fictional explanations of it can really foreground the importance of the community in predator existence. But again, I've yet to play Undying, so just take all of this as an aside from someone who thinks about game rules maybe a little too much.

    \n Some of the comments about VtM are a bit dubious.

    1) Vamp feeding doesn't have to kill. It is not a rules requirement that a vamp ever kill anyone.
    2) You can buy a Herd stat that keeps a pool of available victims handy
    3) There was a specific mechanic for automating hunts

    The fact that this rule exists is what makes vamps social predators. Without it, much of the significance of being a vamp is lost. Vamps can't drop out and move to a cabin in the hills to live out their days, it's just not an option for them. This is the reason they need to control humans, which is the reason they have ghouls and whatnot, and why they can't ignore the human world. That's why it is also important for them to have social powers to control humans, not just physical powers with which to fight each other.

    I always disliked the whole bar-serving-vitae thing, as it IMO totally undermined the point of the setting. Further, the vamp Prince had few enough duties as it is, keeping tabs on feeding practices and the mortality rate is the one "public service" they provide to justify their position. Therefore, I think this rule in VtM filled a vital function in keeping vamps grounded, and was a big part of what made it different from being a monchrome Supers game.

    I can't speak for Undying, but in VtM, if you're having trouble topping up, and leaving a trail of bodies everywhere you go, then you're doing it wrong and should get your act together before the Prince is compelled to take matters in hand.

Your high-Status, genius IQ vampire *should* have some kind of reliable source of blood (whether it's a private brothel or a friend at the blood bank), true. Why don't we see how *you* come up with that for yourself, play it out: use it as a story element which can then be bartered, sold, or threatened by unpleasant circumstances.

    Not a rhetorical question: I can imagine lots of reasons why that *wouldn't* be more interesting. It's just my default approach, because it sounds like it would give us some good material for play, as opposed to just seeing it as a "level up" where you don't have to worry about resources anymore. (For instance, if you have a slave you feed on every few days, what happens if someone else bends this slave to their will? And so on.)
