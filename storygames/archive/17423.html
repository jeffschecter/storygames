    \n Yeah, I play all sorts of games, but have leaned more and more towards indie/narrative-heavy/story games in the last couple of years. They have, generally, provided me with a better value for my investment, both in terms of needing fewer books, less time, and getting more varied replay value.
    The way my experience has run, I get only one kind of experience from playing D&D, sometimes a little bit more variation if run really well.
    If I play Fiasco, I get a different kind of experience, depending on the playset.
    If I play...say...Until We Sink...I get a different kind of experience, depending on who is playing.
    If I play Smallville, I get a different kind of experience as my character and his/her relationships change.

    I'll still go to "traditional" games sometimes, but less and less frequently.

    \n I don't play "story games" but I do frequent a web forum of that name!

    I play indie tabletop RPGs (and things that may or may not be indie, tabletop, or RPGs) because I believe in the medium of "social experience design" (if I had to give it a name) and what it's capable of producing, I like being creative in a group of awesome people, I like producing and consuming beautiful objects and ideas, and I feel like life is too short and limited to just live one life: better instead to lead thousands of different lives, at least in your imagination, at least for a few fleeting moments, so you can have experiences that feel broader, more varied, and maybe more dramatic than your own. Plus, roleplaying (and things that may or may not be roleplaying) is a crucible upon which you can attempt to turn yourself into a better person. Traditionally-published RPGs can do this too, but often lack the individual flavor, courage, ambition, and thoughtfulness of indie RPGs (for institutional and economic reasons, mostly), so I play them much less often these days.

    \n Sharing stories is one of the best ways I know to build community and strengthen relationships. It makes me feel like I'm not alone. Story games are fun, sure, but they are also challenging, sad, tragic, and infuriating. But the experience for me is always a chance to share parts of my self that don't usually see the light of day, and an opportunity to be present for others as they do the same.

    I had the pleasure of playing a couple of games with the brilliant Sara Lynn Bowman at PAX this year. Afterwards I invited her to a gathering of Seattle story gamers. "I hope that's not weird" I said, "we don't really know each other."
    "Whatever we totally know each other." she said, referring to the 6+ hours we'd just spent making up stories.
    Thats why I play story games.

    \n Having thought about this a bit more, and taken inspiration from the above, here's why I play storygames:

    Storygames are chance to share something with friends, to create something with friends, to build something with friends. When I say build, I mean more "world" than "story", but it is only through the story that worlds become interesting to me.

    Learning to play storygames better is learning how to be happy with other people, and how to make them happy. This is learning to be a better human being. Designing storygames is a micro-scale version of social architecture design, which is about building a society that's worth living in.

    Because storygames excite me like nothing else, they're an enormous motivation to learn new skills: thinking skills, planning skills, teaching skills, and social skills. By playing storygames, I can learn those things better and have much more fun doing it. That in itself is worth playing them for.
