\n I love Diaspora. It is, however, really hard to find good art that sells what the spacecraft should look like. There's precious little out there on the net where the spaceships aren't made to look like airplanes or sea vessels. I want to show my players that practical, physics-minded space-faring craft can be sexy too.

Well, I had the pleasure last night of talking to two filmmakers at my FLGS. They are developing a sci-fi movie with some heavy attention paid to actual science. Sure they take some liberties for the purposes of entertainment, but they're actually talking to JPL and actual astronauts to get a good idea of how space travel and planetary exploration should look.

As a fund-raising effort for the movie's development, they have produced an illustrated version of their screenplay adorned with their story-board and concept art. The result is practically a technical readout that displays some really cool looking vehicles with Diaspora-like sensibilities.

Their website has some of the illustrations and more information on the book and the potential movie.

http://slingshot2100.com/

    \n The Dream Pod 9 game Jovian Chronicles has a number of ship art books that are low-rules, but big on art and maps and such of various ships made in the hard sci-fi style. Many of them feature "spin-gravity" systems, which are not in strong keeping with the "1G there - 1G back" concept that Diaspora has as it standard, but the designs are very sexy and practical.

    Movies/TV that have some cool, hard-sci spaceships:

    * 2010: The Year We Make Contact (just a few weeks away!)
    * Solaris
    * Red Planet
    * Mission to Mars
    * Babylon 5's human ships were fairly hard-science

    Anime has some hit and miss examples, mostly:
    * Planetes! (See this for inspiration or read the manga) - It follows the adventures of a group of spacers who clean the space junk out of Earth NEO. It also has plot elements regarding mankind's greater destiny in the black. Recommended.
    * Cowboy Beebop - although it has some serious creep of space opera, it has some very neat ship designs in the harder vein
    * The Mighty Space Miners - insanely cool, too short, hard to find.
    * Mobile Suit Gundam has some dated (1980s) ships with a harder feel, but they give way to silly in some places (most;y around the heroes)
    (If Andy, Ewen, etc spot this thread, they may have some more examples)

    \n One of my favorite real science sf settings is Paul Galacci's Albedo Comics. The people are all anthropomorphic animals from a single planet that 'woke up' 400 years earlier as a multi-species society with interplanetary travel and A.I.s, and enough biotech to know they didn't evolve on the planet they found themselves. They eventually develop ftl travel (really the only non-real science part of the setting) and expand outward. Now they are on the verge of a war - the core worlds against a consortium of rabbit-only colonies with other rim colonies in the middle. The discovery of an ancient human spaceship hints at the common origin of the various anthropomorphic races. (Their DNA is all 95% human.)

    True spaceships are up-is-the-direction-of-travel and never land. Zero-g when the engines are turned off. They look like giant tampon applicators.

    Small space stations are zero-g, large ones fractional to 1-g with rotating sections.

    Orbital shuttles and surface-to-surface interplanetary craft are boxy/wedge shaped and have airplane-like layouts with ladders for when the floors and ceiling became walls. In atmosphere the fusion rockets become fusion jets which are ducted for vertical landings.

    Commercial vehicles use fusion rockets thrusting a up to 1g for half the trip, flip around and decelerate for the second half.

    Military vehicles can do 2+ gees if in a hurry, and 5 or more for very short evasive bursts.

    Space weapons were hypervelocity self-guided missiles. ACVs = Autonomous Combat Vehicles. No beam weapons.

    All fusion reactors and rockets used normal liquid hydrogen, so they had really compact proton + proton fusion tech.

    They also had a hand-wavy way of dumping fusion reactor heat with the rocket exhaust, so they didn't need huge radiator fins.

    Interstellar travel was elegant. Thrust in the direction of the target star until you are roughly the distance of Saturn from the sun (closer for smaller stars, farther for larger stars - the determination was how "flat" the local gravity well was) Turn on the jump drive and instantly appear a similar distance from the target star. Flip over and decelerate to the inner system planet you are going to. So 1-2 weeks accelerating out, instantaneous jump, and 1-2 weeks decelerating in. Cool thing is you can always tell where someone in coming from or where they are going.

    They didn't seem to use any terraforming technologies. There just seemed to be lots of habitable worlds out there. Can't remember how far ships could jump safely.

    There were two editions of the original rpg, with a ships supplement for the first one, including modular deck layouts for 'true' spaceships, and cutaways for shuttles. I haven't looked at Sanguine's current Albedo rpg. Art from the comics can be found with Google searches. I'm sure the original comics and collections are on Ebay.

    \n First, that's a lovely render and looks very faithful to the source material!
    \n
    \nDepending on how hard you want your sf, the Leonov has some troubles.
    \n
    \nThe spin habitats are a good idea, but they have way to small a spin radius. They would have to spin very fast and, worse, because the radius is so small, there would be very steep differences in gravity between your chin and your ears. Generally you're looking for a spin radius of a hundred or more meters. This is solved by assuming the picture above is the "under thrust" configuration and that the habitation modules pop loose on cables a few hundred meters long for spin while not under thrust. That seems cooler to me -- because there's prep time (or consequences!) before thrust, there are things to go wrong and game stories to be had.
    \n
    \nThe other thing is that it lacks a heat exchange system, but again that can (and probably needs to) be folded away out of sight while under thrust.
    \n
    \nIt would be cool to see the Leonov rendered for "on station" work, with habitat and heat exchangers full deployed. It would change from a bullet to a spiderweb, suddenly mostly empty space, with the spindle of the hull at the center.
