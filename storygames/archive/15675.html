    \nProblems I've seen:

    1. In a single session, we may not have enough time for the info on our character sheets, like HX with other players, to move from "words on my sheet" to something we're emotionally invested in. I see players writing, "Bob is my best friend" but then we start playing and it's just words. There is no emotional weight to it.

    2. The other issue is there aren't explicit character goals or ways to externalize or communicate motivations. Which isn't a bad thing for regular play, it just makes it harder to hit on what the players actually care about. Of course even if you do communicate these things, you run into problem #1. Going from just words to emotional investment.

    Solutions I've seen:

    1. Meg from Brilliant Gameologists made Highlight Stats part of the fiction. You don't Highlight Stats Player to Player but Character to Character. Sarah highlights Bob's Hot because "last session" (even if we didn't play last session) things were getting hot and heavy between them.

    Idea: what if we used our HX as inspiration for which Stats to highlight from the character's perspective?

    2. My friend Jon noted that the way Apocalypse World builds investment is by giving players lots of cool stuff. Moves, Gear, places, vehicles, responsibilities, and people they know. If you threaten the Gunluggers' gun... emotions are high and drama is in full effect. Jon had all of us, for each of our cool things, choose an NPC and say how they are connected to it. This built immediate buy in into the setting but bogged the game down.

    Idea: what if you take Jon's idea but only do it to your 2 favorite cool things. You still get buy in but the added benefits of making the process faster and having the player communicate which of their cool things they think are the coolest.

    \n Frontload the situation. Have a couple of fronts and threats and more than one countdown. Outline just how unbalanced the staus quo is. Write love letters to the playbooks based on this situation that drive options toward PvP tension and answering stakes questions through play. This I think is what creates the best immedicay and sucks the players in. We want to play this shit right now! Hatchet City Blues is Vx's example of just this.
    \n
    \nhttp://lumpley.com/apocalypse/hatchetcity.pdf
    \n
    \nI also like to have props: Barter currency (bullet casings, condoms, bottletops, whatever), scrapbooks of NPC portraits to choose from, a poorly detailed but evocative geographical map & map making equipment (large sheets of paper, art supplies map pins), post it notes, matchbox cars, minis from various board games, a bowl of various cool d6s. Whatever you can think of to immerse your players.
    \n
    \nI also like to push provacative questions about relationships during chargen to drive the players to outline further details of their connections with other players / NPCs, similar to DW bonds. For each triangle generated I allow them to marrk an experience circle, so that they need less marked stat checks to advance. This advance invariably happens in play and allows them to choose a cool new move.

    \n Keep the setup tight and focused. I put out the playbooks and let them browse for a minute (but not long enough to get too attached).

    Then I say, "Okay. To keep this short and sweet, you're gonna be a tight-knit bunch. Who feels like playing a leader type? Do you want to be a Chopper and her gang, an Operator and his crew, a Hocus and her cult, or a Hardholder and his inner circle? Any of those are fine. We'll play out a serious problem facing the group and see how it turns out."

    I might say a thing or two about the "leader" playbooks, but generally people get what they're about.

    Then, by doing the setup stuff in the leader playbook (cult, gang, gigs, or holding) you end up with an obvious problem (or several). The GM picks one, thinks of a way to frame it in the opening scene, then off you go.

    Conserve NPCs!
    When you're asking questions during chargen, don't ask about each PC's connections seperately, or you'll end up with way too many NPCs to deal with in the one-shot. Instead, ask group-level stuff. "Who do you get your ammo from? Where do you eat? Who makes your clothes?." Just a couple of those are fine. I generally use 3 or 4 key NPCs in a one-shot, and maybe only 1 or 2 of those end up being important to play.

    \n I'm with Harper. It's all about situation, and maximizing what percentage of play is about situations that most of the group can really invest in. This is one of the biggest strengths of your Dogs games, John S.! Make the players care about overlapping stuff, and make the PCs deal with each other.

    Maybe this is AW heresy, but for a one shot I'd really want to either (a) turn the PC group into an adventuring party, or (b) have them all opposed and trying to kill each other.

    "You do your thing over here, I'll do my thing over there," while it has given me great one-shots in other games, seems like a waste of an AW one-shot to me.

    One technique might be to bring a list of Fronts, ask the group to pick whichever one interests them most, and then ask each player to say why this particular scarcity is central to their character.

    \nBEFORE YOU ARRIVE

    Review all of the playbooks. Chances are, 1 or 2 just don't click for you. You can't get your head around them. For me, that's the Driver and the Faceless.

    Print off only the ones you like. Seriously, if you can't get behind the Driver and the Faceless (or, like, whichever ones), it'll be hard to run a kick-ass game for those playbooks on the fly in a con environment. Also print off 3-4 copies of the basic moves reference booklet.

    Sort the playbooks you printed into some rough groups. For example, I'd do: "People Who Run Things" (The Hardholder, The Hocus), "People Who Work Security" (The Gunlugger, The Faceless, The Brainer), "People Who Wheel and Deal" (The Operator, The Maestro'D, The Hoarder, The Skinner), "People Who Cause Problems" (The Battlebabe, The Chopper, The Quarantine).

    Sketch out some possibilities, in case people have blank page syndrome. Write three sentences about a possible city location ("Set in a swamp. Mutant gators. Nuclear plant leak causes high albino rates. Mudfish."), three sentences about a possible situation ("two biker gangs at war; buying allies. One: large-scale meth distributors. Other: slave traffickers. Brainer war?"), three apocalpytica ideas to barf forth ("three-eyed cats. crazy woman rants about The Great Microwave. Black ink rain."). This stuff will probably never come up, but if you ever draw a TOTAL blank, at least you've got a safety net. These things don't need to relate to one another. They're just a small idea bank. Don't spend too long writing this stuff.

    WHEN YOU START

    Ask, "Who runs this place?" Wait until someone elects themself, and then toss that set of playbooks at them.
    Ask, "Who works security?" When someone volunteers, toss that set of playbooks at them.
    Ask, "Who wheels and deals?" Same thing. "Who causes problems?" Same thing.

    Then, read the text on the back cover of Apocalypse World. Introduce yourself. Explain what the game is about. Explain what playbooks are.
    Tell everyone to pick one of the options you gave them, and then read their flavour text aloud.
    Do the character creation thing.

    Ask lots of leading questions while you idly sketch a map. Start by turning to the person who runs stuff, and ask, "Whereabouts is your community located?" Then turn to the security person, and ask something about outside threats. Turn to the wheeler-dealer, and ask what kinds of things people do to make ends meet and earn a little jingle. Turn to the problem causer, and ask who's frustrated with the local leader and what they plan to do to change things. Ask questions about the town, as you sketch a map of it.

    Great. You've got some ideas. You've got a map. Put it down and say, "Here's our map. We're not going to leave this map during this play session, unless it becomes super necessary. This is the world we're going to play in, alright?"

    Continue asking questions. At some point, someone will give an answer that sounds like an opening scene, and say, "Great, let's start there. You say you're having an affair with the bronze-smith's wife? Well, today, where are the two of you meeting?"

    Then just follow your Principles and make Hard Moves.

    \n I actually really dislike the 1st Session guidelines for both campaign play and one-shots. They just don't work for me and, alongside the harm rules, are among the few parts of AW that I think could really use some work. The kinds of setups that Joe and John are talking about work much better for me in practice. I've actually thought about writing up a convention scenario that's a tighter, more focused version of what Vincent was attempting with "Hatchet City," but haven't quite gotten around to it yet.
    \n
    \nIt's important to also have a sense of what you're trying to do with a convention one-shot. For example, at PAX, I ran AW for a table of 7-8 players and there was just no way that everyone was going to be able to be tightly woven into the game. I was just attempting to show people what AW was, not have a session that was as quality as it would have been with 3-4 players. So, I made sure that everyone got to make a couple moves, but the session really focused on the issues of a smaller subset of the players, specifically the guys playing the Operator and the Hardholder, who were catalysts for everybody else doing stuff. The players loved that session and a bunch of them came back and wanted to play again later, so I guess it worked, but you really have to dive right into it.
    \n
    \nMaking a map as the players are describing where they live and what they do is critical, like Joe says. I sketched their hardhold and the nearby airfield where they received goods (which they traded buried ammo and scrap from the abandoned military base they lived in). The great thing about playing an Operator and their crew is that you an start the session with a moonlighting roll, which gets things underway one way or another. If you just had a successful mission, now you did something probably objectionable or have something valuable and maybe somebody else wants it to. If you botch the roll a bit, you're in the middle of it and that's a great way to kick off a session. In fact, for a convention scenario, I'd suggest going with the Operator every time, though some other options work fine too. The Operator is just the easiest.
    \n
    \nThe other thing that works really well in AW is setting up a couple NPCs as being total merciless badasses that want nothing more than to destroy everything the players care about. Just pick a name off the name list and be like: "Tum Tum, shiiiiiiiiiit. That guy will slit your throat just as soon as look as you. He and his gang are a bunch of dead-on cannibals that everybody is terrified of. They mostly hand out over here (draw area on map)." That gives the game a nice vibe, that the world is full of crazy desperate people, and makes the characters feel sympathetic even if they are murderous bastards, because there are total sociopaths out there to provide some contrast. "My job is 'doing murders' but at least I'm not a cannibal chieftain like Tum Tum."

    \n Most of my AW experiences have been more focused, come to think of it, and it's been great. First, I joined a game that was already in progress, though we started as a seperate group playing in the same setting, before the two groups were combined. The MC sketched out the map of the territory and we decided where we were and what we were doing. The second time, I suggested a game set in Near-Earth Orbit, and the players then picked what kind of space station they had and what was going on. Setup still works the same, but -- with a general premise already established -- the MC has the ability to cultivate a mind full of appropriate apocalyptic imagery and evan have a couple of ideas about Front before the first session begins, which lets you jump into things a bit more. I still think you want to start with the characters handling some normal day-to-day activity, but in AW that's awesome and dangerous, right?
    \n
    \nIn general, I think the official MC play materials for handling Fronts are also not super useful and don't help me run the game very much. The countdown clocks are cool, but listing all the characters associated with it and all the other stuff you have to do... just not as evocative and immediately helpful as it could be. Honestly, the first thing I'm going to do before running AW again is come up with some better Front sheets, listing the Front moves, room for custom moves associated with that front, the stats for gangs or important stuff the Front has, and other handy tools. And tying all that more interestingly to the fundamantal scarcity would be cool too, because that always seems to get lost somewhere along the way. Also, a better Homefront sheet would also be great.

    \n It's funny, people talk lots about Operator + Crew being a great one-shot, and I've heard tales of it working really well, but I have tried and failed to make it work for me. Like I said: some playbooks won't click for you, and you should just omit them. I didn't include Operators in one-shots for the first 10 I ran, for exactly that reason. I didn't get how to play opposite an Operator, for some reason.

    Ask questions like crazy, but always remember this miracle phrase: "Yeah, you're almost right about that, except..."

    Like, you say, "So, Chugger, you know those Rat Men that have been raiding from the bluffs? What's their deal?"
    (This is new. You just made up "Rat Men" and "bluffs", as you were idly sketching a map and the players were making characters.)
    Chugger's player responds, "Oh, yeah. We organized an attack party, showed up, and slaughtered them dead."
    (Chugger's player just invented that detail, too. It's a good answer, but it kind of kills your idea. So you decide to tweak it.)
    You say, "Yeah, you're almost right about that, except a few of you got bitten. And they carried the Rat Men infection back with them."
    (Rat Men are infected people, not mutants? It's news to you, and it's news to everyone else. But it creates pregnant drama.)

    \n Sorry for the minor thread necro here, but I've been checking out this (and similar) threads as I look to bring AW (inspired, to be honest, by checking out both Monsterhearts and Monster of the Week) into my con-demoing repertoire for this year. And, y'see, I find myself really wanting to ignore some of the most common advice by giving my players pregens. Everything I've read so far suggests that chargen is one of the most fun bits of the game, and I shouldn't skip it. Here's why I want to ignore that advice:

    1) In the past when demoing BW at cons (and other one-shots) I've moved away from Luke's advice regarding beliefs and gone for ensuring my pregens all have intersecting, conflict-rich beliefs and instincts. Why? Well, I tried it the other way, and it both took too long for some players to come up with them, and some came up with pretty lame ones despite my influence and encouragement. We also kinda went at the speed of the slowest player, and it led to some players getting gazumped by others from cool beliefs (man, he wants to burn the heretics? I wish I'd played the priest!). On the other hand, when I made an overly-complex matrix of intersecting and contradictory beliefs, players still got to choose which ones to focus on, and nobody ended up with weak reasons for being in the thick of the action. (Like, you want to kill X and/or fuck Y and/or steal Z - which of these agenda do you want to pursue?)

    2) I'm looking at 3-hour slots and don't want to run out of time - I want to create something meaningful and meaty in that time. In my own con-GMing, I tend to think of the 3 most important things as pace, pace, and pace, so I don't want anything in there that might rely on us moving at the speed of the slowest player - if you get my drift?

    I'm thinking of providing pregens but leaving some phases of the setup in as a group exercise - either the Hx relationships ('cause I think the players will dig them, and they have their own pacing mechanic), or the initial setup (because the Hx thing might be confusing!), or some love-letter-style initial move to determine the status within the setup.

    Is this heresy? Is it doomed to failure? Am I just a con-GM control freak?

    \n My advice, try it before you hack it.
    In some other discussion John Harper said this cool thing about trying to meet a game at its own level. I think that is a great approach, esp for something like apocalypse world which really asks for a different set of GMing principals then other games you may have played.

    I worked really hard to MC this game. I'm still working on it. But I can say that it really does work, every time. Even in a 3 hour game slot. You really gotta let go and trust your players to enjoy this game.

    The thing is, making characters and explaining moves takes half an hour once you got it down, and you're still playing the game. Asking questions, drawing stuff on the map, setting up PC NPC triangles, finding out those little details that start to form the world.

    "You're wearing a Suit of Armor? like from the middle ages?"
    "No like from a gaming store."
    "awesome."

    If you want to write a scenario out, pre-gen the characters, be my guest. Me, I don't want to miss out on the surprise.

    \n I agree with Ross and Jonathan about the fun of no-prep one-shots. But just to be clear, the MC should come to the game with a strong creative vision, preferences, and goals. You're not a blank slate or merely a mirror to hold up to the players. Cultivate that head full of apocalyptic imagery. Dream up a bunch of cool NPCs, problems, situations, and slot them into your MC bandolier, ready to be deployed during setup and as you go. Definitely ask questions and disclaim responsibility sometimes, but don't be afraid to lay down the law and have a strong hand, too.

    That said, I would never do pregens for AW. Charater creation has lots of bits of magic that orient the players toward successful play.
