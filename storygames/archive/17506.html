\n Are we doing this wrong? We played DW for the first time last night. Granted, we suspect we had a few too many monsters in the groups we fought (six in each), and perhaps they ought not to have been armored, but Hack'n'Slash seemed amazingly ineffective.

Defy Danger emerged later in the evening as a more general purpose move than we originally took it for. It makes me think of World of Dungeons having one move. It seems the de facto way of changing the tactical situation in some meaningful way (e.g. our bard throwing a tapestry over top of some pesky gargoyles, which allowed us to stomp them with relative impunity).

It was strange watching the bard save the day after the fighter has spent so many rounds knocking tiny stone chips off the gargoyles. Going toe-to-toe with someone seems like the peak escalation of a conflict, so it was strange to have it feel grindy.

What's the secret? Do you do damage plus a whole bunch of knock-on narrative effects? (Like, 3 hit points' damage scares off most small monsters, or has them crashing into one another, taking additional, arbitrary damage?)

    \n Grindy is not what we're aiming for or expecting! Which isn't to say it didn't happen, just that that's certainly not what we'd expect based on our play.

    You mention gargoyles which are, yeah, pretty resilient things. That two armor is tough, but they're made of stone! Even then, I wouldn't expect it to take too long to deal with them. At only 3 HP under that 2 armor they're still definitely within one-hit range of everybody except the wizard (but the wizard can magic missile). Two attacks by even the cleric is likely to kill a gargoyle.

    I'm interested in what the monsters were doing if the fight was getting grindy. Gargoyles are d6 damage I think? Even though that's on the low end they're nothing to scoff at. On top of that their moves give them quite a few ways to just deal damage to the players. They can blend into stone and then take them by surprise, maybe, or take to the air and knock down stalagmites.

    Not every monster will go down easily, but in that case the monster is usually dangerous enough that it's the players who have to break off the fight (or try to).

    Also, re: defy danger\u2014it is a catch-all, but that doesn't mean that it always has to be rolled. Your example with the tapestry, for example, might or might not be. If the bard is, say, on the balcony above and is dropping it down on the gargoyle it's probably not defying danger. On the other hand, if the gargoyle is bearing down on them and the bard is trying to get the timing just right, yeah, danger is being defied, roll!

    In general, that kind of creative thinking\u2014using the environment to avoid making rolls, or deal damage directly, or whatever\u2014is exactly what should happen. Throwing a tapestry over someone so they can't defend themselves and the fighter gets to just deal damage is an excellent tactic!

    \n A huge part of the game is how different groups apply the rules. Some GMs might use Hack & Slash as a last resort in battle, others make it the go-to move. Depending on how you place and use your monsters within the game fiction, a battles can be impossibly easy or just impossible. It allows a lot of flexibility and control. Here's an example I've used previously (in my beginner's guide to DW):\n
    Check this out:
    GM:"The kobold tumbles toward you, swinging a chain over his head."
    PC:"I'm gonna lunge at him with my sword."
    GM:"Sounds like a Hack & Slash, go ahead and roll for it."

    as opposed to this:
    GM:"The kobold tumbles toward you, swinging a chain over his head. He's like a whirling dervish, flitting around the battlefield with this rusty makeshift flail."
    PC:"I'm gonna lunge at him with my sword."
    GM:"He's leaping back and forth like crazy, and that chain is whizzing around like a blur, you're gonna have to Defy Danger to get close enough to even hit him. If you succeed, then you can roll for a Hack & Slash."
    \nThat kinda stuff (usually called "fictional positioning") makes all the difference in how powerful the moves are individually. Does the GM ask for Defy Danger to run across the ledge? Or does she only ask when the player is being attacked on that ledge? Can you just use Hack & Slash any time you attack, or does the GM prevent you with some other threat- an enemy's reach, ranged attacks, etc.

    Basically, if it feels grindy then sort of evaluate why that is, and see if there are circumstances and details that you can bring out to change that. You've got a lot of control over how combat flows in your game; there are very few hard rules about what you're allowed to do and how exactly to establish the threat of your monsters.

    I was just watching Snow White and the Huntsman again and that awesome fight with the Bridge Troll, and I couldn't help translating the scene in my head as I watched into the many permutations of the DW moves (other than just 'I hack and slash the troll'). Leading with the narrative and getting all cinematic.





    Heres some of the interpretations I had, none of which seem grindy as a fiction - mechanical translation (albeit of a pre-determined outcome, but still)

    When they arrive on the ancient stone bridge and see the carcasses strewn about, notice the unearthly silence and step cautiously onto the bridge, Snow White says she uses all her senses to discern the reality of the situation and scores a hit. She asks what she should be on the lookout for. The GM is winging it, has an idea, but offers it back to the Huntsman and asks him, 'so you've been through the dark woods before, what do you think?'

    'Troll!!!!!' Is his answer and he draws his axe in peers through the gloom.

    Or maybe the GM shows signs of an approaching threat and narrates the troll's eyes opening behind the characters. What do you do?

    The GM narrates the troll waking up, detaching itself from the bridge and threatening the characters. The Huntsman authors that he leaps at the beastie hack and slashing with his axe. Unfortunately he misses and the GM makes a hard move against the characters, and puts them in a spot, taking a cue from the troll's moves. He bats them off the bridge into the muck below, dealing damage as warranted in the fiction, in this case a D8 of stun damage. He described the troll roaring at them as they stagger to their feet and bearing down on them, intent on grinding their bones. What do you do?

    Snow White (knowing she is overpowered) says she runs away, looking for a place to take cover, maybe that rotten tree over there? Sure says the GM. This could easily snowball into being defying danger, but they turn to the Huntsman and ask what he's doing. He could be defying danger himself or hacking and slashing, but the players says he is running with the princess, guarding her rear as they flee toward the tree. Hmmm, the GM could go with the Huntsman drawing on their bond and aiding her Defy Danger, but instead goes with saying that it sounds like you are standing in defence of her then? Yup, says the Huntsman. I'm defending her! He rolls and scores some hold.

    The GM describes the Troll smashing the tree to bits, following his instinct to smash! the Huntsman knows its hopeless, but he spends his hold to redirect the Trolls attention onto him, telling Snow White to Run! and leaps into the fray with the beast, narrating that he is ducking and weaving between the Beastie's legs as he hacks away. He scores a hit, but the GM makes an attack (monster move) in return, noting that the Troll takes the slashes from the axe, but his monster move undoes the effects of the Huntsman's attack. Flummoxed, the Huntsman runs away to try and get some distance defying the danger of the troll's smashing club like fists. He misses and the GM hurls him away dealing 1d10+3 damage in the process and giving the Huntsman the tag of stunned. The Huntsman spends another point of hold from his defend to halve the damage he suffers from the attack.

    The GM tells Snow white the consequences and asks, as the Troll is looming over the Huntsman, fists raised to smash him to oblivion unless she interferes. What does she do? She describes running forward and screaming at the Troll to STOP!!!!! Is she defying the danger of the troll's instinct to smash with quick thinking? Is she Defending him? There is no 'right' way.

    Noofy, Great post.

    How you choose to approach a scene fictionally has a huge impact on the speed/narrative flow (grindiness?) and difficulty of resolution in a scene.

    In your example you lay out the same scene resolved in different ways that give it a vastly different feel and probably a different mechanical result.

    Sometimes making someone defend can be really tough whilst allowing them to aid instead might make things easy. The opposite may be true for certain characters in certain situations. I guess the follow on question here is:

    "On what basis do you make the decision on how to resolve the scene?" Both of your examples follow the fiction so I'm not sure the answer lies there.

    There are a large number of factors that play into these decisions IMO. Things as boring as "we only have 10 minutes of the session to go so hurry the hell up" or as important as "Joe hasn't had much play tonight, I'll resolve this thing he isn't in quickly to give him some screen time" can mean that you go with the quick option.

    Factors such as the genre itself, (say Cthulu vs Hornblower) would affect a decision both on speed of play and on deadliness. You may want a Cthulu game to be a slow build and super deadly where you may want a Hornblower game to be fast paced and full of people doing foolhardy things that don't get them killed.

    In the AW book, Vincent talks about stretching and contracting the moves. A single roll might cover an entire scene or a single task. The former allows for quick resolution and high stakes (appropriate for a fictional "last throw of the dice") on perhaps a single die roll where the second allows for more chance of delicious 7-9 rolls, more snowballing moves more granular fictional problem solving, question asking, getting into trouble etc.

    Whether you change the fiction by calling for different moves or no moves at all or do it by changing the actual happenings/situation, you are still changing the fiction. Aren't you?

    \n Player HP and monster HP are somewhat analogous. You can see this in how the damage goes across between players and monsters\u2014the damage range exchanged between entities generally falls within the same range. d4 damage is always the bottom end, not-good-in-a-fight damage. d6 damage means the low-end of good\u2014like a goblin, or a thief who's not in their element. d8 is always a skilled combatant, like a ranger or a formian taskmaster. d10 is the high end of mortal, like a fighter or formian queen. d12, which comes up rarely, is pretty much superhuman.

    So the damage going between things is pretty solidly in the same range. The idea is that an HP is an HP, more or less, everywhere.

    That means that a fighter with high constitution really is one of the tougher mortal things to kill, and that goblins really do pretty much splatter. A wizard is actually tougher than many humans, on par with a bandit king.

    For us, this is a sweet spot, especially since HP doesn't increase much. You play people who are at the high-end of human ability in a world where there are things much bigger and more dangerous than humans. Yes, the wizard has as much HP as 4 goblins, but those goblins have numbers. Yes, the fighter has as much HP as some mythical beasts, but that's because the fighter is strong enough to (maybe) face a mythical beast.

    It comes down to how we look at monsters and PCs. Monsters are (as it says in the book) arrows fired and forgotten. They tend towards lower HP because they're numerous and, individually, unimportant (with a few exceptions). The PCs are important but not untouchable. They die, but not at the drop of a hat. They're not arrows, they're more like a good sword\u2014you use it and appreciate it, you even work to make sure you keep it, but when it shatters you just get a new one.

    This is all a bit rambling. If thinking of player damage and monster damage as different things helps you, great. I just wanted to throw out that that may not be necessary. The fact that a goblin dies from a blow that would only badly wound a wizard is what we were aiming for, and it reflects the world we're trying to portray.

      \n I'm a big fan of "conditional armor," where armor can be bypassed when it makes sense. Because the system is so light and has such heavy emphasis on the game fiction, I think it's totally legit to be like "the goblin has climbed up on you and is chewing your face, it's just disgusting and awful, take 3 damage and armor doesn't apply." You know, whatever actually makes sense for the situation at hand. In games where armor is just a static +AC thing, it leads to a disconnect sometimes between what the rules require and what everyone says is going on. And in DW, you can straight ask the player "how in the world does your shield help here?" and the answer may make all the difference.
