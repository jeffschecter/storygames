\n
\n
\n
\n I just realized something. Even all these years later, Mystic China is still one of my all-time favorite RPG books. It's a sourcebook for Palladium's Ninjas & Superspies, and even though I haven't played a Palladium game in years (and am not particularly planning to any time soon), and while I can't vouch for its factual accuracy, it's by Erick Wujcik, and the whole atmosphere of the book is great. It dives into Taoism, demon hunting, Chinese alchemy, and more with gusto. It's like 250 pages of stuff thrown together seemingly all at random, and yet all of it feels like stuff that would be fun to use. And I did use a bunch of stuff from it, albeit mostly to make my Rifts games even more kitchen sink than usual.

Is there a book like that for you? Something you haven't looked at in a while, something you might not play any time soon, that you still love?

    \nI owned the Mega 2, never played it though, it was competing with a campaign of Empire Galactique already running. Berlin XVIII delivered awesome game sessions, and I would play again with pleasure.

    I have squishy feelings about at least two other French games (by Croc) :
    Bitume, the French take on post-apocalyptic Mad Max. The game was full of stereotypes but humor redeemed it all. I get excited just thinking about when our group of "vikings" found their new drakkar in the form of a steam engine in a jungle covered Paris railroad station.

    Bloodlust, a sword and sorcery type of game with a very nicely designed settings where PCs were god-weapon bearer (a bit like Elric in a way). One rule option was to let players take the role of other players weapon. Weapon bearers were threatened to been possessed by their passionate godly counterpart, taking form in mental discussions parallel to the action of the game. I never was able to make it quite work, but I still kind of want to. Too bad, the book is so far away now.

      \n After giving this some serious thought, it's gotta be:

      Warhammer Campaign

      - I have such sweet memories of this book. For a start, there's the gorgeous cover, the special British sarcastic style of the writing, all the details of The Empire that I didn't know what to do with then (and probably wouldn't now either) that nonetheless inspired me immensely, the yellow paper, the fonts, the fact that this was the first published scenario in the CoC-tradition (investigation, cults etc.) that I read, the fantastic full-colour, two-sided map... Warhammer Campaign really made my head spin. Actually, I get a sudden urge to Story Game it up and play it with Solar System or Burning Wheel rules...

      On a close second place is Warhammer Fantasy Roleplay ( 2nd edition I guess it would be).

      /Peter

      \n The book that leaps to mind is Bruce Galloway's oft-maligned Fantasy Wargaming.

      Really, the oft-maligning is pretty well deserved, BUT... The edition I bought as a kid was the hardcover (as opposed to the book club edition). IMO, it wowed me with:

      * One of the most evocative, well-done covers I'd ever seen at that point
      * Beautiful, medieval, wood-cut looking interior art
      * It was lain out like a real book, with yellowed paper and real typesetting and everything (it was the Burning Wheel of its day)
      * About half the book was just essays on gaming and medieval culture
      * Full of "realistic" magic incorporating astronomy and alchemy, etc

      I dunno. At the time, I was pretty impressed with the book, even if the actual game seemed impenetrable. I still keep it on my shelf, sitting proudly next to my RQ2 and AD&D1e books, and will likely never sell it.

      I really should go back and read it some time.
