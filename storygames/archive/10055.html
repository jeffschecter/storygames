\n So I love heist and caper movies, everything from Ocean\u2019s Eleven to Heat and Ronin, and I\u2019ve been designing a game called House of Cards focused on emulating these films. My design goals are to make a no-prep game that can capture the tension, twists, and betrayals of these films in a single session of play (longer than a movie but not by too much).

My most recent playtest of House of Cards at Go Play Northwest started smoothly but exploded spectacularly in the middle as the gears of my mechanics and story structure seized up. So I\u2019m returning to first principles and re-evaluating everything. Hopefully that\u2019s where you come in.

I know I\u2019m not the only one who loves these films, so I ask you: What do you want to see in a game emulating heist and caper movies?

If you\u2019re anything like me, you\u2019ve played these stories using other systems. So what really worked well for you when doing a heist or caper in another game? Conversely, what totally didn\u2019t work or feel right with the system you used?

Extra Credit: What is your favorite heist or caper film that I must see to get this game right? I\u2019ve watched a bunch, but I\u2019m always looking to add to the filmography.

    \n I'd like to see a game that supports multiple-level whiplash cons, like Confidence or Lucky Number Slevin. I've been contemplating hacking Wilderness of Mirrors to make it happen. The idea that scenario creation is part of character creation in such an intimate way seems like it might be important.

    As for gaming: we've used Fiasco, to great effect, to emulate the kind of heist flick where the protagonists don't come out so well. The coolest thing about Fiasco is how the characters emerge from situation creation (again), and are tied to the caper that will go down starting on the very first turn. There are no filler scenes at the beginning, in the "you meet at a tavern" mold. The second coolest thing about Fiasco is that the number of scenes is strictly limited and as that clock ticks down to zero, the players are forced to keep things moving along to their inevitable conclusions - no dilly-dallying, no equivocation, just narrative thrust. I feel like that really is in flavor for the genre and I recommend implementing a countdown in any action-y game. Hell, I'm beginning to think that every game might benefit from a Puppetland-style time control rule.

    \n Yes! We've needed a good heist RPG for ages now.

    A few of the things that delight me in a good heist movie:
    --The eccentric cast of characters with specialized skills and issues
    --The backstory of why this particular place is worth robbing, and the way that the initial information is almost always misleading or incomplete
    --The hidden agendas that come out: this guy wants revenge on his fellow thief, that guy wants to blow up the place they're robbing, the other guy wants to double-cross everyone and keep it for himself
    --the crunchy detail of how the heist goes down -- the feeling of learning a clever way to do something you'll never do yourself, like safecracking or forced entry or surveillance.
    --the place of contingency, the guard forgot his thermos of coffee so he comes back and interrupts the heist in progress and gets shot. Sometimes they're like parables of randomness.
    --I particularly like the non-linear heist movies, where there's sort of an hourglass shape to the story with a lot of attention paid to what happens before and after the heist, and the heist is sort of the narrow part of the story where everything comes together.
    --I also like the drama between mistrustful crooks, paranoid about each other and about pursuit.
    --Inevitably, something goes awry and the story flips, the focus shifts from following the plan to crazy improvisation, trying to survive the treacherous team member or whatever. I think it's really important that a game like this can handle those shifts smoothly.
    --the reluctant heist, or One Last Job. This is a big damn cliche, but it still works well in movies like Sexy Beast.

    Play experiences:
    I played in an Unknown Armies game where the PC's were private detectives paid to pull a heist on a mysterious (presumably occult) millionaire's pad. I liked the way every character had an obsession right on their character sheet, that drove the play really well because they served both as niche protection and as story drivers (my snoopy character, for example, would get everyone in trouble because he'd watch too much). The occult aspect was subtle but very spooky, things like guard dogs with a hive mind. It was one of those games where the characters really clicked with each other and everybody was headed for a bad end, but I don't know how much of that was driven by the system as opposed to table chemistry.

    I'm playing in a WHFRP online game right now which is basically the aftermath of a heist gone bad, it's great fun but it got pretty participationist after a while. The best part of the game was running like hell to stay ahead of the law and stay alive and get paid; it's flagged a bit since then. One thought: heist stories work better if they're kept focused, almost picaresque. Maybe a setup where a favorite character could reappear on a different job, with no attention paid to what the thieves do in their downtime until they get back into prep mode for the next heist. One thing that works well for it is the gritty and unforgiving nature of the setting and the rules, both of which make death and disfigurement a constant possibility. I also like the fact that the game uses task resolution, it really brings out that edgy feeling to roll well but still not get anywhere because you're looking in the wrong place.

    I'd been looking at running a heist game myself using Fate 3.0, but my guys opted for PTA instead.

    Besides movies, I'd like to recommend a couple books:
    Any of the Parker novels by John Westlake. Existentialist noir heist novels with a dead-eyed sociopath for a hero and a gloomy tone that makes the most unlikely plot twists seem utterly plausible. Here's an appreciation of the series.

    Void Moon, by Michael Connelly. A great psychological heist thriller about a young woman who steals too much from a Vegas casino patron. This one has it all, psychopathic pursuers, tradecraft, betrayals, heartfelt motivations, crazy twists of fate. I saw Connelly read last month and asked him why there's no sequel and he said something very story-gameish: "That book is about the biggest choice the protagonist will ever make in her life. I don't want to follow it with just a caper novel."

    \n I've thought about this before, and one thing that seems central is this:

    Planning is a central part of a Heist movie, but pre-action logistics tend to be very boring in story games. I think that this is because, in story games, you are all still figuring out where the story will go, so including the salient hints and development and ignoring all the mundane logistics can be tricky.

    I can think of two ways to handle this off the top of my head.

    1) Flashbacks, flashbacks, flashbacks. Jump right into the action, and then as the destination becomes clear, flashbacks can illuminate the planning and development stage. Of course, they would actually be informing each other. I like this method a lot.

    2) A mechanic that codifies earlier development, hints, and prep in a way that gives an advantage for or mandates reincorporation of those elements. If you go this way, then it should be designed in such a way that reincorporation is almost certain to occur. If the faceman buys a gun in act 1, then it damn well better have been used by act 2... so, no empty uncertainty in the planning narrative: everything that is planned is reincorporated somehow.

    These two things could be built together somehow.

    Cool, btw. I'd play it.
