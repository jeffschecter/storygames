\n After a few stumbles I've managed to come up with what I think is a pretty good plot for an Unknown Armies campaign. Basically, the PCs in a small town stumble upon a cult affiliated with Hastur/The King In Yellow and are forced to try and stop them from spreading their influence. I find the whole Mythos surrounding Hastur and the King really fascinating -- especially Carcosa itself.

My question however is do you guys think this would work better as a one-shot or a series of sessions (3-4 perhaps)? I know it would depend solely on how the players in my campaign handle things but I still don't want to short-change them --- or drag things out for that matter.

    \n Seconding the 3-session advice. I tend to run a lot of "extended one-shots" that way, particularly when we're first trying out a system. I've also grown fond of running "miniseries" campaigns as well, usually between 6 and 10 sessions. Even with a more involved plot, usually by that time they have wrapped up nearly all of the conflicts presented in the original premise, and it makes for a nice compromise between "ongoing campaign" and "satisfying story arcs"

    \n I used to adhere pretty strictly to a formula: 4-8 sessions to unfold each of 4-8 storylines/modules to establish a larger campaign design, followed by 4-8 sessions to resolve that primary campaign...usually with one additional session in between each storyline to resolve a character subplot. That usually put us one third to one half through our character lives, so doing another one to two equal campaigns after would see us either max level or powered beyond further meaningful stories. This worked well in most games where there's usually a pretty clear distinction between early, mid, and end game character development-wise. The result was 6 months to two+ years on any given set of characters.

    However, that was when we were playing 2-4 sessions of 8-12 hours each per week, which is no longer viable on adult schedules. In the past few years I've taken much more to single story paths and quicker advancement that let us fully develop characters within the single storyline rather than breaking it up. It still takes 6 months to a year of playing, but it's only 1/4 to 1/2 the total hours invested, which works better for us now. The one exception is our own D&D version, which was specifically designed to progress through 6 distinct story arcs and phases of character development. These continue to stretch into 1-2 year campaigns almost exclusively.

    We also do occasional few shots (almost never one shots, except at cons and such), but our groups overwhelmingly prefer campaigns to these. Usually it's due to a scheduling conflict that fractures the group, or a wild hair, or a new game to experience and learn, or to switch from serious gaming to sillyness when spring fever strikes.

    So I guess the takeaway from that is that what works for one group doesn't work for another.
