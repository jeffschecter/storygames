Privilege as constraint: The 'Hood, Rookvale, and other people's lives

\n
\n So for the second time in a year, I've seen a game and thought "this looks really really cool and I'd love to play it but I'm never going to play this with my game group".

I play RPGs with white, upper-middle class Engineering students who watch Dr. Who. I'm just not comfortable about playing a game about inner city communities with the people I regularly game with. If they were white, upper-middle class Engineering students who watched "The Wire", it would probably be a different story. But I just don't trust the group to be aware enough of our own privilege to not create situations that will be embarrassing and awkward. I had much the same response to Rookvale and it's approach to gender. It's not a game I want to play with people who are so cisgendered that they've never heard the word "cisgender".

This isn't meant to be an attack on my various gamer friends, who are all wonderful people (even if some of them are pretty sheltered). Nor is it necessarily a call for solutions: I have plenty of other games I will enjoy playing with my friends, and i have ways of exploring similar themes without putting myself and my fellow players in a situation where possible blindness to our own privilege has the potential to create negative gaming experiences. I ran an X-men game that used mutants as a metaphor to explore themes of minority identity, and it was a fun and successful game, but it also contained situations where I was glad for the metaphor because if such and such a scene had been about (for example) actual two-spirit people instead of my made-up mutant analog, I would have wanted to stop and play a different game instead.

    \n I can totally understand the reticence around playing these games with your friends, but I think that's precisely why those are the people you should be playing them with, if you really want to play them: just start by saying something like this:

    "Look, this game covers some difficult subject matter that we aren't intimately familiar and I don't expect us all to get it right first time, so nobody is being judged here and if we make mistakes, we'll just learn from them and move on. I still think we should play it though, as it will expand our range and provide us with some fresh challenges; what do you think?"

    \n Right, arscott, but that leads to only playing games with white, upper-middle class characters.

    I understand where you're coming from. I really do. But I think it's important to get over that attitude and play characters that aren't like yourself.

    This is such a problem, in general. It's the reason people shy away from playing Steal Away Jordan, a game about slaves: because they're afraid of getting it wrong. But they should be playing it and getting it wrong, because the alternative is not playing it.

    Play it with them, really, even if you're afraid they'll get it wrong.

    \n I feel like these games emphasize what I personally want from roleplaying games, a chance to truly roleplay as someone else in a respectful and meaningful way. Escapism is fun and I definitely play all-out popcorn games, but I am much more fascinated with players having to play as a different gender/sexuality/race/socio-economic status and learning how to understand that character. It takes the right group but I think these sorts of games can really shine.

    \n I want to be clear: My friends aren't creating offensive stereotype characters, nor do I expect them to. And certainly the characters they've created don't always reflect the status quo: In my X-men game, some of the deepest characters were female characters played by male players. In the game before that, the youngest character whose important relationships revolved around his biological and adopted children.

    Jason, it more speaks to what you're talking about as gaming as an exploration of other experience vs. gaming as escapist fantasy. I think with my X-men game, those goals weren't mutually exclusive--The game could be an exploration of identity and an escapist fantasy at the same time. Ditto MonsterHearts. The 'Hood, though, is just as far from their fictional interests as it is from their real lives--and I think therefor that the impulse for escapism will transform the game into something that's either awkward or uninteresting in terms of identity exploration.
