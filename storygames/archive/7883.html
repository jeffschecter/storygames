\n A friend of a friend of a friend in the vegan community named Josh Harper, is currently serving a 36 month prison sentence at the Sheridan Federal Detention Center for in his words "the horrible crime of organizing legal protests, delivering lectures at colleges, and giving unapologetic vocal support to the illegal tactics of others."
\n
\nHe is kept under 21 hour a day lockdown but occasionally has the opportunity to blog through his friends.
\n
\nHere is his blog.
\n
\nHis latest blog post, he talks about how he is learning to cope with the pain of his current situation by playing D&D; 4E in prison. Apparently there is a thriving underground role playing scene in the Federal Bureau of Prisons.
\n
\nHe writes: You haven't lived until you've seen a heroin addicted bank robber with a Firearms Enhancement say, "I ask the Dwarvish cleric if he has any potions of vitality for purchase while I discretely flash some platinum pieces."

    \n A difficult question, but I'd say that I represent the vocal majority at least. The Finnish prison system is pretty strongly oriented towards reintegrating prisoners to society, so the normal attitude at least where I've been has been that people need to get a new chance after serving their sentence. Then there are the different moral reasons for people to end up in prison - it's far from obvious that your crime is actually something your community will frown upon; every year Finns go to prison for dodging mandatory military service, for example. Crimes like that and internet piracy are good examples of things that it would be ridiculous to ostracize people for...

    All in all we have a pretty peaceful and dull country, or that's my impression. Not many prisoners in general compared to USA, so perhaps it's just easy for us to handle these issues magnanimously. I imagine that I'd have a different attitude if the big cities were a constant battleground between gangs and so on, like the situation seems to be in the USA. The types of crimes seem somehow less severe, too: Americans are always talking about these serial killers and child molesters, while the sort of criminals I mostly meet are drunk drivers, repeat offence brawlers, wife-beaters and the sort. I don't know if the criminals simply are more crazy in the large urban centers of America or if the media just paints a different picture.

    But that's sort of a side issue, the point I really wanted to make with my observation wasn't to compare societies, but to question the idea criminal exceptionalism, especially the sort that elevates roleplaying into something special in relation to convicts, who are after all people just like everybody here. My dislike for dehumanizing criminals is sort of a side-issue, no reason to let that steal attention. And who knows? American criminals might be just that crazy, I haven't lived there and seen the reality.

    Most people on here know me as an immersion-first minimalist gamer with really off-the-wall DMT-inspired ideas of what gaming should be.

    The people who REALLY know me, know me as a gutterpunk with a record that once made a rookie cop tell me he was impressed.

    Being an anarch doesn't make you a second-class citizen in our silly real world any more than it does in our silly fake ones. To me, people who haven't lived on both sides of the fence are really kidding themselves about their understanding of anything remotely role-oriented. Classes and races are real in prison, real on the street, and glossed over much of everywhere else, on the surface.

    I work in one of the world's largest software hubs. In my department, I have a famous drug dealer on a stay of sentence, a Zimbabwean refugee, a former vagabond turned zine owner, and a sheltered middle-aged mother of two. Of the four, there's a clear line of competency, leniency, mercy, and trust; and the sheltered housewife is easiest the most disposable person in our company, not because of education (she has plenty) or experience (more than all of us combined!), but in her moxie, tenacity, and wisdom.

    Eating from a trashcan teaches you how good food can taste. That's my $.02.
