\n Warning: Ranting, half-formed ideas and shaky metaphors based on semi-educated babble follows. However,perhaps these ideas can be used to illuminate some aspect of publishing.

Games - defined here as "instances of actual play based on a game text"- are spread through play. They are like parasites, gamers are their happy hosts.

Games are made up of gemes. (Yes, it's a word I just made up by smashing together "genes" and "memes"). Gemes are ideas, techniques and processes - things that are used in actual play.

Games survive based on how successful their combination of gemes are. However, they're born through their seeds - the game texts - interacting with a host who decides to nurture the seed.

This interaction requires, first, that the seed in contact with a host, which happens for example A) if the host gets the seed pushed on him by another enthusiastic host (who may or may not be actually nurturing his own seed); the host is actively seeking out new seeds; C) someone has something to gain from pushing the seeds on the host (money or status).

The seed may not always be nurtured into a game after exposure. That depends, for a large part, on the gemes matching the host's preferences.

You can track both the spread of games, and of specific gemes - just as you can track both the spread of species, and specific genes.

    \n Shouldn't the seed (the game text) be called a "gamete"?

    Seriously, though, if you apply a genetic/memetic metaphor to games, then gameplay is the phenotype of the text-as-DNA. And players become the environment and medium that both influences the development of that phenotype and through which that development is expressed.

    So, ironically, focusing on the text emphasizes the importance of what's happening around the table--the level of social contract, if you will.
