    \n You're barking up the wrong tree, Walsmley. Typical.

    I do hate "bonus dice for being cool". It becomes a millstone and an expectation and I loathe it. Contrast that with an economy that rewards good play, which I fully support. PTA does not work if you are not awesome, and similarly does not work if you don't reward your friends for being awesome. Feng Shui works if you are not awesome, but it is tedious and punishing, so you play to the GM like a trained monkey. And you can't reward your friends. So Sorcerer's bonus die for good narration - don't like it, because it is arbitrary monkey-ism. PTA I already covered. The other two you mentioned are pretty integral and therefore pass my bonus die test (BtI's more because they support the friendly feel of the game, I suspect). Before you ask, TSoY's gift dice are good, too. But any system where "I punch him!" and "I punch him while standing on a barrel!" are mechanically different at the GM's whim is not for me.

    What about the rest of you?

    \n I'm with Jason; awesome dice suck. Why? I happen to think it's pretty simple. Setting up one guy to play both the opposition and be the arbitrator of what is awesome creates a complete and total conflict of interest.     \n In my current design, I have "applause points" which players can give each other for, you know, applaudable stuff. Once given to someone else, they become, essentially, XP, but you can only use them by giving them to someone else - you can't use them yourself.

    If you push someone's twist (like compelling an Aspect - you remind them, "Hey, your character has this advantage/disadvantage, in this situation shouldn't you use it?"), you get rewarded in applause points. Which you can only spend by giving away.

    The very deliberate thinking behind this was, if you're going to encourage someone to point out "Shouldn't you be doing X?" to another player, you should reward them for doing it with a currency that is only useful for saying "You did Y! That's excellent!".

    Having read this thread with interest, I'm now debating whether the Opposition (who isn't a GM) should be able to participate in this economy or not. I'm inclined to think yes, though.

    Posted By: Joshua BishopRoby
    (Doesn't Wushu do something? I'm unfamiliar with the system.)
    \nTo focus obsessively on a single question: I think WUSHU gets around the problem, at least in part by having a very specific and easy-to-satisfy definition of what gets dice: Each detail of your narration = 1 die. I suppose that technically, this rewards complexity rather than awesomeness, but the two are often close in actiony-scenes, and at least the GM isn't given enough discretion-rope to hang the group by. Also, players are encouraged to suggest coolness to eachother, and to set eachother up for coolness. Also, they can veto narration. Thus, all in all, the players have much more control over narration and reward than the gm.

    The other factor might be that, without initiative or limits on how much your character can "do" in a turn, there's nothing stopping you from narrating more as the turn progresses.

    A: "I hit him with my chair!"
    B: "Is that it?"
    A: "Uhh, and he's knocked out, and I swing from the chandelier back down into the brawl!"
    C:"I see someone swinging on the chandelier, so the evening's entertainment has started! I bang on the bar and ask loudly who a dwarf has to kill to get a drink around here."
    A: "Oh! Oh! I land funny, from the chandelier, and hit a loose board, and it launches a bronze spittoon in the air to land on the dwarf, Back to the Future 3 style!"
    All: "Whee!"

    In practice, this can avoid the harsh judgment of lameness, since if other players find your narration lacking, they can tell you so in the supportive form of "Yes, and..." And you can do that to yourself as well.

    \n I love the idea of compells a la SOTC because they reward someone for messing up his own stuff for the sake of story. All too often you have folks at the table trying to avoid losing, avoid weakness, overcome all obstacles and be completely uber. I like it when the mechanics make weakness uber and turn screwing yourself into the path to glory.

    But I do have some gripes about awesome dice as reward for roleplay (I'm confining my rant to GM rewards because its all I've had experience with yet)

    First, I think the concept is flawed. It presumes that rewarding a behavior (roleplay) with mechanical benefit will make it happen more often and better. But my experience is that folks generally either really like or are uncomfortable with roleplaying. You don't need to reward the first group and you're not likely to turn the second group into master roleplayers any faster if you throw them dice. They may try to perform for scooby snacks, but its unlikely to be inspired. This leads me to my second point:

    As others have pointed out its too easy for awesome dice to reward over the top play and ignore subtle, nuanced play. Nothing wrong with over-the-top in the right game, but nothing wrong with subtle either. Its just easier to add farce to roleplay when you're trying to cool it up for the GM than to develop character.

    Point number three, consistency. At the begining of the session, things are starting out slowly and its easy for the GM to spot some awesomeness and toss out some dice. But, in the middle of the session, when things are rockin, it seems like there's the potential for the GM to get busy, I don't know, playing. Now they've got to remember step out of playing mode and into reward and punishment mode and figure out who gets some GM love. The GM should be free to get swept up in all the awesome and not have to remain detached and objective, weighing everyone's performance.

    That's actually point number four, its unfair to the GM to turn them into Simon.
