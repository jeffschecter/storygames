\n This is all my brother's fault for letting me play Guitar Hero. My girlfriend has kindly put Led Zeppelin on while I write this.

Background

Centuries ago, the Gods of Music were cast out of Asgard. Weak and feeble, they wandered the Earth.

Then came electricity. Suddenly, the instruments of the Gods were empowered. Now, they're ready to force their way back to Asgard: with the power of Rock.

You will need

36 six-sided dice
A stereo
Lots of rock music, selected by the players
A map of the world

Characters

Each player chooses five Muses: people or things which his God has lost and which he must regain to gain his power. These should be related to rock songs.

Example: As one of his Things, Blake chooses Silvana, a childhood sweetheart, who has eyes of the bluest sky and hair that reminds him of a warm, soft place, where as a child, he'd hide

Give each player a number from 1 to 6. Roll all the dice on the map. You may choose any die with your number on it as the starting location of your character.

Reclaiming Muses

In some way that I haven't thought through properly, players frame scenes where they're trying to reclaim Muses.

Example: Blake decides that his God, Strat, is in a hotel room opposite Silvana's apartment. Her window is opposite his and he watches her in the light

To resolve whether they get the Muse back, the player playing the God chooses a rock song. The player opposite him is his Judge: he picks a number, from 1 to 6, based on how difficult the thing is to get back.

During the vocal, the player must sing, as his God. In instrumental breaks, the player must narrate.

Example: The guitar solo instrumental starts. Dow-dee-dow-duh-DOW-nah-DOW-nah-dow-dee-dow-duh-DOW-nah-DOW-nah.

BLAKE: Strat stares through the window. He presses his fist against the pane, a tear running down his face, and a crack begins to form in the glass as he says

She's got a face, which it seems to me
Reminds me of childhood memories
When everything was as fresh as the bright blue sky

And his fist comes back and smashes the glass. As it does, her eyes meet his.

Now and then, when I see her face
It takes me away to that special place
And if I stayed too long
I'd probably break down and cry

He reaches out his hand to her.

Oh, oh, oh, sweet child of mine.

And she reaches back.

Oh, oh, oh, oh, sweet child of mine

The judge adds one die to the Rock Pile every time:
- The singer sounds particularly sincere
- The singer sings a verse or chorus
- The narration is particularly good
- Another player joins in on a line of the vocal

He takes one die away whenever:
- The singer gets the words wrong
- The singer narrates when he should be singing
- The singer sings when he should be narrating
- Another player joins in wrongly

He adds three dice whenever another player successfully cuts to a scene involving his God, without changing the song.

Example: Blake has just finished the second chorus of Sweet Child Of Mine.

Avon: Cut to Thrash, in an airport, searching a departure board to find where his wife has gone, and he yells...

Where do we go?
Where do we go now?
Where do we go?

Either the singer or the judge may end the song, at any time. The singer gets two extra dice for ending the song early.

When the song ends, or is ended early, the dice in the Rock Pile are rolled. If the number of fives and sixes exceeds the number chosen by the Judge, the Muse is reclaimed.

-----

Now, this isn't quite right, really, but it stems from a desire to have some sort of Guitar Hero-like music-based resolution. Any ideas?

Graham

    \n Mmm. You see, personally, I don't think it's playable. In fact, it's clearly unplayable, and the challenge would be to fix it to make it playable. If that's even possible.

    You could start with the Lesson from Chalk Outlines (or perhaps Czege Principle): when one person is the author of both the character's adversity and its resolution, play isn't fun. That's a major problem. It's no fun to frame your own scene then solve it yourself. It needs someone else to frame the scene.

    Blake: OK, I'd like a scene about Silvana.

    Zen: How about we set you up in a hotel room opposite her apartment? You can see her preparing to go out for the evening.

    Blake: Sure, let's do it.

    [They start to play Sweet Child Of Mine. Dow-dee-dow-duh-DOW-nah-DOW-nah.]

    Zen: It's near dusk and Strat's watching her apartment obsessively. Suddenly a light comes on and the door opens. She comes in, dumps her stuff, starts getting ready to go out for the evening. She looks like she's making herself special for someone.

    [The intro's nearly over. Zen hands over to Blake, giving him just enough time before the vocal to narrate Strat into the scene]

    Blake: Strat stares through the window. He presses his fist against the pane, a tear running down his face, and a crack begins to form in the glass as he says

    She's got a face, which it seems to me...

    \n Graham,

    The thing that reminded me of the Engel Matrix is the mix of mostly-freeform single-person scene narration and quasi-arbitrary difficulty (that line where you said the other player choose how many successes you need). I know people have talked about them on the Forge a bit recently, particularly those two aspects, so you may want to take a quick look there to see whether they have any comments other than the kinda kneejerk "Don't do it!" you're going to get from some folks (me included, I think: I'm not sure what purpose having another player set the difficulty really has).

    I agree with all the folks who say that it's a bit boring to frame and narrate your own story and just have everyone else act as judge as audience. I think the combination of playing to an audience and having a timer on me would drive me to plan out my scene during other players' goes, so that I wouldn't lose the narration halfway through or botch the lyrics -- there's definitely gotta be some kind of big change somewhere to get rid of some of that pressure to block your scene out in your head instead of paying attention to someone else's. I think something along the lines of Rich's idea would make this more manageable, since there'd be less mental choreography to do in your head between scenes.


    I think the coolest individual mechanic you've got going right now is the way you can pick up another player's song. Right now, that's the main way to bring people together in the game. And it feels all cinematic, too, if you cut just right. But it's also... tricky. It's a really nice payoff moment, but it seems like it's going to be quite rare in play. Any ideas on how to achieve it more often, either through the rules or player techniques?

    Also, storming Asgard and ruling the world with Rock should definitely be cooperative somehow. Because I think that's the big capstone of the whole thing. In an idealized I-have-no-idea-what-mechanics-I-would-use-to-make-this-happen scene I have in my head, all the Gods are ascending and each is doing a short little song-monologue, but then there's a big group piece that brings it together: either this big high-energy axe-grinding rock battle in which they reclaim their thrones or a soft, loving rock ballad that tells of a reunion with their lost loves and old friends. A big, flashy, group moment, where you get to celebrate the thing that brings you all together at the table (i.e. Rock). Unfortunately, I have no idea how I'd make that happen with rules.


    Also, I'm a bit concerned about time. This seems like a sweet game for a pretty big group, and I really feel like it works best if it can be completed in one session. Say, shoot for 5-6 people being able to play it out in three hours? (This translates to about two hours for 3-4 people, I think, which is still respectable.)

    Based on that playtime goal, five Muses per person seems a bit much. Though maybe I'm mistaken... while I think an actual song would tend to be about 3-4 minutes, there's also some scene-framing to do. I think there's going to be a lot of cheerful conversation between scenes, though, if the game is doing its job, so I figure you can say that each scene is around 10 minutes long if you count all those extra bits.


    Okay, here's an actual mechanical observation: you're doing a lot to add dice, but the success/failure check is very simple. I think here we've got some room to spice things up. I'm thinking back to, say, The Shadow of Yesterday... what if the basic success rules are the same, but you can succeed even more if you roll high enough? I don't know what those successes would represent, exactly.


    Since this is a game that engages your real-person powers but is mostly about having fun with your friends (this is how I've observed people playing Guitar Hero: the goal is very much to ROCK OUT rather than compete), some kind of "handicapping" is in order: the most obvious thing being, of course, would be to make lyrics sheet available, but I think you could figure out something else, too. If there's a final product, it should definitely include tips about how to choose songs if you're a beginner.

    -- Alex

    \n I'm really not seeing the 'this game isn't playable' thing. Karaoke and Guitar Hero are not fun because they are easy, and you always hit all the notes, and you totally know all the words, and you've memorized Michael Jackson's dance moves from "Billy Jean" -- mastery and even ability are secondary at best. What's fun is you get into it, man -- you rock out, ham it up, start strutting around the room with your plastic guitar. You channel all the little parts of you that want to let go a little and spazz out. And that's what people appreciate, too.

    So what if you get the words wrong? You lose a die? All it takes is a friend pitching in on the chorus and you've got that die back. Or maybe you don't, and you fail miserably -- you think being a Rock God is easy? It's based on the blues, y'know -- there's a whole lot of Sabbath between you and the promised land.
