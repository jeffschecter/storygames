\n I'm about to start running a 9-episode "season" of PTA with a bunch of friends who are relative novices to the kinds of games discussed here (though they're all familiar with WoD, D&D etc). I have no doubt at all that they'll be completely jazzed by the idea of playing through a TV season - they're all fans of Battlestar Galactica, Lost, Alias et. al. But I'm wondering how good a fit the second edition rules will be for their gaming sensibilities.

I'd be grateful if any PTA fans one here could share their experiences of seasons they've run or played in. What worked? What didn't? I'm especially looking for ideas about rules hacks that you used. All offerings received gratefully.

    \n Play it absolutely straight. It's very, very easy to drift unintentionally, so don't do that. I made a little cheat sheet about how to resolve ties, who does what when, the fanmail economy, etc and taped it to the front of my book.

    The only thing we do differently is in generating our premise - we treat that as a traditional GM duty, and work with whatever the Producer comes up with. This works better for us than a consensus-driven process.

    I'd also suggest a five-ep-and-pilot season, which seems to work best (at lest for us). No down time, always something exploding.

    \n By the rules, "if I win" is set (to some degree) before cards are drawn, by the acting character's player, and "if I lose" is set after cards are flipped, by whoever wins narration rights. This makes a lot of sense in terms of suspense and incentive to bid for narration rights.

    Another way to play is to enlist the whole group in setting "if I lose"* before the cards are drawn. This tends to maximize engagement for everyone and produce better story material. The downside, that narration rights only win you the ability to put your personal spin and color on a predetermined outcome, haven't seemed to me to be much of a downside at all, in practice.

    It also produces more of a writerly, story-workshopping vibe, which you may or may not want.

    *and often in suggesting refinements to "if I win" too

    \n I don't know the players, so take all of this with a grain of salt.

    You might need some expectation management on the fact that rolling for stuff like whether you can jump that chasm or not doesn't matter - my experience with "old-schoolers" tends to be that they want to ask permission a lot before saying what they do. Worst case scenario from my anecdotal experience is that someone will tune out because they don't feel like there's anything tangible attached to the talking. A lot of games have ways you can turn a bad situation to your advantage through strategy - PTA doesn't, really. The cards fall and dictate outcome and narration, and that's it.

    Encourage them to be awesome. A lot. Point out awesome things as a good time for fanmail if you see someone react to it, until they get into the habit.

    Talk through drilling down to the real heart of a conflict. Issue is a stat. It's not a conflict when one guy wants to keep another guy from getting away. It's a conflict when one guy wants to keep another guy from getting away because he doesn't want to look like a failure to his partner.

    Most of my PTA rules hacks have been coming up with more formalized, mini-game ways to do the pitch session, 'cause some groups I've played with have needed some constraints to kickstart them. I've tried squishing together resolution systems from other games to make various Franken-PTAs for various reasons, but I wouldn't recommend it. I learned a bunch, but that all came to nothing.

    So, yeah. Play it as it lies.

    \n In the eventual next edition, I included a process where if you can't think of a series idea, you can draw cards that give you prompts based on more popular series ideas, like maybe you draw "hospital", "dark ages" and "comedy" and bam, you have a disturbing but funny medical procedural set in France in the year 500 CE. You actually draw extra cards and take turns discarding down to three ideas, so maybe you also drew "supernatural" and "modern day" and discarded those two.*

    In the meantime, you could probably accomplish something similar by having the players write down ideas on scraps of paper and then take turns nixing ones you like less, until you have three.

    *actually, supernatural would be cool to include as well. Medieval doctors assisted by annoying ghosts! "Leeches? What's wrong with you people?"

    \n Another wrinkle to consensus-vs-declarative pitching: I like what I like to call the "Judd process," whereby everyone says what they are NOT interested in playing, and then people pitch individual ideas, and then players get to veto things they still aren't interested in. In my experience it's resulted in finding a pitch with a fairly singular vision that everyone is some degree of on-board with (as opposed to consensus-mush, where the show has a million different elements draw from different people).

    If that doesn't work, of course, the producer should just say what the show will be and move on.

    \n Here's how I do it at a convention:

    I come up with a very broad show idea for the convention booklet, this concept is what gets asses in the chairs. Obviously the first thing out of my mouth is "in a home game, we would be putting a pitch together ourselves, if you have ideas about this pitch, hang onto them and we'll talk about them".

    Example: Curtains, the horror-comedy set in a haunted 19th century Parisian theater.

    I have around 10 or so character ideas (for a 4-5 person table) which I put on index cards and I spread them out face up. I say "these are some characters that might appear in a show like this, if any of these spark any other character ideas, let me know." Normally I don't expect many people to suggest anything at this point, if they do, be super enthusiastic about it, but don't expect anything.

    Example: Theresa, the diva, M. Beauchemin, the producer, Jacques, the tortured writer, etc.

    We then discuss which character's we're most excited about. We do NOT pick characters until we have a cast assembled.

    I pass out the character sheets. It has the name and concept. I then lay out, face down, many many many index cards filled with Connections and Traits. Everyone chooses 2-3, and picks one. If they really don't like either, they can draw again. The point is to create a general universe of what Traits and Connections are like.

    Then they write the final 2 Trait/Connections themselves.

    Basically this process is to step people through the biggest blank-spaces of PTA, starting with "I'm telling you what the game will be" to "here's some ideas for how the game might be" to "what are your ideas about how the game might be". This also allows us to do two episodes in one 4-hour convention block, which shows people what Spotlight Episodes are and what Screen Presence does.

    Posted By: David BergBut hashing out the pitch is the coolest part!
    \nThe Pitch isn't play. It's table setting. If that's the best part of your PTA game, you're in a lot of trouble. My advice would be find a way to get The Pitch done in under an hour. If you're playing five sessions, the players will find the empty spaces and fill them in, with play. Better to get into the game and learn how to set up scenes and drive towards meaty conflict than spend three hours rooting around to find an airtight pitch.

    \n Definitely go to read some of the actual play help threads in the Dog Eared Designs archive at the Forge. There are a lot of problems which groups tend to run into during their first times playing, some of which you'll probably have, too. The things I had problems were remembering that the Producer frames every scene (the other players only request Focus, Setting, and Agenda) and working to set good stakes with conflicts. It takes a little practice to make stakes that work well and are fun.

    Go take a read; you'll probably realize a lot more than we can suggest with blind comments here.
