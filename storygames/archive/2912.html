\n Hi all,

This is something I think about from time to time and have discussed in other fora, but it occurred to me today it might have relevance here too.

Essentially, the question is a simple one, how many rpgs have you bought (if you want, do feel free to create a time limit like say over the last couple of years) and how many of those have you actually played?

By bought, I include rather counterintuitively free downloads if you then saved it to your hard drive and freebies given as comps for review, trade or whatever.

By actually played, I mean actually used in a game. For these purposes if you just used it as a general ideas springboard that does not count, actual play means you actually used that game product in a game that you played or ran. Yes, I realise lots of folk count general inspiration as actual play, but for the purposes of this thread I don't.

If you can say why you haven't played the stuff you haven't played, that would be cool too.

I'll go first, and for these purposes I am only going to talk about my story game purchases. Expanding to all rpg purchases would make baby Balbinus cry...

Purchases
Sorceror
Sorceror & Sword
Sorceror's Soul
Charnel Gods
Schism
Trollbabe
octaNe
The Pool
Ghost Light
My Life with Master
Dogs in the Vineyard
PTA
Burning Wheel
Heroquest (not sure it is a storygame, but people often seem to count it)

of which I've actually played octaNe, the Pool, My Life with Master, Heroquest and Dogs in the Vineyard. I have also used Sorceror & Sword as a direct reference in another game, in that I actually referred to it as part of planning that game, but didn't use the actual rules. The ones I haven't played I haven't played for a variety of reasons, some because I think I bought them in a froth of excitement only to find that the game really didn't do that much for me, some because I thought I needed them to understand what was happening in the indie scene (a reason I now view as flawed incidentally), PTA I just haven't had opportunity but really want to play, Burning Wheel I haven't worked out how to play yet.

So, what games have you bought/acquired/downloaded? What have you actually played?

For the curious, the reason for the question is that I think that we may be experiencing a replacement supplement treadmill, where instead of buying supplements we never use and reading them we buy games we never use and read them. The indie equivalent of giving up smoking and gaining weight instead. I also have some thoughts on rpg supply/demand mismatch which are broader than storygaming but will post on those separately.

    \n I have bought a lot of games which I have never played, for a variety of reasons.

    The first is that I haven't had particularly good luck with finding an abundance of gaming groups. It's easy to find one, harder to find multiple groups.

    This means that there's less chance that the group is going to be willing to play something "non-standard." Most groups I've been in will standardize on something (usually D&D) and it's hard to break out of that.

    The second is related; some games require specific types of people to play. I mean a mindset here; you need people who look for particular kinds of things in their gaming. If a group is interested in primarily the "fun, kicking ass and chewing bubblegum, soda-and-pretzels" style of game play, that's not wrong -- but it just means that the other types of games won't be played.

    If even one person is like that -- if one person will be miserable playing something else -- it skews the willingness of the group to try something new and different. We all can agree to play a generic fantasy game; why make one unhappy when we run off to play something else?

    The third is that I'm kind of cowardly. I don't like taking direct risks like "hey, let's play SOMETHING WEIRD which may not WORK but we'll give it a shot!" because I am afraid that will reflect poorly on me. I could propose it and get shot down, in which case I feel stupid, or I could propose it and we play it and it's not enjoyable, in which case I feel stupid.

    It's work enough running games (and I'm usually the Designated Gamemaster, and it's my choice) that I would rather have it be an enjoyable experience rather than one where I end up feeling stupid, so I try to do my experimental stuff less blatantly (by co-opting various genre conventions, for example). I feel it's "risky" enough putting my GMly creative works out there, that I don't want to push further.

    Fourth, a lot of games I honestly get just to read. I've spent far more time reading game books than I've spent playing roleplaying games, or preparing for roleplaying games -- and I've done a heck of a lot of RPGing. I don't buy roleplaying game materials necessarily thinking I'll run the games, but rather that I'll enjoy reading them.

    Finally and fifth, a lot of what I read does end up being used indirectly in roleplaying games I run (or create), but not as originally intended. It becomes part of the background body of knowledge that I bring to gaming as a gamemaster, player, or game creator, and it helps to make me better at those roles.

    Sorcerer (several sessions)

    I need to quit the consumer urge.

    I'll buy something for any of the following reasons:

    - heard about it, inspired to run a campaign of it soon
    - my friends are playing it or want to
    - gorgeous yet pragmatic layout and design (Artesia)
    - covers a topic I enjoy (fiction or non-fiction: See "Asia" or "India" in my case)
    - originality
    - innovative rules
    - know the author
    - like author's previous work
    - GMless but doesn't suck
    - people reference it a lot online

    After that, I'll play it if it does the following:

    - teaches the rules well
    - can be learned quckly
    - continues to inspire after purchasing it
    - someone else picked it up and will run it

      \n- Dead of Night (of course), as GM only, never a player (fun, of course)
      \n- Donjon, as GM only, never a player (fun, but chaotic experience)
      \n- OtherKind, as GM only, never a player (poor experience)

      \n
      \nI created DoN.
      \nI paid for octaNe (and the expansions, which I never used.)
      \nI paid for Donjon.
      \nI downloaded OtherKind for free.
      \n
      \nI probably own 30-40 storygame titles, especially if you're counting stuff like Orkworld, Orks! and HeroQuest.
      \nI probably own 300-400 RPG titles overall, and my overall play:purchase ratio is probably roughly 10%.
      \n
      \nWhen I was younger, I had a lot of time, and not much money. The only thing I had to do was go to class and pass tests. Consequently, I played virtually everything I bought. Then I got rid of it if it wasn't the best system evar. I kick myself now for unloading some of those older titles for the nostalgia and the cost, but bygones are bygones.
      \n
      \nNowadays, I have more money than time. For quite a while, I bought to support my hobby, because pieces of a game interested me. I wanted the ideas, and to reward others for having come up with them. However, I've always had a very, very low tolerance for complex rulesets, needless jargon, and meandering explanations. I never wanted to read pages of description. RPGs writers aren't novelists - their prose should simply inspire me. I don't want to run YOUR great game. I want to run MY great game.
      \n
      \nI want a tight presentation of essentials with elaboration available for when I deem it necessary. Very, very few publications meet my standards (even DoN has room for improvement. :) ) And the majority of ideas I skim past these days seem rehashed, dull, convoluted, or some combination thereof. And quite frankly, I'm tired of buying ideas. These days I strive to buy actual experience.
      \n
      \nI have a wife and child, a job with a strong potential for advancement. I don't think I've bought an RPG in the last six months. Runepunk is on my list, as is anything for Battlestations, but that's it. NOTHING else looks good to me. Instead, some of my money has gone into board games (another 250-300 titles there), but even that's slowing down. I like having my disposable income around to treat my kid. Plus, gasoline is getting annoyingly expensive.
      \n
      \nThe coolest "RPG" I picked up recently was a full set of the Tangled Tales electronic pick-a-path game. I have the Starter Set, both (of the nearly impossible-to-find) base adventure expansions, as well as the Nancy Drew and Edgar and Ellen modules. My kid can already push the buttons and say all the words. Now it's just a matter of actually learning to read the cards. :) A few more years, and we can bring the baby to the actual RPG table to join the other folks to actually play instead of just converse with them.
