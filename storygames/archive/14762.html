\n so the basic idea is that you start with a system similar to abstractions from Burning Wheel's Magic Burner. That is a spell can be made up of various elements, like distance, fire and destruction to throw fireballs at someone. Or the same things together to light a fire at a distance. There is a big list of different ranges, elements and so on for it. I can add more details if someone wants.

the next part of the idea is that since it is really hard to make a spell up from pieces like this you need to pass off part of from you spell caster. So he has a fire to stand in for fire. This makes the magic fire easier to do. And throwing something, even a short distance, to represent the spell traveling.

If the spell caster was trying to affect the emotions of a group of people perhaps he could be at a baseball game and wait for the crowd to cheer a home run while holding a token representing the group he wants to affect.

And when I first came up with this idea 3 days ago I had something about Harry Potter style wands as well. But that is about it, no real plans to develop this any further right now.

    This comports well with ideas expressed in ritual magic "systems" as expressed in history, fiction and gaming. Magic in many traditions is considered to be sympathetic. The more ties the caster can have to the intended object or effect, the greater the chance of success. Voodoo dolls, bits of hair, the names of Angels and Devils, even symbolic logic are among many options.

    \n

    Check out Isaac Bonewits' expression of the laws of magic, excerpted from his book on magic in gaming, Authentic Thaumaturgy for some ideas on other areas in which correspondence and sympathy might make for interesting additions to a game.

    \n I'd like to see a functional system where magic is actually roleplayed like this (I know it's possible and I love the idea, it's just that I haven't seen it working yet). I like the idea of expressing all the variables of a spell as elements, instead of numeric values. I'll add something else from the Authentic Thaumaturgy book: the game would be easier to balance if you could leave this as color/roleplaying instructions, and add faith into the equation to define the consequences: For every person that believes in the spellcaster's powers, the effect gets a bonus. For every person who doesn't believe in him, he gets a penalty. The player can then add mana to improve the effect and roll dice to determine if he gets his goal or not. (this way you could even have a situation where the spellcaster puts an impressive fire show but not a hair of his opponent gets burned)

    I've tried this a bit, however it only works if magic is the main theme of the game. We had spellcasters whose power become greater if they were able to impress the people around them first, using other tricks that wouldn't be considered magic at all. And on top of that, using different magic systems, we could also use the same mechanic to have the cleric of the town actually working powerful "miracles" for the townspeople. It all ends having people actually roleplaying their characters to be mysterious and deceitful.

    Characters can also increase their magic by making/finding amulets, wands, staffs, rings of power, etc. These are actually mundane things that the spellcaster believes to have some occult power or special quality. Perhaps it's in the way it came to their hands. They can copy ancient devil names and use them on their spells, carry around a rock from a mystical waterfall or a ring presumed to be made from a unicorn horn: it's a powerful object if he believes in it's power. Succesfully performiing magic should also affect the faith of the caster, as well. And their beliefs can also became their weaknesses, like a fire wizard whose magic is weak against cold, or a healer against a necromancer.

    Summing it up: now you would have spells separated in elements that should be reunited and put in motion by the spellcaster in order to perform a spell (and I agree with WPTunes, bring everything from Authentic Thaumaturgy) The spellcaster's faith (whatever he believes in) should affect the result as well as the amount of people who believes on him or not. (by the way, this isn't my idea, this is from Isaac Bonewits book) The spellcaster also burns mana, which in turn can be a resource that once spent, leaves the spellcaster exhausted.

    \n Thanks for pointing out Authentic Thaumaturgy. Looks like it will be a good read.

    One possible way to do it mechanics-wise:

    Treat spells as 2-layered recipes. This is what I am currently doing with herbology/alchemy in my biweekly game, but I think the same could be done for magic as well (though, honestly, it might be clunky to use in a game with lots and lots of magic).

    So the player has a recipe for creating an effect. You have a fire and a dried, pressed daisy, and a sling stone. And using the three of those together with a few magic words and some willpower, you can create a fireball spell. If you have all of them handy, the spell is cheap, though it probably takes a turn to get the components out and ready, unless you're holding them out ready to go. More time, of course, if you have to kindle a fire.

    But substitutions could be done. That's where the 2-layer thing comes in. The fire represents Energy/Heat, which is one of the components. And the pressed daisy is Spreading/Area/Explosion. And the sling stone is Range/Throwing. Other components could be substituted in for those categories.

    So the energy of a flowing river could be tapped instead, if you dip your hand into the river while channeling the spell, though this would probably transform the effect into an iceball explosion. And a kernal of popcorn could be used instead of the pressed daisy to represent the explosive spreading (perhaps only one kernal from a whole cob-worth has the right energy potential, so it would take some time to sort through a bag of Redenbacher to find the few good ones).

    (Side note. Cobworth would make a pretty sweet last name for a character.)

    Each of these substitutions would add to the amount of willpower it would take to power the spell.

    And if a component was missing completely, then it could be substituted with pure will, but of course, that would be extremely draining/damaging to the caster.

    The components should have a certain amount of rareness to them. Something that makes it so that you can't always instantly McGuyver up a spell out of whatever is at hand, at least not without spending some time under fire preparing things. Otherwise, there's little difference between a well prepared magician and a player who is good a making stuff up quickly on the spot. Improvizing is fine, but there should be solid advantages for being well prepared in advance.

    Also, certain magic items could act as reusable components for certain spells, like the way Harry Dresden uses his blasting rod and wand and shield bracelet. So if you had the item, you would just need the willpower part. Mechanics-wise, those items would probably take some sort of perk slot to create and attune (so the character wouldn't collect dozens and skip the recipe magic completely), and could be temporarily removed/destroyed (though they could be replaced during a downtime between adventures, if necessary).

    \n I am surprised at the attention this little idea of mine has received. I half expected to a comment or two before it died.

    Now to do some of the hard work that would help make this into an actual game.
    The themes would be very wide open I guess. The maguimagic would in part be the a mcguffin, the props that make the story interesting as you read it. You could use sci-fi trappings or something else to fill in for the magic in many ways. Or perhaps something better will come to mind later.

    Waht do the characters do?
    They are all wizards who work magic. All of the main characters would be someone that can either do the magic or has a vested interest in it being done.

    The players?
    Perhaps it it a rotating spotlight game like Polaris or Shock where you take turns playing the main character and minor characters for someone else's main character.
    The group as a whole needs to buy into the basic premise of how the magic would work for their game and be interested in watching each other do it. And spells can be done as a group ar the table, everyone brainstorming components. Plus nice friendly rules for helping each other with magic and otherwise.

    What is magic?
    The source of magic is everyday things and objects. No wands, no lost magic rings, and no powers from the ancient mystical past. The world is as full of wonder and magic as it ever was, you just need to see it. No magic wands, arcane artifacts or separate magical worlds.

    As for the magical elements. I would be tempted to do it in three parts. Part one would be some basic principals and examples. Part two a sample list of elements, and part three some guidelines on how to customize it for yourself. The idea to should be to keep it flowing fast and easy, not too much time spent referencing multiple obscure rules. I won't be using much from Authentic Thaumaturgy because of that.

    What does a spell look like?
    An odd ball collection of things in a strange place, someone concentrating intensely, someone with a very good reason why he was stealing the ___ but he can't come down to the police station just now to explain it just now officier.

    The actual effect of the spell? From the subtle \u201cWas that really magic ?\u201d to calling down lightening from the heavens.

    Spirits and power that be?
    Making deals with local spirits, gods and other powers in exchange for favours or the use of their power? Not too sure. Calling on Zeus or God for power with prayers does not seem to fit to me. Going to talk to Jesus or Buddah, bring some bread to help them feed the birds at the park while you talk things through. It just seems to me that everything needs to be grounded in the mundane in some way.

    Posted By: tj333I'm not sure I'm putting magic back into magic but I like posts 10 and 11.

    A bit of an open question, how do you handle most of the players learning the rules of in game fiction as the game is played? Someting I have strufgled with a bit myself in other game ideas.
    \nIt all depends on how different is the resolution system for magic vs non-magic actions. If you manage to make it the same, or something that derivates from either one of them, then it's simple to add variations of the same mechanic.

    In the game about magic I used to run, mechanic was just "roll a d10 against difficulty or opposed roll" no stats, no abilities, no bonuses. Then, as PCs became better at something or got some equipment it was "now add a +1, since now you are faster because of X"

    Then there was the magic. The first thing PCs learned, half into the first session was elemental magic. They rolled a d4 to see which element they could channel:earth, water (and ice) wind or fire. And then... they would roll a d10 against difficulty or opposed roll. However, out of combat effects (and for some tactical choices) the number rolled in d10 meant "cubic meters of such element disposed however you want, up to the same number of meters in distance, provide you've got line of sight"

    This simple magic mechanic made incredible combats possible and had players going crazy with tactical choices. The fact that the power of the magic was so random only gave the excitement of uncertainty to the game and since the PCs were only starting to dabble into magicks, it made sense they were only this good. Of course, the first opponents were jellys and rats. But jellys who had a background and a culture, and whose only magician turned the PCs into jellys for a while, to be able to talk it out. Oh, and rats which had a Lich rat for leader XD

    After a couple of sessions we roleplayed a training scene, and according to their choices the characters got different additional abilities. Players could also play these escenes solo and when the group reunited again it was cool to see them show off their new abilities to the group. And these training scenes were only interrupted to explain an additional rule about the new ability gained, like "now you don't need to roll the d4 to see which element your character can use now, you just choose one" and "now your d10 is multiplied by 2 when you do magick"

    Of course, the fun depended on giving the players more options instead of using magic, though making magic made some stuff a lot more easier. I also used a constrain given by the setting, where PCs "couldn't do magic in front of normal people, or The Council of Mages would come after them"

    Dropping the rules by bits helps to keep the magic mysterious. You don't have to explain how NPCs did this or that, since "magicians don't go telling their tricks... for nothing" And so players have to come up with a solution of their own or barter for spells with other NPCs

    To balance the elemental magic being so random I added spells that had other effects with fixed numbers, but that needed mana to be cast. These spells could only be learned in game by convincing NPCs or other players of teaching them (and later in the game, by doing research) and The mana pool of the characters though limited, could be increased in training scenes or by buying mana stones (sort of batteries they had to recharge daily)

    I'd say in a game like this you can give starting players only basic knowledge about one spell and have them barter among themselves or with NPCs to learn about the uses and whereabouts of other objects, spirits, places, etc. and ways to use them. Or you can give them none at all and let them find out how to do magic in the game.

    \n Schools should also provide a theme for the objects used, like chickens and wax dolls for voodoo, animal and human bones for necromancy, rubies or matchsticks for fire magic, and so on. The words, gestures or places should also be related to the school. You can of course use non-canon schools of magic, however the objects should be somehow thematically related, even if it's just for color.

    Not that an object should have an obvious relation. Like, a magician could use the lighter of a dead soldier to summon his spirit, instead of using it as a focus for pyromancy. Not that it couldn't have more than one use, don't you think?

    Otherkind Dice look ok, the only thing I had for tokens was just using them for some resource management, but perhaps this game would run better without numbers. However you might want to suggest what kind of things can go wrong when using magic to the players, and what are they risking by using it.
