\n Those of you who are GMing or playing in ongoing campaigns (or have in the past and expect to in the near future):

Would you rather introduce a whole new game to your group, or would you rather hack your existing games(s) by adding a new feature?

By new features, I mean things like:
- a new setting
- a new combat system
- a new character power
- a new mechanism for pacing sessions
- a new procedure for distinguishing "in game" from "out of game"
- a new set of puzzles and/or monsters
and so on.

    \n You have a hidden assumption here, don't you? You're assuming that not only are we playing or going to play campaigns, but we'd also be doing it with the same people. Therefore there isn't really any group to whom I might introduce a game or changes to a game.

    That said, I'm a new game guy, switching groups or not. The only exception is if the game explicitly works by adding material, such as most universal systems tend to.

    \n Interesting. You guys are all saying what I'd say, in principle, if someone asked me, theoretically. But it runs counter to my own campaign play experience, especially in high school and college (where campaign play is most convenient). Back then, we picked a game based mostly on the setting and other color, tried to play it by the book initially, then quickly ditched parts we didn't like, and added procedures from past play that we did like. At any point, someone might buy a GURPS setting book or a Warhammer FRP module and tack elements from those onto our D&D2 game.

    We were constantly creating techniques that were core to our experience -- stuff like "Raise your hand so we know when you're speaking out of character." If someone had offered to sell us that before we'd come up with it on our own, I have no idea how we would have reacted.
