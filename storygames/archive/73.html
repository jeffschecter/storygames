    What are you playing?

    How many people? What's the spread of newbies to veteran D&Ders? How do you know these folks?

    I have had pretty good luck teaching stakes just through example and stopping people when they pick up the dice.

    "Whoa, dont' roll yet! We haven't set stakes yet. Okay, if you win X an if you lose Y. I sthat cool?"

    And as you go, set stakes that they don't think are cool, shoot for the moon and then negotiate the stakes. I like other players to be able to help with the process.

    Good luck.

    If this is the Superbowl Snub thing (which unfortunately I cannot make, prior commitments), then they're veteran players who are interested ingiving new stuff a shot, IIRC.

    The most important thing to remember and remind the players is:

    Keep them interesting. If they're not interesting, there's no reason to roll: Simply move on until something interesting happens.

    When you phrase stakes, make sure that the losing stakes is just as interesting as the winning, in a story/character sense. Not "Success = Kill baddies, failure = I die. Unless it's the last conflict or something.

    Naw, I wouldn't set hard stakes at first. That could make them feel a little overwhelmed, like "WTF?" You can start as usual with "small but interesting stakes" ("S: The halfling theif gives you the information, F: He doesn't know anything"). I usually set hard stakes not to shake people out of their "passive coma", but only when interesting ("climax scenes") and the like.

    Don't just use hard stakes to slap your proverbial cock on the table to wake people up: Some people might "get it" but the others will just be shocked and confused, I'm willing to bet.

    -Andy

    I'll steal a page from Tony LB on interesting stakes.

    If you can make your players say "Oh hell no..." all the while making it blatantly clear that they absoloutely have the power & authority to challenge what's happening... that's the bell.

    During the Capes demo that Tony ran for us (and for lots and lots of other lucky people) he pulls out this uber-obnoxious superhero, Captain Victory. He narrates Victory jumping up on a huge statue of himself to give a speech about how all us measly villains need to play nice. So I look down at my sheet and see stuff like "Break things", so all of a sudden, not only do I want to mess with Victory, I know I've got the opportunity. Statues fell like dominoes. That demo was maybe 15 min long. He had Lisa and I hooked & invested in something like 30 seconds of narration. Probably less than that really.

    So, that's my suggestion. If yer GMing DitV, then have average, ordinary, non-supernatural townies jumping up and saying stuff that gets under the skins of the players.

    "All Dogs are stupid monkey-heads and you need to get yer doodoo brains out of town."

    or

    "I'm the authority in this town, Dog, and I say that the fence is well built, and there's nothing you can do about it."

    or

    "Those girls are damned to hell for their sins! If you don't do something about them I will. And I'll bring my shotgun to represent my word in god...."

    You know. Or something like that.

    -Eric

    I've been harping on this lately, so please forgive me if you've already seen it elsewhere.

    For rockin' stakes, make sure to include consequences. Like:

    Win: The halfling thief gives you the information so you're able to get into the tower without setting off the alarm.

    Lose: The halfling thief makes you believe he doesn't know anything, and then sells you out to the wizard, who will know you're coming for him.

    Now the roll matters! You can bet everyone will lean forward and hold their breath when the dice are rolled. Setting stakes with clear and definite consequences (that must be abided by) is a clear sign to the veteran gamers that they are not in Kansas anymore. When you roll, stuff happens. Right then and there.

    The scale and scope of your stakes and consequences will take some fine tuning, so be ready to take the group's temperature when you propose large or serious risks and rewards. As long as everyone knows that stakes-setting is a negotiation, you'll be fine.
