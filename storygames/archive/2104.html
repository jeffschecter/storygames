[mainstream gaming] proposed resolution system

talysman
December 2006 edited December 2006 in Story Games

Over in the various threads on games marketed to the mainstream (or, at least, not to core roleplayers,) people tossed around a couple ideas about what might be appealing to folks who weren't RPG nerds. Playsheets that act almost like a boardgame, near-zero prep time, shorter playtime goal, the ability to play when the mood strikes, basic rules that could be placed on a single sheet, easy to acquire game components... things like that. I've been mulling over some of those ideas, since some of them are appealing to me whether or not we're worried about attracting mainstream people into the hobby.

Here is something I've come up with as a basic resolution system with rudimentary suggestions on character setup. I'm tossing it out here in case anyone else would like to use it; I'd certainly like to develop it more for use as a core system (similar to the way BRP is used in several games.) Also, I'd like some help tracking down possible related systems. I'll come back to that in a bit. My design assumptions here are:

  • basic character creation is kept extremely simple, with characters fleshed out in play;
  • there will be additional rules for specific games/settings to limit or elaborate characters, situations, movement from scene to scene, and rewards;
  • game components are restricted to the commonest, easiest-to-get items possible;
  • the basic rules should be explainable in a couple sentences, with nuances explained later during play.

I'm temporarily calling this system "Common Sense", although I've played with other names and I'm open to suggestions.

Basic Character Creation
Pick a background, basically a profession or culture, and modify it with an adjective, to distinguish your character from others of the same type. Examples: "moody assassin", "gregarious vampire", "deranged monk". Pick one unusual talent and one major possession; the possession will have a numeric rating of 3. Specific settings will have limits or additional details on these three character components, or may describe additional necessary components like relationships.

Basic Resolution
If what the character wants to do is possible for that character, decide whether it's an attempt to create a temporary situational advantage or it's an attempt to make a significant change, then do the following:

  1. Toss three coins and count the heads;
  2. Compare that value to any blocking value (either a coin toss by an opponent, or a pre-existing defense or situation;)
  3. If the value is higher than the defense, the action was effective. If the goal was to create an advantage, it's created with the value from the coin toss; if the goal was to make a significant change, the character achieves one-third of the desired goal (for example, if attacking to kill, the steps are "wounded"/"badly wounded"/"dying".)
  4. Any other participants in the current scene get a chance to toss coins; they can block a previous toss, loan heads to another participant (and describe how their actions help the other character,) or try something unrelated to preceding actions;
  5. After every participant has had a chance to toss, you can use a talent to re-toss one coin, adding its value to the results already achieved. This may be necessary to overcome a significant defense.

Boosting and Risking
Players only toss three coins at a time and can only re-toss up to three coins before another participant in the scene gets a chance. To be able to do more on your turn, you can risk something to toss two extra coins. If you toss five coins and get three tails, however, you lose what you risked. Players can also risk a major possession or situational advantage (anything with a numeric rating) to get extra coins (or re-toss existing coins) equal to the numeric value; if the player gets a number of tails equal to the numeric value of the possession or advantage minus 1, that possession or advantage is lost, although the player can attempt to regain it.

There's a couple other elaborations you could do, but they should be fairly obvious: splitting results for multiple actions, splitting simultaneous attack and defense results, doing a coin toss to acquire equipment, and so on.

Any comments?

    Should you be tossing coins for this at all? I should perhaps make this clearer; I was trying to be abbreviated. I see three possible consequences from opening the vault, which would be tested in three different ways:

    • It's open instead of closed. Don't test, it's just open now.
    • It allows something to get in/out that you can use for another purpose. This is a situational advantage, so you toss coins to find out how effective it is.

    Additional comment: I read a brief description of the Prince Valiant resolution system and the basics of it are very similar. You count heads in both systems. The differences are that the "Common Sense System" doesn't tie the number of coins to an attribute or skill rating and doesn't use the extended conflict system, where two players toss coins and take away coins from each other's "coin pools" until one person is eliminated. Also, boosting, reflipping, and splitting results provide a slightly different feel.

    Another game with a coin-flip mechanic is Underworld, which uses varying numbers of coins for standard actions, but always three coins for "mass" combat. Each of the three had to be a different coin, because each one represented something specific. You could use abilities or circumstances to flip a coin from tails to heads, and your opposition did the reverse. Depending on which coins came up which way, you had different types of favorable or unfavorable outcomes. Just throwing that out there.
