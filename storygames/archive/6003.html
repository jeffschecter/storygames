\n I have to admire the chutzpah of this.

White Wolf is running a promotion called "Graduate your game". The short version: They're taking names up until April 28th of folks that are interested in trading in their D&D 3.5 Player's Handbook in exchange for a copy of the Exalted, Second Edition core rulebook. The trade-ins happen between May 26th-31st at your local participating hobby shop, with a cap of 2,500 copies. The conceit is that you're "graduating" from D&D to Exalted.

On the one hand, it's not like players are going to be using their D&D 3.5 Player's Handbooks with D&D 4.0 coming out in June, and like I said, I respect the chutzpah of making a smash and grab for the fans before they get sucked back into D&D.

However, when they say "graduate" to Exalted, they're not kidding. I've joked before that you need a degree in the game to run it. Don't get me wrong, it's a beautiful game. The production values have only improved with the advent of the new edition. There are simply too many rules for me to juggle, and too many Charms to remember (237 to be exact, many of which bend or controvert the rules as needed).

If you do decide to try to run Exalted, for Heaven's sake take advantage of the official recently released free Solar Charm Cards and the Battle Wheel. They will at least save some wear and tear on your book while you're fighting with your players, and who knows, they might even help.

The real irony here is that D&D 4.0 seems to be about streamlining and simplifying things and removing stuff that doesn't work and is going to speed up combat drastically, where Exalted appears to slow it down to a crawl.

    \n I can't say I'm fond of the 'Graduate' wording, for the same reason that I don't like adverts that say stuff like 'Asda have 4,000 products cheaper than Tesco'. It's advertising by directly rubbishing an opponent, not by showing how cool your own stuff is. It's the difference between saying 'Exalted is excellent!' and 'Exalted is better than D&D'. It's not unlike a slightly more subtle variation on the political smear campaign. Make yourself look good by suggesting that your contemporaries are crap.
