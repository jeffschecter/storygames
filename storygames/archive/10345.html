\n A friend outside of the indie RPG scene was lamenting his D20 product low sales. He\u2019s impressed by his perception of what indie RPGs can sell and was considering going that direction. I told him I thought his sales expectations were unrealistic for just selling his game without having a name or some other way to promote it.

How many indie RPGs have sold 1000+ copies purely on the merit of the game itself? Meaning the designer was previously unknown and just put their game up for sale?

My understanding is that many well selling indie RPGs have had more things going for it than just the game itself on sale online.

My understanding is that most people who have sold 1000+ indie RPGs:

- have a previously established reputation
- built a community beforehand or around their game
- license existing properties
- work with known people or cross promote
- service the RPG fan community in other ways first (podcasts for example)
- make frequent convention appearances, run demos, lead seminars
- help others in the community first or requently share their knowledge
- winning awards
- offer free product or services.

A possibly exception that springs to mind is PTA. I think the general appeal of the concept, innovative design, and low barrier to entry have really pushed it far on its own.

Any other games?

    \n So back in the day I used to work on Marketing textbooks, right? In those textbooks, they defined marketing as the process of getting your product into your consumer's hands. Most folks, when they hear 'marketing' they think promotion and advertising. The reality (as described in the textbook, at least) is that advertising is a subset of promotion, and promotion is a subset of marketing. Marketing also includes distribution, payment structures, customer relations, and perhaps most importantly, product design. All of the steps that you have to take to get your product in consumers' hands \u2014 that's marketing.

    Which means the question is even more flawed, really. You can't sell things without marketing. Period. So the question isn't "How do I get fantastic sales without marketing?" If you're selling anything at all, even one copy, you're doing some marketing \u2014 otherwise your product wouldn't be in the consumer's hands. The question being asked is, roughly, "How do I get fantastic sales with crappy marketing?" And the answer is: you don't.

    \n I think there are multiple semi-indepedent forces affecting indie publishing right now:

    1. More people publishing games, whether free, print, ashcan, what have you. However, this is contrasted by...

    2. Individual creators -- especially experienced ones -- taking longer to develop games or using alternative publishing models aside from the "print + GenCon + IPR" core strategy that was near universal until about 2006, when shifts seemed to begin. Things that support this: Ashcan Front, Vincent's recent games like Poison'd, Apocalypse World, Storming the Wizard's Tower, John Harper's free games, etc. Also some experienced folks getting out of commercial publishing entirely like Clinton and Matt. And some newer publishing agreements like what Evil Hat does for Penny for My Thoughts and Chad or what Cubicle 7 is gonna do for Gregor.

    So it's not just that "the Golden Age is over." The entire way we think about indie publishing now is pretty different now than it was circa 2004-2005, at least in a significant number of cases. Some of that is certainly a response to changing markets, but some of it is choices we made individually and negotiated collective choices.

    It's not too surprising, I don't think, that it's harder to move 1000 in two years not just because of increased competition but also increased ambivalence about convention presence or demos or distribution into game stores or all the things that I notice publishers being ambivalent about now. I'm not sure we have a better model for producing lots of sales that can replace "sell from my personal website" or "sell through IPR after debuting at GenCon" even if those models don't work as well as they previously did. I'm not sure the next big publishing model has been stumbled upon yet, unless it's the Danger Patrol model, which hasn't come fully to fruition yet (though the amount of play the Alpha has seen is astounding and makes me optimistic) and isn't making John any money right now (though, eventually, perhaps).

      \n Is 1000 sales a realistic two-year goal for a new indie game from a previously unknown publisher?

      I'm basing my guesses on sales for How to Host a Dungeon. It wasn't promoted at all before Gen Con 2009, and I didn't have any games on the market before that. I don't expect 1000 sales in two years, but I'll come close to it (including PDF sales, anyway).

      My sales have come from these sources, in approximately equal proportions:
      * People in the extended indie community
      * People who obsessively scour the Internet for things related to the game's color
      * People who were marketed to, directly or indirectly, by me: Gen Con demos, the game\u2019s BoardGameGeek page, seeing it on RPG now, hearing me interviewed in a podcast (thanks, Rich!)
      * People who were "marketed" to by people who had already found my game: blog posts, forum posts, tweets and re-tweets, podcast mentions, and reviews

      Over time, the latter two have grown in importance, but the first two remain significant factors. Of the people who bought it, I'd say half were attracted by the game itself (solo play, interesting mechanics, map enthusiasts and so on) and half were attracted by the color (old-school D&D, dungeon enthusiasts).

      So my totally wild guess is that an awesome indie game that comes from nowhere can realistically hope to sell 200 copies in two years.
      If there\u2019s a community out there beyond the indie crowd who are hungry for what your game delivers, that\u2019s good for another200.
      Marketing to that community is worth another 200 (though potentially less or much, much more depending who that community is).
      Having people in your target spread the word for you is worth another 200.
      That\u2019s 800 copies.

      Ways to get more:
      * Find a bigger community that\u2019s hungry for your game. I\u2019ll bet Andy K and Tony LB have some thoughts on this. \uf04a
      * Market the hell out of your game.
      * Be lucky

      All this is useless if you haven\u2019t yet asked *why* you want to sell 1000 copies. This is recipe for heartbreak if you fail to meet your goal, or if you meet your goal and find out that it doesn\u2019t actually make your life any better. On the other hand, there are lots of legitimate reasons to shoot for a high sales target as well.

      \n The Dark Realms RPG Universe, W.H.A.T.? Customizable RPG and Heroes Forever each sold over 1000 copies. But to be fair, they were all also released during a "different time" within the industry. 1998, 1997 and 1999 respectively. A lot has changed since then.
      \n
      \nThe Dark Realms had one printing of about 2200 books, which sold about 1600 of those before going into fire sale and liquidation type sales. A couple hundred ultimately went to con prizes and other give-away type thingys.
      \n
      \nW.H.A.T.? began with a photo copy run of 50. Started slowly sales wise, but after a couple of years and 14 follow up products, it had entered distribution substantially and sold through about 7000 copies before eventually being taken out of print. Originally with the intent to upgrade the game line past the pamphlet style books it had back then, then we failed to bring it back before it lost its momentum. We're still planning a relaunch (and soon), but have some odd duck marketing and distribution plans for it, so are still piecing together that particular puzzle. Its been out of print about 5 years now, officially.
      \n
      \nHeroes Forever had a substantial (for a small press) print run of covers made originally, but its interiors were always done in house in small run/pod fashion. Its nearly complete with its first run of covers (4000 of them) but I couldn't actually tell you how many "printings" that worked out to. Somewhere between 400 and 200 books per run.
      \n
      \nDark Realms rans its first 1000 within its first year. W.H.A.T.? did not, but was running substantially over 1000 a year at its peak. Heroes Forever "may" have hit 1000 after 2 years, but I kind of doubt it. It had an initial strong surge of sales into the distribution channels, then nearly died off without distribution, then has been slowly climbing back up in sales figures each year since shifting to a direct model.
      \n
      \nBut anyways, I would say that any pre 2002 being compared to anything post 2002, or thererabouts anyway, is an apples to oranges comparison anyways. Because the industry really began to change at that time as did how small/indie companies produced and distributed their goods.
