    Posted By: lordgoon
    The literate way to be non-sexist is to alternate between "him" and "her", "he and "she" etc. when using impersonal pronouns. It's really not rocket science.
    \nI think the literate way to be non-sexist is to determine who you want to talk to, think about the language and writing style that's going to best communicate with them, talk to them about whether those language choices are the correct ones, and then write consistently.

    There are a number of potential problems with "they," but there are also a number of potential problems with alternating between gender binaries. The most obvious of which is that you are reinforcing gender binaries, alienating transgendered readers (and a host of other readers). The other potential problem with alternating gender pronouns is that it can be confusing if you're referencing multiple player roles, leading to a lack of clarity about procedural stuff.

    It's not rocket science, because you're not building rockets, but dealing with the fact that language reinforces and structures our thinking about gender and agency is actually pretty complicated stuff. And it's an insulting mark of privilege to claim otherwise.

That isn't even the case for French, despite what l'Acad\xe9mie fran\xe7aise would have you believe. The OED is a good example of past usage dictating what's "grammatical" in modern English.

    Your second sentence implies that singular they is in fact a lapse in language, and not a stylistic choice you seem to highly attached to. I would disagree.

    Your third sentence, though, indicates that you believe it to be a stylistic choice that you don't agree with, one that grates on you. I think this is closer to reality.

    Sorry for the fuckerly tone; I'm rather passionate about this stuff (being a recently lapsed linguist and editor).

    The conventions of standard English are arbitrary, and as long as we recognize that, there are certainly some benefits to keeping the standard around and conforming to it most of the time.* But whenever diverging from the standard takes us further, either for reasons of ethics or intelligibility, we shouldn't be afraid to do so, or penalize others for it.

      Posted By: Joshua A.C. Newman
      Are you against neologism of any kind? Or just ones that matter?
      \nI can speak for myself, in this regard: I'm all for sie/hir/zie/sHe in theory. I love neologism, I love the idea of creating new vocabulary to express gender and sex.

      It's the issue that they strike me as inelegant and forced. Seeing them, I get the impression that they express more political thinking than they do linguistic thinking (if that makes sense... what I mean is still rather muddy in my head). Elegance is, like, the single most important feature of language to me. I want words and modes of speech that instantly hand fluency over to the reader. I want words that feel like they have a natural cadence to them.

      Sie & Hir alienate me. I think that it's because they're partially a hybridization of two genders, but then there's an "i" floating around in there. I get the impression that I ought to know why this word is the way it is, and that there's a radical political message embedded in it that I ought to know as well.

      sHe feels hokey to me, as any mid-word capitalization feels hokey to me - it was a super common trend in branding for a while, and I feel like it reeks of already antiquated nineties sensibilities.

      So, yeah: I'd love to feel comfortable reading new(ish) fabricated genderless pronouns. Sie, hir, zie and sHe really grate on me. Maybe that's an issue with me as a reader, or maybe it's an issue with those words not naturally evolving from common language. I dunno. I just know that if I saw them in any game text other than Shock: or Eclipse Phase, I'd be super weirded out. Seeing them in Shock: was fair enough, because, well: future shock. But it still caught my attention enough to mean that I had less attention focused on parsing the actual text (which might have contributed to any confusions I experienced with the game).

      \n After doing more research, this issue is quite nuanced.

      Many people believe that the he pronoun has been used for all sexes since forever. They are wrong. Singular they has been used for at least 600 years. You can trace back its usage as far back as Chaucer. Using he as a universal pronoun is relatively recent (less than 270 years).

      Some believe that using he as a universal pronoun was invented by male sexist grammarians. This is also incorrect. Anne Fisher, a feminist, is believed to be the first woman to write an English grammar book. And her popular guide, "A New Grammar", is believed to be the first to say that the pronoun he should apply to all sexes.

      Many great writers\u2026 Byron, Austen, Thackeray, Eliot, Dickens, Trollope and more\u2026 use singular they.

      So many people now use they as a singular pronoun, the way it was used for centuries before the use of he became popular, that dictionaries and grammar guides are rethinking their position against its use.

      R. W. Burchfield, editor of The New Fowler\u2019s Modern English Usage, has written that it\u2019s only a matter of time before singular they becomes standard English. Merriam-Webster\u2019s Collegiate Dictionary already finds singular they acceptable \u201ceven in literary and formal contexts,\u201d but the Usage Panel of The American Heritage Dictionary of the English Language isn\u2019t there yet.
