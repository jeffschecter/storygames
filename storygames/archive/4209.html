\n Just when I think I'm getting some traction with the Film Noir game, I go and see a classic movie like 'Out of the Past' and am blown away...not by the dialog or the actors or the sets (although those are all excellent). But by the subtleties of the movie.

A lot of games I am in tend to move towards violence or comedy. I think because those are "easy". It's easier to throw two people into bed or pull a gun on someone than it is to deal with human frailty.

So help me remember some games that find a way to get across the subtleties of human interactions. And how those games do that through their mechanics.

Dogs is about the only one I can think of at the moment; with its rules for raising and escalating. What other games am I missing?

    \n Subtlety, IMO, is more of a player skill thing, than a design consideration. Designers can do things to encourage subtlety (like choice of subject and particular play advice) but ultimately players are going to take the game and run it their own way. (In Dogs, for example, I have seen both very subtle and excessive drama.)

    So, IME, dramatic explosions are "easier", but that doesn't mean people are being lazy. Subtlety is just hard to pull off well.

    Posted By: DannyKCaesar, can you give an example of what you consider subtle?
    \nNo problem, Daniel. These are pretty subtle, but hopefully you'll get what I'm after.

    Double Indemnity: throughout the entire movie, Keyes always ends up lighting Neff's cigars for him. At the end of the movie when Keyes in dying, Neff lights a cigarette for him. It highlights a switch in the roles of the two characters.

    Out of the Past: Jeff Bailey/Markham is continually making references to Kathie moving, often because he can't control where she is or where she goes. "She came in from out of sun"...or..."You're like a leaf that the wind blows from one gutter to another."

    Gilda: Gilda practically does a striptease in front of the crowd at Johnny's casino. The people there just think she's drunk and she's pulled stunts to embarrass him in public before. But really she's trying to live up to Johnny's expectation that she's a tramp.

    Ace in the Hole: the entry to the "Indian Ruins" starts off free, but as the crowds start to swell to find out what happens to the trapped miner, the entrance fee raises to .25, and then .50 and finally to 1.00. It's indicative of the way people in the story are taking advantage of the misfortune of one man.

    You may think I'm crazy, but I'm looking for ways to push these types of reoccurring thematic events during storytelling/gameplay.

    A few games do this. Dogs has traits that get defined during play and reused. SotC has aspects that can be defined during play and reused.

    So perhaps "subtlety" wasn't a great word. I needed to work it out a little more to realize what I really meant.

    \n Assign trait slots to scenes and/or sessions. One player can "seed" a theme or motif. Others can add new ones (each using a slot) *or* tag an existing one. If you seed a trait your personal advantages from it are greater, but if you grow it everybody benefits.

    \nMalcolm, I *really* like where you are going here, but I feel like that's asking a lot of the players.

    If it helps, I'm using distributed control of the characters in this game. I was thinking of actually writing down traits or events that happened on the character/situation cards during play and then allowing them to be reused in later scenes if they could be narrated in. I realize that you lose the spontaneity, but in exchange you may gain a sense of reoccurring story elements.

    I'm playing around with a plotting device I read in John Gardner's 'The Act of Fiction'. To use your example, if you've smoked a cig when confronting a parental figure, maybe having that character smoke a cig later will bring up the same theme of the parental figure around the table. Or maybe confronting a parental figure will have the character looking for a smoke. Obviously the more it is used the more powerful (or lame) it can become.

    I realize that this is a lot more about storytelling than role-playing, but that's the direction I'm trying to go in.

    \n When Remi was working on Young Adult RPG in Game Chef, he wanted to use the improv principle of reincorporation. But there's a saying that goes with it. Something like, "Once, Twice, Thrice--then out." The second time something comes up, it becomes a theme, the third time, it's complete. After that it is not funny or interesting anymore.

    He or someone suggested giving a bonus the second time something was brought into play, then a bigger bonus for the third time--then dropping it after that. No more bonus.

    It sounds like you want to tie simple elements to themes in play. In one PtA game, there was a photograph of my character's dead wife in his lab over his desk. It was sitting near a bottle containing a demon he'd caught. Later in the game when it came into question as to whether the demon had killed the wife, or perhaps was the wife, that picture gained more intense meaning.

    Also, traits in Breaking the Ice often come to gain more weight over time as you get to know the characters. And crossing things off in Shooting the Moon, can be hard core.

    \n The two games I've played that really systematically encourage some of what you're talking about are:

    1,001 Nights

    and

    Schizonauts

    The former does it in part because it's a three-tiered game, so rather than Player ---> Character, there's Player (Level 1) ---> Character (Level 2) ---> Character's Character (Level 3). So the Level 2 characters, who all envy each other, are doing all this subtle stuff to each other by way of their Level 3 characters, because it's the only means they have. But really, the Players may be subtly digging at each other, too. And it can get even worse/better, when the Level 3 characters start disguising themselves as other people...

    Anyway, I'm not explaining myself well, but check out 1,001 Nights. It's an awesome game that doesn't get enough attention, IMHO.

    Schizonauts I can't even begin to explain, as I don't have a copy of the rules text and only played it once (1,001 Nights I've played twice, and own, and want to play more of). But basically it's this game where you each Player has two Characters, only they might really be the same person, or different facets of the same person, but even the Player doesn't know...
