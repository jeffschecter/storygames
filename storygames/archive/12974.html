\n So! What RPGs engage with culture?

Here's why I'm asking. I'm delivering a presentation to a cross-cultural research centre in October. It's gonna be about RPGs and culture. I'm particularly interested in games that position player characters in "other" cultures, and games that have culture clash as a theme.

So, like, Empire of the Petal Throne. Kagematsu. Simon C reminded me about Sign In Stranger. What else? Not just "good" examples. Games that screwed it up are welcome too. (Er, but not Racial Holy War thanx.)

And to make this thread more than just a list of titles... anyone have experiences where they "got culture" out of a game experience? What happened? How much did the game itself contribute to the experience?

Thanks! - Morgan, who is mostly just a lurker

    \n Hi "Morgan the Lurker"!

    I would say that any role-playing game engage with culture in some way. By inventing fictional cultures we get an alternative to our own. We immerse in the fiction, making it as "real" as possible, and at the same time regard it in relation to our own cultural codes. I find that a very important and exciting part of role-playing games, even though it is little spoken of.

    As for games relating to "other" cultures of our world, I am sure you will get your lists of titles ...

    Posted By: morgueEven more so when you enlarge your definition of culture to include things like gender and generation -
    Gender and generation, and local and social origin, are certainly central elements in human cultures. Food and history are central too. Religion will always be, with its highly visible rites of baptism, marriage, funerals ...

    In Norway there is two different ways of writing "Norwegian", that makes for differences in regional culture (and countless ways of speaking it, that makes for differences in local culture).

    All of this may be emulated in role-playing games. Most game don't deliberately do it, but some designers work quite deliberately with it (I do). And some game masters work with such themes, and have their players dive into them with relish ...\n

    Posted By: morgueI'm particularly interested in games that position player characters in "other" cultures, and games that have culture clash as a theme.
    \nOne of the best recent examples is "Barbarians of Lemuria", a fantastic game that puts culture front and center in character creation.

    When you create your character, you pick from one of about a dozen cultures (all fantastical). Within those cultures is a description of the culture (about half a page), followed by "advantages that members of this culture share", and "disadvantages that members of this culture share". You automatically get one "good" one for free, but you can get a second advantage (or other bonuses) by taking a cultural disadvantage. Some were physical (bonuses/drawbacks to certain activities like running, climbing etc based on cultural roots), others were mental or social. There were like 4-5 ads and disads for each culture.

    I really liked this idea: Unlike most pick-and-choose ads/disads system, they were very tied to culture, and culture in turn tied to character.

    Like, I think this would be a perfect system to rework to a more realistic/historic game or even fantasy excursion as well, particularly well-researched games set in say Africa or Europe: Like, I would make a small list for Mali characters, another for Almoravid characters/Berber tribes, another for Ghanan based on that empire, etc.

    Or, say families/domains within ancient Japan or China, etc.

    Posted By: morgueMe too. The katana-ninja axis is wildly strong in (Western) RPGs. Not at all sure why - partly it was just an 80s thing with James Clavell and American Ninja, but surely there's more to it than that?
    \nActually, a VERY important point to remember is that no matter how much Westerners go goober over katanas and ninjas, our goobering/appropriation/fascination has nothing - NOTHING - on Japan's own fascination/goobering/appropriation of ninjas and katanas.

    I was reading this book a few years ago, written by this historian who read through quartermaster reports during World War II. There were a crazy number (ten thousand? something like that) of military-issued katana (given to officers) that were returned to HQ as "damaged", all with detailed written reports. A majority of them were from officers who simply assumed all the legendary katana-stories were true, and were totally surprised when the katana broke (usually not shatter, but curve left/right and become unusable) after they forcefully struck a rock wall or steel pipe/plating with it instead of simply slicing it in half like it were nothing.

    The fiction behind katanas and ninjas, especially fun/action/drama, goes crazy with them.

    We only picked up on the fever that the Japanese themselves expressed towards those bits of native culture. Better that, I guess, than panty vending machines or age-based salary systems.

    Posted By: musashi74Well - now I'm wondering - have there been any Japanese RPG's that deal with their feudal past in a straightforward manner, or any straightforwardly historical Japanese RPG's of any stripe?
    \nI did some searching: The answer is "not... really":
    Out of the entire history of Japanese RPGs and all known indie RPGs:

    2 games focus on "jidaigeki" (historical TV show drama) style play. This is the closest there is. Fictionalized, but no magic.
    One was pro-made in the early/mid 90s, daikatsugeki.
    The other is an indie made game, hiden no koe:
    http://www.ps-hiroshima.com/rpg/doujin/hiden01.htm

    1 game focuses on the heian period, but includes magic and stuff. Heian-yakyoku, an indie made game:
    http://www.ps-hiroshima.com/rpg/doujin/yaeanyakyoku.htm
    There are two supplements for other games (Doublecross 2e, Demon Parasite) which have "playsets" for a heian-themed game, but again it's a highly fictionalized, magicy-fantasy game, even if it uses real place and people names at times.

    1 game focuses on the sengoku period, kind of in the vein of the recent boom of interest in the sengoku period (caused by the Sengoku Basara game series, recent TV dramas, etc): But while it features real historic people and battles, it's also "exXxtreme'd" up a bit with magic, ninjas, etc. Sengoku Kamui, a professionally made game. Cute strategic combat system.

    Other games (like the recent FEAR game Tenka Ryoran) might also have historic places and people, but its extreme'd up with magic and time travel and stuff.

    So basically, the straight answer is "No". But also, "two games, but you have to squint a bit to see it", if you count "emulation of historic movies and TV dramas" as equal to "straightforward consideration of historical matter", which honestly I kinda do (no mecha or lasers).

    Neither here nor there, though. Still, culture aside, Barbarians of Lemuria still has one of the best "your culture comes out in actual gameplay" mechanics (as above) for basic RPGs. And I'm reading How We Came To Live Here, which is super-immersive, but also requires a lot more attention and work in the learning of its unique playstyle (in other words, a lot more upfront learning in order to understand how the game works enough to play; its not a game you can bust out and just start running from scratch, while BoL kinda is).\n

    Posted By: musashi74I'm not saying this is wrong, but part of me had chalked this up to the constant mysticizing of foreign cultures and / or the influence of Dungeons and Dragons.
    \nThrough examples above, I didn't list nearly all of the "historic Japan... WITH MAGIC!" games that exist, as there are in fact many, and I own 4 such games published by major companies in just the past three or so years alone - many of them are very creative as well.

    Not a Western/mysticizing thing. Turns out that Japan does it as well about their own culture. So it's more like a "magic and action is Awesome" thing, which is likely universal.

    There is one major difference worth noting, though. In the L5Rs and Oriental Adventures, they need Monster Hoardes for the players to fight. because there's no goblins and orcs, they tend to take "cultural monsters" and send waves of them crashing against the PCs. 5 Rakshasas/Rakusatsu attack! The next room contains a kappa/cold woman/oni! etc. In real mythology (lol for that combination of words), with one single exception (Momotaro, who basically travels to a giant island of many Oni and kills them all), the monsters are solitary, unique and badass. Like how they are portrayed in Lamentations of the Flame Princess.

    So in those Japanese RPGs which take a historic setting (1700s Edo, 1500s Japan, 900AD, early 1900s Shanghai, etc) and add magic and monsters and "Awesome!", monster hunting is almost always a "many players vs one monster" affair. They play out more like Call of Cthulhu, with the investigation and elimination of one monster, even in the high-action fightier games (ref: "Ayakashi"). That's the only real difference I've found.

    This may be getting way off Morgan's original topic, but I will take comfort in the broad definition of "culture" and go from there

    Andy, I think that your comment that action+magic=awesome may be universal is really interesting and probably right. Also, the notion of generating hoards of 'cultural monsters' happens just as much with western game settings, I'd argue. I mean, certain versions of D&D are "Medieval Europe BUT WITH MAGIC AND MONSTERS!", likewise the Conan stories (and the sword and sorcery settings inspired by them and others) were "whatever historical period/culture Howard thought would be cool BUT WITH MAGIC AND MONSTERS!" Finally, you also see the same "solitary badass monster --> species of monsters to kill in droves" thing happens with western monsters (Medusa, Hydra, Dragons, et cetera).

    I think the latter part might be more of an artifact of gaming than of cultural attitudes, since there is (or was anyway) an expectation that since you have these generic rules that are supposed to handle whatever situation might come up, it was jarring to have "The one, single Hydra" as a foe, since you knew other folks in other groups would be fighting the same one, only hyrda. As for the former (MAGIC!) there's probably a lot more cultural stuff going on there, since you see it in various forms of entertainment, and I'm starting to ponder why you see modern stuff amping up the supernaturalism compared to the present, but more subdued (I guess?) supernaturalism in myth. Is it just because we like everything EXXXXTREME in our current culture?
