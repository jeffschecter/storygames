    \n If you're after simplicity, I think The Pool is a far superior game, and even simpler.

    (And, heck, if your GM is against the whole narration-passing nature of the game, even that's no problem. A shame, perhaps, but not a problem. Just do this, and you've made the Pool into a "traditional" game:

    1. Never choose the "Monologue" option. Always take a die instead.
    2. On an opposed roll between two players, a tie means the GM chooses one of these, roughly in this order:
    * Something interrupts the contest (possibly making it moot, or maybe changing the nature of it, escalating it, or similar)
    * Both sides achieve what they want, simultaneously
    * If neither of the above makes sense, both reroll until there is a clear winner. Either may add more dice from her pool before rerolling.

    There you go.)

    \n Now you've got me thinking. Here's a way to use Risus cliches with (anti)Pool mechanics:

    * When you need to roll, pick up any number of dice from an appropriate cliche.
    * You need a 5 or a 6 to succeed.
    * On a success, you lose the dice you rolled. (Your cliche effectively gets "weaker"!)
    * On a failure, if your cliche is not currently at "full strength" (its starting value), replenish it, adding one die to it.

    In a contest between players, if there is a tie (both succeed or both fail), each player chooses:

    * Give up. Keep your dice, and add one to your cliche (up to its starting value, as usual).
    * Push forward. Discard all the dice you rolled, and prepare to roll again.

    If you push forward and your opponent gives up, you've won, no new roll necessary.

    There it is!

    \n You use Risus's character creation with the resolution mechanics I posted (they're like the anti-Pool variant of the Pool).

    They would definitely address your issues of death spiral and variability!

    However, note that The Pool doesn't do "gradual wearing down of opponents", like Risus. It's always one roll per conflict (although the rules for ties will create many rerolls, for mounting excitement or challenge).

    If the GM or players aren't prepared for the concept of conflict resolution, they may struggle with this part. (In these rules, when you defeat someone, they likely end up with more dice than you, so if the group doesn't commit to the concept of decisive conflict resolution, and just roll again instead, you'll never resolve anything. Each roll has to have a clear impact in the fiction, and settle a conflict of interests decisively.)

    \n The death spiral is a feature of the design. Opponents that are too challenging should be teamed up against, or disengaged, possibly to allow fictional maneuver for advantage, or to overcome by some means other than direct combat. If used, lucky shots and pumping can also be employed to overcome tougher opponents.

    \n Jeff,

    You are quite correct that what you're suggesting is full-on game design. I've designed a few games which do this (like my "Land of Nodd"), but it's a pretty major undertaking.

    I imagine the simplest would be some kind of collaborative scene-setting procedure (a la Fiasco or Archipelago) and then using the die rolls instead of the "that might not be so easy..." phrase. If you get to play your character, but others can decide when you roll the dice, that would work pretty well for a GMless scenario.

    You still need some kind of game procedure to produce consistent play; but a fictional scenario/setup and a group of creative players with the proper skills can sometimes substitute for that!
