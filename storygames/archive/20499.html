\n I'm pleased to say that after lots and lots of work, Microscope Explorer is out! You can get it and start playing right now.



It's over 140 pages of new material for Microscope, including three new spin-off games (Chronicle, Union and Echo), methods for collaborative world-building, Oracles to randomly generate history seeds, tips for improving play, plus a bunch more.

Time travel, family trees, reincarnation -- there's a lot of new stuff to try out.\ufeff If you've got any questions, fire away.

    I was wondering: Usually, you define a start and end period. Is there a reason to use the End Period as a "hard limit". Sometimes I think, it would be exciting to go beyond that, sort of leave an open end and see what happens.
    Is there a good reason this doesn't work?
    \nDeciding the end at the start of the game is actually a powerful part of what makes Microscope work. It gives us the freedom to do big things in the middle because other players don't have to wonder how it derails what's going to happen at the end -- it can't.

    It's also critical that you make that decision about the end at the very start of play, before you know a lot about the history or have a lot of vested interest in the fiction. If everyone already cared about the history and knew a lot about it, agreeing on a ending would be much harder. It's a bit of game design sleight-of-hand.\n

    \n Ben!

    I am working on a game about apocalypses myself, with an oracle like "story-seed" generator. I found that thinking of apocalypses in its original, greek meaning revelation or uncovering is a lot more fruitful.

    Apocalypses are not just change, they are complete overturning and upheaval. Topsy-turvy not of what elements can constitute society, but what can constitute society at all. It is the end of an age, and all that was in that age, heralding something new. At the same time.

    So if floods cause the apocalypse, they must either bring about something completely new, change the old for good, uncover something which changes everything, or leave not a trace of the old left. And maybe them all.

    ---

    The oracles gives us plenty of calamity, but maybe there is something about how you go about exploring the apocalypse? The bookmarks you set are quite important. Maybe the apocalypse will lack oomph if you can see "past it"/"before it" with certainty.

    ---

    maybe a last trend should be added to the apocalypse-oracle-phrase: which ends in
    upheaval
    utter destruction
    complete corruption
    upliftning
    something
    something

    so you could get "spread of invaders gives false hope to empire of ziggurats which ends in complete corruption." Not elegant, i know.

    \n Thanks 3xJ!\n
    I found that thinking of apocalypses in its original, greek meaning revelation or uncovering is a lot more fruitful.
    \nA long time ago I ran an entire superhero campaign that hinged on that dual meaning, i.e. the threatened apocalypse is not what you think, it was really a revelation, not destruction. Good stuff.

    You can definitely interpret an Apocalypse in a positive, revolutionary way, even if there is tragedy and trial along the way. The Oracle might declare that disease ravages the colony world, but it's up to you to decide whether you want to have a new society emerge or close on a barren dustball. You can interpret the same result in a lot of different ways or just examine different aspects of the same history (do we focus on the collapse, the rebuilding, etc). That's where setting the bookends is key.
