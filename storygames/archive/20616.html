\n On a bit of an oWoD nostalgia trip lately, sorry.

I'm hoping to crowd-source some ideas about how best to convert oWoD games to the PBTA engine. Something that keeps enough of the original feel to draw oWoD players, but is solidly based in the Apocalypse Engine.

The simplest and minimal effort "conversion" would be simply replacing the resolution mechanic with the 10+, 7-9, and 6- from PBTA for any actions and use some basic dice pool to bonus conversion formula, like say: dice pool / 3 (round down). So a oWoD dice pool of 1-2 would be +0, 3-5 would be a +1, a 6-8 would be a +2, a 9-10 dice pool would be a +3. That of course has a lot of implications to the oWoD systems, merits, flaws, difficulties, etc. that might not be obvious at first. That might not matter to some, but it could be very important to others. It could work, I guess, but it would probably be easier just to use the originals as written.

The thematic conversion is the approach I think would work best. Take the basic concepts and build a PBTA game around that. But then you get into questions of what's important and how much the original oWoD rules inform the game.

The life-cycle of most oWoD player characters is fairly simple to pull out of the various games. The supernatural thing must gather energy and store it for later use to fuel their supernatural powers. There were various ways to gather that energy, ranging from benign to harmful to deadly.

Most oWoD supernatural things also seem to be doomed in some way. They gain or lose points indicating how close to succumbing to the negative side of their supernatural nature. Humanity, Banality, Paradox, etc. This is mostly tied directly into using their powers. Something like failing a "use powers" roll could inflict a doom point on the character. Get too many and your character is gone. So you'd also need an "avoid doom" move or mechanic to clear out those points.

The various splats/archetypes/races/classes were roughly equivalent across the lines. The Ass-Kicking Rebel. The Primal Outsider. The Crazy. The Secretive Ugly. The Artist. The Super In Touch With Their Powers One. The Haughty Leader. These are basically the same across most of the oWoD lines. Some are combined or subverted. In PBTA these are easy to see as playbooks. How many and how close to the originals vs broad archetypes is up in the air.

Though I do like the idea presented in Uncharted Worlds where you have two intersecting playbooks. You could do one as the creature type (vampire, werewolf, fairy, etc) and one as the archetype (haughty leader, the crazy, etc).

Coming up with the five stats most PBTA games use is really only a matter of picking names a la Simple World. You could make an argument for each line having their own evocative set of names, but considering the standardized set of stat in oWoD, probably not.

Basic moves are fairly simple. Between the various PBTA games there's enough to cover most, if not all, the things you'd be doing without bringing too many moves into play.

Advanced moves are also fairly simple. You'd need one move to gather energy (with a list to pick from that includes some version of "being gentle" so you could wrap the benign and harmful gathering of energy into a single move), one move to use your powers (which could result in doom), and one to avoid doom (reduce those doom points).

Random other elements like particular backgrounds, merits, flaws, etc could be handled with simple bonus moves (+1 when you X), stat swap moves (use X stat instead of Y stat when you Z), and narrative justification moves (now you can X). If they even need to be converted at all.

The only really non-obvious bit would be how in-depth to treat the various powers. Having 5-8+ major game lines, each with 10-20+ broad types of powers, and each of those broad types having anywhere from 5-20+ specific powers is a bit maddening to think about...

And that's where the hack gets so involved and complex that we'd loop back around to the same conclusion as the minimalist approach above, to wit, "It could work, I guess, but it would probably be easier just to use the originals as written."

Any thoughts, ideas, constructive feedback, etc are more than welcome and appreciated.

EDIT: Yes, I am aware of Monsterhearts, no it doesn't come close to doing what I want.

    \n Well, that's fairly easy.

    For PBTA it comes down to the resolution mechanic and the pick-up game style playbooks. But mostly the resolution mechanic. I like that it's player facing. That it's straightforward and universal (mostly). That there's no null result ("no") and that it pushes the fiction forward with every roll ("yes" "yes, but" "no, and"). But also that it's easy to interact with. There's no modifiers to target numbers, it's almost always just 10+, 7-9, 6-. That the bonuses are in a small range (-2 to +3 typically).

    For WoD it comes down to the setting and the openness of the system despite the fact that I loathe a lot of the mechanics. Almost across the board the specific mechanics of how you do things in WoD can die in fire. The distinction I'm making between system and mechanics here is macro vs micro. The system is open. You can do anything your group and the GM wants, i.e. the game isn't micro-focused on delivering one exact play experience. It's "you're vampires in modern New York, here's a rough outline of local supernatural politics... what do you want to do?" (open), instead of "you're vampires in a monster high school and you each have a secret crush on another player and your teachers are the enemy... go" (focused).

    The only other way I can think to describe it right now (it's late here), is the difference between a sandbox and a pre-planned adventure. WoD feels more like a sandbox, most story games feel like pre-planned adventures. It's "here's the rough setup, what do you want to do?" vs "here's what this game is all about, the mechanics are entirely focused here, what do you do in this tiny pre-defined space?"

    Nothing wrong with either. I think I just want the open sandbox feel from the system with the specific mechanics to be innovative story game stuff.

    \n What's to explain? In PbtA, the "openness of the system", as you describe it, doesn't exist. I agree it's a huge part of WoD play, it's a really amazing strength of WoD! But the PbtA system channels, quite powerfully, all player action into broad but predetermined courses, called "Moves". It's the fundamental mechanic of the game. There is no attempt on AW's part to be a generic post-apocalypse game; it doesn't even remotely function as one if you try to use it that way. Monsterhearts, as you rightly note, doesn't function as a generic supernatural-creatures game, it's a teenaged supernatural romance game. Why? The Moves. There are no PbtA implementations worth talking about with generic Moves, though many have tried and failed. The whole inertia of PbtA which is the structure of play doesn't even develop unless you're putting players into the log flume of some really solid, forward-moving Basic Moves that narrow character action into solid choices and consequences. It's important in WoD that maybe you don't have enough dice to throw at a situation and you need to think of another way forward. In PbtA, you just DO IT and the consequences of you not hitting your mark are the game.

    \n OK, long answer:

    I think the bigger differences are twofold:
    1) coherent vs incoherent
    2) established game setting vs dynamically generated setting

    Let me unpack.
    1)Regarding coherence, Vampire can theoretically handle every conceivable modern vampire story out of the box, from a sexy love story with young adults to a horror movie/road movie mashup where a few bad vamps go to New Orleans for fun and.get in way over their heads. Plus all the usual games of a random group of PC vampires in a random cool town. You can do lots of stuff with it but it's not optimized for most modes of play.

    In practice, the game rules don't provide a ton of support for specific kinds of story but a good GM with willing players can do a lot.

    In most PbtA games, there's a specific mode of play, so Urban Shadows really shines at investigation, politicking and dirty business in an urban fantasy setting. Monsterhearts really shines at stories of twisted love and self-exploration. The mechanics and Moves reinforce this focus reasonably well. If you suddenly decided to tell a story about ice-cold urban politics with Monsterhearts, you could, but it would take more work and a lot of spitballing by the GM.

    2) established vs dynamic game setting
    Vampire is famous for its incredibly detailed setting and "metaphor" ranging from prehistory to the future of the setting. This can be very cool, it makes a quick shorthand where I can pitch a game based on a Sabbat war pack or Anarchs during the fall of the Anarch Free State, and everyone knows what to expect and what kind of character to make. It has lots of downsides, too.

    On the other hand, most or maybe all PbtA games require lots of input from players and even content explicitly generated by Moves. When a new NPC shows up, the GM can't really have everything planned out because this generated content can totally change them. The nature of the setting itself can take a while to get established with some setting elements getting changed or discarded after the first session.

    I like playing both but find the coherent nature and player input make PbtA much easier to run as GM.

This despite the dicing mechanism being very similar to WoD: a dice pool with a binary outcome of pass/fail or rather win/lose, plus flourishes such as the rare chance of a total/overwhelming success.     Here, the difference between Sorcerer (once you figure out how to play it, sure) and WoD is when and why you roll dice, not how. Like, in WoD you roll to check how well you perform a somewhat difficult action, whereas in Sorcerer you roll because we figured out there's a conflict of a sort going on: danger, urgency, whatever.     Of course, the above is more art than science: sometimes it's a tough call (is it a conflict or not?), sometimes you're hard pressed to find something interesting to say (how can this situation move forward on a success, and how can it move forward on a failure?)\xa0\u2014 and that's one reason why AW is so good. Roll these dice, and here's how it could move forward on a clear-cut hit, or on a mixed hit \u2014 on a miss, see the attached "how shit might go wrong" list for suggestions.     \n Most of us consider task and conflict resolution to be something different than you've just described.

    In short, they have little to do with the granularity or scope of individual roles, as you've said above.

    Here's the beginning of a classic blog post from Vincent Baker on the topic: hardly the last word, but a common and basic starting place:\n
    In task resolution, what's at stake is the task itself. "I crack the safe!" "Why?" "Hopefully to get the dirt on the supervillain!" What's at stake is: do you crack the safe?

    In conflict resolution, what's at stake is why you're doing the task. "I crack the safe!" "Why?" "Hopefully to get the dirt on the supervillain!" What's at stake is: do you get the dirt on the supervillain?

    Which is important to the resolution rules: opening the safe, or getting the dirt? That's how you tell whether it's task resolution or conflict resolution.

    Task resolution is succeed/fail. Conflict resolution is win/lose. You can succeed but lose, fail but win.
    \nThe short essay goes on to address exactly the issue of scope.
