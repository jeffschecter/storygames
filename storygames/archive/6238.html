\n Ages ago I wrote up an actual play about a glorious Red Box Hack session we had. I spoke of something I called the rumour system (or there abouts) and promised to share it with you. It has taken me a little while, but here it is.
\n
\nRumor has it.
\n
\nWhen I looked at the RBH i fell in love with it, because here was finally the game-rules I needed to recreate that nostalgic dungeoncrawl I so dream of. I did some tweaking, and one of the things I did was to try to mechanically tie the time spent in the village into the dungeon crawl. The idea for this has long been simmering in my head, and there were two reasons why I wanted to do this
\n
\n1. If we are going to play a dungeon-crawl, why not just cut straight to the dungeon? Why play through the whole here is the village sequence.
\n
\n2. I want the pre-dungeon village sequence, but I want it to have a bit more purpose than "just some roleplaying".
\n
\nI had remade all the classes into a set that fit my preference of setting (pseudo-european-sterotypical/old skool fantasy): Barbarian, Priest, Warrior, Mage, and Scoundrel. I restructured them a bit to get more granuality and to do something more than just changing the names of the orignals.
\n
\nOne of the bits I added to them where someting I called Domains. This is simply put a set of circumstances in which this character type excels in. In these circumstances the character gains a +2 bonus, and it is within these domains the characters can look for rumors. (the Barbarian for instance has the domain: The Wilderness.)
\n
\nwhich leads us to the meat of this post. The rumors.
\n
\nWhen in the village or town, prior to setting out for the dungeon, the adventurers may go looking for rumors and local legends surrounding the dungeon. You play this out, flavored by the Domain which the character has. At some point the player has to decide what the character is looking for:
\n
\nA secret - something concrete about the dungeon that is useful (location of secret doors or entrances, known traps ect.)
\n
\nTales of treasure - this adds a new treasure to the dungeon.
\n
\nRumours and Tales - a number of rumor tokens (works like awesome tokens) that when in use gives the bouns but requiers that the player adds details to the dungeon.
\n
\nThe player rolls and compares the result against the awesome token table. How to interpret the results varies a bit depending on which rumour the character is looking for.
\n
\nA Secret.
\nThe player needs only win one token or more. The DM has to give the player a secret fact about the dungeon. this has to be true and helpfull. If there are no more secrets to uncover the DM has to tell the player before the character looks for it.
\n
\nTales of treasure.
\nThe value of treasure depends on the amount of tokens won. The DM needs to come up with a treasure on the fly, and create a short story for it. Alternatvily he can let the player describe this. The DM then needs to place this in the hands/room of one of the monsters. (negative tokens means that one of the treasures in the dungeon sinks in value).
\n
\nRumours and Tales.
\nThe player gets a bounch of tokens according to the table. This work like awesom tokens except the player has to describe how a detail form the dungeon aids the character or hinders his opponent. This detail only works once (the duration of the bonus) and the player has to come up with new details for every token.
\n
\nAnd there you have it. Nothing too fancy or revolutionary. But it works.

    \n Neat!

    I did something else in adapting the rules, which might be fun to mention here. I'll put it back into RBH terms, so that it makes sense to all.

    The monster has more traits than usual, and some of the traits the Monster has come from places of power that the monster has 'tamed'. To get an edge in the fight against the monster, the adventurers can find these places and botch up the connection the monster has.

    Finding and botching up this stuff makes for little mini-quests that can be discrete unto themselves.
