\n So, I'm reading comments by the estimable Robin Laws about the new Beowulf trailer and it leads to this wondering: are Zombies the secret ingredient? Is there any gaming premise that cannot be mashed up successfully by simply adding zombies to the mix? Which?

Which then leads to the generalized issue: what mashups don't work? Why? And why are zombies so darn applicable? (My first thought is that it's because they are genre-neutral in a strange way that, say, vampires or werewolves are not.)

    \n Some recent episode of the Dragon's Landing Inn podcast discussed zombie campaigns, and came to the conclusion that zombies are really a "Players vs Environment" rather than a "Players vs Threat" genre. In the absence of a zombie lord (in which case you'd be back to a traditional "evil necromancer" plot), zombies become a plot device and a restricting factor rather than the threat itself. The threat always comes from somewhere else - the military/government complex, the Umbrella Corporation's operatives, other survivors, lack of supplies, yourself and your fear.

    As such, as has been phrased here in many different ways, zombies become the background and the characters provide the contrast. Thus, you can use it as a mashup ingredient to great effect, much like Joss Whedon added a sprinkle of sci-fi to his western and made Firefly.

    \n Often times, a large game company will realize that their "play design" lacks focus. It's not clear if people will run a social campaign, or a dungeon crawl, or missions for Mr. Johnson. Adding "mindless" hordes to the WORLD (regardless of mechanics) provides an enemy that all people can agree is bad. While zombies can offer what Jason is saying (and wow, I wish they would), most companies don't strive for this.

    It's not a slight on their part. But it's a convenient out. It's an easy way to make bad guys that any GM can drop into the game world. And I can name 10 games right now where they were added as an after-thought.

    28 Days Later did an incredible job of reinventing the fear of zombies in what Danny Boyle calls, "the banality of modern life."

    And I would love to see more games where "mutants" and "hordes" either take on more personality or more impact on the world around them. The Walking Dead comic book could be SOOO much stronger with the right POV (and with less talking from the lead character) and the right goals.

    Ninja are prats.

    \n Not just zombies, but the undead in general are an excellent oppositional force providing a black and white duality between hero and villain, even when the heroes themselves are villains now finding themselves in an anti-hero position. Even the vile thief doesn't want to see the town they reside in completely eradicated by the walking death. The great thing about zombies also, is that for the players themselves, the fear that they encompass can really get to the players in a well orchestrated suspenseful session.
    \n
    \nI've always liked them, that's why I ran a campaign for a year and a half, which came to be known as the Armies of the Undead. For months on end they never saw light or another breathing creature, and of course once light was in their grasp, an unfortunate event put the countryside under eternal darkness. In the end, the players killed each other, and the survivors were taken to Ravenloft. Job well done.
