\n I've been thinking that it's been pretty rare that I've been actually scared by a book, film, or story. It does happen, but just not that often. I'm wondering if any of you have had any game moments where you were actually scared, a bit frightened, or just freaked out. I've played a few horror genre games, and often I'll be role-playing the fear my character experiences either narratively or mechanically, but I've never had any similar experience myself as a player. Has anyone been lucky enough to experience a bit of thrill-ride fright or unsettling creepiness? Any details would be awesome. Thanks!

    \n At least two decades ago, playing Chill in the wee hours (~2 am of an all-night session) by candlelight. Can't remember the plot or situation; just remember how quiet the house was, how we were all focussed on the table and play, and how one couldn't really see much outside of the pool of candlelight. Definite creepiness; nothing's come close since--though I've been startled and a bit anxious in some boffer LARPs (but never in a MET parlor LARP, go figure...).

    \n I was 13 or 12 years old. My best friend Glenn and our respective 9 year old brothers were playing RedBox D&D using B2: Keep On The BorderLands until the wee hours of the morning.

    I remember they got to the part with an evil temple, and the place is filled with zombies. I tried to make the living dead as scary and merciless as possible in each encounter.

    An air conditioning unit just outside of the townhouse kicked in, causing the plate glass/sliding glass door to momentarily *KAchunk*. I think I leaped about a foot in the air.

    Posted By: jessecoombsI've played a few horror genre games, and often I'll be role-playing the fear my character experiences either narratively or mechanically, but I've never had any similar experience myself as a player.
    \nOne of the ways I've found to bridge the gap between the character's experiences and the player's experiences is to use a mechanic that instills some sort of fear or nervousness in the player. Since the stupid federal government told me it was "illegal" to write a game with Russian Roulette as the central mechanic, I went with Jenga instead.

    This post over here is answering a different question, but definitely hits on why Jenga works to bridge the gap. The physical nature of it is hard to ignore and drags the player experience right in line with the characters.

    So, with that little bit of self-promotion out of the way (buy Dread!), check this out . . .\n
    Posted By: jessecoombsHa. Definitely a lot of external stuff, like darkened rooms and candles. I wonder if it's possible to play a scary game during the middle of the day?
    \nIt is! Or at least in my experience it has been possible. I much prefer a dark quiet place to play, and that's how I used to try to set things up whenever I played Dread with friends. But years of demoing the game under harsh con lighting and in crowded rooms, I've discovered that a horror game can be played to full, terrible effect under almost any conditions as long as the players are ready to buy in. The player buy in is the killer. If you don't have that, you don't have horror. But if you do have it, the atmosphere is far more durable than would be expected.
