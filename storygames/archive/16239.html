At Misery Tourism Games, we design role-playing games about vice, inhumanity and suffering. On this site you'll find terrible games about terrible people in terrible situations.

We don't design games to make a moral point or push an agenda. We don't design games to offend you or your sociology professor or your congressman. We do it because we believe there is fun to be had in exploring tragedy and depravity with your friends in the safety of your kitchen, den or mother's basement. Yes, fun. These are fun games. Or they should be.

Have fun.

    Posted By: stefoid
    One question - why a secret ballot?
    \nThe Secret Ballot allows both the players and the characters to act anonymously. This helps to shield them from guilt and responsibility for their choice. This is supposed to mimic the anonymity and diffuse responsibility present at a Klan rally or in a lynch mob. This lack of forthrightness should add to the level of distrust and paranoia experienced both in and out of character.

    Also, it's a reference to sham elections under Jim Crow.
    \n
    Posted By: stefoid Is the ballot supposed to be something done in the fiction, or is it purely a game mechanic?
    \nThe ballot is conducted in character, within the fiction, at least superficially. This doesn't mean players can't allow out of character concerns to influence their vote, since they only have to justify their choice (in character) in the case of a tie, and then they can always lie (both about who they voted for and why).\n
    Posted By: stefoidAnd should the characters have any relationship with each other, which could be good for guilt-by-association.
    \nDo you mean general relationships, or shared secrets? The former can be introduced during the SFR scenes. The latter would be very difficult, considering the existing mechanics. Either way, I wanted the bond between the characters to be superficial and based upon pretense and outright lies.

    \n So, someone sent me a whisper me to ask why the vampire in Passing is real. I think this is a really important question, because the fact that the vampire actually exists could be interpreted as a covert endorsement of the idea that there actually is some basis behind racial fears. Since I think this is a valid concern, I thought I'd copy/paste part of my response into this thread. Perhaps I'll expand on this with a blog entry later.\n
    The vampire vs. no vampire issue was probably the hardest design decision I had to make.

    Basically, the vampire is real so that the players will feel, out of character, that they have the power to prevent the game from ending horrifically. This makes it possible for the players to engage emotionally with the hunt for the vampire, and makes them extra desperate to find him, to protect their consciences. That level of engagement really needs to be there in order for the game to work as an exploration of racial paranoia.

    If the vampire isn't there, it's a sad story about monstrous people doing a horrible thing. The players will know how things will turn out, and they will distance themselves from the game. They know who they are playing and what will happen. It's safe and antiseptic, like a historical reenactment. An alternate, less horrible outcome has to be there to give the game stakes, and its the stakes that create the fear and confusion that lets the players actually experience the irrationally of racism.

    Also, note that even though the vampire is real, he's still just another white guy. In fact, he's probably an old colonial European. So evil may be real, horrible things and horrible people do exist, but the racial (or ethnic or sexual) other isn't to blame.
