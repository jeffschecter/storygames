\n There's this meme that emerges every so often in the indie roleplaying community. The last time it came up was almost exactly a year ago (Feb 2008), which spun off the Owlbear criticism group, which ultimately came to naught. Remember that? That was back when I didn't like Graham. Seems so long ago. And then Ralph just brought it up again.

The meme goes like this: if we are tougher in our criticism of games, then indie games as a whole will get better.

While I think the intentions surrounding this meme are commendable (improving games, etc.), I believe the meme is potentially poisonous for a productive social environment. I'm going to try to explain why.

1. The Internet Makes This Tough

The indie games community exists in three places, 1) locally, in various communities, 2) at conventions, and 3) on the internet. While the first two places are great for communicating, the internet, as many people have noticed, is terrible, especially when it comes to talking about highly emotional and touchy topics like the quality of someone else's creative work. However, when people talking about being tougher in their criticism, they almost universally mean criticizing other people's work by posting things online.

2. Criticism is Inherently Caught Up in Status Games

Whatever the intent of criticizing someone else's work, it inevitably becomes a status issue if you are criticizing someone you don't know very well. In retrospect, I used to argue about theory with Ron on the Forge partially because it was a way to feel important, like I was actually contributing, and get attention from folks with a lot more social status than me. And when high status folks criticize folks with a small amount of status in the community, folks who have just come out with their first game, for example, it creates the sense that the old crowd isn't welcoming the new crowd in.

Other status issues can also manifest. For example, the fact that the first printing of Shock: was missing certain critical rules never threatened Joshua's status in the community because of his personal relationships with folks. But if a similar thing happened with a designer who was more on the outside, who lacked personal connections, a harshly critical environment could drive them away. Also, criticism of the work of folks you don't know happens differently than that of folks you do know. If you don't know someone, you're more likely to publicly post about your problems with their work. Whereas, if you do know someone well, you can tell them in person or email them or post publicly but in a more personal and direct manner.

3. Focusing on Game Texts Before People is Backwards

It's much easier to provide constructive criticism when you know someone reasonably well and/or if you're both relatively secure in your status with the community. But it sounds like Ralph doesn't know Greg Saunders from a hole in the ground, yet he implies that Greg's game is unplayable and should never have been carried by IPR. Ouch! Not a good way to begin a friendly, productive, helpful relationship with someone. Offering harsh, public criticism of a game written by someone you don't really know places your concerns about the game text before your concerns about the person behind it. Otherwise, why not contact a person directly, get to know them first, or approach public criticism more cautiously, because you don't know the author or how to offer criticism in a way that will really be helpful.

4. The Community is Only as Strong as Our Relationships

On the whole, I'm much more excited about a people first, texts second approach to offering criticism. The indie game community is very clearly not like the commercial book publishing community or even mainstream game publishing in that we almost inevitably move from taking interest in each other as fellow players, designer/publishers, and customers to taking interest in each other as people. This is our greatest strength, I would argue, far more important than having games that are flawlessly crafted.

    Based on my background in writing workshops and the like, 'people first' is absolutely the opposite of a good idea -- as a rule, in a workshop scenario (which is semi-public but generally socially closed) you should never talk about the person, and instead limit your critique almost exclusively to the work being critiqued. Which is that while there is definitely more value in a critique that fully incorporates a personal, engaged knowledge of the individual who produced the work... limiting yourself to giving or receiving that kind of critique is not going to do anyone any favours. I was proud of myself, mainly for two moments: (1) Challenging Dave Cleaver's pessimistic hermit to paddle out as far as the horizon to see the dawn and thereby regain his hope (in the fiction) -- and convincing Dave that he wanted to take that challenge, even though at first he thought it was crazy (at the table).       "Best game ever," I said, expansively, fully expecting him to agree. "How'd you like it?"

      "Mmm...not so much," he said. "It was my least favorite of all the games of Ganakagok I've played."

      This of course took me aback, and so we talked about why he had that response. It turned out that Andrew wasn't taken with exactly the prosaic but intimate quality that I thought was a strength in that game: it didn't feel mythic to him in the same way as other sessions of the game had felt.

      "Yeah, I get that," I told him. "It's not like the time you became the sun and had to spend the rest of eternity screaming in pain as you burned in the sky."

      "So what I learned," Andrew said a little later, "is that if I want that mythic quality, I have to push harder against what other players want."

      Notice that in our discussion of the game we'd played, we both were able to reflect upon what had happened, and why, and how responsibility for the outcome of play could be apportioned between the system and the people at the table. In short: we talked about the game, and we learned something about it. That was criticism. It emerged from our roles as players in the game, not from identifying ourselves as "critic" and "criticized" in some sort of formal way.

      I offer that in order to expand upon what I meant by "case-by-case basis."

      \n Speaking as a writer who has belonged to a regular crit group for the last--shit--11 years now, I've given a few hundred critiques, written and verbal, and received maybe eighty or so. Our group uses the Clarion rules, which can be pretty simply summed up as (1) critique the manuscript, and only the manuscript, that's on the table; (2) no personal commentary about the author, though pointing to apparent skill level or intent as manifested in the manuscript is OK; (3) nothing ad hominem or insulting ("This story blows goat cheese!"); (4) substantive, constructive comments should be the focus of oral critiques, not nitpicking over punctuation errors. In practice, most folks at the table try to point to some aspects of the story they like before dealing with what doesn't work. Typically, we try to explain why something isn't working, and suggest some possible solutions. We also pose questions to check our understanding of the author's intent.

      This isn't Pollyanna-ish feel-goodism for the sake of happy puppies, hugz, and starbrite flower sprinkles. I've seen some stories that had *nothing* good to recommend them other than that they came to a merciful end before I started to spurt blood from the eyeballs. But you still try to find something good to say: "great effort," "congratulations on submitting such an...ambitious manuscript," "I'm a big fan of this genre," "I liked the scene where your character was strained through the chainlink fence," etc. That's basic respect, IMO; baseline validation and support for the act of putting your work into a public forum.

      Critique is not a one-way process. There is a grace and philosophy to how you receive a critique. You can ask questions, follow up on particular comments, check to see if the critic understood your text properly. Anytime you invest yourself in a creative work, there will be some parts about which you'll feel especially and inevitably defensive. Some critiques will trigger the hell out of you, sometimes because they come from so-and-so, the mouth-breather at the end of the table who spends his crit minute droning about the minutiae of black powder weapon ignition and how you fucked the pooch with it in your story. If you submit something with a thick ego-wrapping, and you can't take a deep breath and let it flow over you, your creative career is probably destined to be nasty, brutish, and short. Have your feelings, but don't expect everyone else to be the caretaker of them.

      If you put your story into the stream of commerce, I think you're opening yourself completely to criticism. I bought Dogs in the Vineyard. Do I know Vincent? Not personally. It seems a bit absurd to assume that my critique of his game is or should therefore be less valid. Ignore the rants, but accept that you'll get a few from the wilds. Deep breath. Move on.

      If you put your playtest version up for feedback, you're asking for feedback on the manuscript, not speculation about your personal life, etc. If constructive criticism is offered with respect and a decent focus on the manuscript and the critiquer's reaction to it, I think the recipient should suck it up, regardless of whether defensive feelings come up. Some people will be more thin- or thick-skinned than others. That's not something we as critics or potential-consumers-with-feedback have control over. I assume that if someone puts something up here for critique, they're admitting their baby is a work in progress and acknowledging (tacitly) that people are going to poke some holes in it.

      Everyone wants validation, but beyond the basic boundaries of respect, validation has to be earned.

      Edited to add: Part of the value of public criticism is hearing how other critics are reacting and then responding to that as well. You learn about as much by critiquing as you do from getting the critique.

      In this type of climate it's okay to be negative but you have to effectively be pre-empting public opinion. It's possible to validate people's purchasing decisions but it's also possible to validate their prejudices and groupthink. This can be achieved by saving up negative criticism for use against safe targets. Targets in whom people have no social capital invested. So, for example, you can be as negative as you like about RAHOWA or FATAL. You can also get social capital from tracking the fine-grained nuances in public opinion so, if at release, you'd gone on RPGnet and put the boot into the Serenity RPG from the point of view that it's nowhere near as good as cinematic Unisystem I think there would have been social capital to be gained there.

There's not much to be gained from bigging up or putting the boot into something like Grey Ranks as people have no social capital invested in either liking it or hating it so validating people's prejudices one way or the other is likely to net the critic very little kudos.

      \n Jonathan, ultimately the final review of a game comes down to actual play. But there are a number of fundamentals that come from the text.
      \n
      \nTo continue with your analogy, for a person who is a vegan or is gluten intolerant analysis of the menu can determine whether its even worthwhile to GO to that restaurant. Only after that basic hurdle is exceeded does the taste of the food matter.
      \n
      \nSimilarly with a role playing text there are certain fundamental questions that can be answered by the text.
      \n
      \nYou cannot play the game without someone having learned the game. If you are learning the game from the text then the text must convey certain procedural requirements that enable play to happen. Evaluating the quantity and quality of how well the text conveys that information does not require actual play.
      \n
      \nIn fact, actual play can often obfuscate the very thing you're trying to evaluate. If the rules don't contain critical information but your group is capable of "just winging it" and inventing a solution on the fly so that play can continue, and you go on to have fun...how do you review that?
      \n
      \nIn my view that's an example of successful play and a failed text. Too often reviews based on actual play focus on the success of play and forget (or never even realize) how much of that success came from the group's own play talent rather than the quality of the text's communication. This is a problem, because it is not reliably reproduceable. Other groups may not have the same ability to just wing their way to a solution. Or perhaps they do...but the solution they wing their way to was completely different from yours and totally incompatable with how the game plays. Such a review...one that ignores the quality of the textual communication...actually failed to convey the information that was necessary.
      \n
      \nIf what one is trying to evaluate is "does this text give me sufficient information to play without having to make up a significant number of procedures myself" then actual play is entirely not necessary to this. It can be helpful it that it can identify edge cases that come up in play but that you hadn't thought of while reading the rules...but if the rules are completely devoid of even the basic cases, this becomes immediately clear just from reading.
      \n
      \nExample: The rules say "Players determine the next scene and play continues".
      \n
      \nI don't need actual play to immediately see the questions that will need to be answered in order for play to happen: namely WHO determines, and HOW do they determine it. Is it by consensus? If so, what happens if consensus can't be reached? Is it the GM? If so is the GM expected to incorporate other players' ideas as standard procedure? Is it whichever player has a good idea? If so, what happens if two players have good ideas that are incompatable. Is the answer to all these questions...just talk it out until you reach a decision?
      \n
      \nWhatever the answer is, it should be stated in the text somewhere nearby. If not, then my group will have to invent the answer ourselves. Because play literally CANNOT happen AT ALL without an answer. SOMEBODY in the real time event of actual play will have to actually speak words and have those words carry weight and be accepted. If the rules don't give guidance for who that somebody is...we'll have to figure out a method ourselves. This is true of the game designers own group, as well. Somebody actually spoke words that allowed play to happen. Basic rules writing 101 says "be sure to identify how those things happened at your table and write about them in your text"
      \n
      \nI don't need actual play to be able to identify that this information is missing from the text, any more than a vegan needs to actually eat a steak to know that's made of meat.
      \n
      \nReviews that focus only on a game's text? Not a complete picture...but definitely not a complete waste of time either.

      \n I am of the "only actual play reviews are really worth anything" camp, yet I agree with Ralph's analysis. How can this be?!?! Maybe I am a contradictory SOB and I should change my mind on something. Let's see.

      At some point the text is not going to tell you things, at some point your group's own procedures and (more important in some cases) actual contributions are going to take over. It doesn't matter how detailed the game is, eventually you are going to have to make a character, say "I pick the lock" or "I want to roll my Angst score" and right there everything the designer has done is for naught and probably they would hate what you just said.

      What a decent RPG review will produce (beyond the usual 'hay there are a lot of grammatical errorz' or 'teh art is niec' junk, which is the content of most non-actual-play reviews) is a detailed story of how you got from the text to the game. Did you get stuck on anything? (A bad procedure!) Did you miss something? (A hole!) Did you find something that you shouldn't have? (An exploit!) What did you do? Was it cool? If something wasn't cool, what happened? If something was cool, what happened? ...and so on.

      I gave an example in my other thread of an "all-thief" AD&D game, and how it was nothing at all like the "standard" AD&D game. Could we still have produced a decent critique of AD&D if we'd done an "all-thief" campaign? Of course we could! In fact, we did! We analyzed the hell out of that game, and AD&D, and how they related, and I thought about it for years after!

      But what is the value of such a critique? Why do I say that's the only critique worth reading? That kind of critique has no commercial value. Nobody is going to read it and go "oh, well, it sounds like AD&D is worth twenty dollars" (or whatever). Nobody is going to read it and go "oh, well, the designers of AD&D sure did a good job there". Maybe that is what Ralph is getting at? The OP? I don't really know.

      The value of such a critique is in improving play, both our own (through analysis, expression and response) and other people's. Plenty of people who we talked about it with said "that sounds fucking great" and came up with their own one-class AD&D play and had a good time with it. Certainly it was neither what the designers intended under any reasonable definition of intent, nor was it anything they disallowed. Hell, the critique can be beneficial even if we never played AD&D again! And that is fucking amazing.

      I guess the reason I only put value on actual-play critiques is because I'm a player, not a designer and please God, please don't ever, ever let me just be a consumer. Of anything.
