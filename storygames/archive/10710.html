\n So over in the "What would you do with $50" thread, I suggested that I could print 50 copies each of 10 single-page, free games and send one or more of them, on order, to anyone who mails me a SASE (self addressed stamped envelope.)

Graham was very encouraging.

Here are my additional thoughts, from the thread:

1) $5.50 US per game, max 10 games, minimum 5 games. 50 copies each. Once they're gone, they're gone. (the 50c is paypal fee offset).
1a) Games must take up at most two sides of an 8 1/2 by 11 sheet of paper (A4ish for internationals) and be in black and white.
1b) I can do minimal binding work. This means I can cut the sheet in half and staple it for an 8 page booklet, or cut the sheet in quarters and staple them for a 16 page booklet. If you need help laying out the 16 page booklet properly, I can help with that. If your game is large, it may be folded to fit it in the envelope.
1c) Games should be attractive and legible. No 7 point fonts please. I reserve editorial authority to reject any game, or accept-pending-revisions.
1d) This is not for playtesting. The goal is to provide short, high quality games to people for the cost of an envelope and a stamp.

2) Time limited. Basically when I leave the US, I have to turn the shebang over to someone else or just can it.

3) To participate, mail me a letter-sized SASE and indicate on it which games you would like. If you're taking more than one game, consider including extra stamps or an extra envelope (both of which will help with international customers).
3a) Ask for as many games as you like. But do keep in mind that this is for players, not collectors. If you take a lot of copies there will be less for everyone else. Thus, please don't ask for a game you don't have an inkling to play, or that you already have a copy of you made yourself.
3b) If I don't have any left of the game you want, I will send you another game that I think you might like.
3c) International customers can use an international SASE, which I don't know how works but I'm pretty sure there's a way, or they can just paypal me $2 to cover costs.
3d) Maybe Joe will run it for Canada, or Graham for the UK, or what have you. In which case I'll send them 5-10 copies of each game and they'll dispose of them. Or they'll handle their own thing, like this, but different in however they want it to be different.

4) I may include small promotional material for TAO Games in the mail-out. A product list, probably. I may also piggyback some requested Clover playtest copies. This violates 1d, but it's the perk for running the show.

5) If you participate, it would be great of you to write about your experience with the program, with playing the game you got, etc. It's even better if you do it somewhere that isn't Story Games, like RPGnet or RPGsite or whatever.

I think that the sponsors for games shouldn't be the authors. I don't want to take money from authors for this, but rather from people who are enthused about the whole idea, or want to evangelize a particular game that they love to play.

Here are my thoughts on getting this off the ground.

1) I need a stable of games. I can provide a handful of games (2 or 3) to start out with (off the top of my head: Beatrice and Over the Bar). I would like to have at least two not-by-me games represented. Let's call 5 games my minimum to kick the project into gear. I'm planning on rustling up some stuff on RPGnet and the Forge as well. I think getting to 5 is definitely possible.

2) I need money. I will take donations (5.50 funds a full fifty copies of a game, plus paypal fees.) If I get donations in excess of $50 (ten games), I will use those donations for the purchase of a binding stapler and a guillotine cutter (about $30-40 each), which will enable me to produce better quality pamphlets, and from home. After those costs are covered, I will reject all donations. Further donations should be sent to the authors of the games.

3) If anyone is feeling up to it, I would love to have a sidebar icon for my website so I can give the project a home on the web.

After this gets off the ground, it will need you all to promote it. Post about it at other sites, on your blog, etc. It won't work unless you make it work.

Are people interested in this? Willing to help with 1, 2, or 3? Would you participate?

    \n Hmm, no good diagrams easily available on the web. Here's how to work it out for yourself:

    For a quarto gathering (4 leaf / 8 page booklet): hold your sheet of paper upright (long top to bottom); fold the top half behind the bottom, then the left half behind the right half. Pry the resulting booklet open (don't tear the bolts yet) and write the numbers of the pages on each one in large numerals. Now unfold and you will have a diagram for how to lay out each side of the sheet.

    For an octavo gathering (8 leaf / 16 page): hold the sheet of paper sideways (long left to right); fold the left half behind the right, the top behind the bottom, and the left behind the right. Number as before and unfold for your diagram.

    When constructing hold the sheet as before with page one in the bottom right corner and fold as above. Staple in the middle of the final fold and rip out the bolts (use a blunt paper knife, letter opener, or ruler--not a sharp knife) and there you have it. There a lots of other folding formulas, especially when you get into wired formats like duodecimo, but this one is easy to remember and relatively hard to mess up. Should significantly reduce construction time.
