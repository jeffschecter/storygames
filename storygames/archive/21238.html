\n And the improvements one might make to them. The last thread was closed because it was slightly inflammatory. I'd like to avoid that here.

As a silent observer in the OSR community, I have learned a few general trends about the OSR playstyle and preferences.

1. The emphasis is on usable content. There's a swathe of usable content in the form of adventure modules and premade dungeons. All of those are usable content because the work is done for the GM in advance. Random tables fall into this category also: they're an easy way to generate new content and inspiration for new content. It's all usable in the game.

2. Following from #1, rules that are fiddly, overly-complicated, and/or can't be summarized into one or two dice rolls, it probably doesn't belong at the game table. Anything that needlessly bogs down the game is a net negative because it detracts from playing the game. This is exemplified in the saving throw system: saving throws are a binary yes/no. Does the poison kill you? Yes: roll up a new character. No: might want to run. Adding in more granularity has its benefits, but it slows down game.

3. The dungeon just works. It provides a pretext for exploring the unknown. It's dangerous. It's strange. It offers puzzles, decision points, risk/reward management, and roleplaying opportunities. It doesn't have to make sense, so it can be as weird and exotic as you like. It is a game within the game.

4. It's Conan, not Lord of the Rings. Lethality is a feature, not a bug. Avarice and cunning are rewarded. Heroics are not. Gold-to-XP reinforces this. You get the gold, you level up. You charge into combat, and you die.

5. Resource management is important. It's the game.

What are some ways that this can be altered, innovated, or streamlined? For me, the number one issue is tracking time. I find meticulously tracking the ticking clock to be a pain in the ass. I much prefer abstract time. Not quite "encounters" or "scenes," but something in between pure abstraction and down-to-the-second beancounting.

I divide the game into chunks depending on the time scale we're working on. In the dungeon, time is essential because it influences your torchlight, your food/water, wandering monsters, etc. Every time the players do something that will take some time - this is largely determined by DM fiat and negotiating the fiction - I make a tick mark. That represents about ten minutes or so, making it easy to match to spell durations and resources.

Six ticks in an hour. Your torch lasts six ticks. Better get movin'. (Note: Torchbearer's system of "turns" is far too punitive for my tastes.)

I'd be interesting in hearing others' thoughts, especially @Eero_Tuovinen and @2097 (our resident D&D experts).

    \n Yeah, that's the default D&D time-keeping rules, Valyrian. The rules have, since the first edition of the game, called the fictional 10-minute increment a "turn" - I like the full name "exploration turn" myself because it's less ambivalent.     The original '74 rules have a lot of good stuff in them, and while people seem to disagree a bit about what the good parts are, for me personally the exploration rules are it: those are the structural core. When the text is telling me about the exploration turns and enforced rest periods (every hour, or once in six turns, needs to be spent resting) and all that stuff, that's where I recognize actual game design instead of just random mechanical outpour of an unnamed process (which is, honestly, a big part of the books).

    More generally, you probably know what I think is the interesting bit about old school D&D mechanics - the constitutional refereeing method for developing rulings. The actual rules, those are just the cruft; one reason for why I haven't written out and published my own houserules as a game text is that it's the exact sort of thing that's been misleading the eye ever since TSR originally decided to approach this D&D thing by writing down the rules instead of the rulings process.

    There is nothing really special about the D&D rules mechanics, not any more than say twenty or so other RPGs that feature unique mechanical innovations pushed to their limits. Sure, the hit point system is gold for its purpose, but so are dice pools and scene framing and learn-by-doing experience simulators; there are many games with that level of innovation and dinstictiveness. The real thing that makes D&D special as a rpg to me is that it's a refereed wargame with rulings instead of rules. There are not many other wargames in roleplaying, if we understand the term to mean a challengeful game with a genuine commitment to how things really work. I know of no other rulings-based game (and arguably rulings-based refereeing has been a minority interpretation of the game since almost the beginning - players have always wanted TSR to be the big daddy and provide official rules). We're talking of some rare, unique stuff here.

    I realize that others disagree, but for me the issues like unified vs. organic mechanics, or using dice to resolve traps and social situations, those are noise compared to the core system of rulings-based refereeing.

    \n The dice rolls are there to simulate real-life uncertainties, of course. It would not be true to life, and the choices it presents us, if the players knew the outcomes of risky courses of action in advance. (Having the DM determine the consequences without recourse to tools would be possible, and it is done occasionally, but in general it's an unhygienic method prone to bias.)

    Massive amounts of attention are slathered upon the issue of how much arbitrariness is best, of course, and how much circumstantial factors should determine various situations; most of the old-school D&D mechanical innovation is about getting the best possible wargaming sim out of as little mechanical overhead as possible - the game's so expansive (and has a bottleneck in the form of the DM) that the individual parts need to be light-weight and powerful for it to remain playable.

    This role that the dice have is precisely why it's usually possible to bypass dice rolls in this type of D&D, and why it favours some rather... strange rolls compared to many other types of rpgs. It all arises organically out of an effort to simulate the events in the fiction. For example, consider these situations from real play:
    * The adventurers are facing a horde of 20 skeletons in a narrow hallway. They "finesse" the encounter by describing in detail a tactical arrangement that utilizes the established qualities of the skeletons (mindless, light-weight) and their prepared equipment (portable mantlets, boar spears, plenty of men) and environment (narrow hallway, lack of time pressure). The referee determines that there is no credible threat present, and the entire situation is resolved without a single dice roll.
    * The adventurers surprise a villain in his lair. Lacking any better idea, the referee decides to roll a 50/50 check to see whether the villain is home. Totally arbitrary.
    * The referee knows that there be trolls in these here woods. He rolls a 1/6 every hour spent in the woods to see if the trolls might stumble on the adventurers there. He ends up rolling about a dozen times during the session, and never rolls a '1'.
    * An adventurer is falling into a deep crevasse. He happens to have a large net wrapped loosely around him, and the player argues that it just might be possible for the net to catch onto something early in the fall. As all other possibilities (character skill, outside interference, etc.) have already been exhausted, the referee allows a 1/20 miracle check to see if the adventurer is really, really lucky. He isn't.

    Those kinds of mechanization choices are not normal for most kinds of roleplaying games: you don't usually waste time establishing and rolling for minutiae with low odds, you don't determine important events with arbitrary rolls, and you don't allow players to skip rolling for important events. These practices only make sense in D&D because its creative agenda is so wargamey.

    \nYou generally roll the dice at the moment you manage to phrase the issue that needs to be determined; alternatively, you table the issue and determine it later when it becomes relevant, maintaining it as an unresolved possibility until then.

    The target numbers in old-school D&D are generally based on the following four ideas:
    * The rules formalizations provide ready-made target numbers for the situation, potentially adjusted for circumstances. This is usually the case for common issues that are central to the thematic landscape of the game, such as combat. The formal framework simplifies situations that would otherwise be too cumbersome to simulate in detail.
    * The tested idea is "unlikely" or "far-fetched", in which case the target number is usually 1/6, or 15%. It's proven to be suitable odds for this sort of game with organically emergent possibilities, because it's not too common, and you can thus roll for all sorts of possibilities as they occur to you during the course of play. 1/6 is often substantially more likely than the fictional premise would "really" justify, but that's a known and intentional feature as well: D&D generally wants to bias itself somewhat towards active events, but not so much that the players can count on this as part of their strategic formulations. 1/6 is a sweet spot in this regard, as it's too rare for anybody to count on it in a mission-critical context, but still common enough that it generally occurs a few times in each session of play.
    * The tested idea is "imponderable" or "arbitrary", meaning that the referee and the group don't really have the means or the patience to work it out in detail right then and there; it is intuitively justifiable for it to go either way. The odds are 3/6 (or 50/50 as we tend to call it).
    * Either of the above two categories of odds (1/6 and 3/6) is routinely adjusted by a step to either direction as the result of table negotiation in establishing the appropriate odds for the precise situation and circumstances. For example, 2/6 is generally used when 1/6 seems intuitively too harshly unlikely, or when the situation is clearly more biased than 3/6 would suggest. 4/6 and 5/6 are, of course, simply the opposites of these scenarios. When 1/6 seems too likely many GMs simply rule the odds "impossible", but I personally like to jump to a 1/20 in those cases (players are happier when they get that chance, no matter how slim); as with many things in the game, it depends on the GM's technique.

    As can be seen, I don't think that the way you choose the timing and odds for the dice rolls is arbitrary at all. It's an earnest attempt at fair refereeing, letting events unfold consistently with the rules and campaign setting. The GM goes to the dice to avoid bias in decision-making, to preserve uncertainty of consequences and to abstract elegantly out of the need to simulate complex, yet secondary systems.

    \n Let's not let this thread turn into gamestyle-warring. Player skill vs. character skill is embedded within the OSR by design. Once you've resorted to the dice, you're falling back on character skill, more or less, and OSR-style games model this with certain mechanics.

    Why? Because the founders of the hobby were playing a game rooted in wargames. Those wargames had attack throws. You may as well inquire why saving throws were grouped by category over the more cogent design of Fortitude/Reflex/Will - because that is how it was done.

    Specifically addressing some of @Rickard's inquiries, a good portion of player skill is navigating the fiction and, in some cases, playing co-DM. As always, the actual DM is the final arbiter of a situation, but this co-DMing takes place in the form of asking (leading) questions and making suggestions.

    "There has to be a free stool that I can grab and use as an improvised weapon, right?"

    "You mentioned that this cavern had glowbugs. I would like to capture some in a jar and use it as a makeshift torch."

    "Given that trolls live in close proximity to the town, does it seem fair we would have heard talk of their vulnerabilities or tactics?"

    The role of the DM is then to feed information back into this situation, taking the player prompts and weaving it into the fiction. In greater detail, I will attempt to address a few questions:\n
    I would say that deciding the dice roll is biased in the first place. Why should they roll at any specific moment?
    \nOne might interpret this as "adversarial GMing," but the majority of OSR has moved away from this style of gameplay. You'll notice some texts talk about the GM in terms of "Judge" and "Referee," which (correctly) carries the connotation of fairness and impartiality. When the GM turns to the dice for an answer, he is saying, "This thing could happen, but it also could not." What fairer way than to leave this to chance? The dice are unbiased. As you note, calling for a dice roll itself is a form of bias, but it is a reduction in overall bias.

    The dice are a limit on the GM's authority.

    \n I think Rickard has a fair point here ("outgoing" randomness is much more difficult to handle "hygienically", and does, in many respects reduce the impact of player skill), but I haven't yet seen a good application of this concept in practice.

    Non-random designs and systems are often "solvable" (which is usually undesirable in this kind of gaming) and/or too complex. (For instance, we could represent each combat as a Chess game played against a Chess AI of the appropriate difficulty level, with the weaker side starting with fewer pieces, proportionately to its armaments and weight class... but it would detract too much from the game, no matter how interesting or fair that setup might seem to a given group of players.)

    D&D's everpresent "random rolls", in my opinion, have three functions:

    1. To give the GM a way to make quick judgement calls without bearing the entire burden of decision-making or stalling a game altogether. For example, the "wandering monster" abstraction does a great job of simplifying the GM's ability and/or need to figure out where any given monsters are at any given time. It's a simplification which makes her job much easier.

    Instead of tracking the current position of every monster in the dungeon, the GM only has to have a method for determining when to make a "wandering monster check", and the players thereby know how to interact with that and make their own relevant choices.

    2. It is much, much easier for humans to agree on a probability of something happening or being the case than to agree on a definite outcome. This helps with social coherence at the table: we might argue all day long about whether the vampire is at home in his lair, but if we're given the option to settle on the *odds* of it being home, we're far more likely to reach agreement quickly.

    That makes it easier to settle on rulings in the heat of play (a key feature of the "GM as Referee" mindset).

    3. A general aesthetic desire for randomness, excitement, and unpredictability.

    The OSR aesthetic delights in that final point - I think the high variability and randomness present in play, from character creation to the outcome of fights and random magical effects, is a key element of the "OSR magic", and has become traditional.

    I think you could definitely create a functional "challenge-based adventuring dungeon crawl" game without that aesthetic (does Torchbearer fit the bill, perhaps?), but it would feel distinctly different.

    I also think @ValyrianSteelKatana is quite right in that a lot more innovation might take place in the OSR world if people were more willing to experiment with new designs and shake off traditions. There is a lot happening already, but it tends to be small-scale rather than large-scale.

    For example, @David_Berg's "Delve" has very similar design goals to a lot of OSR-style play, but wouldn't be recognized as such by most OSR circles (I think) due to a lack of familiar features like classes, rolling ability scores, a high occurrence of random rolls/tables and unpredictable events, and so on.

    I'm looking forward to seeing what happens in this sphere as the next few years go by, in any case!

Over the course of a longer campaign played with the same people, however, how a player can leverage skill to their benefit in the game becomes more apparent. (Part of it is the averaging nature of multiple random inputs over time, but a bigger part of it is the development and awareness of "how we resolve things" turning into a means for the player to better predict future outcomes and make appropriate strategic decisions.)

    Where Mr. Burgun is wrong (according to me) is in assuming that, therefore, randomness can never be the best choice in game design. I think we could make a pretty strong argument that random procedures in a game like D&D are the BEST solution for that particular environment/type of game, even if a focus on player skill is the desired outcome. The virtues I listed in my previous post, above, might be enough to make random determination of outcomes the best possible tool/solution.

    In fact, I have a sneaking suspicion that we're all in agreement here, reading over everyone's posts - we are merely highlighting different aspects of the discussion in turn.

    \nExcept the rules are not completely totally random with no connection to the stuff they are meant to represent. If every action in D&D were handed over to a coin toss, that might make sense. But the random rolls reflect things in the game world. Again without knowing what specific situation, OSR game and mechanic people have in mind it is a bit hard to address. But if I make a first level mage for the very basics of the game are minimally explained, then I can pretty easily gather it isn't a good idea for my mage to attack a large creature with a dagger. And so much of the skillful stuff has to do with the interactions between the rolls. If I am playing that mage, you can bet I am being more cautious and playing my character smart so he doesn't bite it in the first hour. When people talk about player skill, they are not talking about the player's skill at mastering a system or working through the logic of a gaming procedure, they are talking about their skill of navigating the world the dice help emulate.

    That said, I think your thought experiment here illustrates the dilemma very well:\n
    Bedrockbrendan said:

    If every action in D&D were handed over to a coin toss, that might make sense.

    \nThis is the logical extreme position to take: for example, in a "simplified" D&D where everyone has the same hit points, armor class, and "to hit bonus" (or ThAC0), a combat becomes arguably much more based on luck, with fewer ways for the players to affect outcomes. Imagining this hypothetical game, we can see what a heavy role luck plays in the process of play.

    Now, if we add character differentiation (like armor class, to hit bonuses, and so forth), and then assign modifiers in play (perhaps for cover, fatigue, encumbrance, etc), we are making each resolution point less random - less like a "coin toss" - and creating an interesting and asymmetrical playground. The more we do this, the less "random" resolution becomes.

    In a theoretical world, a D&D-like game could have so many minute little modifiers added on to its basic resolution mechanics that we no longer need to roll - we just add up the modifiers until success or failure is certain. ("Ok, I have +6 to hit, he's off-balance, reducing his armor bonus, and I've aimed for 3 seconds for another +4... all I have to do is step out of line-of-sight for an additional +5, which means any roll would be an automatic success. I've established my positioning for a +12 to 'maneuver', which means I can step out of line-of-sight automatically... so I hit!")

    That game would no longer have any randomness at all.

    Notably, though:

    1. Many D&D tables (especially various versions of really old D&D) come surprisingly close to "every action handed over to a coin toss". In old-school D&D with few modifiers (for instance, early D&D before stat modifiers), every starting character has the exact same odds of a "to hit" roll, for example, and may have exactly the same odds for attempting a variety of other important tasks (usually a 1-in-6 chance).

    Combine this with the way a DM may choose to adjudicate actions, and I've seen some D&D play where most rolls have almost exactly the same odds of success. (e.g. A game where Ability Checks are used for most actions, and the starting characters all have stats in the 9-12 range, and the same ThAC0.)

    It's not an entirely uncommon practice, in my experience with D&D players. (I've also seen some people simplify combat by announcing to the players that "a 14 or higher hits!", ignoring "to hit" bonuses and armor class, for instance, or giving all starting characters the same saving throw numbers.)

    2. Even in those games, D&D can remain a game of player skill. The more the game heads towards "coin toss resolution", the more players have to become really good at communicating risk and reward - if you're truly playing in a 'deadly, OSR style', the game becomes very much about avoiding rolling in the first place.

    The communication and negotiation of the fiction can be very strong here, and if the players are skilled at using that to create meaningful choices, the strategy involved in figuring out how to maneuver into positions where you make the fewest rolls, or roll at times when the risk is minimized for you, becomes the real game.

    That can be a really fun and challenging way to play.

    There's a wide, wide continuum at work here, but I firmly believe that the "true game" of old-school style D&D is about negotiating fictional challenges, which can happen at any point on that spectrum (just in slightly different ways). Which point any given player or group prefers is probably just a matter of taste, so long as their underlying methods of communicating with each other and negotiating fictional challenges are strong.

    Rickard said:

    If D&D is most true when challenging the player, then why the dice rolls?

    \nI think the answer doesn't stem from some principled purity, just the game's design decisions on what to focus on.

    This particular kind of play is about adventurers coming up with schemes to avoid having to confront lethal dangers. Falling damage, save vs. die poisons and petrification attacks, starting out with 5 hit points--all of these establish that if you do engage the mechanics, you're in very dangerous territory.

    I see the randomness as a way for the GM to disclaiming responsibility in situations that are possibly lethal, but where a design decision has been made not to have the players coming up with clever schemes.

    For example, creating a wooden structure to artificially narrow a cave mouth so that only one kobold at a time can get out is relegated to the fruitful void - this play style embraces player-GM conversations about this sort of thing. It doesn't embrace clever descriptions of the particular way you slash at the lead kobold (in an otherwise straight duel) as a way of gaining advantage.

    You could certainly write a freeform swordfighting game where the challenge is to convince the GM of the effectiveness of your stance and fighting technique, and its utility in dealing with kobold spear techniques, but this just isn't that game.

    What you describe is system mastery ... the Player skill at using the crunch of the game to their advantage.

    Personally I find it utterly alien to the "old school" way of running things, where the whole point is to avoid engaging in the mechanical bits of the game, where the Referee expects the Players to engage first and foremost with the fiction in a creative out-of-the-box way.
    \nWhat's special about D&D and similar games is that engaging with the fiction and system mastery aren't opposite goals: the rules shape the fiction, which is therefore "well behaved" with respect to how players use game mechanics (e.g. confronting a mad wizard suggests pumping up magic resistance and elemental immunities or damage reduction, in anticipation of being blasted with destructive spells), and the system players try to take advantage of is realistic or pseudo-realistic, allowing players to make an easy translation from sensible fictional plans (e.g. "hold the orc while I cut his head off") to effective game mechanics (e.g. "if you make a Grappling roll the orc's dexterity bonus to AC will be nullified").
    The difference between a "munchkin" and a competent player is that the former exploits loopholes, defects of the rules that should be fixed with more realistic improved rules, while the latter exploits good rules and therefore how things "actually" work.

    The alignment between systems and fiction is also strengthened by a formidable palette of characteristic monsters, magical items, spells, etc. that bridge the abstraction gap between general rules and individual stories and situations.
    If, for example, all magical weapons have a bonus to the attack and damage rolls, attack and damage roll bonuses become important (in the fiction, for example by providing a clear objective to wealthy characters looking for the best equipment, not only in player-level strategy) and the game as a whole becomes about maximizing attack and damage bonuses.
