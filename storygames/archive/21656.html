    \n This is a fun analysis!

    I assume you factored in the odds of hitting, as well (that would explain the 91% down the line)?

    I do find it meaningfully different, though, that a 1e assassin always has decent odds pf killing an opponent of a similar "level" this way. For instance, a 12th-level assassin has a 45% chance of killing a 12 HD opponent. In contrast, that's not true at all for the 5e assassin - that same match-up is a 1% chance, and, in many other cases, isn't possible at all.

    I wonder about the assumptions, as well. Like, what kind of assassin's weapon deals a d8 in damage? Do high-HD enemies really tend to have an AC of 10?

    I remember this issue coming up, in fact, when I played 5e, and it was one of the things which turned me off the game:

    It feels to me that the game has made such an effort to include various "cool" abilities, that it has forced the designers to increase all the numbers to match, while leaving the baseline the same.

    For instance, my character (5th level, for what it's worth) snuck up behind a bandit guard (effectively 0-1 HD) with his knife. I wanted to take him down, right? Our 5th-level party could mow these guys down almost effortlessly with our various combat powers. (My character wasn't an assassin, though.)

    Stabbing the guard, or slitting his throat, made a great deal of sense in the fiction. However, because all the numbers are slightly inflated, I knew that it was a stupid idea. My dagger deals a d4 in damage, and a typical humanoid has 11 HP. Even with a critical hit, I had no chance of taking him down.

    On the other hand, I remember how we dealt with this back in my D&D days (it was 2nd Edition!), and it worked pretty well:

    I don't know if you recall the wave of supplementary rules which appeared in D&D around that time, but there were a LOT of them. Between us, we had a lot of sourcebooks and optional rules.

    So, what I ended up doing to reconcile the mismatch of hit points and obviously dangerous and frightening situations was to allow an automatic critical hit in the "Combat & Tactics" book, which had horrifying tables of effects for such terrible wounds. You could lose a limb or an eye or be bleeding to death, etc, and there were different tables for every different type of damage (bludgeoning, impaling, etc).

    This worked pretty well, because it was scary but still usually non-lethal - get stabbed in the back, don't bother defending against an attack, fall from a great height... roll an effect from the critical hit table.

    The "hit point conundrum" is always tricky in D&D! Fun to consider various solutions, often from many different directions.

I'm not familiar enough with 5th edition to say how much of a damage bonus an assassin may expect to gain, but I assume that it's substantial, the way the math has been trending in recent editions.

    My simple and generic ideal for the assassination odds thing is that while anybody should be capable of amassing a 1d6 damage blow against another character, it should also be possible to gain as much as say 3d6 with nothing but surprise and careful planning - that's just "realism thinking" based on what hit points mean in the simulation, what the genre of the game is and what's possible for humans to do and so on. Perhaps double that for a character dedicated to surprise murder - an assassin. However, I don't mind it if the lethalness of assassination caps out at roughly that level: once you have over 20 hit points, I'm cool with the idea that you "cannot be assassinated" in the sense of being taken down without a chance to react. (Not without supernatural means, anyway.) I think that D&D genuinely works better if higher-level characters are more resistant to death; the game is supposed to turn more romantic and cinematic and dramatic at higher levels, and a high-level player character - or a NPC for that matter - getting one-shotted without warning by an assassin is contrary to this design goal. If we want grim and gritty even at high levels (which I consider e.g. 8th level to be), then the scaling hit-points and such are a mistake to begin with.

    (Perhaps the biggest problem in having assassination remain a highly feasible tactic at high levels: once the 15th level Fighter has their 100 hit points, isn't it pretty wonky how killing that same guy by throwing him off a building is impossible, and killing him by sending in swordsmen by the dozen is impossible, but a lone assassin with a dagger can still put him down in his sleep? Is that normal to you, wouldn't you expect that fighter guy to do the movie hero thing and wake up at the last moment to engage in a quick and desperate nigh-time duel? Of course you can still kill them, and the assassin might well not understand the rules of the world they live in, but we as players know that the guy with a 100 hit points won't be going down without an extended action sequence.)

    This is all to say that I rather favour the 5th edition assassination math over the 1st edition. I am not in love with the stupid weapon-based damage dice and the assassination damage being weapon-based, because that means that the best assassin tool at mid-to-high levels is what, a Great Maul or something like that? But the principle is solid, and it makes being caught unaware very serious at low levels, but merely a dramatic set-back at high levels, which is just fine with me.

    Where it gives me difficulty is when trying to formulate real plans, from a character perspective. It requires some mental gymnastics to explain why, e.g., stabbing the sleeping is guard isn't an acceptable course of action.

    \nThe way I try to finesse these things, stabbing the sleeping guard should always be a feasible action, because sleeping guards are mostly 1HD critters, non-leveled humans. I just barely might make them 1st level if it's some sort of an elite force, or we could be playing a module that has weirdly statted guards, but most of the time if it's a no-name "normal human" then you will find a dagger to the throat producing the expected results.

    I might as well throw in my own assassination rule while we're at it - this is the current one, it's something that I've fiddled with a lot over the years: a successful attack against an undefending opponent (whatever reason, doesn't matter if you're using social surprise or invisibility or attacking a sleeping foe) immediately "bypasses hitpoints", causing a death save and likely death. This surprise effect can be countered by an immediate expenditure of 10 hit points, in which case the successful attack is treated normally. In other words: you can pay 10 hit points to "wake up at the last moment" or "just have a feeling" or what have you.

    (As a point of general interest, I'm considering just outright allowing characters to cancel any status effect targeting them by paying 10*[spell level] hitpoints to shake it off. Seems like a good guideline for ensuring that the mechanical hygiene of the game works and zillionth-level superheroes don't get taken down by some piddly "it's Sleep but I forgot to put in a level limit" 1st level spell.)

    You can see the intent of my rule in the above exegesis: I think that D&D's intent is not for high level characters to die to sudden strikes, and I think that D&D mainly measures high-levelness via the hitpoint currency. If you can afford to pay my 10 hp "combat awareness tax", I'm fine with glossing over the mortal danger just like a movie combat sequence would.

    The way this works, you would be justified viewing my rule as "a successful surprise attack causes +10 points of damage." The difference is rather technical. I have various other crunch (feats and stuff) that characters can use to try to extend the feasibility of assassination to higher levels, but ultimately my design goal is for a surprise attack to be absolutely lethal at low levels while merely being a flashy way of starting a fight at high levels, so don't expect to ever do an instant death assassination strike or something like that.

    An untested idea regarding high-level assassination, though: I could see some sort of a "deadly courtier" character build that gets to pile up "secret damage" while lulling their victim to a false sense of security during a social encounter. Sort of like assassin shrouds in 4th edition D&D: you spend rounds doing things that are not technically speaking "attacking", but that nevertheless guarantee that when your attack comes, it will cause an abnormal amount of hp damage.

    (In case you didn't get what I mean with "secret damage", it's simply hp damage that occurs without the victim realizing that it's happening. For example, dulling your victim's senses with subtly poisoned wine to make them easier to take down later. It's a way to make a high-hp target a bit softer before the decisive takedown.)

    Paul_T said:

    Or do you have a philosophy around this distinction, as well?

    \nMainly my philosophy is that I want to have my cake and eat it too [grin].

    Specifically, I want both of these to be true at once:

    Circumstances matter: When you're run over by a train, you're fucked. You cannot avoid being run over by a train with a "character build", you actually have to keep away from the tracks.

    You cannot short-circuit the combat mechanics: When somebody has a lot of hit points, the correct play should not be discarding the concept of "attack" and relying solely on attracting your enemy to play on the railroad tracks in the hopes of having them run over by a train. It should still be strategically and tactically meaningful for high-level combat to evolve into proper wuxia bullshit, rather than evolving into "who fails their saving throw first".

    Now, the problem is that affirming one of these principles does not actually do anything to resolve the problem inherent in the other. Rather, they seem pretty contradictory to each other, don't they? How can we have a game where you can have real ploys while also ensuring that those real ploys do not become the only game in town? On 1st level it's easy, because your enemy is just as dead after a sword stroke as they are after a snake bite - one's just an unlucky damage roll, the other's an unlucky poison save. On 10th level, though, playing by orthodox rules with minimal innovation, my Fighter frankly stops even carrying a sword - that's a fool's game against enemies like Giants, with dozens and dozens of hit points; the real winner carries sacks full of poison snakes for those sweet save-or-die effects. Or thrown potions, or a severed medusa head, or whatever the GM will allow me to have that gives me spell-like effects that bypass hit point math.

    You see the problem, yes? If we accept that save or die even exists as a concept, and if we assume that an "attack" genuinely does not grow in strength as quickly as hit points scale, then what is one to do to prevent characters from starting to rely on anything and everything that bypasses the hit point math in favour of attriting the enemy's hit point pile?

    (Why do the attacks not scale with hit points? In modern D&D they do, because modern D&D strives for the idea that combats should last 3-5 rounds at every level, and be essentially the same throughout the level range - numbers grow bigger all around, but the outcomes are the same. I believe this to be an error as regards the best nature of D&D; the game is more true to its overall picture if high-level combats grow naturally longer, and characters are more protected from sudden death due to having a greater HP buffer in relation to attack strength; high-level D&D combat should be less luck-based and more strategic and even dramatic, there should be room for a character to simply swashbuckle a few rounds while looking for their opening without it being a big deal. To achieve this, though, we must accept that a 10th level Fighter may have ten times they hit points, but they do not hit for ten times the damage. They probably hit a bit harder than a 1st level Fighter - feats and magic weapons and whatnot - but not as much harder as their staying power has grown.)

    \n Assuming that I've articulated the problem clearly, my best solution to it so far is as follows - and I certainly invite anybody with a better idea to tell me!

    Mechanical localism (name I just invented) is the design principle that certain ideas of warfare in D&D are essentially tier-relevant, and should not be expected to be feasible over the entire power range potentially used in the game. Sleep is a classical example of a mechanically localized spell: it is explicitly effective only against enemies with 4 HD or less, doing nothing against stronger opponents. It is something that very much has its place in the goblin dungeon, but a wizard who brings that one to the Grand Convocation of the Druidic Order will not be successful in the eldritch duels therein.

    I'll note that 4th edition D&D is very explicit about embracing mechanical localism as a design principle; it is massively concerned about ensuring that characters use level-appropriate combat powers. I'm not directly influenced in my thinking here by 4th edition, but it's a good parallel example of how somebody might go about working this stuff out.

    There are certain mechanical flags around which mechanical localism can be constructed in D&D. Arbitrary HD limits like in the Sleep spell are just one crude way to go. Power Words are a subtler example: if you currently have X or more hit points, they do nothing, while otherwise they fuck you up rather severely.

    There are also strategic concepts in D&D that are only relevant against high-level enemies. Often this is because they are simply horribly inefficient and unnecessary at low levels. Some are nice and flavourful, like how magical dueling can become a flashy counter-spelling exercise or strategic guessing-game, but too often these are stupid things like how it's easier to wrestle, disarm and tie up a high-level knight than it is to fight them.

    The "hit point cancel" that I referenced above in the context of assassinations is something I've been pondering in this context: if all dangers in D&D had an implicit "cancel cost", that could, if correctly calibrated, bring about a happy medium between the two desirable principles I mentioned at first: if a 1 HD poison snake's save-or-die poison would instead of "die" cause 10 points of damage, that would be the same as death for most low level characters, but without making that snake a better weapon against a high-level threat than a sword.

    I've come to believe that D&D probably needs to go all the way on this and make hit points the truly universal safeguard of character, while simultaneously keeping those cancel costs harsh in a level-parity context. Like, you can opt to take 10*[monster HD] damage instead of suffering their special attack outcome. That kind of harsh - that's much more damage than an equivalent-level Fireball, it's so much damage that the only situation in which doing the "cancel" even makes sense is when somebody is trying to get you on a technicality, with a low-level threat.

    I emphasize that I never, ever want for it to become common that a tier-appropriate tactic gets "tanked". However, I can accept it when the dice get lucky - after all, I can accept characters tanking sword blows, so why not other things? If you can survive a 1d6+10 damage assassination strike, well, perhaps you're just past the level of awesome where that sort of thing gets you. We can accept your character being too cool to die when they get showed off a roof, so why not be able to avoid assassination - narrowly avoiding a surprise attack (and losing hit points in an abstract way to pay for it) is such an action movie hero move that those movies don't even bother to stop and explain it. The audience just accepts that of course this hero's story didn't end here just because a jungle cat surprised them.

    Also, note something very important here: all those status effect attacks are still supposed to be useful in the high-level combat environment that I envision. Sure, bind the high-level fighter's wrists; sure, poison them; cast Hold Persons on them. They might break the bonds, avoid the snares and shrug off the spells, but that 20 hp they paid to cancel that Hold Person is a fuckload better than a swordstroke at 1d6 in terms of getting them down to a hp range where you actually can hold them down. I'm not trying to take away tactics so much as ensuring that hit points actually are capable of providing some protection to a character.

    If all this still causes distress, consider this Fighter class feature:

    Heroic Reaction: An 8th level Fighter gets the extraordinary ability to retry a save by paying 10 hit points for it. They can also save against effects that do not normally allow it in this way. Only once per effect.

    Would that be better? Note that it's much, much stronger than what I'm envisioning here. Just written as a class feature rather than an universal rule.

    How about this one:

    Heroic Retreat: A 9th level Fighter gets to "cancel" a disadvantageous tactical situation and immediately retreat from an encounter with any followers; declare this retreat on your own initiative. This costs 10 hit points, plus the amount of enemy HD present in the encounter. The cost is doubled if there is no venue of retreat available, and tripled if the Fighter is personally bound, wrestled or otherwise prevented from escaping by immediate circumstance. If the cost is too much, the Fighter is defeated, but their companions still escape.

    Note that this is all basic wuxia stuff, you wouldn't blink an eye if a sword saint in a movie did these things. So is an 8th level Fighter allowed to be a fantasy hero in the way the dramatic fiction has it, or are they destined to die in the ditch like their 1st level peers?

    I hope it's obvious why I don't find the traditional remedies compelling, by the way: it is ultimately futile to try to patch a leaking ship one patch at a time when the cause of the leaks is left unaddressed. This is precisely what early D&D tried to do, mostly by adding spells and magic items that would protect high level characters from low level threats: you wouldn't have to fear poison snakes after getting a Cure Poison spell. That's clumsy, though, isn't it? Is it really a good idea to harness not only the game designer and the GM, but also the players, in a never-ending alligator repellent arms-race? First it's poison, then it's disease, then it's mummy rot... I don't think that this is a meaningful flowering of game mechanics when all we really want is to ensure that a 10th level character cannot be one-shotted by trivial 1st level spells.

A group used to low-level play will find even a mid-level Fighter a concerning thing in combat: why would you expend your combat action for the round on attacking somebody when you know that they're just going to tank that hit? If they're just 3rd level or something then you might hope for a quick take-down by ganging up on them, but pile those levels on, and they become tougher and tougher...

    The natural response in almost any rules-set, I think, is to start thinking in terms of action economy warfare: throw a net on them to slow them down, have your strongest guy wrestle them to prevent them from using their deadly sword-attack, have a cow sit on them until the combat's over... anything and everything to prevent them from using their hit points and attack bonus effectively to ruin you. Once you perfect this form of combat, you're basically no longer attacking hit points - you're playing a vague game of "how precisely do I need to describe pushing a needle in the eye of an immobile combatant before the GM admits that they've lost, despite their still having hit points left?"

    And all the time as the players plot, the GM has to wrestle with this specter of the spirit of the game: which specific effects can be saved against, which can be thrown off using great strength, which should a high-level combatant be vulnerable against? Is it intentional that the rules don't give a high-level Fighter any real defenses against say large nets that can close them down on round one of combat?

    \nExactly. This is what I mean when I say that D&D combat is a perfectly legitimate conflict resolution system, not that different from say Heroquest extended contests (does the newest version still have those, I don't remember). You track your points, and they tell you when the conflict is at an end. There's a lot of organic resolution detail arising from the fictional positioning, but the underlying reality is that you're risking your points against enemy maneuvers, and the subgame ends when one side falls to zero.

    A classic advice that tries to salvage a less formalistic interpretation is to never add called shots into your D&D framework. It is an acknowledged problem, similar to critical hits: you might add verisimilitude to your game, but you will also cause corner cases to crop up. If you don't allow called shots, then you won't have to deal with "what if they slip a noose on him" and so on.

    I've become convinced through actual play, though, that this maxim about called shots is fundamentally useless. It is only helpful as a superficial stop-gap measure because "called shots" are just one type of possible status effect attack. What about those poison snakes? What about cheap spells? There is no good reason whatsoever to prevent a fighter from doing a called shot to throw sand in an enemy's eyes if you're simultaneously allowing e.g. green slime grenades (an example of a non-spell status effect attack that has appeared in published TSR material). Thinking that you've resolved this kind of issue by forbidding called shots is just saying that it's fair for a wizard to shut down anybody and everybody with a single save-or-die, all day long, while for some reason even realistic tactical maneuvers arising from immediate fictional positioning don't do anything for a fighter.

    The logical end-point of forbidding called shots and continuing to forbid everything that side-steps hit point damage is that you kill the positioning-based combat system and get something like the numberless D&D-based video games have: character standing in line, hitting each other in turns, with the only player participation really being the choice of retreat. I know that some prefer to play this way, of course.

    So yeah, I strive first and foremost for consistency. Either you remove all status effects from the game and have it play strictly on a HP basis, or you allow everybody to do status effects and accept that fights are won by fictional positioning ("I sit on him until he gives up") rather than hit point damage. I prefer the HP basis because that is in the spirit of the game, which I do not believe dominant status effect attacks to be.

    \nIsn't this the mainline of D&D derivatives, though? The vast majority of trad style games starting with Runequest and ending with WoD games do precisely this: hit points are a measure of physical well-being, and they can be circumvented by appropriate tactics. They aren't that important, either, as in most games you don't just get more of them for no reason like you do in D&D.

    (That bit's important for the big picture: if D&D didn't have scaling hit points, then bypassing hit points with tactical play wouldn't be a problem either, just like it isn't a problem in Runequest. In-built hit point inflation is the single most defining mechanical conceit in D&D, par none. If I had to name one feature of the game that makes it "D&D", then that would be it.)

    The history of trad design being so generally disappointing, it's not surprising that this prior art does not actually mean that you couldn't do better. Like, I personally would probably still not equate hit points with physical injury even if I decided to treat them as a non-universal concept.

    The primary alternative that I've considered myself has been interpreting hit points as a gestalt measure of real combat readiness, particularly the capability to uphold an armor class by dodging and blocking incoming attacks - a character with hit points remaining is ipso facto capable of doing whatever their combat styles requires them to do to still have an armor class and be able to take combat actions. (Walk around, keep their arms up, etc. for humans.) Physical health plays into it indirectly, but many other things do too, including things like personal balance and whatnot. Characters falling to zero hp are very literally "incapacitated" - not necessarily injured or dead, but unable to act to defend themselves for whatever contextual reason.

    The main issue with this type of hit points is that they fluctuate rather wildly. For example, there's no logical reason why a character stepping into a bog shouldn't lose a chunk of their hit points under this interpretation, as they lose tactical mobility. And they should gain them back after moving to hard ground, because "standing in a bog" is not exactly a permanent limitation for your combat capability. Same for a character getting wrapped into a net, or one who's being wrestled, etc.

    If this was the definition of a hit point, it would probably be best to treat attacks and other difficulties less as "hit point damage" and more as "hit point commitments": when you are hit by status effects like "standing in a bog" or "injured leg" or "exhausted after a long fight", you commit a number of your hit points to ignoring these difficulties; as long as you can make such commitments, you can keep fighting. Hit points are released from such commitment immediately when the given status effect ends or becomes irrelevant. Injuries are unlikely to end during a combat, while something like "being entangled in a net" could well end once you manage to untangle yourself.

    What I like in this HP interpretation is the notion that hit points are very literally a measure of berserk vivacity, which is something that players generally like as a theme in D&D. Your HP number essentially tells us how many nets or nooses you need to be wrapped in to stop you moving and fighting. "My character is so crazy - so many hit points - that he can have two guys wrapped around his legs and still keep fighting."

    The natural response in almost any rules-set, I think, is to start thinking in terms of action economy warfare.

    \nThoughts.

    Stocks and flows. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stock_and_flow

    Hit points are a stock. Actions are a flow. Normally the action flow is tied to the flow of hit points out of the stock (delta-HP), implying an approximate turn count until a stock of HP is depleted. Attacking the action flow means you drive up the implied turn count until your own HP stock is depleted, and then everyone agrees "you won't lose for 2000 turns so you win".

    Attaching the "10 HP (maybe by level or whatever) to get out of a sticky situation" rule, we disallow attacking action flow disconnect to HP stock, since you can get your actions back by voluntarily lowering your HP stock, aka turn their attack on your actions flow into an attack on your delta-HP flow.

    The reason this can work so well is because it's your choice where to take the hit, so if one is way worse for you than the other, you'll take it.

    "you can get your actions back by voluntarily lowering your HP stock" -- another way to do this is to say you can always buy additional actions, for a large HP cost? Actions you can take despite conditions? Pay 10 HP, get 1 free action. Feel free to heroic-dying-breath-action.

    "Hidden HP loss" feels great for this... pay 10 HP, get out of jail free, and those 10 HP come back if combat's over, because you didn't really take any real damage just "wow we're closer to beating you".

    \n Good stuff, Johann.

    An observation on swingy and plodding in general: I think that both are good and distinct atmospheres to have in D&D, I would disagree with anybody saying that one or the other needs to go completely.

    A practical conclusion from my earlier wandering pondering would be something like this:

    You want low level combats to be swingy and desperate because that's the setting, that's the theme: to survive in a brutal and unforgiving world, to take risks for great rewards. The rules deliver in spades with the low hit points, as one-hit kills are rather common, and getting a hit in is easy as well.

    You want high level combats to be plodding and deliberate because that makes room for combat content: combat becomes more complex as there is more space in it for things to happen, and less randomness. The theme is more like wuxia and less like hyper-realistic war movie: the important actors all have names and reasons to be there, and many combats end before the killing blow. Combat between equals is entered with gravitas, because both sides have much to lose.

    As I've said before, I think that the really interesting and unique thing about D&D is the way the game shifts from one of those thematic universes into the other gradually, as characters advance, without ever having real top-down rules enforcing it. At some point you just notice that you have so many hit points and contingencies that you're not really in danger of sudden death anymore from mere men with swords.

    This atmospheric shift is also not simply one-way, there are ways for low-level combats to turn plodding and high-level combats to turn swingy - and this is a good thing, an excellent thing. For instance I like it that low-level characters can engage in various risk-reduction exercises (improved field chirurgy to save the injured from death, for example), and that high-level characters can find themselves in sudden mortal danger if they face a level-appropriate, epic threat. A dragon with a 30 damage fire breath or something like that is not a plodding threat, it's a sudden return to the swingy world of one-hit death.

    This is why I like the idea of guaranteeing high level characters with hit point cancel, but only against inferior threats. The cost of paying yourself out of trouble needs to scale so harshly that it's really just a corner-case solution, such as when a 0th level farmhand tries to assassinate a high-level Fighter. Thence the "10*[level of threat]" hp cost that I've been spitballing - it ensures that a character practically never has the hit point budget to shrug out of a serious, level-appropriate threat.

    Paul_T said:

    Johann,

    What kinds of rules come into play in those "swingy and distinct" fights against similarly-levelled humans? Are they standard, or ruled on the spot? How do you choose to handle these, and does it ever create odd choices for the players?

    \nI use a slightly modified version of DCC's "Mighty Deed of Arms" mechanic. It's one of the Three things I love about DCC.

    A fighter gets a bonus damage or 'deed' die (ranging from d3 to d8) which may be used for special attacks. These have to be announced before the attack roll and typically have difficulties from 2 (knock prone) to 6 (decapitate). If the deed die fails, the entire attack fails. It's a neat little sub-system which also doubles as a reasonable damage bonus for fighters.

    So a 3rd-level fighter has a d5 deed die and might try to cut off an opponent's hand. That's a difficulty of 3, so the fighter must hit the AC and roll a 3, 4 or 5 on the d5.

    We have established baseline difficulties for all common maneuvers as well as certain modifiers (targeting a fighter or a large monster typically raises the difficulty by +1) and improvise everything else as it comes up in play (to be referenced later, if we remember).

    It is important to note that the nature of the target and circumstances are important. For instance, blinding a cyclops is easier (one big eye) and knocking over a four-legged creature is more difficult. You also need a suitable weapon (a blade to cut off tentacles, a spear to nail someone to the wall etc.).

    Characters other than fighters can trade all regular damage for a d2 deed die. Also, the deed die can be increased to the minimum necessary to succeed at a -2 penalty to the attack roll per step.

    So a farmhand seeking to decapitate a name-level fighter (or 'slayer') would need to trade all damage for a d2 and increase this to a d8 at a penalty of -12. He'd succeed on an 8 but is not entitled to roll because at -12 he cannot possibly hit the slayer's AC.

    Philosophically, special maneuvers should only be profitable if the attacker leverages the circumstances of the situation at hand. If you are fighting an amorphous blob on a featureless plain, the deed die just provides extra damage.

    Various creatures have similar attacks (wolves trip, T-rexes swallow etc.).

    So far, the rules work as intended: Most of the time, the fighters just inflict extra damage or perhaps set up the party thief for a sneak attack (by knocking a target prone). Occasionally, they go for something specific (e.g. hamstringing a powerful foe to enable a retreat). And against very powerful enemies or in desperate circumstances, they sometimes take the route of going for an instant kill (which usually has a 1-in-8 chance) or de facto equivalent (e.g. immobilizing a foe without ranged attacks, to then be killed at the party's leisure).

    \n Reminds me of a few other systems I've encountered that use random dice rolls for pulling off dramatic maneuvers or stunts.

    The Dragon Age RPG has a dragon die. You roll 3d6 + mod for task resolution, but one of your d6's is supposed to be a different color and is your dragon die. You generate a number of stunt points equal to the dragon die's result (so between 1 and 6 points) and bigger and badder stunts require more points to pull off.     13th Age also has flexible attack roll maneuvers for some classes, but its the bread and butter of the Fighter class. Basically, you have different stunts or special abilities that trigger off different natural die rolls (it uses 1d20 for task resolution). So there are triggers like "natural even roll", "a 16+ hit", "natural odd roll", and so on. Several maneuvers trigger even on a miss, as well.

    While these types of approaches are fun and interesting (albeit a little gimmicky), for my personal tastes they sacrifice a lot of player agency before the altar of RNG. I personally prefer approaches where the player knows what they can reasonably do beforehand and has various options (both mechanical and fictional) for increasing their odds of success.

      \n Hi Paul, I'm certainly no expert when it comes to 5E but I can gave some insight as to how I would handle these sorts of situations in 4E and, to a certain extent, in 13th Age as well. 4E certainly has the "combat cantrips and special powers" and "inflated hit points at 1st level" in spades; however, there are also several factors that mitigate the effect you are describing to greater and lesser degrees:

      1) Coup de Grace: I'm not sure what the coup de grace rules are in 5E, but in 4E if you attack a helpless enemy adjacent to you your attack automatically does max damage (that's how crits work in 4E) and, if the attack does damage equal or greater to half their maximum hit points, they just die immediately (no save). If you're using a magical weapon or implement, you also do extra dice of damage on crits equal to its enchantment bonus.

      2) Minions: Unlike other versions of D&D, 1-hit mooks don't only exist at the CR 1/8 to 1/2 range but at all levels of play. That "5th level guard" may go down in one attack depending on how the GM builds the encounter.

      3) Action Points: In 4E, each PC (and some NPCs) has a small pool of currency they can use to get an extra action in; in 5E, they gave this to the Fighter only but it allows you to get two attacks in once per encounter if need be. If you get the drop on an enemy and beat their initiative, you could get up to 3 attacks in (surprise round attack + 2 attacks with your action point) before they can even respond. If its a striker like a Ranger or Rogue making these attacks, that enemy is almost guaranteed to be dead even with higher hit point totals.

      4) Skill Challenges: Unlike other versions of D&D, 4E actually has a somewhat robust conflict resolution system that doesn't involve rolling initiative and whittling down hit point totals. As a GM, this would absolutely be my go-to method when it comes to an "assassinate the NPC" scene. Combined with the coup de grace rules above, its unlikely the NPC is surviving the knife stab.

      For what its worth, though, I actually agree with you. The fact that "4 slashing damage" means something different fictionally depending on the hit point total of defender has always bugged my sense of verismilitude as well (the well known Schroedinger's Hit Points effect).       \nNot to be too flippant, but however the GM wants it to.

      Okay, in all seriousness, the skill challenge system in 4E is an extremely flexible framework --- especially after the much improved presentations in the DMG2 and other sources --- that can be utilized in a variety of ways by any given GM. The core of the system is the players have to make X* successful skill checks by a certain threshold. The default threshold is "before they make 3 failed skill checks", but it could also be "before a competing group of NPCs makes X successful checks" or "before Y amount of time has passed" or "as long as it takes, but something bad happens whenever someone fails a check or refuses to take action". Also, there are some skill challenges that are deliberately designed to take place over a vast swath of campaign time so a given challenge doesn't all have to be resolved in one scene.

      Honestly, the closest analogue to skill challenges I can think of from a more recent game are the progress clocks and countdown clocks in Blades in the Dark (which were of course influenced by the countdown clocks in Apocalypse World). When I first came across them in the BitD text, my immediate response was "these are AW-ified skill challenges".

      Now, as to my own 4E games the way I have typically done "combat skill challenges" is to leave them open ended (so the threshold is "as long as it takes") but every time a PC fails a check this simulates them "losing" the exchange and they either take damage or lose a healing surge (depending on the nature of the combat). Its actually very reminiscent of how battles are often handled in PbtA games except instead of each NPC having an individual harm track the battle as a whole has its own harm track. I also follow online advice from other 4E GMs and will allow more combat-oriented resources like action points, encounter powers, and daily powers grant bonuses to skill checks (typically +2 to +5) or give extra successes.

      Like I said, though, the system if very flexible and you can implement any given scenario in a variety of ways. It all depends on your needs as a GM.

      * Where X is a number that goes up --- from 4 to 12 --- depending on the complexity of the challenge. There are 5 levels of complexity and each level gives XP equal to a monster of the challenge's level. So a 4th-level complexity 2 challenge would give XP equal to two 4th level monsters split between everyone that takes part in the challenge. \n
