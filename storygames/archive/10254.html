Rob Bohl mentioned this coyly in Things To Watch, but I wanted to make a proper announcement to our core audience here: Rob and I just started a new project we're calling, "Oo! Let's Make A Game!" The idea is that every week, we'll get together and collaborate on an ongoing RPG project. We don't know where it's going to go, but we hope that not only will it generate a nifty RPG, but it'll help other people figure out what (not) to do as they design.

\n

We're coming in each week having done some homework, but not having discussed anything. By the end, we'll have made some decisions (I hope) and go away to chew on it for another week.

\n

If nothing else, we giggle a lot. I've been told that my girlish charm makes people quickly lose any respect for me, so if you've got any at all, I prescribe a listen.

\n

Give us a listen! You can subscribe to the feed, even though it's a little wonky and contains two very old posts from my blog, one of Sarah Palin ranting and the other of an episode of The Durham Three where I was a guest on the show. I recommend that you don't listen to the Palin one. It'll just make you some combination of angry and embarrassed.

    \n Is there such a thing as humanity? Can (ought) we overcome anthropomorphism/centrism? What experimental societies are possible with new technology? Moreover, how does technology relate to our constitution as subjects, and our understanding thereof? How does technology feel and how does this experience of it change the self? What, if any, is the distinction between artifice and nature? What are their qualities? How do we understand ourselves as simulacra? Is there such a thing as an origin/original/identity? &c.;
    \n
    \nQuestions of specific technologies (like ubiquitous computing, functional immortality, uplifted animals &c;) may or may not be relevant to the treatment of transhumanism.
    \n
    \nNietzsche is useful here: "Man is a rope stretched between the animal and the over-man \u2013 a rope over an abyss. A dangerous crossing, a dangerous wayfaring, a dangerous looking-back, a dangerous trembling and halting. What is great in man is that he is a bridge and not an end: what is lovable in man is that he is a going over and a going under. I love those that know not how to live for they are those who cross over."

    I don't think it will be overwhelmingly difficult to build an RPG around the themes of transhumanism.

    \n

    The fundamental question, as noted in Oo!, is one of identity. "I'm a Jew." "I'm an American." "I'm a human." The grammar assumes that those are intrinsic qualities of a person. If your mind is software, your location is variable and perhaps multiple, and your body is mutable, the verb "to be" starts to change meaning.

    01:00:59 long & 55.9 MB big

    In this episode, Robert Bohl (designer of Misspent Youth) and Joshua A. C. Newman (designer of shock: social science fiction), discuss what the transhumanist science fiction game they're designing\u2014before your very ears!\u2014should be about. What is transhumanism about? Where does the design want to focus?

    - We start with listener feedback: Simon C, Paul B, and Sven Holmstr\xf6m
    - Sufficiently Advanced
    - Eclipse Phase
    - The Cyborg Handbook
    - The Wikipedia article on transhumanism

    - The game is (possibly) about "identity, change, and human nature."
    - 25:00: What we know is in the game so far: a transhumanist game that supports narrativist play that involves character generation during play, wherein you see how your characters survive the technological singularity
    - Charles Stross's Accelerando
    - Possibly stealing Ganakagok's tarot-like situation generation
    - Vincent Baker's game Rock of Tahamaat, Space Tyrant

      \n My low-status thoughts for this episode:

      * Listening to your struggle to nail down the "what do the characters actually do in the game" question was interesting. On the one hand, I greatly prefer to *play* games featuring characters with drive and agency. OTOH I'm not sure transhumanist fiction really features those kinds of characters. It is IMO one of the reasons other TH RPGs have sort of stumbled. There really is an element of victimhood to the genre. I guess if you go back to Accelerando as a guidepost, in the first story you have a character who is the *cause* of major changes, and the last couple chapters kind of show life in his shadow. That might be an interesting angle to pursue: what if you are the change? That kind of inverts the "oh god everyone now speaks Hindi via tweets aggregated and redistributed telepathically, how will I keep up?" angle.

      * Another thought: it seems that in the existing ludography of TH gaming (Sufficiently Advanced, GURPS Transhumanist, Eclipse Phase, possibly Freemarket), the event that led to humanity transcending itself has already taken place, and now the game is "about" exploring the gap between how real humanity "is" versus how the fictitious future-humanity "will be." Since you're focusing your game on the period leading up to that transcendent event, I'm not sure how, exactly, to reconcile that with being proactive and actualized. That gap -- between being actualized and being a victim -- might be the place to play the game.

      * As I'm typing this I'm already expecting Joshua to tell me I'm 100% wrong, which strikes me as funny on several levels.

      * Measuring your modification: Love the idea that you simply cannot communicate with folks whose modifications don't match your own. Agreed it needs to be quantified. I wonder if maybe it needs to, rather, be *qualified* in some way. IOW one advancement isn't per se "more advanced" than another, but rather they're merely incompatible. Maybe "advancedness" is a measure of what iteration the current hotness is. So like the game starts with, say, "spoken communication" as the baseline for communication. Somehow "telepathy" gets introduced. Telepathy is a different qualifier so it's incompatible with "spoken commo," while also offering specific benefits. but then the *next* advancement is "ubiquitous holographic telepresence," which is entirely compatible with, indeed requires, "spoken communication", as well as being "more advanced" than "telepathy" was before it. So now you have competing advancements leapfrogging one another.

      * Also you might broadly expand this notion of "advancements" beyond communication. Like...new technologies might belong to extremely broad families of what-characters-do. Communication is obviously one of them. Economy is another. Inflicting force is another. And so on. And those would follow *the same limitations* on incompatible qualities. Some future economic standard may be totally incompatible with, say, the US Dollar, or more broadly with abstract credit. Maybe some post-credit economy arises based on ... something else. But now it's in a death struggle between that not-based-on-credit economy versus future advancements on our modern based-on-credit economy.

      * Kept listening and it sounds like Joshua's going a similar direction.

      * I'm not getting the focus on gender. It feels like a *lot* of time on a subject that doesn't feel especially productive from an actual-play standpoint. Yes, I get you're talking about a play option. It feels meh. ... I think one of you agree with me on that.

      * I hope you figure out a metastructure to the game that lets you play more than a "Lady Blackbird"-like scenario. I think that's a cute way to build a game but it seems, um, unsustainable. Maybe as a demo.

      * Once the rest of the podcast became about your self-enclosed-scenario structure, I got kind of bored because that's not really an interesting format for me. So that's the last of my input.
