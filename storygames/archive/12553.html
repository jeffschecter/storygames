    Posted By: Paul Czege
    Questions about your sales numbers:
    \nRemember to use the "sales" tag to get more of my LJ posts re: sales. However, I only have good numbers for last three quarters; before that, I just tracked lifetime sales.

    Rest of this post is my anecdotal experience, sometimes foggy (if not properly backed with numbers) memory, and blantant wild-ass guesses from 12 years in the game industry as freelancer and nanopress publisher.\n
    Posted By: Paul CzegeDo these sales numbers represent the whole of your hobby-related income? Or are you doing other contract work?
    \nThey are the whole of my self-published income. I do freelance writing and editing work in the game industry (for example, Dresden Files, Unknown Armies).\n
    Posted By: Paul CzegeDo you have a day job? What percentage of your income comes from sales of your games? What percentage from contract work? What percentage from the day job? How have these percentages changed over time?
    \nDay-job: yes. On average, game sales are approximately 2% of my income [1], freelance work between 1% and 4% [2].

    [1] Can go higher, in a year with a new release.
    2005 (T&J) and 2009 (S7S) were possibly 5%.

    [2] Can go higher or lower, depending upon number of gigs I get, size of gig, rate I agree to, etc.
    2009 (DFRPG) could have been 7%.\n
    Posted By: Paul CzegeThe typical sales curve for a game is an early spike, and then a tapering off. Are any of your games defeating that pattern? Are any of them on a rising curve? If so, what do you think accounts for that?
    \nNo, but the rate of decline of the taper is not as sharp for two of my products: T&J and ZoZ. The slope is flatter, at a higher sales level. There are occasional downticks (for unknown reasons) and upticks in that slope/tail, this latter usually related to reviews, awards, and new releases. [3]

    [3] Size of uptick related to widespreadness/word-of-mouth of source.

    Reviews uptick sales of a product [4]. Back catalog sales effects minimal.
    Awards nominations uptick sales of a product [5]. Back catalog sales effects minimal.
    Awards uptick sales of a product [6]. Back catalog sales effects very small.
    New release gains a small uptick in sales of a product in back catalog [7].

    [4] Review effect greatest in 1st qtr/inital 90 days of release; maybe one or two dozen copies over qtr. Afterward, 5 to 10. Increase this when there are several new reviews out at the same time.

    [5] Nomination effect seems good for additional 5 to 10 copies that quarter. May add to flattening that product's long tail slope.

    [6] Awards effect seems good for additional 10 to 20 copies that quarter. Pretty sure this adds to flattening the long tail. Also, 3 to 5 additional sales in back catalog that qtr seems to be usual, sometimes products that have sold a single copy in months.

    [7] New release effect seems good for additional 5 to 15 back catalog sales that quarter. Unlikely to add to flattening the long tail of those products UNLESS those products already have reviews (several), nominations, and awards. \n
    Posted By: Paul CzegeAnd thanks for posting them.
    \nMy pleasure. If anyone has further questions, just ask.

    If anyone has comments or insights or anecdotal data of their own that supports or undercuts my anecdata above, please share!

    Thanks!
