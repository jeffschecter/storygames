\n Following Luke's not for everyone thread, my particular bugbear is reviewers who subtly question, after a positive review, whether a game would be fun to play.

It's always reviewers who haven't played the game who do this. And it's always raised as a polite question, usually after praising something else about the game. It's a subtle, undercover way of saying "I don't like it".

This particularly bugs me with reviews of my Cthulhu scenarios. They play very well, both when I run them and when others run them. And the standard review I get is praise, followed by "but would it work in play"?

I remember a similar phrase being used to dismiss Steal Away Jordan. Would it actually be fun to play? (Yes, it's bloody superb.)

    Posted By: TomasHVMIt's like a reviewer saying that a novellooks fine, "but would it be nice to read???" Such a reviewer would not last long amongst literates ...
    I don't think it's QUITE that black and white. A closer analogy would be a review of a musical instrument or, perhaps, a Lego set: a review like that CAN be solely aesthetic, based on "unboxing," without having to actually play the instrument (err... hmmm... OK, I'm not 100% on that parallel) or build the set's model to speak to the merits of the products.

    ...

    OK, lemme try again: On the CONTINUUM of utility of a review, RPG reviews without play are summaries of rules and setting elements, and some presentation of packaging/aesthetics. If one presents it as such (and readers acknowledge it as such) that is totally valid "review." At the other end of the continuum is the review written by someone who plays the game for many, many sessions (or campaigns!); has played many, many similar games over the years (no one's spoken to authority or credibility yet); has maybe designed a similar game or other game types (as contrasting experience); and has advanced training in literary criticism, logic, mathematics, and psychology (and game theory--but that's sort of all those things in one super-set).

    If the only thing we use, as consumers, is the Uber Reviewer end of the continuum, then we won't have a lot of data points. But we shouldn't weigh data points equally, so the slew of "unboxing", superficial reviews should be weighted appropriately (i.e. lightly, as compared to the Uber Reviewer).

    Make sense?

    \n I think the phrase makes the review useless. If you have to ask, you have to play it.

    On an aside, I do think some games can be reviewed without play. Let's say next month Chaosium releases Elizabethan Cthulhu, a complete game like Dark Ages Cthulhu or Roman Cthulhu but set in Elizabethan England.

    1. I wasn't able to review this without playing it but did so anyway. I've wasted your time.

    2. I don't think it would be fun, but I can't substantiate that.

    \n Also, game books aren't novels, aren't films, aren't meals. Analogies can be useful, but sometimes I think they get in the way and I think this is one of those occasions.

    The analogies are easy, but they're wrong.
