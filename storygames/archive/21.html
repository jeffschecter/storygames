Clueless N00bs

Jason_Morningstar
January 2006 edited January 2006 in Story Games
This could also go in Actual Play, but I'm interested in it from a design perspective.

At NC GameDay, I had a table with myself, five other enthusiastic, aggressive indie gamers, and some dude who was completely unclear on the concept. He was interested - he'd signed up well in advance - and he wasn't disruptive, but his participation took a game that could have been king-hell awesome and made it just awesome. He slowed things down, he turtled, he didn't really participate much at all, and afterward, under close questioning, he said he had a great time. He certainly laughed and tried hard to understand the system, so I can't fault him personally. But as a type he brought my game down for everybody.

I bring this up because it was intensely interesting to me as a designer to observe him, and see the effect his participation had on the games we played (The Shab-al-Hiri Roach, PTA, and Face of Angels). I want to be welcoming and accessible to people like him, and I want to help them appreciate this kind of gaming - what are the best ways to make this happen? How do I balance the satisfaction of hard-charging Forge goofs with Rifts players who stumble up to the table? In a convention setting, is that even my job? How do we design games that are easier to grok for "newbies"? How do we format convention play to facilitate this? How best to handle them at the table? Share you experiences and suggestions!

    I think part of it (I was in the Face of Angels game with Jason, and the guy he's referring to is "Bob") was that... well, it was like going to a Yoga class. Bob wanted to give Yoga a shot, and walked into all the Advanced Yoga Madness classes.

    On one hand, we were very nice and helpful, helping him hold positions, and giving him basic cues why we do what we do.

    But at the end of the day, because we spent a lot of time helping him break into the World of Yoga, it was harder for us to do what we came to do, which was some crazy mad power advanced yoga.

    I think I may have a kind of solution:

    When you're at home, or in a Meetup group, your gaming will be directed towards the Mad RPG Yoga that you're looking for.

    When you're at a Con, expect that you're going to have to take it slow, and bring people into the game and up to speed. Go over the rules, direction, and style of play SLOWLY. Literally, half the experience is for Your Enjoyment, and half the experience is to get people interested in these Story Games. Now, if it turns out that everyone in the Con game is ALSO a Crazy Mad Yoga Master, then feel free to go nuts together, kicking it into high gear.

    But otherwise, Con games are as much an uplift (not to indicate that X is BETTER than Y, but I use the term uplift here to mean "to get them used to, to warm them up to") experience for people new to these games as a fun experience for all. I think that might be the right attitude to view them. Recruit and Assist, and get your own mad fun on in personal games and Meetup games.

    ...still didn't have the tools to frame scenes, set stakes, collaborate with other players, or narrate effectively or entertainingly, even after lots of good examples.

    This really feels like the rub to me. Other than examples, what went into giving him these tools?

    Not that good examples aren't valuable, but to be frank, if you were already on the same page with the other players, their examples may have looked very good to you without necessarily being useful to someone without context.

    As to th eimportant stuff:
    I want to be welcoming and accessible to people like him, and I want to help them appreciate this kind of gaming - what are the best ways to make this happen? How do I balance the satisfaction of hard-charging Forge goofs with Rifts players who stumble up to the table? In a convention setting, is that even my job? How do we design games that are easier to grok for "newbies"? How do we format convention play to facilitate this? How best to handle them at the table? Share you experiences and suggestions!

    I'm just going to posit that you may not be able to do both at once. Hardcore Indie-heads have a lot of accumulated lore, practice and support, and they have a lot of enthusiasm for chances to play with similar folks, but that body of lore can be as daunting as a new player walking into Advanced Squad Leader. Sometimes you can hit just the right note - you can have a whole group that really wants to support and help (but not overhelp) the new person - but unless you've got that whole-group investment, the GM has to juggle priorities.

    That makes for games like you describe. If the goal of the game had been to teach new players, it probably would have been much better for th enew guy and less good for the old hands. If you had decided to play purely to the old hands, the game probably would have been more awesome, but at the cost of alienating the new guy. So you split the difference and pull the guy in some, but make a less good game, and all in all, that's not a bad outcome.

    What's most telling here is that there are two potential issues: A concern thatthe game wasn't as good as it coudl be for the experience players, and a concern that the game didn't sufficiently draw in the new guy. Reading the post, I feel like the former is the one more heavily on your mind, which may be an answer in and of itself.

    That may be me misreading your intent, but I'd be curious if you think so. There's a lot of weight to that implicit message - enough to make it very important, but also enough to not make me wan tto extrapoalte further without firm footing.

    Anyway, short form, as in game design, the trick seems to be in having a clear vision of what your goal is, and running with it.

    -Rob D.

    Andy, you have a crazy good memory. You didn't even play in that Dogs game. You were off Roaching with Clinton and crew on the other side of the room.

    The "my character's eyes are hazel" woman seemed to think that she was supposed to fill her spotlight time with detailed description. What she was missing was that her exposition was supposed to matter to the entire group, not just to her. It's a social skill people learn or don't learn -- knowing when your audience is bored or not. You know the type who doesn't have the skill. They start telling you a story about something they did. They get bogged down in meaningless details. Maybe they have little "arguments" with themselves about some stupid detail. "So last March... er, maybe it was April... it must have been April because Mom was in town... but it might have been the end of April or the beginning of May..." Whatever! It was Spring. Move on. Hazel-eyes-chick couldn't tell that we didn't care, and everyone else at the table could. She needed to focus her exposition on details relevant to her character's motivations and keep the color to the bare minimum necessary to hold the audience's interest.

    Remember, your character needs the "underwriting" (thanks, Vincent) of the entire play group. Everyone has to buy into the character. Otherwise, it's not gaming sex, it's gaming masturbation. Fun for you, but dull to watch after a while.
