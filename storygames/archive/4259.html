\nWushu is another game that seems to inspire many people to mod / hack it into something else. What about it inspires this, and where do you want the rules to go?

(This is pretty patently a pull at Malcolm to talk about Budo.)

Personally: I come back to attempting various Wushu remixes, because it's great at supporting people to initially run free at describing great action sequences; but then, I find myself wanting some other rules to help players entertain each other even more (i.e. make those descriptions better), basically to take it to the next level.

    \n Great timing, I just bought Budo today!

    I've been tinkering for a couple years now with ideas for a Wushu-based "god game" where the characters are small-time gods and demigods. Inspirations include American Gods, Dogma, and The Prophecy. Gaming influences include Nobilis and Unknown Armies. Why I like the Wushu system for this is that the system is fairly simple and abstract, but capable of rather different play experiences given small rules tweaks and a strongly defined setting.

    I'd really like to see someone incorporate personality mechanics into Wushu. There's a rudimentary Honor stat in Wire-Fu, if I recall correctly, but I'd like to see a game where personality mechanics are hooked right into the Wushu engine.

    Basically, I really liked Wushu but I wanted to moderate the freewheeling feeling with some of the character-centered aspects of Japan's cinematic martial arts and traditions. I also wanted a system with characters that were a bit less "disposable" and viable across a long term.

    In koryu bugei (old form martial arts) specific skills are actually pretty rigidly defined. In some cases you have arts that only do one thing, like attacking with a drawn sword in a standing position (battojutsu/battodo). In others you have detailed subskills in a larger tradition. For example, in the Tenshin Shoden Katori Shinto Ryu they teach almost *everything*, but few people really know it all and seniors typically consult densho (notes about a particular practice) to make sure they don't forget specifics. In anime and more fantastic period pics you have characters with different specialties. I pop example would be Naruto (though I'm not a big fan of it). Characters are very "RPG-like" in that they have consistent, specific talents from episode to episode unlike, say wuxia, where you lean about a talent after the fact. I wanted more stuff like that.

    I also wanted the play experience to be a bit more "coldbloodded" and mechanics centered. But despite all of these interests I didn't want to get rid of Wushu's essential charm go too far in any direction. I think one of the problems with recent thought in game design is that it tends to extremes. If you have a design agenda where system's in the middle there's a tendency to go for heavy or rigid rules when all you want to do is turn the dial a bit. Budo turns the dial in a more system-heavy, sustained play direction, but then it *stops* at a certain point, where I want it to be.

    I've thought of taking the Budo rules and using a future iteration for one of the games I have percolating in the back of my head, but right now building Aeternal Legends is the big thing -- and working on KotHS . . . and WW stuff
