\n Hi again, folks, I've been off-forum for a while. Other projects, you know how it gets.

Anyway, to be entirely honest this one isn't a game (yet - it might well be in the future). It's a fiction project. But Story-Games is the most astonishing collection of lateral thinkers I know, so...

Imagine you have this stuff. It's technological rather than magical. It's relatively cheap and available. Using simple computerized controls, you can get it to morph, in real time, into any shape you want. It will then stay that shape until you get it to change again. So by doing this repeatedly you can make it move around as well as morphing.

When it's first invented it's kind of grainy, feels rubbery, comes only in silver and when it moves it looks like a 1950s monster movie. As it develops, though, it becomes controllable to microscopic scale, can change texture to a degree, can change colour, moves smoothly and has considerable tensile strength. You can embed sensors and other electronics in it, and it can have internal moving parts, like wheels.

My question is: If you had this stuff, what would you do with it? You personally, and people in general?

Bonus question: What issues can you see it creating as well as solving?

    \n First it would be used by science. Exploring places that can't be reached by conventional ways, in which robots can't adapt enough and human would be in too much jeopardy...

They have changed the ways of science, war, business and politics, and influenced everyone and anything (both directly and indirectly). Of course, the higher you stand, the more control/safety/power is within your grasp.

    \n What happens when its form control systems crash? Does it just go soft, or does it pull random forms from its database, or what? Humans make a lot of unreliable technology, and it is most often the cheapest or best marketed, not the most reliable, that gains market dominance.
    \n
    \nHow often can it be re-used? I see the designers working in some sort of artificial limit on it, so that you have to buy more later. I also see "hackers" finding ways to extend or even remove that limit. When that happens, material capitalism crashes because the putty can replace multiple items, which is a problem, because software capitalism (the patterns for the different forms) is a little unstable as it is.
    \n
    \nHow strong is it? This may limit a lot of what can be done with it, and it's another place for artificial limits. What colour is it? If it can turn into clothing, then keeping up with the fashion industry is as simple as downloading the right design (magazines become distribution channels, instead of just advertising). Your clothing may even change while you're wearing it, which brings up the reliability issue, and lots of embarrassing situations.
    \n
    \nThe Holo-projection system may seem limiting at first, until you realize that it could physically hold its own projection system, or possibly react to someone else's projection system unless there was some sort of signature worked into the holo-projection (more opportunity for problems or hacking!).

    \n What Gu does is commoditize form and function as a form of data. Now you purchase data (Gu models), which is more or less infinitely copyable, and the material is just Gu and the energy comes from wherever and is purchased on credit card.

    People make money by creating new models.

    Paul, you don't get up and put your clothes inside it. You don't have clothes or a desk. You have whatever you have downloaded. You don't have multiple sets of clothes. You have Gu and clothes models. You don't have furniture. You have Gu and furniture models. You don't even have to undress. You just have your controller create a bed for you, get in, and your clothes absorb into the sheets. When it's time to get up, you just get up and the Gu floor morphs clothes onto your body.

    The problems left to solve are energy purchases, Gu storage and transport, where the controller resides (if anywhere), and model purchase and storage. Data security, intellectual property, and financial transaction security become the real problems. See RIAA and Napster for examples of where that's going. Also, Gu might come with new environmental concerns. How does it interact with wildlife? What happens when a rogue AI gets some?

    Can Gu build a Gu manufacturing plant? Imagine the plans got tossed out on the future equivalent of BitTorrent. Everyone can download their own Gu plant. Assuming there's not some really hard to get natural resource (like Pollonium) that Gu can't create (intentionally?), the Gu patent holders are now in a bad situation. Sounds like the making of a cyberpunk novel to me. Is it cheaper to make your own Gu plant and Gu-raw-material-mine than to buy the Gu? I assume so.

    What if someone puts some "bad Gu" out on the street. A cheap knockoff. It's defective, or inferior.

    What if someone puts anti-Gu out on the street. Nano that loves it some Gu, yuuum yuuuum! Houses start dissolving.


    I think Neal Stephenson contemplates something much like Gu in one of his Victorian-cyberpunk novels. Every household has a fabricator machine and a utility line that pipes the good nano gunk into the house. They just purchase the blueprints for stuff. His gunk is way more limited than Gu, but some of his nano stuff is self-replicating.

    Dave: Some great stuff here.

    1. Can it change its volume? Not its mass, but by developing internal cavities it can change its volume up to a point (the point, obviously, at which it's too thin to support itself).

    4. Cutting edge - hmmm. Needs more thought. Perhaps that's an early safety feature which is later superseded by better intelligent interlocks to prevent people cutting themselves (or, more importantly, each other).

    5. Fashion industry - what I see happening is that the really wealthy people use old-fashioned "stiffstuff" which can't change its shape. It's conspicuous consumption not to use Gu. (Much as a really hoity-toity kitchen would be almost without plastic.) So there's a high-end fashion industry which uses traditional materials, and a low-end fashion industry which is much more like downloadable skins for your Gu-suit.
