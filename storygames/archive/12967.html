    \n It's not the time issue, it's that people unfamiliar with the freedom the rules grant them in naming their own skills occasionally pick something they will never be able to use, or which will be ultra-powerful and therefore reigned in by the DM. By making the characters ahead of time you can choose skill names and provide pre-defined explanations so they understand exactly what range of actions they can take with that skill, and how cool it will be to use it. Also, it lets you pick skills which will be useful against the Donjon scenes and monsters you have prepped, so you don't have characters where three of four skills are useless / uninteresting.

    Same goes with items - you can make a bunch of really neat magical items or items with cool skill uses. You can make up a dozen characters ahead of time without much of a problem and let them spend time choose from a wide range, instead of pondering exactly what "Language: Bohemian" will let them do and if "Charming snakes" will be useful to them.

    Another option would be to create a dozen characters and leave one or two skill fields blank, to be filled in either before play or (my preference) during play.

    With regards to the dice, d12s are definitely preferable to d20s. But don't be fooled - you want lots of ties! Ties result in a higher number of successes, which creates the opportunity for more interesting narrative effects and shorter, deadlier combats. Particularly good for a convention scenario!

    \n My first advice would be to actually register for games, and then show up to play them. There's some pick-up and demo play, but most folks are slot play and they book up (or get abandoned after five minutes of no-shows, so the GM can play something else).

    Failing that, bring your own copy(ies) of game(s) and try to run your own pick-up game. Less than ideal, though--I've demoed Icehouse for an entire Saturday, with only a handful of takers. Obviously, the longer form games will be even harder to get pick-up (as folks bail to do something in a slot, later).

    Worse comes to worse, peruse the Dealer Room and hit the Auction. I pretty routinely buy enough discounted/used stuff at the Dealer Room and items at the Auction for the net savings to offset the cost of a day pass (not that I pay to attend, as I've always run 3 or 4 slots). Then I'm sure you can find Are You A Werewolf/Cultist/Mafioso game running into the night (I might even be running it, if the Sat night one-shot LARP is identical to one I've played already at the designer's house).

    Make it your own. Go register and get into OGRE. Come to play.

    \n The Mountain Witch really sails with players who want to get dirty. The best times I've had with it have been when players make ronin characters in shades of gray. It sours things quickly when a player comes to the table expecting to be a white hat or a black hat. And the best characters are the ones that we don't know all about until the game unfolds. The players should be open to discover things they didn't know about their character as they climb the mountain.

    All of the games in the Indie by Storm session are tightly focused games, and I love them all for different reasons. It's going to be up to who shows and what they want to play, but it's a mix of games I really want to show people, so expect high enthusiasm either way.

    And of course, there's the GM Improv contest. There were three games on there, last I checked. Should be around six or seven if last year's any indication. The guy who runs the Scion game consistently scores high, and the guy running Paranoia this year won last year (running Maid). It's fun to see the GMs come with pre-built pieces and try to spontaneously wrap them around a plot they've been handed. And you get to score the GMs on their performance, so instant feedback?

    Don't Rest Your Head is mad fun with the mechanic. Every roll feels like a huge gamble because you can see the Pain and the Exhaustion and the Madness right there, waiting to take you out. (And the setting is pretty cool too.)

    Kobolds Ate My Baby is just crazy random goodness. If you want a game that feels like a carnival of underpowered hungry misfits, you should try it.

      \n I, too, had a great con--MACE is usually solid, as it's a good mix of meeting folks and seeing the same folks year after year.

      My Friday Night Fiasco used the Vegas playset, around the late 60s when The Strip was beginning to take the limelight away from Freemont/Downtown. It was rollicking and weird--I'll throw out a short AP when I remember to bring my notes into work--but I screwed up in an interesting way:
      * I had folks keeping their die in Act I.
      * To "fix" that, I switched us to giving away the die in Act II (explaining multiple times to the players that I'd gotten it backwards, so they wouldn't judge the rules based on my screw-up)

      Turns out... it was kind of interesting. It "broke" the fan-mail-esque aspect of giving dice in Act I (i.e. as players we can easily make sure the person we most-want to make a Tilt call gets to) but it made for a more collective determination of a character Aftermath, rather than that being something a player can totally manipulate (i.e. always let the others frame, pulling the right color die to end up with several of one color and few or none of the other). I'm not sure the collaboration on Aftermath was a good gain against the loss of fan-mail-ness in Act I.

      My Saturday Morning IAWA didn't make, so the one player and I shifted over to Jeff's Fiasco, set On The Ice. I will post an AP when I have the notes (Jeff hasn't got time, so he said I could handle it). It ran VERY smoothly, in part because Jeff and I could back each other up on the various rules nuances. It was... wack and weird, of course.

      (Those who played in that one: On the way home after midnight that night, I--NO SHIT--heard a story on NPR about penguin smuggling for cooking as a delicacy. Those who weren't in the game: one plot element was a Chinese restaurant serving penguin because it's easier to get than chicken in the Antarctic.)

      Saturday Afternoon Donjon went well, too--my favorite session, I think. I had Arnold backing me up on the rules (which saved me from a LOT of page flipping and buried noses) and so I could actually focus on adjusting the plot for Facts the players injected. Fortunately (or maybe not?), not too many "narrative Abilities" were made, so i could keep them mostly "on rails" to get to the encounters I had planned. No AP for this one from me, but here's an Executive Summary:
      * Warrior, assassin, and mountain troll trying to find missing townsfolks. (Note: We should have had individual goals for the adventure, too, to further-drive Fact narration with successes)
      * A "test drive" encounter with some adders, to make sure everyone had the basic hang of the system. (Note: We didn't--Donjon demands the use of combos (success dice passed forward) or Fact manipulation to get really solid dice pools. Not enough of that was done until much later in the game.)
      * The "entryway" encounter with a couple of snakemen. The entryway shows many "statues" in poses of horror, flight, and cowering... but other statues show specific poses and stances, even though the faces are still rictuses of terror.
      * The party discovers the "Posing Room"--a room with two huge armatures, holding a young woman in a particular stance--into which the last batter snakeman flees, to use the woman as a hostage.
      * Party "calls bluff"--not a bluff--and the snakeman kills the woman, triggering the Big Bad (who's actually pissed at the snakeman, and hardly notices the party until they attack her):
      ** A slug-like, stubby snake's body the size of a smartcar, with a obese woman's upper torso.
      ** Hair and beard--yes, beard--encrusted with some spackle-looking substance.
      ** Same substance is also dribbling down her chest and torso, hardened into plates (Armor 4).

      Basically... a Puke Medusa. Jealous of the fairest in the land, she captures them, poses them, then vomits up this spackle stuff, to encase them.

      The mountain troll doesn't kill her, instead knocking her Discernment down to 1 and winning an Intimidate check to make her tell how to turn the people back. The Solution: her antacid medicine, properly diluted, dissolves the spackle. Fade to Yuck....

      LOTS of fun at the Auction, eventually scoring Killer Bunnies Blue, Yellow, and Violet (great--now I have to buy Red!) and a couple of other card/board games that looked good.

      Wrapped the Saturday Evening up with Joey's Mechaton short game, which I managed to win on ONE roll, at the end of the game, where I'd lose instantly if I didn't roll a 5 (NOT a 6!) and thereby hit the objective (which we'd ruled earlier would cause it to move 1 unit for every five, in any direction, to represent it being "hockey-pucked" to a new spot). Shifted to within 1 unit of my mech, JUST outside of 1 unit of the current controller's (Joey's) mech... FTW! Got a nice Darth Vader Lego key chain and a Vincent Baker Mark 1 Lego mech for my effort, too! Thanks, Joey!

      Even if I stopped going to cons year-round, I'd still go to to MACE every year. Best local con, period.
