\n Does color mechanically matter?
\n
\nI have a hard time when what you say doesn't matter mechanically.
\n
\nFor me, above all things... GM or no GM... Stats or no Stats... even playing a character... color mattering is #1.
\n
\nIt's what makes RPG special. I love video games, card game, board games... many games where what you describe doesn't mechanically matter but when it comes to RPGs... I want it to matter.
\n
\nColor for its own sake doesn't work for me. I do it. But it feels incomplete.
\n
\nColor that just determines what stat you roll or as justification of using a trait also feels empty.
\n
\nYet, color being much more important than mechanics also feels off. I prefer structure to complete freeform.
\n
\nOne of the lamest feelings I experience in an RPG is where we say awesome things and then roll a die, and what we said has no consequence on that roll. I sometimes enjoy the dramatic irony... say something awesome... fail anyway or say something risky or doomed to fail... succeed! But not every time. It loses its impact pretty quickly.
\n
\nIt feels less lame when I roll and then roleplay. At least one is informing the other and they aren't separated. But still it feels a bit empty.
\n
\nYet, this doesn't bother me when I play an RPG with map and minis. The act of positioning my mini for some sort of tactical advantage makes me fulfilled. I can deal with color not mattering mechanically here. Positioning is my color.
\n
\nSimilarly, when I play a game like Mutants and Masterminds where my character obtains a car in the fiction... but mechanically, the car doesn't affect anything till I earn Power Points to pay for it after the fact (and I could have bought a car even if I didn't obtain one in the fiction)... it feels disconnected. It's usually a game balance or resource management issue. But I want the fiction to trump all of that.
\n
\nRock,
\nJohn

    \n Hey hey hey, my currently ongoing campaign uses the game that was written for this: Stalker is the critically aclaimed Finnish diceless roleplaying game based on the Strugatsky novel. Its core idea is that instead of dicing, conflicts (it has a modern conflict resolution system) are resolved by having the GM evaluate the concrete description of the action that the player provides. This description is then numerized in a clever way and compared to a target number. It's really screwy as an experience at first, as you're constantly feeling like it's more deterministic than it really is, but it does at least ensure that description matters: in fact, I would go as far as to say that FLOW (the rules-set behind the game) forefronts the action in a supremely immersive way on the expense of consequences, as creative energy is directed at narrating and experiencing the action in minute detail.

    I have to say that I don't myself need quite this level of connection between the spoken word and the mechanics of the game in my routine gaming. Rather, while I agree with you that the fictional situation is #1, it's enough for me when that situation is in equal parts informed by and informing the rules mechanics of the game. The Solar System strikes a good balance to my tastes in this, as it gives you a bit of condition modifier harshly limited, a bit of stakes negotiation that forefronts fictional positioning, a bit of this and that, but ultimately the system is still formalistic: whatever else happens, the game always comes back to a simple Ability check. (It's interesting to compare my preferences in this with Clinton's - a close reading of the original TSoY rules text leads me to conclude that a character probably can't get a car in that game without paying the Advance for it, while in my rules text it is explicit that the fiction trumps the xp pacing, but at a cost: you get your car and get to pay for it later, but meanwhile you're paying a penalty in a different currency for not being able to pay the xp just yet.) I like this level of connection between the fiction and the mechanics, where both are strong and indispensable partners in the system.

    \n One of the advantages of free systems where the GM assigns bonuses is that the players' colour can give them those bonuses. Unlike J Walton, I think this is freeing, not stunting. To give an example, in My Life With Master, the emotion die is practically the only way which a player's roleplaying contribution can affect a mechnical outcome. Where players feel they are being "judged" and consider that to be negative in principle, or disagree with the GM's judgement in particular, this isn't going to work, but for most groups, it's fine.
    \n
    \nOne trick I've found useful is to look at the players' contributions through the lens of their character's stats, particularly if there is a disparity between the player and the PC s and assign bonuses accordingly.
    \n
    \nFinally, the point at which you narrate colour is important - if you start narrating amazing colour before resolution, there will of course be disappointment when it doesn't happen.

    \n I'm afraid all I can do is commiserate with you John.
    I, too, prefer color to matter, but I have not yet found that perfect balance.

    In my recemt Star Lost campaign we adapted an idea I got from Amagi Games which we called "wouldn't it be cool if..." cards. Players could create a scenario (wouldn't it be cool if x happened) and vote on how much they would like it to happen by paying from a meta-game currency called Chutzpah to place a bounty on the card. Whoever got the outcome to occur would earn the Chutzpah voted onto it. Chutzpah is spent to fuel re-rolls, add additional dice to rolls, or take over narration in the game \u2014 all in an attempt to foster agency in the players. It worked, to a certain extent, but sometimes not.
    For example, in one scenario the characters were in a restaurant/bar dealing with underworld sleeze. Chutzpah could be used to add scenery to a location layout (which were typically built on the fly). Someone added in a decorative fish tank. Someone else thought, "wouldn't it be cool if..." the fish tank accidentally exploded during a fight. Awesome. We loved it. Everyone was trying to get the fish tank to explode, short of shooting it directly (and thus spoiling the spirit of the idea). Someone even Jackie-Channed off the damned thing to avoid a burning puddle and land a kick on a bad guy. And we never got the tank to rupture, leaving the outcome up to dice rolls. It was a little disappointing. Sure, someone could have spent Chutzpah to narrate that the tank explodes, but that would have cost them what they earned from the bounty. The fellow who caromed off of it could have spent Chutzpah to tweak the dice on the "does it burst" roll, but I think he was tapped out after the roll for the attack. So, no win. It was all pretty funny and became a running joke in the final days of the game (and still crops up in other games we play). I don't think it bothered my players as much as me, but it stuck in my craw as a failure of the system. There should have been a way other than GM fiat to affect the outcome of the "rupture" rolls without just spending Chutzpah. But I had no stunting rules in place. I thought Chutzpah was sufficient. And, maybe it is. Maybe I'm just annoyed that we couldn't explode the damned tank.

    Posted By: JesseI've learned that [standards] are actually fairly concrete observable standards when you really hunker down and pay attention to what's being said and to the group's reaction.
    \nI've found the same thing about Exalted's stunting guidelines. Something like "+1 for flavorful description, +2 for flavorful description that makes use of the environment and +3 if the whole table does says something like 'holy shit!'" doesn't have a whole lot of "grey area" in practice. It's usually pretty obvious.

    As for "Can't the fiction be important in ways other than [making] you more successful?", the Exalted example is a slight help here as well, even though this isn't really obvious (or, perhaps, even intentional). But after you play for a while, you notice that, aside from the "make me more successful" stuff, stunting actually does two other things that are sort of only marginally implied in the rules: 1) it lets you bend rules. Now, this might just be seen as some other flavor of "making you more successful", but it feels different in play. 2) it provides a mechanism for the player to inject things into the setting. For example, suppose a player does some kind of acrobatic stunt using a tapestry that runs from the ground floor to the third floor ceiling. All well and good, but no one ever mentioned the tapestry before. The player just assumed there was one there, and now there is. And what's more, this tapestry is now begging for other players to use it in stunts that "make use of the environment". In other words, in addition to making his character more effective, the player also just gave the whole table more opportunities for memorable shit to happen. It's like a Jackie Chan scene... you might not notice the ladder is there at first, but as soon as Jackie picks it up, that ladder now matters to everyone in the room, at least for a little while.

    Exalted does many, many things badly, but it's stunting system has always impressed me. It's very useful for the type of social stuff people have mentioned above, where you get a mix of what is said vs. skills the character has both mattering in any given situation.

    \n I've got into the habit in my local group of calling this sort of thing 'stunt fatigue'. It kind of encompasses a number of different potential drawbacks to the 'cool description for bonus' approach-

    a) Players tend to shoot their creative bolt early in the session/scene, when they have lots of ideas, and can often dry up just at climax where they would have wanted the most dramatic description.
    b) Description can have a rote quality when it's done just for a mechanical bonus (OK, this time I back-flip and hit him with a soup spoon).
    c) Players can sometimes be so busy working out what they're going to do next they're not listening to the current dramatic description, rather ruining the point.
    d) Sometimes all the elaborate description can slow things down when you just want to resolve the damn scene, already. Players get bored, particularly if their character isn't in that scene.

    Which is not to say that systems that reward description are defective. But I generally prefer a light seasoning of description-heavy actions to a bowl full of the stuff.

    Posted By: jenskotEuro, can you tell us more about Stalker. Specifically how the GM evaluates the player\u2019s descriptions?
    \nThe GM in FLOW considers the player's contribution in two categories, Idea and Roleplaying; the former basically concerns the immediate realism of the character's proposed course of of action in the fictional context, while the the latter is about whether the proposed course matches with the abilities and nature of the character (and whether it's entertaining for the rest of the group and dramatic - entertainment concerns are usually scored in this category). Basically you fare well in the game by describing action that seems likely to succeed in achieving the stated goal while remaining true to and expressing with clarity who and what the character is. Both of those categories are scored on a 1-5 range (there are various scoring tools in the book to help in this), and the results are then multiplied with each other to gain a final score, which is then compared against a difficulty level also determined by the GM. Characters have skills that can influence the final score, too. There are some neat fine details like how failed attempts still change the situation (a very modern conflict resolution "draw rule", in fact), how successes and failures can be graded and so on. I especially like how the player characters have hero points that are also hitpoints: whatever the adverse effect of failing a challenge is, the player can negate it by spending a point. As combats and such are by default lethal challenges, this basically makes the hero points into hitpoints as well. The math is elegant, as the player performance has an exponential scale while difficulty scales linearly.

    The weirdest thing in that whole system is that it's all in the GM's head, and the players basically have no mechanical input into it whatsoever aside from using the get out of jail free card when a challenge goes awry. To an outside observer the system doesn't differ from GM fiat in any way whatsoever, which is pretty neat if you're into immersionism and mechanics not "getting into way". From the GM viewpoint it's pretty different from pure fiat, though, as you're on your honor to utilize the authoritative yet reasonably objective standards of evaluation provided by the game. I myself make the scores public when playing, in fact, instead of just telling the player that he succeeded or failed; this can be pretty intense, as any pretense of the adjucation being something else than an expression of the GM's creativity is swept aside: your goal is to get rated well, and you only get rated well by having a good creative relationship with the GM. Matrix Games have a similar feature in how they rely on the players judging each other's contributions.

    Stalker is among the most honest authoritative GM games that I've seen alongside Dread. It also differs from the latter in that while Dread encourages the players to quiet down and listen calmly to the atmospheric storyteller, FLOW encourages active narrative and active participation: the life and death of your character depends on you being sharp and on the ball, visualizing the GM's world correctly and suggesting courses of action that make sense. I like the fact that there are now two game texts in this common yet underdesigned genre that I can respect as pieces of game design.

    \n As has been pointed out, the things that the system mechanically rewards will tend to create a reward cycle that the players will tend to tap into and feed.

    A system that gives bonus dice for stunting or making the table laugh will tend to get a glut of that sort of behavior. If that's what you want, great.

    A system that awards +2 for the high ground will tend to get a lot of characters climbing up on tables and stairs and outcroppings. If that's what you want, great.

    John, do you find this sort of system color-effective enough for your tastes:
    The fictional circumstances naturally dictate what kinds of approaches will be feasible.
    The player describes the way her character is going to approach the situation.
    Because the character is better at some types of activity than others, this means that the color has affected the probabilities of various outcomes.

    So, if the character is really good at sneaking, and the player wants the character to succeed, she will try to find a way to describe her character's actions to bring that skill to bear on the situation.
    And depending upon the fictional situation, she might not be able to credibly do that, and might have to rely on a less effective plan.

    \n Not to sound like a broken record or a shill, but the BW approach really fixed this problem for me.

    Prior to BW, our go-to game was Exalted. I think anyone who's ever played Exalted understands the weird disconnect between an awesomely described stunt and a mechanical result that does not reflect that awesomeness. Some of that is poor technique: we should be describing stunts up to but not including the final execution. But some of it is IMO just crummy design. It's extra-crummy when the net result is players constantly fishing for more dice, without actually adding anything substantial to the preresolution fiction.

    So I think it's the intent/task split in BW that was the big fix for us: You can describe an awesome character action, the character can even actually do the awesome action, but the technical execution of the action is divorced from the intent behind why you, the character's author, were doing it. This works less brilliantly in the context of combat, where the actions usually *are* the intent.

    I also like that working up the initial die pool is almost a purely technical exercise: what's my skill, what are some FoRKs (thereby reinforcing the fictional approach I'm taking), oh hey here's some help dice (pulling everyone else into the fiction-making), and only after all that do I describe how it looks in the fiction, lobby for an advantage die from the GM, who may also grant an advantage die of his own based purely on fiat and taste. It's a nice back-and-forth, that pool building exercise.

    Anyway, yeah, not at all a fan of games where the fiction isn't required to be taken into account in the final resolution. Which is maybe what you were saying in the OP.
