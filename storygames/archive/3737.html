The Chorus as a role

\n
\n
\n
\n\nTonyLB \n\n\n
\n
\n\nJuly 2007 \n edited July 2007 in Play Advice
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n So here's a role that a player can take on in a game: They are the ones responsible for creating the illusion of a world with many, many, MANY people. When someone needs to fill in the details of a school janitor, or an alien overlord, or anyone else who is not the focus character of any of the other players, this guy has the responsibility and authority to do that.
\n
\nThey get (usually) broad ability to make up new characters at the drop of a hat, limited only by plausibility (and sometimes not even that).
\n
\nNow this is often folded in with the package of roles called "GM." To me, though, this looks separable from many of the other roles. This role of populating the world (which I'm going to call the "Chorus" role for the purposes of this thread) doesn't require (for instance) the ability to frame scenes. It doesn't require the ability to be the arbiter of the rules. It doesn't require most of the rest of the GM package.
\n
\nHow else can we play with this role? How else can we organize it?
\n
\nWhat if it floats from one person to another?
\n
\nWhat if two (or more) people are fulfilling this role simultaneously?
\n
\nWhat if nobody is filling the role?

    \n This is fertile ground, IMO. As a long-time GM/SG/HG/what-have-you, this can be a lifesaver.
    \n
    \nBack in the day, I ran a very large Ars Magica campaign (I think on average we had seven players show up, could go as high as twelve for some sessions).
    \n
    \nI'd hand out NPC assignments to folks not in a scene and let them have some authority. In all honesty, that was the height of my illusionist GMing, so I'm not sure I ceded any real authorial power to these folks, but still, it worked well...
    \n
    \nIt really helped bring the game to life, as the varied perspectives on the NPCs made them all the more real.
    \n
    \nI'd say the fiddly bits of who, how it works, will depend on local needs.
    \n
    \nOne thing I'd like to see, is a system like Wraith - but instead of having another player portray your shadow, you've have another player (aside from the GM) play your character's aversaries - and they'd get benefits for their PC depending on how much trouble they could make for you!

    \nI've done that. Both in a spontaneous manner, when some PCs were not present ("Make some guys up who might be there.") and as a standard rule.


    As for the latter, one player was chosen to play the chorus and another was chosen to play nature (i.e. weather, light effects, scenery). The basic idea was to hand these jobs to someone else, because as a GM I tend to forget about them, when I have other GMing things to do. It worked reasonably well, although the same thing happened: The players forgot about nature and chorus, when they were engaged in their PCs.

    But with the right players, I'd do it again. I know a few people who are just awesome in one of these aspects.
    \n
    What if nobody is filling the role?
    \nPlayers might start using monlogues. I've seen that in InSpectres, when we played it without a GM. Instead of meeting an NPC, players just phoned someone speaking for their character and making pauses, when the other person would speak. Then we explained to the other PCs, what we found out.

    A friend of mine also made up an RPG that drew heavily from theatre. So there were a few places (i.e. stage make-ups) chosen in advance and a few characters. Others could not be introduced, so players would do all the classical tricks like looking out of a window describing what they saw, receiving letters etc.

    \nI think you could look at some LARPs and how they handle this. Of necessity, NPCs tend to be downplayed or left out entirely in LARPs, because a GM can only play one at a time and such. So a lot more of the game becomes about the PCs interacting and providing antagonism for one another. This can work in tabletop settings, too.

    Levi Kornelson's Dustbowl Prophets, for example, explicitly calls for minimizing the importance of NPCs and making PC interactions more important. And reinforces this by rewarding escalating a PC relationship (all of which tend to lead toward conflict if sufficiently escalated).

    Posted By: TonyLBIhaven'tseen (at least not anywhere near as often) the idea of giving someone other than the GM the authority toinventNPCs in order to play them.
    \nWe do this. It's tricky, because when you get tagged to create a new NPC you don't want to come on so strong that it disrupts any of the elements of a scene. Here's a good example:

    In Contenders, Remi's guy goes to the evil crime boss, who is dying of consumption. I say "The crime boss is hacking his lungs out, but you can talk to his lieutenant. Clinton, Go!" And Clinton comes up with a skeevy dude on the fly, connects him to the crime boss and Remi's guy, gives Remi a work scene, and it is all awesome. That skeevy dude is established and returns over the course of the game. I just sit back and cough a lot.

    Here's a bad example:

    We're playing Heroes Banner, and Clinton tags me to play one of the billow maidens, these nine underwater evil godling ladies. So I get all evil (again on Remi, hmm) and billowy, and start describing things that we're going to do to Remi's guy if he doesn't get us this thing we want, that I then make up. Basically adding a major plot point to the game and stealing focus from the giant evil dude that Clinton was playing. And it turns out I went to far and it wasn't cool.

    So in both cases the person with authority tagged another player to jump in and create an NPC out of whole cloth, but there's a fine line related to the now-shared authority when this happens.

    \n In games where there's more of a traditional GM, I tend to think the (usually implicit) social contract is that the GM has to tag someone with this authority - as Mr. Morningstar's examples suggest. In my experiences, it was always the GM handing out permission to create the NPC.
    \n
    \nEspecially given my tendency to have tracks laid for the "its-my-story" railroad, I would try to restrict the types of plot elements the chorus could introduce.
    \n
    \nIf there were either examples of how to do this in a game text (which means leaving the details up to the social contract level of play), or if there were out and out mechanics for it, that would help.
    \n
    \nMost of the time, in my experience, handing out the Chorus role happens as a bolt on to other rules.
    \n
    \nI would say for this to really sing, you'd need games with fairly easy to no prep character creation (even if those easy to use rules are just for NPCs vs. PCs).
    \n
    \nSo, it'd be much easier to bring a full NPC into the mix in a game of Capes, than say in a game of GURPS or D&D.

    \n Hmmmmm, interesting. For some time now I've been allowing pretty much any players in my games to introduce NPCs at just about any time.     I mean it's already there in Phasmaphobic's spec. If resources do what you want them to do, no arbitration will be necessary, which is good.

    \n

    Hackeysack in a collaborative game. The rules are simple: you can't use any body part lower than the elbow to touch the sack and everyone has to touch it at least before going back to the server. The more you do that, the awesomer.

    \n

    There are some ancillaries: being able to keep the thing going around your own body for a long time is cool. Keeping it for too long is being a jerk and showing off without other people being able to play. Rotate so one player doesn't always have the sun in their eyes. Don't take someone else's shot. Don't apologize when you miss and the bag hits the ground.

    \n

    With the exception of the "don't hold onto the sack for too long" rule, there's no judgment required. You collaborate. Sometimes players will give the benefit of the doubt when a suspicious part of the elbow gets used, but mostly, everyone follows the rules (with local variants, like the teenaged boy "if you drop it someone throws it at you hard" dickneckery). Sometimes, there's a dispute over whether someone's taken someone else's hit, but it's usually pretty obviousl What you're doing is clear.

    \n

    I want my games to be like hackeysack or soccer, and in neither game do your fellow refs or players get to say when something you did is valid. It either is, or it isn't. The ball is in bounds, or it isn't. You either kicked that guy or you didn't. The refs are there to observe that the rules are being followed (because the game is competitive and the interest of both teams is in lying in their own favor), or the fellow players keep you in check because you're fucking up the game for everyone by breaking them.

    \n

    Now, imagine that the refs in a game of soccer could judge on how good a goal was at awarded points accordingly. Suddenly, it becomes more like figure skating, where national rivalry interferes with judgment, where there are scandals about what the judges mean. This has become a game where you play the ref, not the rules.
