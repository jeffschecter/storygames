\n Over in this thread right here, Joel and Mike (stupidgremlin) brought up the Marvel Superheroes (sometimes known as FASERIP) game, about due for some lovin'.

Last night at GoPlayPDX, Joel showed us his dice idea, and this morning I've been inspired. I'll let Joel bring up his dice idea if he wants, but it directly leads into idea the first: fixing up the character sheet. Joel's idea is a reaction to the big colorful chart, but I'm looking at cleaning it up, bringing the relevant information to the character sheet under the attribute. The character sheet will have a box on it for each of the attributes, plus additional boxes for powers, skills, etc. This box will contain the attribute name, the rank name, the rank value, and a mini-chart with two columns: one for dice ranges, the second with white, green, yellow, and red spaces. You just write down the dice roll ranges for the rank. For instance, a character with Remarkable Fighting would write down 01-35 for white, 36-65 for green, 66-94 for yellow, and 95-100 for red. When you're using Fighting, it's a lot quicker lookup than cross referencing the full chart. The stats boxes could also contain the relevant portions of the battle effects table (with miss, hit, slam, stun, kill, etc.) to save another lookup.

At some point I'll throw together a mockup of what I'm talking about (but I'm not a graphic designer, so it'll be thrown together in OpenOffice and exported to PDF). It could also be used with only slight modification for Joel's dice idea.

Idea the second: Besides the "usual" kinds of stuff (relationship maps and so forth) the first idea that comes to mind is players "betting" Karma points on the things they want to do in the session. For instance, in this session Spider-Man's player wants to see some three-way relationship drama between him, Mary Jane, and Gwen, so he decides to bet 30 Karma on that. He's not too interested in villain fights this time around, but does want to see some of his rogues' gallery put in an appearance, so he throws down 10 each on the Green Goblin and Sandman. Payout is at 2-1, so when he resolves the MJ-Gwen-Peter thing he gets back 60, and 20 each for the two villains.

There's two corollaries to this:

1. The bigger bets are the bigger payoff and so are more of the focus of the session; the other things are sidelines at best.

2. These bets become GM budget. When Spider-Man puts down 30 Karma on that relationship thing, the GM now has 30 Karma to spend to make things interesting, plus 10 each on the Goblin and Sandy.

And 1 and 2 taken in conjunction can also mean that maybe the GM can sort of poke the bigger issues into the smaller; like, for instance, Peter gets distracted on his way to get to MJ or Gwen by the Goblin robbing a bank. While he's on his errand for MJ, the GM decides that 5 of his MJ-Gwen budget are going to be used to put Gwen in harm's way in this scene.

    \n So, I tried running this as-written in Summer 2010 for 3-4 sessions, and it was a mess. I'm actually gearing up for a one-shot in a few weeks, so I'd appreciate comments & analysis on the text as written, especially from the people who are like, "This game needs to be heavily reworked."
    \n
    \nHere's stuff I think the game does reasonably well for its era:
    \n* Licensed setting/characters with enormous amount of fan-enthusiasm. Lots of published support material.
    \n
    \n* Characters can be pegged to certain objective performances, permitting comparison. "Wolverine is a better fighter than the Hulk. How about that?"
    \n
    \n* Therefore there's an objective sense of difficulty missing in Forge-supers games with freeform traits, like Capes and With Great Power. In this game, If Aunt May wants to defeat Galactus, she will have to goddamn work for it. (This is potentially very problematic, though; see below)
    \n
    \n* Lasting character improvement is glacially slow (just like in the comics), but the hero-point mechanic works around this by basically permitting characters to function above and beyond what they'd normally be capable of. So you don't really gather up XP to improve yourself, rather accumulate and then burn resources to kick ass. There's nothing innovative about this in 2011, but it's still a decent solution to the unchanging world of comics.
    \n
    \n* The hero-points are rewarded for crime-fighting. If you want to be effective, you need to be heroic.
    \n
    \n* RPG rules written by Spider-Man!
    \n
    \n
    \nHere are some places where I think the game falls down:
    \n* Using objective difficulty - the Hulk really is stronger than Spider-Man - ignores the fact that in comics, everyone can beat everyone else, depending on the needs of the plot. There's always some lateral thinking approach that works wonders. The trouble is that here, the GM is the controller of whether those lateral approaches exist in the first place. You need to factor in some degree of Director Stance here for the players to really get creative.
    \n
    \n* Fixed damage vs. fixed damage-reduction leads to an arms race.
    \n
    \n* No minion rules. If Nightcrawler needs four punches to take down a street-thug, something's wrong. (Nightcrawler's whole deal is that he's pretty good taking out mobs of wimpy guys.)
    \n
    \n* Combat is pretty static as written: run up to a guy and exchange blows. Combat in the comics is dynamic, with all kinds of crazy things happening left and right.
    \n
    \n* The Resource system is screwy, both in the Basic Game (where it's basically gold pieces and you spend it on everything) and the Advanced Game (where it's treated as a standard ability score so you don't have to handle routine expenses, but is harder to save up every penny for a huge expenditure)
    \n
    \n* The Inventing sub-system is built around the Resource system. So if Resources are wack, Inventing is wack too. There's this huge bottleneck of funding to complete any serious invention.
    \n
    \n* Scenario creation rules are non-existent.
    \n
    \n* The published modules are uniformly terrible. Like, curse-Johannes-Gutenberg-for-making-it-possible terrible.
    \n
    \n* If the game is meant to be run as a sandbox, there needs to be more support given to that style of play. (I.e., more random tables that present fun situations)
    \n
    \n* Wholly original characters don't have enough "flags." Canon characters only have "flags" if you're familiar with them from comics.
    \n
    \n* The hero-point system has penalties that seem to be affected by a Hobson's Choice, i.e., "You can save the world or you can go on a date with Mary Jane." This would seem to invite pretty coercive GM'ing. (Also, it's not really a choice, since saving the world earns you +40 Karma or whatever, while ignoring your girlfriend only costs -10 Karma. Saving the world is the rational choice every time.)
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \nI'm sure there are other problems too, but I think an attempt to Awesome-ize the game requires taking a look at what does and doesn't work.

    \n My friends briefly tried Marvel Supes back in the day and quickly went back to Champions because of many of the problems James listed.*

    Icons pretty much fixes all the problems I had with FASERIP. I use house rules that are more FATE like, as well as a stunt point system that replaces Icon's rules for stuns and slams. If you want to use old FASERIP characters and modules, Icons' 1-10 ratings map exactly to FASERIP's Poor->Unearthly ranks. Icons combines Strength and Endurance into one Strength stat, so in converting most Marvel characters I'd just use the Strength score, and add Invulnerability for high Endurance characters.**

    Icons has a lot of minor flaws in power write ups and lack of clearly written examples and procedures, but there are resources on the 'net that address these.

    *At the time the deal-breaker was that any character with a decent movement power could do Shift-X damage by moving through a bunch of areas before hitting the target. Probably wouldn't bother me now, so go figure.

    **If you are feeling super-nostalgic you can rename Icons stats from Prowess, Coordination, Strength, Intellect, Awareness, Willpower to Fighting, Agility, Strength, Reason, Influence, Perception.

    \n Nathan, it's true you could Stun an enemy, but that requires two things to happen:
    \n* You've gotta get a red result on your attack, which happens roughly 5% of the time, not counting Karma expenditures.
    \n* and the target must fail an Endurance feat, which for crummy enemies happens roughly 50% of the time
    \n* my recollection is that this is basically how it works in the Advanced game too. I think you get to make multiple attacks by a Fighting feat, but the issue really is how durable your second-rate enemies are relative to a single attack, not how often you can try to hit them.
    \n
    \nSo that method would work on roughly 2.5% of all attacks (significantly more often if you're using an edged weapon, but still). Alternately, there might be some deus ex machina in the environment - like trapping everyone in wet concrete, or an ultra-sonic stun ray - but that either requires the GM to set it up in advance or for the players to have some authority to create such things as needed.
    \n
    \nI can totally see a house-rule where minions automatically fail the Endurance feat, but it's still something that would only occur 5% of the time not counting Karma. (I figure if you need to spend Karma to take out a minion, you probably shouldn't be a superhero. But maybe I'm stacking the deck too much.)
    \n
    \nBTW I'll admit that the idea of "minions" isn't appropriate to all superhero games: maybe in a pulp 1930's game, even ordinary joes ought to put up a good fight. And I'm not sure how well "beta-supers" thrashing hordes of minions accords with Silver Age comics. But by the mid-1980s, when the game was written, this was a pretty common thing even for relatively low-powered characters like Daredevil or Captain America to plow through armies of guys in a panel or two.

    \n Many options come to me. One stranger option would be to use color coded dice, with te degree of success mechanic built into the dice. I'd have to work out how, but it be a strange different tangent of design on MSH.

    However, I love MSH mostly as is, I even have hardcover rulebooks (that cost more than the original boxed set to get done by the way.)

    I'd likely want to make sure karma is given improved more soap opera influence. Make sure it worked for that style of comic a bit better. For example the more problem Peter Parker faces (deals with but does not resolve) the more karma he gets to succeed when he needs. Essentially the more he suffers at one thing the better he does at another. Resolving a situation means he need to create another problem to face to earn karma. There is no free ride.

    Secondly he can suffer serious negative changes, any hero can, and that gets them a lasting change to their hero. Of course the change could also come with a hidden negative. Such as the Black Suit, which helped him at first butt became insidious later.

    No advancement rules at all, at least not without using such drama tied to it.

    I've always seen the game as resource based game with a randomizer cost reducer (dice rolls.)

    I like the biding idea above, but I always have problems with games that make you the player/GM think about a mechanic as a mechanic too much. Pour whatever changes back into the characters viewpoint, make sure it comes and interacts with their point of view. Spidey makes a choice, make sure that choice gets him a rule effect, good or bad.
