\nPoetic roleplaying
This form of roleplaying games was invented by Tomas HV M\xc3\xb8rkrid (Oslo, Norway) in 2007. It is a very simple idea: make a roleplaying game that shall be played out in 15 minutes, not one second more or less. When 15 minutes is up the game is over (use a timer, if you will). That is it!

The point?
- to focus on athmosphere rather than action...
- to focus on sensitivity rather than dramatic development...
- to see if it is possible to explore poetic states rather than the epic or dramatic or prosaic...

It is simple to write, easy to play, and so rewarding in terms of gamer and gamesmith experiences!

Feel free to try it!

    \n "Poetic roleplaying" found it's way to Knudepunkt in Denmark last weekend, without my participation (Matthijs Holter; "Five characters seeking an identity"). I have made three such poetic games myself, all relating to soccer in some way.

    Brimingham - Stoke 1-1: a poetic fable about dull soccerfans on a pub in Birmingham after a dull match. Tested: very strong effect! A low key and very still game. An energydraining and beautiful experience!

    Kretsfinalen! (The county finals): a game about players in the wardrobe after winning the junior championship of their county by an astounding 8-0! Tested: makes for very loud gameplay. The game went from an ordinary situation and into weirdness within 15 minutes. It ended up being a thriller with the players about to get gassed in the wardrobe. Showed with shocking clarity how very, very long 15 minutes can be in real time gameplay!

    Kampen mot nedrykk (Battling relegation): a game about the last 15 minutes of the last match of the series. The players are players on the soccer team HamKam, playing Rosenborg on their homefield and trailing 0-2. To keep their place in the top league they have to win. This game has not been tested yet. It's the only poetic roleplaying game with a resolution system!!! ;D I aim to test it shortly.

    I am sorry that I don't have the games themselves to post here. They are only on handwritten sheets. You will have to make your own... :-)

    \n Here's two. The first has been tested.

    Five characters in search of an identity

    The players play characters that know they're characters in a role-playing game. They haven't been assigned an identity yet. They can talk to each other, but not to the players or GM. They're free to speculate in what sort of characters they are, what sort of game it is etc.

    After a while (3-5 minutes) the GM assigns the first character an identity. After that, he/she gives the other characters their identity as he/she sees fit.

    The identities are:

    Mother
    Little sister
    The dead dog
    God
    God

    Prejudice

    For 4 or 6 players.

    Each player writes a negative descriptor on a piece of paper - such as "thief", "liar", "pervert", "no sense of humor" etc.

    The players divide into two teams. Each team is given a name based on the first letters/syllables in the players' names. (Matt, Joe and Heather might be called "Hemjo", or whatever).

    The papers are divided into two piles, one for each team. That shows what prejudices the other team holds for them. (Example: Hemjo people are liars and perverts).

    The first team now has five minutes to talk about the second team. They must tell anecdotes from real life about the second team which confirm the prejudices. After that, the second team does the same to the first team, for five minutes. (Example: A player from the other team tells the story of when Joe cheated at a math test. Another player remembers when Heather made an unfitting remark about a young boy.)

    \n Well, what is a "poetic state" actually? I'm a published poet, and have problems with the term. Poetry may delve into very different stages. Walt Withman and Allen Ginsberg are a bit difficult to place in the same "poetic state", so to say.

    But still: the idea is to facilitate focus on a state rather than a drama, although several of the games tested has shown high drama sneaking in, in spite of this (or to spite the idea?). And then the idea is also to facilitate easy gamedesign, making it possible for talents to write a game and get it tested in no time. Nothing make the gamedesigners heart beat as fast as a real playtest of his own game! And thirdly: to make games in this format, allow you to make hilarious experiments with the form, and get away with it. ;-)

    \n "That thing which poetry possesses that is not form", I like that! You've certainly hit the nail on the head, Mendel. The intention is to create "the other experience", yes.

    I do not make a distinction between "drama" and "prose" in this respect, though. I use "drama" in the loose sense of "conflict narration". But this is besides the point, so please let us not make it a part of this debate.

    One of my games; Birmingham Stoke 1-1, is in my view the most successful of poetic roleplaying games so far. It succeeded in placing the players in a very special state of mind. It can best be described as "no energy, no clarity, no hope". After 15 minutes all players shared this state, and had internalized it, as part of their seemingly simple character (simple on the sheet, but surprisingly complex and powerful in real play).

    Yes, I have been involved in theatre-improvisations, but this is not inspired by that, however close it may seem to some of you. It is first and foremost a kind of roleplaying game, and inspired by the possibilities opening up in this form now. In my view dialogue based roleplaying games is growing up these days, developing into a real artform (and about time too).

    \n Hi Brennen! I have once participated in a workshop on "theatre-sport" (improvisation where the public gives theme and setting for scenes, and the actors compete against eachother, not sure about the English term), but I am not into impro-acting as such, so I do not feel qualified to pinpoint the differences.

    There is differences, though. I do not connect the two forms at all, not any more than I connect ordinary roleplaying with acting.

    I believe you ask me that question due to the method given in some roleplaying poems, where the lack of mechanism in the method may give raise to such sentiments. Methods without mechanics are a fairly common sight in Norwegian RPG-design these days, so it is quite natural that some Norwegian roleplaying poems share that trait.

    My first three poems were split in this respect. One of them had no mechanics, but a fairly stringent method. One of them had no mechanics, and a weak method (the game disintegrated, and did not keep to my goal for it). And one of them had very heavy mechnics ("heavy" for such a small game), letting the dice rule how the main conflict developed.

    In other words: the poetic-monster has changed shapes from the beginning ;-)
