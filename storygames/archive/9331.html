One Magic Thing

\n
\n
\n
\n\nJDCorley \n\n\n
\n
\n\nMay 2009 \n edited May 2009 in Play Advice
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n There's a lot of attention to magic and fantasy in RPGs, both in modern and historical and fantastical settings, but there's one thing that I haven't ever seen a spotlight on, and it's a real surprise because it really is pretty amazing, with roots stretching all the way back to the birth of the novel.

The world is as it is for the players, but there is One Magical Thing. Gary Hobson gets tomorrow's newspaper today. Jaye Tyler hears animal knick-knacks giving her advice. Ned the Piemaker can bring the dead back to life for one minute. Usher's mad crime of burying his sister alive literally tears his house in half. The Red Death manifests itself physically when the contagion can't enter the palace. Early "Dial H for Hero" was also like this, with only one superhero (albeit a magically empowered superhero who didn't know what powers he would have until the right moment) in the whole world.

This has some connections to magical realism, but in much of that genre, magic suffuses the real world thoroughly. (It's not just that Remedios ascends into the sky while folding laundry, Melquiades dies in Singapore but is too lonely while dead, so he comes back to his family, Pilar lives to be 145, and so on.)

Some challenges I see for gaming are:

- Often the magical effect revolves around one person. Spotlight time is difficult to apportion. Close ties must be bought into and worked, sometimes past the point of plausibility ("You gave the paper to Chuck again?!")

- The creation of new material around the magical effect is difficult to sustain over even a modest campaign length. In particular, much of the fun of the technique is following the ever-widening effects of just a tiny touch of magic, which requires a high degree of planning and investment. ("Lick the light switch!")

Some good things I see for gaming include:

- In the "extended" versions of this, such as comics and TV, the characters often screw around with the magic effect in the same ways gamers always do - Jaye tries not to listen to the animals, Gary tries to throw the paper away, changes things in the paper, tries to play the stock market with it, and so on. This actually adds to the fun, and shows depth and curiosity on the part of the characters. Curiosity and exploration are a big part of these subgenres, even the horror ones don't come to any kind of conclusion until the character understands the drive behind the horror. Samara haunts you because her mother drowned her in a well - OH NO WAIT her mother drowned her in a well for haunting people and killing them with her mind.

Any thoughts on this?

    \n I think it is important that OMTs are something normal (e.g., a newspaper, a notebook, a type of figurine) that fail to act in a conceivably normal manner. A faster-than-light space ship isn't an OMT because faster-than-light travel is conceivably normal behavior for a space ship. An alien device which grants superpowers isn't an OMT because it isn't normal in the first place. A sneaker that grants the ability to run to an alien world might be an OMT, but only if the depiction of alien worlds doesn't change the domain for normality, and it very likely would. A sneaker that lets the crippled run like a trained athlete is on much firmer ground as an OMT.

    I don't know why it is important, though. Preference? Tradition? Purpose?

    \n It might be a question of scale at that point: when the one magic thing is something that masquerades as a normal object, it has profound effects on the person who uses it but can go unnoticed by society at large.

    If the magic thing was bigger, weirder, or otherwise more noticeable, then you might start wondering why the world isn't profoundly different as a result: the more people who know that, yes, you can tell the future or that fate intervenes directly in our lives or that the dead can be restored to life, the less likely it is that the world will remain "normal."

    I think it's interesting to note that in most of the one-magic-thing setups, the people who have access to the magic thing tend to keep it a secret, because if they told anyone they would either be considered crazy (Gary Hobson, Jaye Tyler) or would be a freak (Ned the piemaker, Ralph from "The Greatest American Hero"). They don't understand the magic thing fully, and they usually have no resources beyond direct experimentation to work out the rules that apply to it. Most of them ask the question "Why ME?" and are torn between a sense of responsibility (to use the magic thing wisely and well) and a desire to be normal. The one-magic-thing story is a personal story, dealing with desires and obstacles on a personal level: if you have the one magic thing, you don't topple world governments or reshape societies.

    \n Your OP made me immediately think of the children's fantasy novels by E. Nesbit and Edward Eager, which generally involve a small group of children discovering One Magical Thing and getting into all kinds of scrapes as they try to figure out how it works. Five Children and It is the defining classic of the genre IMHO, and involves a bunch of bored English kids in an Edwardian manor who find a weird creature by the seashore that grants them wishes. It was made into a pretty good movie with Eddie Izzard as the voice of It. Yes, it's the same setup as Narnia, but unlike Narnia and other British kids' fantasy it keeps the action firmly set in the mundane world, except for the effects of the magic.

    I think the key elements of the genre are:
    1) The One Magical Thing, which is explored but never explained,
    2) The realistic world surrounding it,
    3) Don't tell the grownups!

    \n More ideas:
    - thing in pieces, everyone in the group has a piece
    - thing is in the hands of an NPC, PCs are out to get it
    - thing is in the hands of a PC, PCs are out to get each other
    - the thing is not a possession, such as a place

    I think that it is important to understand the "one magical thing" is an antagonist of sorts in that it presents problems that force those around it to change. It does this by not fitting in, often by pushing a power balance beyond the "realistic" limits of the setting (e.g. a giant robot suit/mecha making a kid capable of mass destruction, forcing the character to change from innocent to warrior-god). The change in the characters may end up being more interesting than the specifics of what they are capable of. John Wick has a fairly good rant on this.

As you suggest the exaggerated-ness of the power is important, but that is only because in our society there is almost no amount of individual, personal power that can give one true, unlimited agency. I think from this perspective -- which is definitely not the only angle on the concept -- what the One Magic Thing does is that it forces the character(s) to take on a level of agency that most people simply cannot handle. (The 'counter to reality' otherness is important because it means the power has not already been accounted for by the existing power structure -- it's not the same thing as, say, becoming President of the United States, because even though you are not currently President there is currently a United States, and all its power relations will remain in place no matter who takes over.)

    Now agency doesn't actually mean responsibility, it's just that that's where our society tells us to take it (with great power comes great responsibility? Um, not really, no.) The way the One Magic Thing forces change is simply by giving the protagonists enough power that their actions have real, obvious consequences. This is already true of everyone of course but our society has been built in part to allow us to hide this fact from ourselves.

    Being able to know the future or kill anyone by looking at them funny reminds you that what you do actually matters, and for most people that requires a significant shift in perspective. I mean imagine, for example, that one day Superman got even more ridiculously powerful. It wouldn't matter. He wouldn't change at all, he'd just be Super-er-man. He'd be more effective, sure, but that's the thing -- he's already effective. Superman never gets to hide behind not being powerful enough -- all his most meaningful struggles are with figuring out what to do, not whether or not he can do it.

    (It may be useful to consider this same dynamic in relation to say, Dogs in the Vineyard.)

    Aren't there also cases where the OMT makes the consequences obvious bytaking awaypower from the protagonist? I can't actually think of any, but it seems like there must be. Or else there is a hole waiting to be filled.
    \nGroundhog Day works like this, robbing Bill Murray of agency to the point that nothing he does seems to matter. Even killing himself (repeatedly!) doesn't change anything about his situation. He can't affect the world at all in any fashion that lasts more than the day. Ultimately, this serves to highlight the consequences of his actions on himself, thus causing him to grow and change. By the end, he's going around doing nice things for people without any expectation at all that it makes a difference - every day, he saves the kid who falls out of the tree. Every morning, his action the previous day is rendered moot as far as the kid is concerned.
