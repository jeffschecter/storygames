\n Due to advanced pdf technology and the kind heart of some geek in the print-chain at Wizards of the Coast who probably doesn't have his job anymore, I ran 4e off of the 3 core books last nite.

I need more involvement in the game before a final opinion (hence the "part 1" above) but I do have some comments about it.

First off, let it be known I haven't run anything called Dungeons & Dragons (Advanced or otherwise) in over 25 years. I played it as a kid for a while and then turned my back on it. I never found it all that fun and, importantly, it didn't make much sense to me. If I became interested in a D&D setting, I played it using another rules-set altogether. I played 3e a few years ago but the DM threw out the majority of the rules so I never got much experience with the actual system with him. I had other DMs eventually (damn, it's weird typing "DM" but it actually applies here) and, frankly, the rules-fest, rules-referencing feeling of full-bore 3/3.5e was fairly craptacular.

D&D 4e is very encounter based as a means of progressing characters and these encounters are entirely battle-map based. You will be fighting an uphill struggle if you don't use a battle-mat or some other grid map to show spacing. Seriously, it wil be very difficult to coherently describe the scene given the amount of information that needs to flow between participants.

All that tactical play, however, results from the large amount of powers and abilities that characters and their opponents can bring to bear on the battlefield. The rules are explained *very well* and the encounter mini-game replicates a fast-moving, board-game and minis experience. If you're looking for that, this game delivers. It's fun to play and fun to run; I wasn't house-ruling anything and playing with every rule presented and it still ran pretty quickly given our brief read-through of the rules and nascent experience with 4e. Play was dynamic with many options for both players and DMs to take; also, the old-fashioned "sit back and wait for your turn" style of play has been abolished; game play is very back and forth as you can interrupt other turns for a variety of reasons (powers, combat options, etc.)

I'm looking forward to improvising an encounter at our next 4e try-out to see how easy or how hard it is; improvising is my default style of play -- I don't have encounters planned usually -- but the clearly presented rules and the excellence of the core book Monster Manual write-ups would seem to lend itself to putting an encounter together on the fly.

I'm also looking forward to running a skill challenge which, as it reads, is part Duel of Wits from Burning Wheel, part Extended Contests from HeroQuest, part Bringing Down the Pain from Shadow of Yesterday. Skill challenges in combat are even trickier but any D&D rules system that let's you use bluff skill while fighting in order to get a combat advantage is a big plus in my opinion.

Reports that 4e is very close to being a story game are hogwash -- 4e's default premise is kill things and take their stuff and game mechanics in between encounters supports no particular style of game -- but I can see that 4e hasn't been designed in a vacuum. Then again, all that talk about 4e being a video game is nonsense as well. 4e characters are more kick-ass, their powers more dynamic and battles more fantasy-gonzo-exciting than 3e. As long as the folks involved with playing the game (either as PCs or as a DM) are imaginative and want to tell a participant-inclusive story, there's no inherent video game here.

Is it my favorite fantasy rpg system ever? Nah. Did I want it to be? I couldn't really care. I'm not trying to validate D&D in any way and I have no agenda to resurrect my childhood gaming experiences. I think a lot of people trying to find the ultimate system will judge it according to absolutes. I won't be giving up Heroquest or Fate 3 or Adventure, etc., to play 4e exclusively. If, however, you're looking for a game that has sandbox-style roleplay punctuated by a high-power tactical encounter mini-game, it seems as though this game will pop for you. If you're looking for In a Wicked Age with more strategy, move on. One thing is for certain -- if "fun" is defined by exciting and coherent game play, 4e is funner than any previous edition of that venerable beast called Dungeons & Dragons.

Part 2 next week.

    Okay, I just reread that. I'm just not seeing it.

    4e delivers a core D&D experience without a lot of the bullshit that stunk up earlier editions. In other words, you can do 4e with almost no prep, without memorizing many huge tomes. I handed character sheets to a bunch of folks, some of whom hadn't played D&D since 1st edition, and they picked up the rules without a lot of hassle and played their characters effectively and had fun. That was much harder to do with 3e, and even harder in 2e.\n
    The focus that 4e has on encounters is even more significant than prior editions since so many powers are based upon that as are the accrual of action points. It's almost treated as another unit of time and certainly as a measure of pacing; rules support this.
    \nSure. But you said that a) "D&D 4e is very encounter based as a means of progressing characters."

    I don't think this is any different than previous editions as a means of progressing characters. To earn XP in any edition of D&D, you had to succeed in an encounter. Earlier editions had loosey-goosey rules that told the DM to give out very small XP rewards (100-200 XP) for good role-playing. I think 3e said you could earn XP for overcoming an obstacle without killing it, but it was still an encounter. 4e goes further than any prior edition in making the idea of quests that could have nothing to do with combat encounters part of the core advancement rules.

    The biggest confirmation of my initial assessments of D&D4E are the huge levels of hate it's getting on forums that have a preponderance of 3/3.5 edition players. They say things about it that they consider negative which I consider positive. D&D4E is going to be a system shock for a lot of people who have never reflected much on the whys and hows of their gaming. For example, people are shitting bricks over the minions rules. Many can't conceive of the idea that a given monster might not be important and that you may not want to spend a lot of time dealing with it in the game and that can have an impact on the mechanics related to that monsters (shock! gasp!). Instead they say it "makes no sense" and that "an ogre shouldn't be killed by stubbing it's toe" (minions have 1 HP regardless of what the monster is).

    The best move the D&D4E designers made was getting rid of the ideas that the rules provide an absolute frame of reference for the abilities, characteristics and capabilities of everything in the possible game world. Now if a given monster doesn't matter in an encounter with the player characters other than as a mook, it's a minion with 1 hp.

    I'm looking forward to many more "it makes no sense" comments from current 3.5 players who can't wrap their brains around mechanics designed to produce results rather than to represent characteristics. Every "it makes no sense" comment I read warms my heart that D&D is finally focusing on its strengths.

    \n Mind if we dump some general thoughts in here, too? (I didn't want to start a new thread).

    I just unraveled one of the reasons I dug the new D&D:

    I love cinematic combat. In games where combat is abstract, this gives me the freedom to narrate some crazy-ass Blade II style whoopass. Feng Shui does this well, but I apply it to a lot of my games. If I ran a game of Wuthering Heights, Heathcliff would have been raised by Shaolin monks, and Catherine would have known Dim Mak.

    So, in games with no real tabletop frame or maps needed, I can generally get by. However, with some 3E campaigns I was running or played in, it didn't feel nearly as awesome as more cinematic games (and most likely, this recursively goes back to 2e as well). It came down to the fact that it did not matter if I described my actions awesomely, or just said "I swing", the tactics in the game came down to little more than putting my fighter right in front of another dude, and swinging over and over until they fell down.

    Now, there's so many more options for individual characters, options available to parties (people setting each other up for tactical advantages), and maneuvers on the board that are available (because of the action-move-instant action system, shifting, etc), that *I don't care about the cinematic as much*.

    I'll have to think about this more after 1-2 more sessions, but I think the addition of rules-based stuff keeps my tactical mind so engaged, that I don't need to have backflips and kung fu all the time anymore.

    Anyway, just a thought.

    \nI play more miniatures games than RPGs by a long shot. And done my share of playtesting of both miniatures games and hex based historical games. Including games ranging from a hard core historical conflict simulation grind to very results oriented designs (in the same way we might talk about various modes of play in RPGs). Ofcourse system matters when it comes to miniatures. But a lot of the points that you brought up are about tolerances for deviation from perfect representation. It's a common thing in miniatures gaming. Some gamers always insist that everything be painted and that every single piece of gear a given character might have should be perfectly represented on the miniature. Others are fine with an unpainted guy with a sword and shield even if their character wields an axe. And then you have people who actually prefer an incorrect representation.

    These issues have been discussed to death in miniature gaming circles (do a search on theminiaturespage.com's message boards for some of these topics) and unlike the discussions about RPG mechanics mattering and the types of decision making they produce, a lot of these issues don't produce different results as far as game play is concerned. You can make arguments about aesthetics and practicality, but you'll get the whole spectrum. Some people have no problem setting up a 20 foot by 8 foot table every few weeks and playing a monster game. Others play in smaller scales on 2x2 boards. Other people take the scale of a given rules set and reduce the scale by 65%, use 10 mm tall miniatures instead of 28mm and then convert inches into centimetres so they can fit a huge game into a small space. Nothing about this changes the actual game play. Nothing about it changes the decision making process in game play. The vast majority of miniature wargames assume that people are playing on surfaces larger than their kitchen table.

    As to who provides the miniatures, once again you have a huge spectrum. If I'm trying to convince people in my gaming group to get into a new game, I'll often paint up and provide two small forces so I can get them playing without requiring them to do work first. Other times I play in games someone else provides all the miniatures for. One of the local stores has been tossing any common and uncommon that doesn't sell over a certain amount of time in a box that can be used for store sponsored gaming events. Putting something like "the GM is responsible for providing all the miniatures for the monsters" in the rules would be daft. It would be the same as other games insisting the GM brings the dice and not the players.

    Sorry if you didn't like my earlier response or this one, but your questions seemed to be coming from a place of little or no experience with miniature wargaming. I hope this expanded answer is more satisfying and shows that I didn't mean "matter of individual gaming group dynamic" in the same way as opponents of system matters who say that game experience depends entirely on the GM/Players and not the rules.

For example, a rules set using miniatures might say you have to use only the miniatures they produce, they have to painted with paints that company makes and the GM has to provide them for all players. But that becomes functionally meaningless as soon as it hits the players. People are not going to go "oh, looks like we can't use my miniatures, Joe, you have to buy them. Or I guess I have to run the game. Oh wait, I used artist ink in a wash on this one, not their branded ink. I guess I can't run it either." Even GW can only rule out other maker's miniatures in their own stores and sanctioned events.

    I just think there are areas a given rules set might try to give rules for that it has no business doing. I think giving advice is as close as it can come while maintaining relevance.
