\n I'm putting together a hack of Apocalypse World for my In Nomine group. I've been rethinking what the basic moves should be based on how (my) In Nomine games seem to go, and I decided that we need clearer and more explicit rules making a distinction between tricking people and using leverage to make people do what you want. My experience with Apocalypse World and its various hacks is limited, though, so I was hoping some folks here might help me determine whether I'm "breaking" the rules or putting stuff together with obvious loopholes. Here's what I've got, anyway...\n
Convince Someone (new move)
When you you tell someone something they're inclined to be skeptical of, roll+smooth. On a 10+, they believe you. On a 7-9, they believe you, but the GM also picks 1 as appropriate.
\u2022\xa0They'll want some additional proof of your claim before they let you do anything that could get them in trouble.
\u2022\xa0They'll decide within a few minutes that they were right to be skeptical of you.

Convince a PC: On a hit, they still have the option to read a situation. If you rolled 10+, they get -2 to their roll. If you rolled 7-9, they can roll without penalty.

Coerce or Manipulate (blend of Go Aggro/Seduce & Manipulate)
When you use leverage to get someone to do what you want, the type of leverage determines your roll. If you're threatening with something they don't want, roll+hard. If you're offering something they do want, roll+smooth. On a hit, if they refuse to what you want, you can choose 1 of the options below. On a 7-9, they also have the option to make a counteroffer or demand additional positive incentive; if you refuse that, they have the option to accept or refuse your original offer, as above.
\u2022\xa0Gain +1 ongoing against them until you lose your leverage.
\u2022\xa0Deal them harm immediately (if following a threat of violence).

Coerce and Manipulate PCs: On a 10+, choose 2 options below. On a 7-9, choose 1.
\u2022 If they do it, mark an experience.
\u2022 If they refuse, deal harm immediately (if following a threat of violence).
\u2022\xa0If they refuse, they're acting under fire.
\nAdvice is appreciated. Thanks!

    \n Convince someone:
    I think I'd add
    "The first sign that things aren't how you've claimed, they become hostile."

    For PCs you need a different mechanic entirely. The game makes it impossible to force a PC to change their behaviour; you can only incentivise them to do so. Giving them a read person roll isn't good enough, because if they fail the roll then you've forced them to change their behaviour.

    Instead, I would go with the seduce/manipulate rules for PCs. They believe you and get XP or it's acting under fire (delete as appropriate). I would go further and say if you lie to a PC, regardless of if it was a strong ht, they gain +1 Hx with you if they ever find out. (Meaning it will be harder to lie to them in future, as they'll presumably opt to interfere.)

    Coerce/manipulate:
    The weak hit rule seems a bit, uh, weak. If the NPC can bargain but you can ignore it and get the benefit of a strong hit by doing so, why wouldn't you go for the strong hit?

    Out of curiosity, why brigade the two together at all? Why not just use the RAW?

    \n Yeah, I screwed up the Convince vs. PC and 7-9 Manipulate moves. Good suggestion about +1 Hx, too.

    If someone can convince me I ought to be using the original rules as written, it'd certainly make my life easier. Right now, the reason I'm trying to reorganize things is that I have a really hard time following how the fiction of the game fits with the names/concepts behind moves, and I know my players will have a hard time too. I'm also trying to avoid some of the most common confusion I see around Go Aggro/Seize by Force/Seduce & Manipulate I've seen around forums as I search around in an attempt to figure out how this stuff works. My problems in a nutshell:

    1. It confuses me whenever "threat" is used to describe Go Aggro, as I feel like that word implicitly includes the possibility of an empty threat. There is no such thing as an empty threat with this move. I coupled it with Manipulation partly because I wanted to give PCs the option to back out of delivering harm (as you could with a real threat), and partly because the 7-9 results on Go Aggro don't actually look like win conditions to me. Perhaps it does deserve to be its own move, but I'm not sure I understand it yet. From where I sit, Go Aggro feels like it should actually be called "Seize by Force": You want something from somebody, and they either give it to you or you (attempt to) injure them so you can get it. But then, we already have a move for Seize by Force...

    2. It strikes me as weird that NPCs can't turn down a deal at all on a 10+. Keep in mind that I'm working with a setting in which your enemies are often functionally immortal, and whatever you do to them is probably nothing compared to what they're bosses will do to them if they turn traitor. I don't see a demonic NPC being manipulated into giving you the info you want just because you rolled a 10+ on your empty threat or breakable promise with Seduce & Manipulate, so I thought the move might make more sense if they had the option to refuse at some penalty (the one thing I do like about a 10+ roll on Go Aggro).

    3. I also don't really get how "promises" are supposed to work with Seduce & Manipulate, but that makes it sound like this move is better suited to making deals than simply convincing somebody of your identity or motives. I thought it might be worth it to have a basic move associated with the Smooth stat that is basically just smooth-talking: convincing someone of your sincerity, whether honestly or not. The "promise" mechanic feels a little tacked on when you think about it in a scenario like "I'm pretending to be a cop to sneak onto a crime scene." Yeah, I'm sure you can figure out a way to work a promise into it if you stage a little mental rodeo around it, but why struggle to make the fiction fit the rules?

    All of that said, maybe I can just go with the RAW (either Apocalypse World's or Monsterhearts) for the most part if I rename and rephrase some stuff. I'm happy to be schooled on why I don't get stuff and how I might rethink it so it's easier for me to sell to my players.

    \n Well, the way it was explained to me, an empty threat would be a manipulate roll. If you succeed then they accept "I wont beat you up" as exchange for whatever. If you get a weak hit then you have to demonstrate that you are willing to beat them up (or perhaps somehow demonstrate that you won't beat them up after they've done what you want?).

    Go aggro is for when you're pretty much beating someone up and they have to do what you want to get you to stop.

    But anyway, I wouldn't use this system for a game in which your adversaries are serious important characters in their own right. Remember, AW explicitly tells you to look through crosshairs at your NPCs. That's probably why they are reasonably manipulable, because they just aren't the stars of the story. That's the same reason the PCs aren't manipulable in this way. That said, FWIW I wouldn't let an NPC be manipulated with just any promise, it has to be something they want that you could plausibly give them. You can't just say "I'll give you this orange in exchange for a lifetime's service", or "I'll give you all the gold in fort knox in exchange for..."

    \n Why would you use the Apocalypse World rules for a totally different game?

    AW is not a game where people have something bigger back home they care about, it's a game where people are led around by their body parts and don't know where the next meal is coming from. It's not a game where someone's identity or motives matter too terribly much, it's a game where you have to get clean water _now_, and you'll help whoever can give it to you. If your game is not like that, of course you'll have to change it.

    Why would you use the Apocalypse World rules for a totally different game?
    \nBecause I've seen these rules applied effectively to other totally different games (see: Monsterhearts, Dungeon World). The core mechanic is excellent. You do have to rewrite the moves (and possibly the Principles), however, to make it fit with other genres and settings. (And I appreciated the advice from Vincent specifically recommending that Go Aggro, Seize By Force, and Seduce/Manipulate should all be redesigned for all hacks.)

    Along those lines, Apocalypse World has different Principles from some of its hacks. I appreciate how "look through the crosshairs" works in that game, and also how it's adapted effectively for Monsterhearts' "treat your NPCs like stolen cars." I haven't decided what that Principle will be like for this hack, but I'm generally comfortable with the idea that NPCs are expendable, ephemeral playthings. What I do have a problem with, though, is when my players get lazy, and that sometimes necessitates NPCs who won't crumble with a single, poorly-thought-out Manipulation move. And, hell, I feel like that even goes for Apocalypse World. Every once in a while you're going to come up against someone who's a little too smart to fall for the bullshit line you use to get an extra spoonful of soup from the homely cook at lunchtime, you know?

    \n Maybe angels and demons don't threaten each other, what would be the point? Maybe instead they foretell what is to come (which, to mortals, might sound like veiled or not-so-veiled threats).

    When you tell someone what will come of their action, roll+Conviction. On a 10+, there is no doubting that what you say is true. On a 7-9, what you say is generally true but choose 1:
    -it does not change things
    -it is not the whole of things
    -it is not unavoidable

    So, when you say, "If you harm this mortal, here in the lord's house, then the fires of heaven will rain down upon you," it's not going agro because it's not a threat, it's foretelling an eventuality. You roll. On a 10+, it'll happen just as you said. On a 7-9, it's a "Yes, but" where you speak truly but the details are moot, they point out the other consequences you have left out, or they reveal a loophole by which they can avoid what you have foretold.

    \n I am leaning toward using something like this move instead of the Convince Someone move written above, adapted from Storming the Wizard's Tower:

    Convince Someone
    When you want someone to believe what you tell them, roll+smooth. On a 10+, hold 4; on a 7-9, hold 2.\xa0When hold runs out, they may not believe you anymore. Lose 1 hold anytime one of the following happens:
    \t\u2022\tYou do something threatening, bizarre, or otherwise straining credibility.
    \t\u2022\tYou ask them to do something they wouldn't normally do.
    \t\u2022\tThey do something for you that they wouldn't normally do.
    \t\u2022\tTime passes.

    As Vincent noted when he suggested this, this move wouldn't really work out in Apocalypse World, where even the NPCs are going to be somewhat paranoid and hostile. I am excited to try it for my group, though, as it's really versatile and adapts to smarter NPCs more easily. The dopey guard who just wants a credible explanation will let them by for 0-1 hold, but the alert guard who actually asks hard questions and demands ID will burn through that hold really fast.

    As for Apocalypse World not accounting for competence of NPCs: Remember that you can always decide that a specific NPC has a move like, "She's pretty tough to pull a fast one over. Roll at -2 when trying to Seduce and Manipulate her."

    \n If you're hacking In Nomine and creating completely new moves that have no real relation to how In Nomine already works, that's going to be a HUGE project. Honestly, since your local group is already familiar with In Nomine, I would let those apples fall far, far closer to the tree.

    For example, keep the same attributes and forces! In Nomine is already a 2d6-based system, so you don't have to change much. Just let characters have +1 in one of their two linked attributes for every force they have in that sphere. So if I have 3 Celestial Forces, then I can split +3 between Will and Perception. If I have 6 Celestial Forces (+6), both my stats are at +3 (since +3 is nearly always the max in AW hacks), which basically matches how it already works in In Nomine.

    Then you just write some new moves for the existing attributes, like what Dungeon World does for the existing D&D stats.

    \n It's actually turning out to be pretty easy to turn the most used skills into basic moves because we really use only a tiny handful of the skills in the original rules. Fighting and weapon skills get bundled under one or more basic attack moves (and decoupling skills from stats that determine your durability eliminates the disproportionately pro-swordfighting mechanics of the original). Lying and Fast Talk are probably just getting bundled into that Convince Someone move above, though I may have an additional Strike a Deal move that more resembles the manipulation skills from other AW hacks. Tracking is pretty well covered by my modified approach to Read a Sitch, which is more like "investigate a scene." Plenty of the rest can just be grouped under Act Under Fire, if I feel like it, a general movement-related move for stuff like Move Silently, Climbing, Running, Swimming, Dodge, etc. The one skill my players actually take that might not make the cut is Lock Picking, as everyone who takes it ends up annoyed that they never get to use it as long as there's a Windie in the group.

    My plan right now is to start characters with their resonance move, their choir attunement move, and one other move from their Choir Playbook or Superior Playbook, which may be a song, attunement, skill, or Role. Roles are kind of weird in original In Nomine, as it's heavily implied that you really ought to have one (in case you need some cash or get pulled over for speeding..?), but they bog you down with a day job cost a disproportionate number of character points. My working plan is just to say that Roles require GM permission and allow you to collaborate with the GM to make a custom move. (Like, for the ever popular private investigator: "When you consult your sources....") You'd also be able to roll+decades spent in the Role in order to see if your Role masks disturbance you've caused through performing your Role (7-9, some of it, 10+ all of it), but I have literally never had a PC actually do this. My players take roles like "Financial Investigator" and "Ecological Consultant" instead of whatever combat-oriented stuff SJG might have had in mind (Cop! Pro wrestler! Bazooka tester!), so I just rule that they can shred paper and turn over soil without causing a disturbance.

    \n Well, it's up to the players and the GM. I mean, you can make anything, regardless of how mundane, a source of conflict and tension, or you could declare that anything, regardless of how "exciting", like nude bounty hunter or whatnot, is just a colorful character detail that you don't want to actually come up in play that much.

    Putting something as a gig implies that it will occasionally be a source of weal, and occasionally a source of woe, and a good percentage of that is going to be off-screen: you're more going to play the results of doing it than the doing itself. What it means to be a nude bounty hunter outside of the actual nude bounty hunting.
