\n 5e campaign. I'm playing a rogue. I hit XL:3 and had to pick a "rogueish archetype" \u2013 burglar thief, assassin, or arcane trickster. Oops. My character was definably not any of those things. My character's alignment is chaotic good. They have probably never stolen anything except as a prank; they had definitely never killed a human or demihuman until XL:4, and they didn't know any magic besides a racial cantrip.

"So why is this character a rogue?", I hear you say. Well, it's because the character is definably not a barbarian, bard, cleric, druid, fighter, monk, paladin, ranger, sorcerer, warlock, or wizard. Rogue was just the closest fit, mechanically.

I ended up picking the "arcane trickster" archetype since it only changed my character's skill set, and not their personality or backstory. Except now, of course, my rogue suddenly knows magic.

\u2026

Anyway, it was very jarring from a role-playing perspective. I don't want to make this a specific gripe about the rogueish archetype class feature in 5e, or about the classes in 5e, or even about D&D, since I think character advancement in a lot of systems has the potential to throw some wrenches in the works of role-playing.

I know there are systems that do this better (IMO this is one of the things that FATE gets right), but I'm more interested in the question of how you, personally, role-play character advancement in a system that forces your hand a bit (or a lot).

(edit: it's "thief", not "burglar")

    \n I've generally avoided games which force my hand *that much*.

    However, in your circumstances, I'd probably do one of three things:

    1. Look over the numbers for the "burglar" and the "assassin" and see if it's possible to interpret those numbers as fitting my character even though the label (e.g. "burglar") doesn't fit. Then it's just a matter of telling the other players, "Hey, I'm now a 'burglar', according to the rules, 'on paper'. But my character isn't one - he's not actually burglar at all. Cool? Please don't forget that."

    2. Try to enjoy the "discovery" process of what the rules are pushing on me. ("Hmmm, ok. Looks like I have to be a burglar/arcane/whatever now. What does that imply about my character?") Maybe it's a change of heart or it will lead me to roleplay my character differently. I'd try to fit it into his personality and work with it: maybe he's so angry at the Bad Guy in my campaign that he becomes obsessed with thoughts of murder. It is coming from a somewhat good place (the desire to stop the Bad Guy), but he goes too far and starts turning darker: he now thinks that killing humans is worthwhile, because it's preparing him to stop a Bigger Evil. He's twisted into something I didn't expect, and I see what mileage I can get out of that.

    3. Work with the other players and the DM to see if we can retcon or add something to the fiction to make it fit. ("Hey, how about your character's been trying to teach me some magic over the last few years, because she believed in me? It never worked... until suddenly, today, some magical power responded to the exercises! And we'll take it from there...")

    \n Why not create your own archetype? Doesn't seem that hard, and creatively speaking it seems like the superior option. This is also, of course, exactly how I deal with this issue myself in this type of game: when the game's supported mechanical options happen to lack the literary idea that we're actually playing, obviously we'll expand the mechanical chassis to cover the lack.

    Is your character a scout, fictionally speaking? I'm just guessing because that's often what the character background on a good rogue boils down to. Doesn't seem that difficult to eyeball what the archetype thing does in 5th edition (I don't even know, haven't read the class description for Rogue) and just slot in a new "Scout" archetype that instead of confusing your character concept actually expresses it.

    The other thing I like to do when appropriate is to be a hard-ass about the logic of power in the game. By "logic of power" I simply mean, why is it that your character can do these things he does? Maybe a Rogue can only be a powerful hero by being a criminal, maybe that's the source of his mojo so to speak. A character wanting to be a rogue without being a burglar is like a paladin who doesn't want to be honorable - they'll need to switch classes! From this perspective one could interpret the D&D tradition of not having a vanilla "Adventurer" class as a deliberate choice and statement about the kind of world and fiction the game has: if you want to be powerful, you either commit to the gods, become a bookworm, become a mercenary or become a criminal. It's a cynical world-view, and the early D&D certainly supports the idea that the game is set in a pretty cynical world.

    (For the record, my homebrew D&D specifically replaces Rogue/Thief with an "Adventurer" that conceptually also encompasses Indiana Jones exactly because it doesn't make a lot of sense for most campaigns to explicitly not support the concept of a goody-two-shoes heroic swashbuckler who isn't a soldier.)

    \n It is pretty retrograde by my standards, yeah, but not at all uncommon in the wild as you say Feels like, while something like that seems like a good idea in the short term to get a grasp on the complexities of the game, in the long term a game of D&D (regardless of the specific creative framework) will definitely be choked and lessened by a methodological reliance on the rules as written.

    (I know you didn't ask for advice on this, but I'll give it anyway: if the campaign seems to work well over the long term, I think it would be fruitful to encourage the group as a whole to take more responsibility for mechanical interpretation and development. The longer a game goes on, the more it can gain by taking personal responsibility for adapting the rules to the desired fiction.)

    In that kind of creative environment my own recommendation would be to simply not bring in concepts that aren't on the official list of playing pieces: make characters, places, monsters, treasures out of the building blocks that do have the official stamp of approval, and just outright put instinctive personal creativity out of your mind. I would feel constant counter-productive frustration if I were being blue-balled constantly by having good ideas and then checking the directory to see if they're currently supported by the game text; much easier to stop having those ideas and instead just go to the directory in the first place and pick and choose from the alternatives offered there.

    Thus, for instance in this case, I'd probably just pick the least appalling of the Rogue archetype choices, and play that to the hilt. Probably I'd develop a sordid tale about the pecuniary difficulties of my character, and how those led him to a sad career of robbery, murder, confidence games, street drugs and sodomy. If that's what the game supports, then by Jove my character shall be the best Rogue he can be!

    (Admittedly my playstyle in this context tends towards hopelessly satiric soon enough - it's some sort of pressure valve psychology, the only way I can deal with having to play a corporate cookie-cutter character balanced by official game design masters is by expressing the character in a manner right out of Discworld or something. Very difficult for me to stay serious, in other words, but at least I can just barely convince myself to stick to the RAW if it's so important to the group at this time.)

    \n Aesthetics, I imagine. I myself, for example, would find it cooler if my character was so skilled that he could do what is strictly-speaking impossible, instead of having a magic spell that allows him to cheat the laws of nature by speaking rote litanies or whatever it is that an arcane trickster does to perform his spells. The spell thing is a lazy explanation that doesn't really argue for my character being cool, you know? For a wizard it's different, he's so amazingly intelligent and driven that he's willing and able to seize divine secrets for himself and utter the blasphemies to twist nature - that's cool in its own way, like a fantasy version of a heroic scientist. The explicit flavour of the arcane trickster, on the other hand, seems to be that he's a dabbler in magic, and apparently doing magic is so trivial that he can get somewhere without any appreciable commitment to it. He's like the half-ass of heroic archetypes

    Of course, a simple solution would be to pretend that all of your magical confidence trickster's spells aren't actually that - use the mechanics, ignore the fluff. Your character's not casting Sleight of Hand, he's actually performing sleight of hand because he's so skillful. The main problem traditionally is that it becomes difficult to explain the arbitrary usage limit per day; perhaps the best approach to that is to pretend that doesn't exist either. In 4th edition D&D it was a sort of a narrative conceit that your character just never happened to have an opportunity to do his cool moves more than once per combat or day. Maybe you could do that move all day long in a practice field, but in real combat it depends on your enemy giving you just the right sort of an opening, and fiction-wise we're pretending that this is what's preventing you from using your special snake venom strike, instead of a spell-slot mechanic. So it's not your ability to perform the move that's limited to once per combat, it's the enemy's willingness to fall for that trick. This sort of mental acrobatic may not be ideal, but it may help some people swallow the somewhat artificial fantasy scheme of D&D.
