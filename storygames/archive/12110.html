\n Inspired by this thread where we got into a little bit of a debate over the existence of simulationism, a new thread. I moved this discussion over here to let that thread focus on its actual topic, GNS analysis. Having the premise of the discussion debated in the same thread isn't very conducive to clear thinking, I imagine.

So, continuing where I left of with the resident GNS sceptic, Jason:\n
Posted By: JDCorleyPosted By: Eero TuovinenMy sense from our interaction is that you, Jason, probably mostly play in a narrativist mode, so maybe you just have trouble believing that somebody could actually play that daredevil-cop thing without actually being interested in having the character face a genuine moral conundrum over what amounts to a character quirk in a certain sort of cop buddy movie.
\n(Well, I don't believe in GNS, so I don't really identify with any of the modes, I think it's all garbage.)

But again, you are drawing a distinction between two things that are exactly the same. There is no "genuine" moral conundrum in any play. That's why it's play! Nobody is really going to be shot and killed when a Dogs in the Vineyard PC guns someone down. It's a fantasy, it's imagination, it's what-if. We figure out what happens and are interested to see what happens next.

I think what you imagine is that the person who is primarily interested in replicating buddy-cop drama (or comedy) isn't interested in his character's decision as to (for example) cover up his partner's mistake to protect him from Internal Affairs versus ratting him out, because, hey, that happens in buddy-cop dramas all the time. But I think that turns things on its head. The person who is primarily interested in buddy-cop drama story gaming ishighlyinterested in things that happen in buddy-cop dramas all the time. They're highly engaged in those moments. They have to be, by virtue of their interest. If they aren't, if they really don't care what their character does in those moments, or what will happen to them as a result of their decision, they really aren't as interested in the material as we thought they were.

(We're discussing the buddy cop genre as an example of something you might draw from in highly dramatic gaming. Jason's essentially saying that there is no such thing as GNS mode, while I'm trying to explain to him how people can be interested in doing dramatic emulation of cop drama for different reasons.)

You know Jason, I think you convinced me; GNS is clearly simply confused terminology, better get rid of it.

Or, less ironically: I'm not actually just speculating here, I've played my fair share of simulationistic games, including highly stylized genre-sim. My own experience is that I'm interested in affirmation of the dramatic rules of the genre in question when I'm playing a game like this. That is, I'm interested by the cop getting chewed on by his Sergeant because I'm excited about my competence in knowing exactly what scene needs to follow from the one in which the cop caused a political scandal by breaking into the mayor's house and getting caught in his bedroom. I'm also interested in seeing the dramatic logic unfold: what will we think up in the dialogue to justify why my character is still on the force and on the trail of the drug lords even after his faux pas? All this works to reinforce my character's nature as a daredevil and rules-breaker who gets away with it glamorously, very unlike his partner the by-the-book cop. This interests me because I signed up to play the game because I wanted to wisecrack like Eddie Murphy and get away with it to begin with.

In contrast, a narrativist approach to similar content would be to put the elements celebrated by the sim approach under critical consideration: was it worth it for my character to break procedure if it means losing his job? What are the consequences for the police commissioner if he lets me get away with it? These sorts of questions do not occur in genre-faithful play because the genre has already presupposed them away: it'd be a rather strange and iconoclastic cop adventure comedy thingy where the daredevil archetype would be fucked with a baton like that in the first act of the movie. Now, it's notable that you can do narrativism-within-a-genre, but that sort of play tends to gloss over the scenes that don't have genuine stakes; if we know that our genre prevents really serious consequences to the daredevil cop, then we simply don't play the official reprimand scene, or only deal with it in passing towards whatever it is that we see as a genuine conflict in the genre.

I think that these are pretty different approaches, myself: one viewpoint is to reaffirm predetermined thematic value by acting out genre conventions, while the other is to determine the thematic message yourself by recognizing and resolving dramatic situations. One approach to character identity is to affirm the player's character vision, while the other is to allow the pressures of the story to shape that vision. I don't really see how these are the same thing aside from the fact that both deal with stories and story-creation.

    \n Eero's post really highlights to me the main problem with GNS theory: people keep coming up with examples of play to illustrate the various creative agendas, even though any experience with actually playing a RPG makes it completely obvious that GNS theory is at best a very poor lens to view real-life moments of play through. The people in those examples are...well, they aren't people, that's for damn sure. They're robots or aliens or some kind of hyper-specialized freaks, because real people could/would never adhere so faithfully to those entirely arbitrary little CA boxes.

    GNS is a theory in the glorious old-timey philosophical sense -- a clever little scheme which can exist only within the thinker's imagination, not a scientific theory that describes a real-world phenomenon and can be tested. It works and seems coherent enough in the big picture ("What do people want out of games, generally, and what can games do to provide those things to people?"), but falls apart in a really ugly way when you get down to detailed cases. The moment we cross the line from talking about GNS theory in a purely speculative, wide-angle way to talking about GNS theory as though it can tell us how two different people would play a buddy-cop game, we're pretty much screwed. GNS theory can't do that, it has nothing meaningful to say on a personal level like that. It cannot account for the complexities of actual human behavior except by pulling so far back that all those complexities can be safely lost in the distance and can therefore be treated as simple, uniform dots: this one is gamist, this one is narrativist, etc..

    \n I think the question "is this game or instance of play Nar or Gam or Sim?" is just a really limited question, and we who talk RPG on the internet go crazy trying to make it mean more than it does.

    "Hi guys, here's my Narrativist game!" provides only a teeny bit more orientation than "Hi guys, here's my tabletop roleplaying game!" The interesting parts will come further down into the details, where common-sense vocabulary often does just fine. If you can answer, "What's your game about and how does it achieve that?" it doesn't matter if you know GNS terms to classify it with.

    And as far as broad categories go, "Right to Dream" is amazingly broad. To someone who'se never played G or N, it's literally the whole of roleplaying. It's essentially "roleplaying that manages to be fun without a G or N to hold it together." So what does hold it together? A lot of the stuff that holds any social activity together. Listening to each other, trying to engage with each other, investing in the activity, etc. "Affirming the right to dream" and "constructive denial" and "aesthetic constraints" are just more specific ways of saying that.

    To say anything more interesting, we need to chop Ron's "Right to Dream" into smaller categories with newer and better names. His simulationism essay actually takes a stab at this, although his categories (High Concept, Purist for System, etc.) don't thrill me personally.

      \n The basic problem with the GNS theory, in my experience, is that it gets applied far too broadly. As soon as you start talking about gamist *players* or simulationist *games*, it's like trying to carve a roast turkey with a scalpel, and the results wind up being messy and unappetizing. And *none of them* are absolute.

      Gamism, simulationism, and dramatism are the three inputs into how the players in the metagame determine what happens in the story-world of the game. Pure gamism asks the question, "What would make this game most challenging and interesting for the players?" Pure simulationism asks the question, "What would logically happen in the world?" Pure dramatism asks the question, "What would make the best story?"

      So as an example, suppose I'm running a D&D game. The players swagger into a market on market day. I ask myself, who would likely be in the marketplace? That gives me the simulationist aspect. I ask myself, is there anything here relevant to the characters' histories and goals? That gives me the dramatist aspect. I ask myself, if they provoke a combat, what kind of challenge should there be? That gives me the gamist aspect. So if I decide that Sir Reharad, the sworn foe of the party's paladin, is trying to buy provisions for his next quest, and spots the party across the plaza, that's got elements of all three -- with the dramatist aspect probably foremost. If instead I decide that a band of thugs is going to overrun the market to steal money from the vendors, that's again got elements of all three, with the gamist aspect foremost. And if instead I decide that there's a fishmonger and a couple vegetable carts, and someone selling cloth and someone else selling rare gems, some of which might be plot hooks, that's once again got elements of all three, this time with the simulationist aspect foremost.

      So in that formulation -- which is not the same as the Forgeite formulation, but which I think is a good deal more useful if the goal is to talk about action-resolution and not to enforce an orthodox view of Good Roleplaying -- then yes, simulationism exists.

      \n A lot of people seem to be saying that the problem with "GNS" is that it doesn't appropriately describe moment-to-moment play. That's fine: that's what the Big Model's for. It has terminology and what-not for the little things that happen in play. Whether you agree with it or not, it's pretty clear on both of those things, I think. "Creative Agenda applies to the big picture; here are some terms for the smaller things."

      The thing with G, N, and S is that they are "the three currently recognized Creative Agendas". The model has plenty of room for others, and there's always talk of splitting Sim into various categories: clearly for someone like Tomas there is a world of difference between the heavy immersive thing and the group of gamers who get together to draw cards from a deck and roll dice and enjoy the resulting story that sounds very much like a Batman comic.

      That said, the way you can see Creative Agenda at work is not in the type of story being told or something similar, but in the real-life reactions of the players at the table. Do they support that thematic moment your character's involved in? Are they bored by it? Are they offended by the challenge of a certain mechanical interaction, because it threatens the role they perceive their character as filling out? Over time, these things can (but do not always) coalesce into a shared understanding of what a given game is "about".

      I'd say that those three categories (Right to Dream, Step on Up, and Story Now) are the most contentious things about the Big Model. I personally find it very fruitful to look at the Big Model thus:

      "Here's a bunch of terminology describing the various elements involved in a roleplaying game. Now, when a group of people sit down to play one with a common purpose in mind, as a creative exercise they all engage in together, they can be said to have a common "Creative Agenda". That's the thing they're all really grooving on together. There's a variety of these. The purpose of talking about Creative Agenda at all is to recognize that there are many different possible Creative Agendas, so we can better analyze problems in play and better design games to help a group of people get on the same page and enjoy what they're doing."

      If anything, the biggest gain the online roleplaying culture we're discussing has drawn from the Big Model is an understanding of the variety of playstyles. Once you recognize that the group over there is looking for something different from gaming than you are in your weekly game, you can open your mind to figuring out what it is and playing along, enjoying this new thing you hadn't tried before. That was a huge eye-opener for a lot of people, and I think quite an accomplishment. (There are, of course, many people out there who came to this understanding on their own--perhaps you are one of them, JDCorley--it's not some brilliant new invention, but a simple observation of what's going on. For the rest of the people who heard about it, though, it was a worthy new idea.)

      In any case, I think it helps to think of the Big Model in those terms: yeah, it's a model. It talks about Creative Agenda being important (this group of people playing this game, now, and all trying to get the same thing out of it, as a powerful unifying basis for play). But the three categories are very, very broad, and don't begin to describe every possible permutation. Ron Edwards himself often says things like, "we played this game as a light Narrativist exercise, with a big serving of tactical challenge". There's an infinite number of possible Creative Agendas out there; the only aim behind "G, N, and S" is to vaguely aim at recognizing three camps or varieties that many of them might fall into.

      But if you can't get behind those three, you can still get a lot out of the Big Model and the concept of Creative Agenda.

      It's much more useful and important to recognize the specific, real-life Creative Agenda in action in your current/favourite/most wonky roleplaying group, and how to support that, than to try to figure out which of those three broad categories it fits into.       \n Zac in Davis: the essence of the *problem* with GNS is that you treat it as a Creative Agenda. It's not. It's one of the considerations in action-resolution.

      When I say action-resolution: one of the characters in the game tries to do something. What is the outcome of that event? Does the character succeed? How do we decide?

      The simulationist input says "What is likely to happen in the world?" A hypothetical pure simulationist has a model that mimics the physics and psychology of the world, and determines that for inputs A, B, and C, you get a certain chance of outcome D, a certain chance of outcome E, and a certain chance of outcome F. The model might be explicit, or it might be based on gut feeling about what is likely or unlikely to happen; actual resolution might involve a GM rolling dice and consulting a table (possibly one made up on the fly), or the players reaching consensus about what is likely and reasonable to happen.

      The dramatist input says, "What is likely to make a good story?" A hypothetical pure dramatist has a model that considers the events in the game as part of a story arc, and considers things like rising action, conflict, escalating tension. Again, the model might be explicit, or it might be based on gut feeling; actual resolution might involve a die roll or some other randomizer, with bonuses or penalties to steer the story shape, or it might involve consensus, or some other mechanic.

      The gamist input says, "What is likely to make a tactical challenge for the players?" A hypothetical pure gamist has a model of the characters' capabilities, and matches the events in the world to the characters' abilities and the players' skills so that it makes for a good game. Again, specific mechanics might be objective or subjective.

      I explicitly reject the usefulness of the concept of Creative Agenda linked to this model. When you tie the three inputs into action-resolution to things outside action-resolution, you get heat rather than light, because once you do that, it obfuscates far, far more than it explains. In fact, I watched the rec.games.frp.advocacy newsgroup degenerate into a perpetual flamewar over willful misunderstandings of what was then called the Threefold. It *doesn't work* as a player-categorization tool, or as anything other than a model of how people decide what happens next. It *doesn't work* when you try to use it to explain what players want out of the game overall. It especially doesn't work when one of the three inputs is elevated over the other two -- yes, this is *Story* Games, but it's far more useful when it's treated in a value-neutral way.

      \n Actually, I think the issue you raise is core to the reasons for my question.

      Imagine three sets of observers:

      Set one is conditioned and trained to recognize play as either "blue", "red" or "green".

      Set two is conditioned and trained to recognize play as either "yellow", "purple" or "orange".

      Set three has no preconceived conditioning.

      They all observe the same gaming group.

      It seems to me extremely unlikely that anyone in set one would would describe what they saw as anything but either "blue", "red" or "green". That's one thing I think is interesting. Another is the question I actually asked: would all of them see the same color of play?

      Likewise, I suspect no one in set two would see anything other than "yellow", "purple" or "orange". And would all of them see the same color? And what would their reaction to the observations of set one be? And is it possible that what set two sees as "definitely yellow" strikes set one as "well, they were sort of trying for green play, but failed, making the whole thing incoherent"?

      As for set three, suppose all six of the colors were explained to them after they made their observations. Would their natural response be monochromatic? That is, would they say play was definitely one of the colors? Or would they say something like "sometimes it was green, sometimes it was purple?" If you forced them at gunpoint to pick one of the colors, would they all pick the same one?

      Also of interest to me: what color would the gaming group itself say it was playing? Would they all agree? If they don't, what does that mean about the opinions of the observers? And, if some observers think they "failed" at reaching a pure color, does the group itself perceive that failure? And if they do, how much do they care?

      In other words, I'm sort of wondering how much the notion of "if all you have is a hammer, everything looks like a nail" applies to "game theory" discussions.

      \n I think theory asserts that we can get some use out of looking at everything as nails, so yeah, let's wield this hammer.

      I think the problematic part is keeping track of what the "nail" perspective is good for, and what it's not good for.

      If you're not committed to wielding a hammer in the first place, you're probably not going to get mileage out of nail-vision. If the theory says "red or blue" and you say "reddish blue", you're not using the theory.

      Hopefully, your gamers are simultaneously red in one theoretical paradigm, yellow in another, and a rainbow in a third, and all three groups of observers can agree within their group and compare notes with the other groups amicably. "Looking at it as Red shows this!" "Ah, interesting! Well, looking at it as Yellow shows this!"

      If each group can't agree internally (red or blue? yellow or purple? rainbow or polka dots?), then either they're not skilled at using hammers, or the hammers they're using are shitty.

      It appears that GNS is either a shitty hammer or a hammer that takes a high level of extremely specialized skill to use.
