Posted On: GameChefTell us about your game.

Durance is a game about the inmates (on both sides of the law enforcement divide) in a penal colony on a distant planet. It's a meditation on power and how it is exercised in extreme circumstances. It is a dark, intense game.

That's not what it's really about, is it?

No, you got me. It's about the colonization of Australia. In fact, Port Jackson in 1788 is offered as an alternate setting in the book.
\nAs someone from Sydney (Port Jackson in 1788), with ancestors who were involved in the events...

...or is this the bit where those same people who made hysterical claims against me decide to back off because a "big name" indie game designer has decided to use a politically charged situation as a possible setting for their game?

    \n This is a little rambly. Sorry.

    I don't think there's anything wrong with using one's own culture or someone else's culture to explore social issues. Of course, the further you step from your own culture, the more careful you need to be to get it right, because getting wrong out of ignorance is ugly.

    I generally have no problem with exploring difficult things in gaming. I just dislike when it's done sloppily, carelessly, or with naive ignorance of what's going on below the surface.

    I think it'd be pretty sad if people stopped borrowing from real-world culture to enrich their games. Games would be poorer for it. Art always has something to say, and I think game design is art, and I think role-playing game play is a kind of art.

    To get the Godwinisms out of the way, let me go there. I don't have to share my heritage with Jews to be outraged by the events of Nazi Germany; at the same time, I can't understand those events the same way a Jew can. Maybe playing a carefully constructed game about it can help me gain some small amount of understanding. Playing a badly constructed game could give me the wrong idea, and people (especially Jews) would have every right to be mad about such a game.

    That said, I'm a middle-aged white American dude and I don't know much about the complications of cultural appropriation, and I'm eager to learn more about it.

    \nChanging the thread from Gaming to Forum Discussion.

    ---

    Status games suck.

    It also sucks when people use the tools of Safety, Inclusion, or Fighting Back to leverage power for bullshit status games with their peers. That doesn't happen here as frequently as other communities/sites that I've seen (including - actually almost exclusively- self-proclaimed "inclusive/safe" communities), but when I see it happen it ties my stomach in knots: Watching the destruction to fuel someone's personal power trip, powerless to call bullshit on it.

    So I try to ensure that sort of degeneration doesn't happen here. And that the tools and language of Safety and Inclusion are kept sharp, for proper use (ie Not Status Games, almost always by North Americans, it's part of our online culture). You can only hit the fire alarm falsely too many times before people give up on trying to see a real fire. But I can only go so far, only work so fast, as the issues at heart require sensitivity, especially when sorting out the real from the bullshit.

    Because there is a very real trend of cultural appropriation in media and gaming, it's good to be aware of it so that you can recognize clear examples, so that you can avoid it in your own work. If someone you respect calls it out, it's good to give them an earnest and empathetic listen, try to see what they are seeing. It's also good to be called on your potential transgressions, if you're unreflectedly perpetuating it without realizing it.

    But again, in a world of American Monoculturalism, The Internet, Peer Impression and Status Games, there is a real threat to turn calls of cultural appropriation into another ignored false fire alarm (pulled for peer recognition and kudos, pulled for status games here or elsewhere). It's a difficult thing, and in my opinion (therefore "opinion of this site") you should err on the side of empathy rather than the side of casual dismissal.
