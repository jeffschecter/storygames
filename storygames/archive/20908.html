\n As I get older I have less and less time to spend playing games and I'm more and more attracted to less complex games. I'm also starting to prefer games that are more collaborative than GM driven, and just less "traditional" in general. I want to play as many games as possible and that means that if a game is crunchy it better be worth taking the time to read and learn it's rules; my time (and my gaming club members' time) is limited. I have to get rid of some of my games to make room for others and I would like your help. I like how fictioneers.net deals with rating crunchiness. I know crunchiness is subjective and involves a lot of different factors and those factors could be debated forever, but fictioneers.net provides a good practical guide. So I'm hoping this post won't become an in depth discussion or debate on the nature of "crunchiness." Here's the fictioneer.net basic Crunch Rating breakdown:\n

Crunch Rating Definitions
DIFF LEVEL (1-5) \tCLASS \t\tEXAMPLE
1 - Super easy \t\tminigames \tDoctor Magnethands
2 - Focus & Imagination storygames \tDread
3 - Mildly crunchy \tmodern rpgs \tApocalypse World
4 - Moderately crunchy \ttrad rpgs \tD&D
5 - Highly crunchy \tcrunchy rpgs \tSpaceMaster
\nDon't get to hung up on the "class" categories to much...they are just loose groupings for the purpose of a general guide and there are many exceptions. Fictioneers also classifies whether a game is mostly a GM type of game or mostly a Collaborative Storytelling type of game. Perhaps a good practical guide, for the purpose of this post, would have a breakdown like this:
1 - Mostly GM
2 - Mixed
3 - Mostly Collaborative Storytelling

The problem is that fictioneers.net doesn't list all of the games that I'm considering getting rid of. I want to get rid of most of my games that rate a 4 or 5 on crunchiness scale above, unless I think that they are worth investing the time into. I also prefer games with more Collaborative Storytelling elements. Here is where all of you come in...I haven't read the games listed below so I'm a little unsure of which ones I should get rid of and which ones I should keep. (I tried to list the games that seemed like they might rate a 4 or 5 on the crunch scale). I'm hoping you will look at the list of games below and rate a couple of the ones you are familiar with in terms of the "Crunchiness" scale above and "GM/Mixed/Collaborative" scale above. A rating for D&D might look like this:

D&D - 4:1
Which is shorthand for:
Game - Crunch Rating 4 (Moderately Crunchy):Mostly GM

    \n Throwing my own opinions in here for some games not yet discussed:

    World of Dew - 4:2

    WoD (and Blood and Honor) have a simple chassis with a TON of additional complications, which results in it running a sort of paralell with D&D here - there's a single core rule and lot of add ons. However, the fundamental mechanic involves rolling for who has narrative control rather than rolling for success, which moves it pretty firmly into the "mixed" category for collaboration, possibly even edging towards collaboration.

    Ryuutama - 3:1.5 :P

    Ryuutama is pretty simple at its base level; There are four stats represented by dice sizes, and you roll them in pairs. There's a fair number of different cases where you roll specific things for specific tasks, but there are very few edge cases or additional rules. It's definitely lighter than D&D. In terms of collaboration, it is fundamentally a GM driven game, though it also includes a number of scenario creation sheets with the suggestion that the players be able to fill out some/most of them, which bumps it a bit more towards collaboration than D&D.

    The One Ring - 4:1

    The One Ring has some unusual stuff going on - enough that even though it's probably less complicated than D&D overall (At the very least, it has far less "list based" crunch), it often confuses people who are used to other systems, and the rules are a little bit oddly delivered, which means there may be some confusion. While the basic mechanic of "roll a d12 + d6s equal to your skill" is pretty straightforward, there are a lot of peripheral things like counting 6's on d6s, conditions that cause 1-3 rolls on the d6s to not count, the ability to spend Hope to add a stat to your roll (or a Favored stat rating if the skill is Favored) and so on that I feel this creeps up into a 4 for crunch. It also doesn't have any particular mechanics that I can recall for introducing player contributions to the game - making it a class 1 for collaboration.

    \n Yes, but the adventures in Sorcerer are also short, only 1-3 sessions each, which means that the player characters will be spending quite a bit of time in the game dealing with setting and situation choices that the players contributed; campaign play is essentially about jumping back and forth between "scenarios" and "downtime", with the players having a fully equal voice when it comes to setting up the new scenario - they decide via the relationship map and Trigger who their characters are, how they live, what they're doing as the scenario begins, why they're doing it, and more. Because the game features a very character-driven structure, no GM plot whatsoever and player-mandated starting situations, I categorized it as "mixed"; the GM is mostly limited to refereeing the rules, setting up some NPCs for the PCs to knock over, and bringing phantasmagoric color, just like other games with weak GM roles.

    (In fact, a comparison: Legends of Alyria has exactly the same type of GM role as Sorcerer does. The Mountain Witch is comparable as well, the only difference being that TMW doesn't front-load the player authority, but rather has the players choose their moment during play to reveal their character backstory.)

    I can see how one might categorize it the other way, but I think it's a bit misleading to categorize Call of Cthulhu (or D&D) and Sorcerer in the same way if the intent is to have a sense for how collaborative the overall gameplay is. The lack of a "Rule Zero" and the careful buy-in the game establishes at the start with the chargen rules and the relationship map and such makes it a very different game in practice, much more like the other games characterized as "mixed" on that list. Practically the only part of the Sorcerer fictional space that is not under significant player influence all the time is the supernatural; the demons and how magic works and all that wacky stuff is for the GM to figure out and present, but that's mostly a big fat exception in a game that otherwise invites the players to set up their own relationship maps and use conflict rules to get their way against all comers.

    \n Just a matter of taste. I don't know how to say this without offending people who like those games, but I've personally found them somewhat vapid - I have my difficulties enjoying mainstream games at the best of times, and those are pretty much quintessential '90s adventure games (thinking particularly of the two AEG titles). The settings of each are not to my tastes (reasons ranging from a dislike of lazy historical fantasy to dislike of mild high fantasy), and the gameplay is same old, same old. Of course there's a lot of effort put into them, and the art of 7th Sea and Legend of the Five Rings is colorful, and there are always likeable details in big games like this, but overall I'm left indifferent to them.

    I'm particularly diffident about 7th Sea because its brand of ersatz-European historical fantasy really grates on my nerves in practice, when I read the game text. The fact that I've been playing a lot of historical fantasy over the last few years doesn't help things - the more competence you have for playing a game set in historical Europe, the more pointless the parade of knock-off historical details in that game starts to feel. LotFR is of course the same, with the dubious virtue of being set in the Orient, meaning that it's less my history and more their history, making it less annoying for me personally, even as I know intellectually that it's just as bad (or good, depending on if the ersatz-approach to historical fantasy has utility for you).

    (To make it clear: I have nothing except subjective arguments against these games. Take a person with a different background, who reacts differently to their aesthetic propositions, and who doesn't have similar games already filling up their needs for mid-crunchy traditional adventure games, and they might be completely reasonable in loving those titles. I've never gotten to the stage of actually playing these, but a read of the game texts indicates entirely passable game design in technical terms, no worse than other games.)

    \n (I think that the way many people played early versions of D&D, in actual practuce, could qualify as a "2" - "focus & imagination/storygames", but the actual texts, as written, were pretty "crunchy". Don't forget the extensive combat tables - determining your roll under number based on your level and the opponent's HD - charts for polearms, counting up encumbrance to determine your movement rate, rules for tracking turns and light radius, duration of torches, overland travel in the wilderness, rules for getting lost, percentage modifiers to experience point gains, morale modifiers, weapon vs. armour modifiers... even the simplest D&D products tended to have all of the above and more. While many people ignored these in practice, that still makes Basic D&D, in my opinion, as crunchy as a "modern RPG" like Apocalypse World.)

    (Granted, "crunch" is very subjective. Still, I'd be surprised if most people found Apocalypse World's, say, experience rules more "crunchy" than Basic D&D's. In Apocalypse World, you mark experience when you roll a highlighted stat, and when you reach five you get an advance, which you pick on your sheet. In D&D, you are directed to add up and hand out XPs at various points in the adventure - defeating a monster, finding treasure, etc - all these must be tallied up - then dividing it between the party members, with half shares going to the hirelings. Each time, every player must apply his experience modifiers, such as adding +15% for a high stat, recalculate the total, and compare it to an individual score for his class, which is different for each character. If he levels up, there will be a number of steps to follow, which must be looked up on that class's progression in the book.)

    (I think even 70's Basic D&D's strict "by the book" play is pretty crunchy.)
