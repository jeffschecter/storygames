\n I know we're all busy talking about DD4E right now, but here's a thing...

DRYH, SotC, DitV, TSOY - There are a plethora of abbreviated game names in the nebulously defined indie/story/hippie-games arena. Usually the abbreviation is the last thing we arrive at in the creation process, but what if it was the first?

Rules:
1) Take the abbreviation from the previous thread post.
2) Turn it into a game title and tell us about the game
3) Make up a new abbreviation to pass to the next poster
4) Wash, rinse, repeat

I'm first, so I'll just make one up:

GRUBS

Grand Reified Universal Battle System

The end-all be-all of battle simulation games, GRUBS is guaranteed to provide player satisfaction regardless of the genre or setting...as long as your players like BATTLE!
Over 3000 optional rules modules (grublets), which can be mixed and matched to provide a maximized gaming experience.
Comes on DVD-ROM or in a complete set of 200 expandable binders complete with color plates and overlays.

    \n Fiction Knights of Lemuria - Before there was Thursday Next and the high-flying team of SpecOps literary enforcement agents parachuting into Hamlet to see why everything after Act 3 was in quatrains or to explore the mysterious lost Chapter 31 of The Grapes of Wrath, the dashing Knights of Lemuria were were responsible for literature's safety.

    Each player brings a library book to the game, and describes some problem occurring in it. They portray the Inner Circle of the Knights - whether it be during the 18th century when they were posh aristocratic young gents, or the 16th, when they were brave souls braving the new seas of novels as the rest of the world was also explored. They debate the potential impact of the literary dilemma on other books or the world, and select one as Top Priority.

    In the next session, they play the actual Knights and must solve the difficulties of the book (the game is GMed by the one whose book was selected) - and the connected difficulties in a rollicking swashbuckling ad ven chah. At the conclusion of the session, everyone produces a library book from their bag and there's a brief round as above to begin the whole thing again.

    \n to RAPE AND PILLAGE

    A game of time-travelling mayhem. Characters are a gang of Eternal Pillagers - creatures that travel through time looking only for bloody and horrible action. Every scenario is different; some take place during Viking raids, some during the crusades, some in the players' own home town in the near future.

    Before each play session, players go through certain rituals - beating their chests, punching each other in the arm, and yelling loudly - to get into the right state of mind. Rape and pillage resolution is based on yelling certain phrases loudly (hence the capitalized letters); however, a certain subtlety must be used, because sometimes the second loudest player gets the bounty (human or otherwise).

    \nMars Orbit: Red Blood in Danger
    \n
    \nThis child-of-the-80s-cold war rpg puts a thin veneer of science fiction over the US / Soviet conflict, pairing propaganda with near space action. This underground game circulated gamer circles in the US, and it was thought to be one of the first soviet rpgs ever. In 1997, it was revealed that the origins of the game were fake and that it in fact was a game written by an american game designer to train agents to recognize soviet propaganda items.
    \n
    \nSome people on the internet still claim that this is just a bad attempt of trying to cull one of the most successful non-us underground sf games in circulation. A reprint in 2004 was pulled from ware houses because of a Cease & Desist that was fielded by an attorney 'known' to be closely related to the CIA. Two weeks later, the production pdf started circulating on peer-to-peer networks. Several renowned game designers are cited stating MORBiD is the reason why science fiction RPGs never were commercially successful.
