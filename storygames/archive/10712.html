The state of webcomics and its similarity to indie games

\n
\n
\n
\n\nElizabeth \n\n\n
\n
\n\nOctober 2009 \n edited October 2009 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n So Jeph of Questionable Content just made a blog post on what he sees as being the shift in webcomics culture: what's changed, what's stayed the same. It makes me kind of curious what lessons from webcomics are lessons that can be applied (or are being applied!) to indie games.

Some of my favorite points:\n
\u2022 The idea of critical analysis of webcomics has largely died out. Sure, people still blog about webcomics and "review" them and stuff, but it's become a tiny, tiny niche sector. I think this is mainly because there's not a whole lot of point to reviewing something anybody can go look at for free and make up their own mind about! Is this a good thing? I have no idea.

\u2022 It's gotten easier and cheaper to start your own comic. Hosting is ridiculously cheap at the entry level these days. Site tools like WordPress and ComicPress and Blogger make putting together a basic website relatively simple and painless.

\u2022 Community is still important. Makin' friends with other creators, getting linked by other, more established comics, and not being a jerk on the internet are still the main ways new comics get that first leg up.

\u2022 There's still no 100% guaranteed path to success. People have been having the same cyclical conversation about "how do I make my comic more popular" on blogs and message boards for the past six years and nobody has yet found the BIG SECRET FORMULA FOR WEBCOMICS STARDOM. This is because there isn't one. It's a combination of talent, work ethic, dumb luck, and timing. And yet some people still spend more time talking about their webcomic (or being bitter about its lack of success) than they do trying to make it better.

\u2022 The sky is still the limit. We're nowhere near saturation as far as potential audience goes. Back in 2003 I thought there would NEVER EVER be another webcomic anywhere near as popular as Penny Arcade. Now we've got XKCD and Cyanide & Happiness who are either as big or bigger. Anybody who thinks we're all competing for some fixed amount of potential readers is completely mistaken. It's not a zero-sum game, which is great because it means there's plenty of room for everybody, new folks and old hands alike, to grow!
\nHe also attributes a big part of why people are making more money doing webcomics to Topatoco, which does distro and also I think handles printing for T-shirts and etc (though I'm not sure, I should find out though). Do you think something similar is feasible for indie games? There's IPR, but I mean a one-stop merch shop that also covers or at least does the go-between stuff for merch creation? What do you think the RPG equivalent of some of this stuff would be?

    \n On the T-Shirt front, a lot of PoD (as they relate to shirts) companies use heat transfers. This is fine for simpler things and text, but for anything more complex or with detail you'd be much better off with a silk screened shirt. Brunetto Shirts (http://www.brunettotshirts.com) is fantastic for this, as they don't charge a screen fee. A one color design on their cheapest T-shirt can be printed for$6/shirt at a small print run of 12 pieces. For $75 you can start a T-shirt business. Just putting that option out there. Almost ALL webcomic creators use them (they're the printer for Topatoco even) for their shirts.

    \n In gearing up for the launch of my own webcomic project I've been reading up a bit, and a friend pointed me to Webcomics.com, which is home to the Webcomics Weekly podcast where they talk about all different aspects of doing webcomics (as well as posting recordings of convention panels). I listened to their Comic-Con panel and "New Media" podcasts episodes.

    1. They're going crazy leveraging different kinds of social media to build (and better maintain) readership. Twitter, Facebook, SumbleUpon, Reddit, that kind of thing. Some of it is to give lazy readers reminders when new comics and such come online, and some of it is to put links to the comic in front of people who might otherwise have never heard of it.

    2. When the time comes to publish in print, a webcomic is in a sense worth more than a comic submitted to a publisher, because it's already had an "advertisement" going on for a year or more.

    3. Paid subscription models basically only work for porn.

    4. "Support" for a webcomic isn't just about money. In the long run people can potentially do more for a webcomic by giving it publicity than by sending the artist money or buying merch. There's a webcomic called nemu*nemu that actually has a "street team" offer where they have flyers you can print up, and if you send them a photo showing you've posted one up in public they'll mail you a little surprise.

    I think the whole thing about perceived limits on audiences is partly because people underestimate how many new niches might exist. The experience Jake and Ben and company had at Kumoricon running RPGs for anime fans, certainly suggests that anime fans are a prime example of a base where RPGs have a whole lot of potential. There are a lot of webcomics aimed at video gamers or sci-fi fans or whatever, but like RPGs, webcomics can be about pretty much anything, and potentially appeal to just about anyone.

    \n Neko: That's awesome that you're starting a webcomic. I do a webcomic too. In fact this Wednesday I hit my first year. If you've been going through the webcomics.com podcasts make sure you listen to the one with Scott Kurtz interviewing Merlin Mann. It's VERY good and inspirational. Also, VERY MUCH DITTO on your #3 point. Subscription models will NOT work for this sort of thing. You may make enough to pay your phone bill, but it will never be a main source of income.
    \n
    \nI'm a very big fan of the webcomics/free content business model, and would love to see it applied to more things. It's a LONG TERM business model though. Never forget that unless you love what you're doing, this isn't the model for you if you want to make money anytime soon. It can take years to build an audience large enough on this model to make any more than pizza money or your webhosting costs back.
    \n
    \nYou have to be approachable, personable, and LOVE that you have fans. You have to treat them as people, and not cashflow. This model almost cannot work without community and social interaction with your fans. Most of your money will be coming from merch/tie-in sales, and it's quite common that merch (especially T-shirts) has NOTHING to do with the free content you're distributing. It just has to be awesome/funny/clever/etc.

    \n I actually had the intention of producing supplements to my first game The Eighth Sea in a webcomic format.

    Every second month I'd write up a mini comic, maybe a dozen pages in length describing an adventure in an interesting time or place, this would then be accompanied by a few extra pages describing some key places and people in that location, some story hooks and maybe a new rule that tweaks the game to fit the flavour of this particular setting. The places described could be anywhere from Atlantis to the Malayan Straights, Viking fjords to Maori villages. Anywhere that there had been a seagoing culture could have been a prime candidate for one of these mini-supplements. I had thought of the option of selling them as PDFs, a dollar each or an ongoing annual subscription (with a minimum of six guaranteed supplements) for five dollars.

    Once I accumulated a dozen or so settings, I was going to compile a few together under a common theme ("Four Ports in the Carribean", "Resting Places of the Great Old Ones", "Places of Myth", etc.). These books were going to be printed POD style, with a few optional rules for campaign ideas and bonus material.

    I never got around to it though. I'm due for a revision rewrite of the game soon, so once the new version is released I might go back to that idea.

    There are plenty of cool ways that webcomics have tried to tap new technologies, and a few lessons that we could really pick up from them.
