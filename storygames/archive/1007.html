    I'm going to take a little stand for "exotic."

    1001 Nights is many things. It is an extraordinary game, and a brilliant piece of storytelling work. It is not, however, in any way an accurate rendition of medevial Arabian culture. It is not a game about that. It is a game about the world of the 1001 Nights, particularly as viewed by English-speakers through the victorian era translations. In that context, *exotic* is precisely the right word to use.

  • IceCreamEmperor
    July 2006
    I haven't been following the development of the game, so this is all very contextless aesthetic/design reaction:

    I find the arrangement of the text to be extremely hard to read, jumbled and bordering on incoherent -- in particular the way 'and / One / NightS' is arranged, which I feel makes it difficult to figure out what words are meant to follow each other. Luckily most people already know the entire phrase due to cultural awareness, but otherwise it seems quite possible to mistakenly read it as 'a thousand and nights one', or even 'a thousand nights and one.' Placing the small joining words to the left of the large important noun (rather than above, frex) seems like a bad idea to me, typographically. The natural hierarchy of words suggested by the grammar is supported by the size of the text -- Thousand and Nights are larger -- but is undermined (I feel) by the placement.

    The giant S on the end of Nights also, to me, looks terrible -- while it's possible it may look better than the small s, that only suggests to me that the whole thing needs more work, since the way it looks now just adds to the whole 'jumble of text' impression. I'd definitely recommend putting more work into the typography.

    Contrariwise, I like the image quite a bit. My only thought, based on no knowledge of the game but familiarity with the 1001 Nights, is that it would be cool to see more nesting in the image -- when I think of the 1001 Nights stories, I always remember most strongly the amazing number of levels and stories-within-stories that unfold during the telling. Doing something Escher-like with the architecture in the image might be cool -- though really it already suggests that to some degree, having arches-within-arches might further emphasize that element. Of course, if that element is not important in the actual game, this suggestion would make much less sense.

    And, even more random and dismissable: I would move the black line down a bit, it seems too thick at the top and extends the bottom element too far up into the sky. Rule of Thirds, I say!
