\n TV Action is a game design that I've been kicking around for a couple years now. It came out of a specific game I ran in late 06-early 07, Slayers East. This game was a revival of a much-loved game from five years prior, but the earlier version had seriously burned me out and I was reluctant to go back.

The trick of it was, the players in SE loved to dive down into their characters and not worry about anything but playing those characters in the moment. And that was great, except that we all wanted SE to emulate the form of a TV series. Turned out, surprise surprise, that emulating a tightly structured form like a TV series imposed some burdens on the game that didn't mesh well with how people were playing.

These rules set a balance point between these seemingly contrary needs. TV Action is an entirely traditional GM'd game overlaid with a jacket of metagame indie stuff. Players take turns to be the focus of episodes; when they're in focus, they get to do that immersive dive they love so much, and when they're not, they share GM duties and generally work on keeping the TV-episode structure going strong.

It worked well for us. We got some incredible stuff out of play, and this engine felt like it was contributing and definitely it wasn't inhibiting. At the Gametime_NZ livejournal I recorded the process of development that got the game this far and then the episode-by-episode rundown of the game in progress. The game website Slayers East has more info and context and some sweet group photos.

Anyway. Here it is, I hope some folk might like to take a look. Maybe its something of use or interest to the SG crew. I'm definitely interested in hearing any feedback or ideas - I'm not done with these rules, not by a long shot.

TV Action

    \n Hey Robert, thanks for the interest.

    To answer the easy question first: no, no-one other than me has ever run this. And relatedly, I don't have any pressing design questions apart from "does this work beyond our particular group?" These rules were a custom-made hack of game rules we knew into something that was particularly suited to the people we had, the way we played in that game, and the kinds of stories we wanted to produce. I do not know if it generalises to other people/contexts at all. I hope it does - it would be neat if these rules scratched a general itch - but whether it does is not for me to say.

    When you have an episode/session as Screen Presence 1 (outright stole the Screen Presence stuff from PTA), you're a minor character in terms of the story in the episode. You can still turn up a lot, but the story focus and interest is distinctly not on you. So first and foremost, your role is to be cool with that. In the TV structure there isn't space for everyone to be the focus - in SP1 episodes you sit back and take the pressure off.

    SP1 characters have only a few 'Saves', the resource that lets you adjust a dice outcome into something more favourable. High SP characters get more potential mechanical intervention; low SP characters get less, because the episode isn't about them.

    Beyond that, SP1 involvement as GM is framed as invitational, rather than obligatory. Some of our players were really into it, others weren't so into it, so each player could decide for themselves what involvement they wanted given the baseline of 'this episode isn't about you'.

    Invitations included:
    * to run NPCs as needed
    * to take direction on how to run these NPCs to make the episode go
    * to frame scenes - two scenes in the episode, of any description (including the ability to bring NPCs 'onstage')
    * to use all of the above elements to push towards the mini-climaxes that were act breaks, push the episode's theme, stop it getting extensively sidetracked, and generally make it feel as much like a TV episode as possible

    In practice, SP1 characters were almost always brought in on the basic plan for the episode, so they knew which direction I was trying to push and they could lend that sort of weight.

    (There's a whole other discussion about how we avoided predetermining the episode story, considering we had two or three people each ep pushing in a particular direction. Mostly it was because we tried to push for particular Bangs, while leaving open how the players would respond to each Bang.)

    That make some kind of sense?

    I suppose my main goal is to have this game serving others as lots of the games I\u2019ve seen serve me \u2013 as inspiration fodder.     Secondary goal is conditional on response. If there are people who are excited by the purpose of the design and would like to play, then great.     So, TV Action vs PTA.

    Now I love PTA, I love it hard, but it wasn't a match for this group on account of its denial of actor stance. You can do a lot of actor-stance stuff in PTA, but it keeps booting you out into author or (usually) director stance. We did discuss it, but pretty quickly it was obvious that it was wrong and we needed something that did most of its work in a traditional RPG mode, because that's where the group felt comfortable.

    TV Action says "Yeah, you can have your actor stance through all your big moments. You can't all have it every week - some weeks you got to help out - but the reward is when you get your moments, you won't get booted out of your zone."

    That's the big one. It might even be the only one. TV Action is, very deliberately, an attempt to make a game that can be played in a "traditional" way that is as informed by PTA as its possible to get.

    Hmm. I guess that sounds right as an answer.

    \n Using SP1 people as GMs would, I'm fairly confident, be as functional as what we had. In the specific game this system was dev'd for, we were playing a variation on "team of folk vs. big bad", so it made sense for there to be one person running the Big Bad consistently. That way, even though episode-to-episode the players exerted influence over the story, the overall season arc had a coherent flow and could deliver surprises etc.

    The original inspiration for this format actually came from a GMless approach to another game. I am a huge fan of Wraith but the intense personal focus of each character didn't seem to lend itself to normal group play - the characters had lots of reason to hang out alone doing their own thing, and not much reason to get together and spend time with others. Back in about '99 I was making notes on running Wraith so you'd have only one player each week - everyone else would be collaborating to run the game around that player, and stepping into play as their characters as and when they came onstage. So each week you'd get a session told from a different point of view, exploring the world around these loosely linked characters but keeping the focus on their individual journeys rather than imposing some forced rationale to have them team up all the time.

    I never ran that Wraith game, but I'm sure it would be functional. That idea of switching around the role of "player" was the spark for this system.

    \n Paul/J. Scott - thanks for that - the "take the lower" / "doubles = 0" model might well be a better choice.

    The 'beat the difference' system is from Ian Marsh's Time Lord (available free here).
    When we started our Slayers East revival game we discussed whether we should keep it or not. There was universal agreement that we wanted to keep the kinda goofy dice mechanic, so we did.

    My major innovation to the core mechanic is stalemates. In Marsh's original rules, if you didn't roll over the target number, you failed. That was often confusing in play and because the probability curve is steep, it can make small differences hard to overcome. I added a rule that if you equal but don't beat the target number, you end up in a stalemate, with no success but no cost of failure either. It worked out much better in play - way less confusion about what a dice result would mean, oddly enough. And the existence of a 'stalemate' outcome tied in well to how Saves seemed to want to work.

    I usually favour conflict systems that deliver stuff to the narrative/fiction/game every time - and a stalemate outcome seems like the opposite of this, making some dicefests end up with nothing changing at all. In play, however, stalemates came up just infrequently enough that they weren't frustrating, and often the context of a conflict would change during the stalemate so that it did feel as if the fiction was advancing effectively.

    Again, I have to emphasise that this was a hack on to an existing game, so some of the decisions (like the difference dice mechanic) might seem kinda eccentric!
