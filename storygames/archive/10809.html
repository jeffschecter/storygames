    \n There's plenty of reasons people don't finish games, though mostly, I think it comes down to time vs. content in most cases.

    There's a point when people are satisfied, and frankly, a lot of games depend on repetition to extend gameplay times. Do I want to spend another 20 hours collecting 200 coins/stars/dealybobs to open up another level? Maybe not. Is shooting 50 guys in a minute much different than shooting 30 guys in a minute? Maybe not. This is the kind of stuff that's easier to put in a game than either another unique level design, new gameplay mechanics, or extra story.

    RPGs have the benefit that you're not forced into pre-designing all of your content- you can alter it to better fit what everyone's into, and that's assuming you're not playing a game that has people doing that as part of play anyway. Typically, though, when people -do- lose interest, the campaign "falls apart" AKA people stop playing.

    \n Lots of video games (especially in the Action and Horror genres, less so for RPGs or open-world games) offer multiple difficulty modes. I mean, as developers, we want you to get to the end, because that's the only way you'll see all the cool stuff we spent money/time making. Those of you who wish you could make it to the end of games, but run up against the natural limits of your ability to wrangle a controller, do you make sure you're playing on the easiest difficulty level for games that offer the option?

    Adaptive difficulty is a cool feature, but it doesn't map smoothly to every game, and it's also a tricky thing to do, something that usually needs to be designed for early on in the project. It's not something you can, nor should, put in every game.

    Nintendo actually is working on games that will just play themselves for you, that you can choose to interact with when you like, but otherwise leave on autopilot. Maybe this will be the greatest thing ever, although it certainly doesn't map to my personal predilections. If it proves popular, this would essentially open up a market of gaming consumers quite different from anything that exists right now. Exactly what experience that group is looking for, and how best to cater to them would have yet to be determined.

    \n I'm a video game programmer and JD is absolutely right and represents one of my main criticisms of how the video game industry works.

    It comes down to this: We don't build games to last. I don't mean just in terms of repeat playability but I mean to be steady sellers. A game is considered "dead" from a business model perspective six months after it's release. We aren't thinking in terms of building games that will still be selling two years from now steadily.

    Part of that is the increased emphasis on graphics and the way that advances in technology push that such that games simply begin to *look* old. But that isn't the whole story. A lot of it has to do with just not thinking about game play and business practices in terms of producing a durable product that will still be fun to new people in a year after its release.

    I'd love to work for a studio focused on producing a steady of stream of smaller continuously selling games. Something that worked more like a board game company rather than the more Hollywood like multi-million dollar "project" based approach focused on flash-in-the-pan summer blockbuster mentality.

    \n It's hard and costly to set up and balance multiple difficulty levels, and creating more dynamic storylines with forked endings like Deus Ex or other alternative paths is rarely encouraged precisely because the majority of gamers don't finish SP campaigns. That was true when I was at Sierra ten years ago, and it's true today. Sierra called putting a lot of effort into a dynamic ending for a game "leaving money on the table." With many companies making longer games (say 40+ hours of gameplay), the ending is what gets short shrift, in part because...yeah, see above. And because it's often coming together toward the end of the dev cycle. There's a feedback loop in operation there. Less compelling ending...fewer compelled gamers.

    The drop-off in SP completion isn't happening because of too many games, though it may be impacted by the prevalence of huge timesuck MMORPGs like WOW, where the social dimension makes up the primary crack-addicting ingredient. I suspect the behavior has more to do with a fatigue level that creeps in after a certain amount of time on a game, maybe because many games, the longer you play them, distill down to a flatter experience. Put another way, once the novelty wears off, the game's charm wears off, so that increasing difficulty level transforms into a barrier rather than motivation. Some players, maybe the die-hards or players for whom that game's experience really resonates, may continue on regardless.

    Have there been any studies on why people quit games?
