    \n
    \nGM-less and solo play pretty much work the same way. You need a pre-populated dungeon (or create one on the fly), and the order of steps involved in crawling through the dungeon eliminates the need for a referee. It's a little like a board-game in that respect, but with more options. And rather than concern yourself with the intricacies of range to target and who is attacked, creatures act by a given attitude (likeliness to attack or run) and focus (attacks closest, farthest, weakest, etc.).

    \n Tunnels and Trolls was certainly an inspiration, as was Fighting Fantasy and even the Fantasy Trip. GM-less play is something that I've been working on for years. It was only recently that the last part of the puzzle hit me over the head. I was developing what will be the Advanced version of Ancient Odysseys (based on my genreDiversion 3E rules) and I starting thinking about the scaled down 1d6 lite-mechanics I had on the backburner for GD3E. They were simple and almost as flexible as the base 2d6 system (and quite similar to miniatures games like New World Disorder which don't need GMs). It struck me that these would work perfectly for GM-less play, because I removed most of the improv and GM-required stuff. Thus, by providing a methodical framework (sort of like a miniatures game order of play), the GM was no longer needed unless you need to tell a specific story rather than simply explore a dungeon.
    \n
    \nI'm also exploring the possibility of doing the same treatment to the second edition of Tombstones n' Tumbleweeds, a western miniatures game. It would essentially become a miniatures/roleplaying game depending on how you run it. Miniatures would be an integral part of the game, whereas Treasure Awaits does not use them unless you utilize the included Conflict Action Map game aid (for displaying character positions when in conflict).

    \n I should emphasize "compact boxed-set" (like the original Traveller game). The thing that really makes this possible for me is that I actually have my own POD setup. I've been making my own books to order for some time now and also experimenting without different sizes and ways of doing things. It can really be a pain though, because it is a lot of work. I wouldn't encourage it - between the amount of money you need to lay out, practice required to learn the subtleties of the equipment, and time it takes.
    \n
    \nRather than getting boxes printed (which is way expensive), I print my own labels for the front and back. I had a prototype slip-cover at one time, but it was too much work to make and it was easy to rip when taking it on and off. I also experimented with affixing the label to the entire outer box surface, but it bubbled over time.
    \n
    \nHere's some pics of it now (not final book covers): http://www.pigames.net/store/blog.php?entry=1494
    \n
    \nAnd of the original slip-cover: http://www.pigames.net/store/blog.php?entry=1436
    \n
    \nThe three books are printed on my color and BW Xerox machines and saddle-stitched (stapled) on my booklet-maker. They then get a single trim. The loose sheets are printed 2-up and cut in half.
    \n
    \nFinally, I add 2 dice from inventory and the boxed set is done.
    \n
    \nWhile I went with 5.5x8.5 digest for this game, I'll likely do full 8.5x11 for the Advanced version of Ancient Odysseys (and just as a softcover). It's a question of reducing the process to as few steps as possible. In the end, I can make them one set at a time to fill orders.

    \n Pre-order is going to last a few weeks. There's some final playtesting for the sample dungeon to be done, proofing, and layout tweaks.
    \n
    \nThere's actually a fair number of tactics involved. Each character must be positioned as either farthest or closest to the enemy (and traps), or sneak (another position) and get behind the enemy (another position). The usual ranged and close combat attacks are in there, but so is aiming, deferring actions, blocking, entrenching, evading, escaping, changing positions, spellcasting, countering spells, foraging, and looting. And you don't want to stick around too long, unless you desire a counter-attack.

    \n
    \n The solo and GM-less rules need dungeons or anything compartmentalized into chambers. A GM can run the game outside of dungeons.
    \n
    \nThe Conflict Action Map is all about adventurer placement: either farthest (ranged attacks), closest (close combat attacks), sneaking (avoiding attacks), behind enemy (sneak attacks). The difficulty of ranged attacks are based on the target's size, although distance can also be a factor when using optional movement rules.
