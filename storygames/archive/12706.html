\n Ok, so the deal is that I've taken an eight year hiatus from RPGs to explore board games, go to school, and start a family. Now I'm back! Oh! how things have changed since 2002! I really want to catch up on all the innovations in gaming, especially indie/story games. I'm not made of money (still a student), so I want to efficiently acquire and read the best of what I've missed.

Your mission, should you choose to accept it, is to come up with the best 9 games from 2002-2011 and list them in the order that they should be read to get the most out of them and cover the most gaming ground (that's right, its OK to include the best of coming soon).

Ready, set, go!

    2010: Apocalypse World, Freemarket, Blowback, etc.

    \n Lady Blackbird (because: keys, pre-generated characters & situation, beautiful free product)
    Primetime Adventures (because: "the pitch session", fanmail, card-based mechanics)
    My Life With Master (because: collaboratively-built antagonism, set scene types, sketch-like setting, unheroic main characters, hardcoded endgame)
    Shock: Social Science Fiction or Polaris (because: rotating GM, spotlight scenes)
    In A Wicked Age (because: clever die mechanics, oracular situations)
    Montsegur: 1244 (because: historical, pre-generated characters & situation, hardcoded endgame, structured freeform)
    Don't Rest Your Head (because: reading multiple things in a roll of the dice, explicit advice on responding to flags)
    Mouse Guard (because: a burning wheel game, beliefs)
    Apocalypse World (because: MC principles, sketch-like setting, picking from lists, exception-based design?)

    Things that almost made the cut:
    Spirit of the Century (because: a fate game, fudge dice)
    Some structured freeform, beyond Montsegur

    \ncarry is both of these things - it, in fact, is designed to play in only one session. It's about a squad of marines in Vietnam dealing with issues'n'such, which may or may not be something you want to explore. But there's that. Disclosure: I wrote it.

    1001 Nights is a wonderful, progressive, underappreciated game that is also no-prep, one-shot friendly and good for random assortments of people.

    Both Remember Tomorrow and 3:16 (from Gregor Hutton) are no-prep (though 3:16 might take about 10 minutes to come up with a planet), and can be played in one session (though I think 3:16 benefits from campaign play as well).

    The best no-prep one-shot game is InSpectres, tho.

    Like you, I got back into RPGs, after an extended absence, and I got into indie/story games several years ago. I mostly play with friends, and - to the extent that those friends are into traditional/classical games - I try to suggest other stuff that I like.

    \nSorcerer and all of its supplements (Sorcerer & Sword, Sorcerer's Soul and Sex and Sorcerery).
    This is the indie RPG that kicked my head in and demanded attention.

    Dust Devils
    Can we even get a hold of this anymore? Shit. This one's great. It was my first indie RPG that I played.

    Polaris
    It has great writing for the setting stuff and divvies up the GM's responsibilities in really neat ways. A GMless game that you won't notice is GMless.

    Dogs in the Vineyard
    Probably the best game text we've got (though Apocalypse World might beat it). The way it communicates setting and technique to make it go is clear and beautiful.

    The Shadow of Yesterday
    This is just a great fantasy RPG, the sex, drugs and rock and roll of fantasy worlds with a system that backs it right up.

    1001 Nights
    This is a game that I should play a whole lot more, it should just be on my shelf, ready to roll in case any gamers visit and want to get their game on really fast.

    Burning Wheel Revised - Monster Burner - Magic Burner - Adventure Burner
    Its dense and its too much game for someone with your schedule but this is my list, so here it is.

    My Life with Master
    The horror isn't in what your character finds but what your character does. Great stuff.

    Shock: Social Science Fiction
    Get together with two other friends and play a game in a few hours. Make up a world and burn it down. Great stuff.


    I think that is it. I was going to try to distance myself from the list but eff it, here's where my game table's been and back again.

    Hope this helps.

    Good luck getting back to the table!

    \n Now, as previously mentioned, I haven't played a lot of the "classics," or the indie bible of games that helps you understand theory, or any of that. I can't tell you which games are capital-I Important. I can tell you which games affected me, made me a better player/GM, and a more thoughtful person. I don't know if that's what you're looking for, but here goes.

    Dogs in the Vineyard: I have never played a Dog that I liked. I never "had fun." I was deeply satisfied and a little uneasy about my experience. I'd never played a game that hit me that way before in my life.

    Polaris: The ritual language and shared GM responsibility was a real revelation for me.

    Bliss Stage: By the author of Polaris, the mechanized relationships in this game changed the way I think about character interaction, and has informed everything else I have ever thought about game design.

    Annalise: This made me love a genre (vampire/horror) I was never really interested in. The distributed GM responsibility you get with Polaris, turned up to 11 with secrets and intrigue and one of the most clever dice mechanics ever thought of.

    Apocalypse World: I've been playing this game since late summer of 2008, in every incarnation, and I own two copies of it. (Or is it three?) This is, quite simply, the best game I've ever played in my entire life. It started out that way, in rough playtest, and then it got better.

    Mist-Robed Gate: The most visceral gaming experiences of my life have been MRG. And it emulates the genre (wuxia, house-of-flying-daggers-style blood opera) so, so beautifully. It's a delicate, nuanced, amazing game. I love love LOVE it.

    S/lay w/me: When I sat down to play this with Ron "Sorcerer" Edwards, I blushingly admitted I'd never played one of his games before, even though he's the Godfather of indie RPGs. Ron just beamed. "Good! This is the Ron Edwards game you should start with." I agree. It just.. It's hard to explain. It's all of the lessons of indie RPGs distilled down to its very essence. Learning S/lay felt less like learning a new game and more like remembering how to play a game I'd forgotten. It's a masterwork.

    The last two slots are left for games that I have yet to play, and therefore, have yet to blow my mind. But I know they're out there, somewhere, waiting.

    \n Story Games Boston is still meeting (at least as of two weeks ago) at Pandemonium on Wednesday nights.

    The group tends to be smallish, and generally what happens is someone or someones pitch a game on the mailing list and people decide based on that whether they want to be there or not.     It tends to pick up in the fall, because of college students returning and fewer clement weather distractions.

    \n Further update:
    Ok, so my quest to get back into RPGs is going really well. I'm still waiting on my book orders (.pdfs are nice, but I really need a print copy to pore over), they should be in this week.

    I've joined a PBEM of Apocalypse World and am absolutely loving it thus far - the world being created is way cool! AW has jumped to the top of my must purchase list and will get ordered about 10 minutes after I get my next paycheck.

    I've also joined a PBF of Agon over at RPG Geek. I'm enjoying it as well, though it is still in the character generation/prelude stage.

    Though I miss the traditional table-top style of play, I'm finding that there is a lot to be said for the more descriptive and deliberate nature of online play. In games where players have a lot of input on the game, having time to add more detail really makes for vivid settings. I also find that having it all written out makes it easier to look back and draw in past descriptions and facts, taunting each other with details that beg to be acted upon. Lastly, I love that I can fit two games into my busy schedule without bartering with the wife for enough time to meet up in person to play.
