    \n What Hans means is that there are certain game systems that are very low on the scale from toolbox to a complete experience. GURPS, Hero and some other games (say Solar System, for a more narrativist example) all assume that the group gains not only system mastery, but also an ability to adapt and apply the system's concepts to their own campaign setting in inventive ways. A game like that when properly learned and utilized maximally teaches you about half of everything you need to make your own rpg, it seems to me.

    \n When you begin play, agree to a seed content. This should consist of who everyone is and what they should do in play.Ex: One of us is a villain, who's job it is to cause trouble, and the rest are heroes who fix that trouble. Write this down on an index card and put it in the middle of the table.

    Everyone writes down a one-sentence character concept.Ex: I'm a veteran adventurer with nothing left to lose. Everyone writes their concept on an index card and puts it in front on them.

    Start with a simple focusing structure. Scene framing, perhaps. Write this down too.

    Everyone draws five index cards.

    Begin playing as a normal freeform. At any time, someone can write a new content, principle, or mechanic on an index card and put in the center (applying to everyone) or in front of yourself (applying only to your character/role). They can also play a card to "knock out" a rule already in play.

    Maybe there should be a turn structure? Dunno. Just a rough sketch.

    \n It's definitely possible to write a game that teaches about game design. There are many games that have "holes" in them where they ask someone to do a little bit of design work. Not just things like GURPS, but the "rulings not rules" part of old school games or the "custom moves" part of Apocalypse World. In a game that was focused on teaching, though, I think there would be a bit more structure to those sections, a method for generating new rules content rather than leaving it more open-ended.

    Seems like such a game would be better, though, if it focused on teaching a particular style or type of design work. Trying to leave it open ended, so it taught you how to design whatever game you wanted, would probably involve it being so vague that it's basically an outline of the process but doesn't really give you much specific help. Whereas, if it was going to teach you something specific -- like how to design a good D&D monster, or how to write a Fiasco playset, or how to set up a relationship map -- it could be much more educational.

    \n Then I would say it's impossible. There is soo totally different to design a game that is narrative compared to a strategic game. Or a game that simulates what happens to the PCs compared to an immersionistic system. I've designed all types in my examples but the last one.

    If you at least narrowed it down to a few things that the system could handle, it would be much easier. Like having a structure as follows (for a game that simulates what happens to the PCs):

    1. Write down a few design goals. What do you want your game to be about?
    2. Decide on a resolution for your system.
    3. When you add stuff to your system, you must include something that you have already written about your system.

    It's a poor example, but what I wanted to show is how you can introduce ideas through structures, like that it's good to have design goals and that you should always try to tie different subsystems together in some way.

    \n 100 Blank White Cards does come into mind.

    Still, I think we're attacking the problem from a wrong angle. Is the purpose of the game to have it's players create a game? If so, then nomic, 1000 cards and similar games already fill that niche, though i reckon there is still room for more.

    Then again, wouldn't it be more of a learning experience if the game would introduce several concepts which are crucial with game design. In the process of the game they would be demostrated to the players along with why are they important, what do they achieve, what happens if you neglect them etc.

    The former approach will produce a game. The latter would go closer to educating a designer, which I think is the goal stated in the OP. So the key to the game is what concepts is it to demonstrate and how to do it effectively.
