\n

\n
\n
\n
\n In GM-less collaborative story games, I'm liking random tables (or similar randomizers) more and more. Up to the point that I consider using them instead of established conflict resolution mechanics.
Example:
A PvP (player vs player) conflict arises. You ask the question: "How is this conflict resolved"?
1. i.e. random character table delivers "melancholic" and "travel"
2. i.e. Rory's Story Cubes give "rainbow" and "ladder"

1: they fight, one PC leaves with bitter resentment.
2: they overcome their problem and find peace.

Is this too far fetched / not enough crunchy for you guys? Or would you consider such a resolution mechanic in a story game?

    \n Okay, I'll be the odd man out and ask the obvious question:

    Where are my decisions actually making any difference in this architecture? Is the only choice that I have to reasonably make merely whether or not to invoke RNJesus? The outcome is truly random and unconstrained, meaning that there is no reasonable influence (aside from interpretation) that I can have mechanically on the result.

    I don't often resort to this, because my definition of "game" extends a long way but \u2013 since player decision makes absolutely no difference and since the player cannot actually influence the results in any way either through narrative positioning or meaningful interaction after the resolution, where's the game?

    This could just as easily be written as a "micro procedure" like thus:

    Set a kitchen timer for five minutes.

    Begin telling a story.

    When the timer chimes, pick up three story cubes, roll them, and integrate the results into your ongoing story.

    Reset the timer. Repeat.

    I'm not sure it brings anything. I'm not sure that it makes decision-making meaningful \u2013 or any more or less meaningful than it was before. It doesn't resolve anything, it doesn't create anything, and \u2013 if were being generous \u2013 all it does is provide a single source of random inspiration. That's not even a game element.

    As such, it's kind of silly to suggest that you could build an architecture for a game around this as the sole "resolution mechanic." For that, the mechanic would actually have to provide resolution. It pointedly does not.

    \n Finally looking at the OP first post, I believe the mechanic could work if it were more crunchy. Story cubes may not be enough. I'd say, make a list of things you would like to see in the game, put them in pieces of paper and when there's a need for solving a conflict, each player picks up a paper, one of them takes the role of the opposition and they decide who frames the scene and who narrates the resolution. The amount of letters in the word or the initial letter may have an influence on that, the rest of the players could decide on a different each time.

    The thing is that basically, this is a totally different game. Fictional positioning does nothing here, there's no competition between players for narrative agency, you don't "lose" if your character dies. It's more like playing with Legos instead of playing Chess. There can be a different sort of competition: staying with the Lego analogy you can play at building the strangest thing, the tallest one while keeping stability, etc. In narrative terms you can go Fiasco and try to put your character into the worst kind of trouble and forget about how it's going to get out; it doesn't matter, you're playing an actor in a movie who is trying his best for going for an oscar at whatever role he is playing. His character may be stupidly hilarious and die in the first scene, but if he can make that a part everyone will remember, that's the goal. He can even make everyone forget the main character who survives it all and gets the girl in the end.

    \nImprov is much about cherish spontaneity by saying the first thing that comes into mind. To avoid to censor yourself and let creativity flow by mixing what everyone says together. It's a nice thought, and it works really well, but if you play for a longer time with the same group, even if it's with different games, people will start making up similar things every time. You need the game to give input, which most collaborative storytelling games I played don't. (And that's a subject on its own.)

    That's why I, at the moment, am so in love with random tables, but tables that has the right kind of input that spawns creativity. Rory Cubes is an example of a good one, because they don't state what happens (like the end state in Fiasco) but asks instead how the images relates.

    \n Been a while, had some trouble with my account.

    Random tables are great to spark the creativity, especially if the game already has momentum. I consider such tables to introduce creative conflict, not resolve it. I am totally in love with tables too. Players can approach an entry in different ways, which creates shared creativity.

    I just published my storygame Alien\xf2r for real (will create thread momentarily). In it, players have to pick from a random table and create a condition to challenge the (rotating) storyteller. If the storyteller doesn't apply it, the conditions can be 'hit' by other players (everybody roleplays their character is is storyteller every once in a while).
    The extra crunch is there as well: including conditions from tables gives a player more leverage on the story later on. It's a bit more complex than it sounds, because the storyteller isn't just the person that holds the strings like the classical gm.

    I wish you'd all check it out and let me know what you think of the particular use of random tables. Is it efficient? You can preview for free on drivethrurpg.com or my deviantart. I'd love to hear your ideas.
