    Jason Morningstar's comment: "Once I had this happen to a lengthy paper in college, and I re-wrote it from memory. And it turned out better..."

    YES!

    I'm a reporter -- I write longish articles (1,500 - 5,000 words) for a living, averaging about 3,500 words a month, over 40,000 words a year.

    Every single article I do, I write twice.

    I do a rough draft -- often so rough it's barely legible and full of notes like "insert details here" and "find that thing that that guy said about the stuff."

    Then I print that out, mark it up, and start with an ENTIRELY NEW BLANK DOCUMENT.

    Maybe I'll use the exact wording of some small part of the first draft, but that's only if I decide, manually retyping every single character, that there's no way to improve on my first try (damned rare). Sometimes I'll be writing for paragraphs at a time without even looking at what I had in my rough draft. Usually I am looking closely at that rough draft as I write the new one, but every single sentence gets changed in the process.

    Every.

    Single.

    Sentence.

    Believe me: The result is a much, much better article, every time.

    Typewriters used to force this kind of discipline on you. Word processors, though I love them and have used them since junior high school, make it all too easy to move this paragraph there, cut that sentence, add this bit, swap this and that, without ever stepping back and thinking, "Does this still make sense as a single, coherent train of thought? Or have I stitched together a Frankenstein's Monster of ideas that no longer fit together?"

    Sometimes losing the original document is . Not great, probably, for game designs, news articles, or anything that involves crunchy structures balanced on precise mathematics or other data; but great for writing settings, fiction, op-ed pieces, or anything else where the details matter less than the flow of the whole.
