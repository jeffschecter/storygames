\n So this summer at the college where I work, I'm helping to run a four-day institute for high school teachers on European Art, Culture and History. The theme for one day of the institute is going to be "The Humanist Idea of the Well-Rounded Person." The central texts we'll be using will be Castiglione's The Book of the Courtier and the "What a noble mind is here o'erthrown" soliloquiy from Hamlet.

"I am sure," I told my colleagues (who are warily fascinated by my nerdy gaming obsession) "that I can find an indie RPG set in the High Middle Ages/Renaissance/Enlightenment with a chargen process that nicely illustrates some of the ideals of this brand of humanism. I will take up an hour of the day helping the teachers to make up characters, then run them through maybe half an hour of a sample scenario."

Sitting at my desk after the meeting it has occurred to me that I might have been bullshitting them just a tad.

So, um, help! Can anyone think of a game that might fit this bill, or one that could easily be adapted to do so?

    \n Personally I'd build something that does exactly what you need, rather than kitbashing another game. What are the parameters? How many students, and in what setting? What are your goals? How much time do you have and how will the materials be delivered?

    My impulse would be to premake all the characters to highlight various modalities, then present them with a situation they must address instantly. A trial, perhaps, so you can get lots of people involved in various ways. Put the Pope on trial. Put Humanism on trial.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarPersonally I'd build something that does exactly what you need, rather than kitbashing another game. What are the parameters? How many students, and in what setting? What are your goals? How much time do you have and how will the materials be delivered?
    \nMy aversion to doing this isn't so much that I don't think I couldn't make a reasonably decent job of it; it's more a matter of apearences. "Here's a scribbly little ruleset I just came up with" is much less likely to draw them in than "here's a shiny .pdf with real art, designed by a respected member of the indie gaming community (i.e. not really me) that others across the country have played and found enjoyable."

    Chargen is realy the whole point of the exercise, so it would have to be a system that constructs character more narratively than just via the generation of stats. There'll be somewhere between eight and fifteen teachers taking part, in a University lecture hall, for two hours max. If we do get in any gameplay, they'll probably have to be broken into groups.
