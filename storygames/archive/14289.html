    \n I agree with the commenters who are suggesting that maybe you two just don't have enough common ground to enjoy playing an RPG together (or at least not certain games).

    However, this being the internet, our prognosis based on a limited selection of things you've written about the situation... could easily be completely wrong.

    If you think we might be wrong, and decide to try playing AW with this guy, there's a great tool built-in to the game:

    Ask the players questions.

    When he says he wants to do some weird thing, ask him, "Why does [your character] want to do this? What does he hope to achieve?" (And make it clear that you're asking because you'd like to give it to him, not because you want to "block" his hopes. Answer positively, with encouragement: "Cool! I'd like to see that in action. Let's play it out and see what happens.")

    Ask also things like, "OK, so your character just does these weird things? So how do the other people in the holding see him? Do they think he's crazy, do they enjoy his antics, do they fear him?"

    Let him fill in the gaps you don't understand. When you don't understand something, just tell him, "OK, so how does [the hardholder] react to your shenanigans?" He might say, "Oh, he considers me his top agent provocateur, because he knows he can talk me easily into pulling some crazy stunts." And then you know what he wants from the game: have the hardholder instigate him into some oddball stunts.

    I think then it'll be clear pretty fast whether he's hoping for something different from the game, or if he's just not really interested in playing a "story game" about real characters, and needs to play some video games or board games or something else instead.

      Posted By: DeliveratorViletta, that's very nice, but I've read enough to be absolutely 100% certain that their goals really fundamentally are in conflict.

      This isn't about indie vs. trad, by the way. People like the dicking around guy are horrible to deal with in D&D games of every stripe, too, or Vampire or GURPS or what-have-you.

      It's like what jenskot said above\u2014the guy just doesn't care about fictional characters and worlds and is unable/unwilling to engage with the game world on that level.

      Matt
      \nMeanwhile, I've read enough to know that you can't be 100% certain that their goals really, fundamentally are in conflict because I've played with that sort of player before, and managed to make them an asset.

      You say the guy is unwilling or unable to engage the world? Let's go with unable; the guy's apparently new at this. Here's the thing. Humans learn. Especially when coming into something relatively fresh. This guy can learn to engage the world in a more appropriate manner, and I've seen it happen. Quickly, even.

      The key is to get him invested in the world and the NPCs. The key to getting him invested in NPCs is to know what he likes, what he's going for, and then give it to him through the NPCs. This guy likes silly zaniness? There are plenty of perfectly reasonable ways to bring that into an otherwise serious game. In my case, I loves me some crazy witches, so I tossed a crazy witch doing all sorts of ridiculousness into the mix and my goof absolutely loved her. Investment achieved. Cue threatening of the crazy witch, and engagement with the world is achieved. Huge step towards solving the problem, that.

      And this guy is definitely worth it. The whole, "Roleplaying is serious business," gig is all well and good, but it's always useful to have someone who will actually do something on their own accord if you don't want to have entire sessions with the party talking with the barkeep about family. "I shoot the cultist," is awesome. It's interesting, it's appropriate, it sets the tone, it complicates the situation, and solves the question of why the party's getting all wrapped up in this mess; they shot the ruddy cultist! You can't tell me a player with enough chutzpah to wipe out a city isn't a magnificent asset, once properly framed and channeled.

      \n Although I'm a fan of picking the right people for the right activity, I've had success working through these problems in game.
      \n
      \nI once had a player who wouldn't stop stealing spotlight. So I gave him an intelligent sword that he could talk to. So when it wasn't his turn, he would roleplay his character and the sword talking to each other. It kept him busy and engaged!
      \n
      \nI make rules lawyers in charge of the rules except for rules governing their own character's actions.
      \n
      \nIf the group has a mix of people who love and hate combat... I make everyone roll initiative... combat or not. Then everyone has 2 minutes to do anything. Roll attacks, explore, narrate flashbacks, have conversations. And I will design combats so that non-combat turns can influence combat indirectly. That way everyone does what they want while still working towards common goals.
      \n
      \nIf someone wants to power game, I have them focus their optimizing energy towards designing threats for the PCs!
      \n
      \nI've had players who just wanted to play assholes. So I asked them to play NPCs. I told them... go crazy... as long as you are ok with the PCs killing you. But if they do, you can just play a different NPC next time. Not everyone wants to play a protagonist.

      \n Wow, here's where I'm embarrassed for creating this thread. The first session was pretty damn good and the longstanding player who brought the dicking around player in was very serious and set a good example which lead the player this thread is about into doing the same. He didn't dick around at all, sure, he was quirky but I like his character and he came up with some cool shit (he isolated a piece of the maelstrom onto a cassette tape and carved it into a belt on the Chopper's bike - like etching a vinyl record - to experiment).

      He did withdraw a bit on some of the questions I asked and fell back on the note in the Savvyhead's playbook about taking -1HX with everyone because "you don't really have time to get to know them with all your other work going on." He used this as an excuse to defer questions about the other players, like, "If you could only save one of the characters, who would it be?" His answer was no one as he doesn't really know them very well. We'll crack that though as the other players were working to engage him as well. So, yeah, strange. I guess my email combined with other players setting a good example really pushed this guy not to act out. I hope it continues.
