\n Here is how it works:

1) Locate a gaming product you no longer need:

Standard version: find something on your gaming shelf you either regret buying or realize you will never actually use.

Hero version: go into your friendly local gaming store and buy something from their gaming shelf that you think they are never actually going to sell (hint: try the bargain shelf or the d20 section).

2) Get a post-it note, the larger and brighter the better, and write "FREE" on it in big, dark letters. Place this on the cover of the product.

3) Get another post-it note. Find a URL that points to your favorite game. Write this URL on the post-it as legibly as possible. Only write the URL, nothing else. Place this post-it somewhere inside the product.

4) Sometime on October 10, abandon the product in some sort of public place. Such as:

...a table of a coffee shop
...a seat on a subway or public bus
...an information kiosk
...the table of your local public library
...somewhere at Comic Con NYC
...a bench outside your local high school or university

    \n So, every day I walk from my house to downtown, I pass by about 5-10 free bins.
    85% of free stuff on the side of the road is trash. But in the past couple months, I've found a really cool suitcase and my girlfriend found some jeans and a pair of shoes.
    Clearly, that suitcase was something that someone else no longer wanted. Same with the shoes.
    We love them.

    I don't see how that's a bad idea.

    COUPLED WITH:

    As gaming experts (which we universally are on this board), we have a super critical eye. I can't play PDQ games anymore because I have all these fancy criticisms of the mechanics. I used to LOVE Dead Inside when I was first into gaming. I used to LOVE Dead Inside when loving things was easier, when I was bright eyed to it all.

    I would be very unsurprised to find that new people are able to love the things that I've moved on from.

    COUPLED WITH:

    There are reasons why this is a neat idea beyond outreach.
