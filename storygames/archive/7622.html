Story Games Name Project: Worth a Second Attempt?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nAndy \n\n\n
\n
\n\nSeptember 2008 \n edited September 2008 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n So, a couple years back a lot of us, together, crafted this thing called the Story Games Name Book: A lot of people grabbed a piece of history and culture, stepped in, and created 1d20 name lists to go with it. After some time, it was laid out into book form, put on lulu, and sold for "Cost, plus one dollar: That dollar which would go to a charity."

So, we learned a lot from the experience. I think Jason was mentioning that he picked "the wrong license" or something for Creative Commons: Essentially, the book (which is awesome BTW, I use it in almost every session, particularly my L5R campaign) is priced high enough that it simply can never appear in a retail store. And what's more, it's like 11 bucks. For personal use, that's totally fine. But I was feeling the sting when I was buying about 5 of them the other day to take to Japan as gifts to my RPG friends over there, thinking "damn, I could have printed this with GoB or another POD for like $5 a pop, tops".

I'm wondering if a second project might be in order? Here's what I'm thinking:
1) Figure out if the license from the original allows us to lift the content if the original authors give us permission (like me for the Egypt/Japan section, etc) and put it in another book.
2) Choose a license as free of constraints as possible. Something on the "CRN" side of the fence even. Like, "anyone may print or use this, for whatever projects they want to do, forever and ever".
3) Collect submissions to fill in the holes left by those entries we couldn't carry over from the original (or, going back to formula if the original license doesn't allow us to republish any of the material even with author permission).
4) Find someone to lay it out for a paltry fee, like $100.
5) I print it, something like 200 copies, using as cheap a printer as possible. The price of these copies will be "cost", plus "50 cents" (for the layout guy's fee), plus one more dollar (to go to some charity).
6) After that, the print proofs are posted wherever, and people are free to print it as they see fit: Throw it on Lulu, give it to their local POD, whatever.
7) If #5 is successful, I print 200 more, with the extra charity dollar, and so on.

This way, I'm thinking that "cost" might come down to something as low as like $5 per copy, depending on the printer. Possibly far less if we do a bulk printing (1000 books at two bucks a pop or something with a traditional printer).

...I guess this is just a public thought I was having the other day when I was picking up a couple of copies for friends, and thinking "dang, this could have been a lot cheaper", which Jason had also remarked on as well. Hindsight 50/50 and all that, but I'm wondering if it's time to think about a Second Edition at perhaps in the upcoming year...

-Andy

    \n Andy, the original creators always retain their basic copyright under any Creative Commons license; they've just set out provisions under which they share some of their exclusive rights (like, say, "if you attribute this to me you can redistribute it"). So given authorial permission, yes, all the lists can be reprinted.

    Actually, anybody who wanted could make a full or partial copy of the SGNP and distribute it however they liked--they just couldn't charge for it, and they'd have to release it under the same license, with full credit. According to my copy it's under Attribution-Noncommercial-Sharealike 2.5. If you want to make it close to public domain without actually surrendering copyright, you'd want to reduce it to just Attribution (and probably updated to 3.0).

    \n Yeah, some thoughts based on the above:

    Organization: Tag, I'm it. I'll get started later this month when I have less stuff on my plate (Tenra)

    Free version: Yes. As Jason mentioned, all the original stuff is available electronically as well. My intention is to have a print PDF (for the printer), and basically taking that very version and making it into a lighter screen/print version.

    Brendan, Jason: Thanks for clarification on the existing license. Jason, later on I'll need your help telling one CC license apart from the next. Perhaps releasing all material as straight public domain would be easiest to deal with. Something to think about.

    Layout: Josh, thanks man for volunteering to chip in. Actually, I have been meaning to learn some InDesign myself, so I might use this opportunity to take a crack at it (also see form factor, below). And yeah, I'm totally all about your idea on "text only". I might even require xls/Google Spreadsheet to boot.

    Profit: Thinking about how to handle this. I can envision a cut for a layout guy (if one is used), and a fulfillment guy (to the tune of about $1 per book shipped) for the first 100-200 copies or so. Then, after that, the print PDFs will be made available so that if someone wants to print their own copies, they can take them to Guild of Blades or Lulu or wherever and do them themselves, for whatever price they care to charge (so that someone could keep it in print, sell it through IPR or whatever). I figure spare change can be donated to some charity if it manifests.

    Art: Yeah, we'll probably lose the interior art, but I can look into that.

    Advertising: This is a cool cost-saving idea, but it can also become Just Too Much to deal with. I'll think about this.

    Previous contributors: When the time comes, we can contact all the previous contributors to see if they are interested in re-submitting.

    Form factor: Currently, I'm not too married to 6x9, simply because I think 8x11 might be more economic in the use of space. I'm also looking at more of a rectangular form factor (so, 11x8 or 9x6) for the same reason: Economic use of space. I believe my X-something pages of Japanese names could have fit maybe on two back-to-back landscape 11x8 pages, including pretty borders and headers.

    Additional Lists: While MOAR is almost always better, I think we have to be careful as well: There is enough "silly" content in the existing version I think, and I definitely want useful-at-the-table focused content. To that end, I'd be hard pressed to add more lists, rather I'd focus on expanding the ones that already exist (and add new lists only if they definitely fill a needed gap).

    Gaming Content: Someone mentioned it in one of the other threads, and I agree: I love the lists that contain cultural tidbits of some sort: Clothing, food, scenario hooks, etc. To that end, I'd almost want, for every list, for there to be at least one "1-20" list of *something*, *anything* cultural to go along with it. It can be serious (clothing) or silly (like my "things found in a vending machine"), it could be oracle like (in other words, sentence fragments like "an old man in an elk hide, with rabbit bones in his hair" "a medicine drum, worn through in a corner, handed down for four generations), or whatever. These can really make a lot of those name lists more usable, IMO. At the same time, it's a Names List, so that's the focus... the Other Stuff list would be a footnote that makes the names more usable. A pinch of spice, like.\n
    Posted By: Jason MorningstarI'd encourage more judicious editing, rather than a "submit it and we will put it in" attitude.
    \nAhh, yeah, in other words, This Here. I'd not be like, "We already have Polish, why do we need Hungarian", but rather "Do we need another list of Retro-Future Laser Princesses? We already have two Edgar Rice Burroughs lists... sell me on it", etc. And I'd totally favor lists that also included cultural info, scenario hooks etc.

    Wiki: Yuck. That is, for formatting for the book, I'd want it in a template. But someone can take all the data later and wiki it up themselves if they want.

    The Important Thing: This is going to move slow, like a giant sea turtle with a city resting on its shell. We here tend to explode into action and excitement, what I'd like to do is start slowly, with measured, calculated actions aimed at slowly producing solid results.

    Thanks everyone. Keep suggestions or thoughts incoming. Once I hit a critical mass with organization, we'll see things start moving.

    -Andy

    \n Andy:

    Ben's right about everything there. CC-Attribution is what you want. I still license stuff that I haven't officially released as NonCommercial-ShareAlike for some reason. I re-license it at CC-Attribution on release now.

    ShareAlike generally isn't a great idea: it forces the hand of anyone who would re-use your work, and makes them use the same license, which isn't awesome.

    For a good real-world example, the BSD Unix operating system uses a license much like CC-Attribution, where you can do what you like with the software, commercial or non-commercial, open-source or not. Linux uses the GNU license, which is a lot like CC-ShareAlike. This is one of the reasons Apple's OS X - now the largest Unix-based OS on desktops in the world - used BSD as its core, because they could. They've made great progress for BSD, but couldn't have done that with a share-alike license.

    (Also, be aware, this discussion causes flamewars the world over, and my opinion is just that, an opinion. Others have reasons for supporting the GNU model.)

    - Clinton
