\n I'm sort of embarrassed by how excited I am about this little tale of dungeon exploration.

We've been playing D&D at BWHQ. Thor posed a question about how exploration worked in D&D. The only way to truly answer the question was to play the game. So I broke out my Moldvay red basic books and B1 In Search of the Unknown. We've played four sessions.

I'll refrain from comment about the quality of the content of the adventure.

We had six characters (and six players): two fighters, a dwarf, a magic user, a cleric and a thief. It was a robust first level party. One of the fighters had recently been killed by a zombie. His player took control of a bandit NPC working with the group.

Since we're playing Moldvay D&D, we have a mapper, a back up mapper and a caller as the rules insist. When we started out, there were many groans about mapping. Some of the players new to D&D wondered aloud why we had to do such a thing. One veteran of another kind of D&D made lots of disgruntled noises about mapping. The roles of the mappers and caller rotate each session. The rules suggest it and it's a good way to get everyone involved and make sure no one is left out.

Our story begins with our characters standing around a magic rock, each eating chips of it. Don't ask. It's In Search of the Unknown. While eating said rock, one character is turned invisible and one is teleported to another unexplored room of the dungeon. The teleport victim was a fighter and one of the mappers. Fortunately, the main group still had its back up mapper (the former bandit). Spooked, the five characters remaining in the main party still eating the rock decided to head out. But before they left, they wanted check out this one little tunnel on the map. They got confused and a little turned around\u2026and were ambushed by five hobgoblins who had been hiding out in a small room at the end the questionable passage. The group had passed this way before but the hobgoblins had remained hidden\u2014since they were out-numbered. Now it appeared the group had been diminished: it seemed there were only four remaining. So they ambushed them and demanded all of their treasure. The group parlayed but couldn't sway the hobgoblins. Neither would they give up their treasure! Thus, a bloody melee ensued. The invisible character\u2014a rather formidable cleric\u2014just missed her surprise attack and was subsequently killed by an enraged hobgoblin. The valiant dwarven veteran commanded his friends to retreat while he held off the attackers. He was slain after sustaining two brutal blows. The clever mage tossed oil to cover their retreat and the two surviving thieves and the mage used their map to beat a hasty retreat to one of the dungeon exits. To use the map, they had to describe to me the turns they were taking and the rooms they were entering. I didn't describe the dungeon to them. I only confirmed their location by repeating familiar descriptions.

Meanwhile, our lost fighter was in the dark, alone\u2026but he had a map with him. He used his sword as a walking stick and managed to find that he was in a sort of cul-de-sac with a webbed entranced. Huddling into the corner, he shredded some hemp rope and used two crossbow bolts to spark a fire. This was resolved with a Wisdom check. He has a Wisdom 11. He managed to pass the test. With light, he could see that he was alone in an unfamiliar section of the dungeon. Furthermore, the player had just witnessed his friends get slaughtered in a fight. He was on edge.

After burning his way out of the webs, he began to carefully navigate this area. In order to create a sense of dislocation, I stopped providing compass directions (as is customary in our dungeon exploration). I described everything as right, left, forward and back. And I only described what he could see\u2014only 30' around him by torchlight. After a 10 minutes of map navigation (and an hour of in-game exploration and thus two wandering monster checks and one torch expended), he managed to find his way back to familiar territory. Once he was reoriented, he sauntered toward a passage that lead to an exit. But there he spotted multiple sets of beady, black reflective eyes attracted by his torchlight (my next wandering monsters check came up positive). These creatures seemed to be mucking about with a pit trap that the party had disarmed earlier. He beat a hasty retreat down a long side passage and hid. There, much to my surprise, he described his character dousing his torch and moving back in the direction of the creatures.

As we all know, D&D underdwellers can see in the dark (but usually only out to 60'). This plan was only going to get him killed. Or so I thought. There were three possible passages he could have retreated down. All were longer than these creatures could see. So I rolled randomly to determine which one they were checking and didn't tell him. He told me he wanted to touch the right hand wall with his hand and move quietly along it, following its curve. At this point, all of the other players were watching and listening, cringing at his careful steps.

I told him to make a Dex check. He had stepped out behind the creatures and was moving away from them. They weren't looking in his direction, but they were RIGHT THERE. He has a Dex of 8. You should have heard his voice crack when he passed the test. He moved away from his pursuers with a firm plan in mind to follow this wall until he could reorient himself. Well, as he moved in this direction, I told him he could smell faint smoke. When he stopped at a junction, I told him he could hear the popping of bones and the tearing of sinew in the distance. And he could hear a quiet voice whisper in a language he couldn't understand. He continued to go forward. An arrow whistled through the air and cracked against the wall next to him. He flinched, but he kept going. Unfortunately, his map of this area wasn't completely accurate. Describing how his character moved, telling me which hand he was using to follow the wall, he ended up in a room with no exit. And his pursuers were the self-same hobgoblins who had run off his companions. They followed him into the room and blocked the exit.

With nothing to lose he says, "I parlay. 'I suppose you want my treasure.'" We rolled on the reaction chart (plus his Charisma bonus) and came up with the Monster Uncertain result. I rolled a D6 looking for a result of a 1 to see if the hobgoblins spoke common. One did. They told him to dump his treasure. He readily did so. We rolled again on the reaction chart and he got an "Enthusiastic" response. So they laughed, clapped him on the back and told him he could be on his way, but before he left, "Could we borrow a couple of torches?" "Sure, what for?" "Don't ask."

Shaking but relieved to be alive, he took a moment to try to light another torch and failed. Still shaking and still in the dark, he pressed on. Shortly, he came within in sight of a glowing cave that the group had already plundered. His light-deprived eyes drank deep. And he took a moment to use the light to ignite his own torch. Moving confidently but cautiously now, he made a circuitous journey to his goal: the stairs up to the first level. The path to the dungeon exit that his friends had escaped from was now blocked by at least two groups of creatures.

Another wandering monsters check came up\u2026positive. Bad luck for him. A huge spider dropped from the ceiling 40' behind him. Both character and monster were surprised (I suppose the player was too). Without hesitation he blurted, "I run!" They had fought two of these creatures already. He knew how tough and deadly they could be. I referenced their respective movement rates. The spider pursued, but it appeared that they had the same pursuit rates. The player even tried to have his character toss his armor off so he could run faster, but he had a torch in one hand and the map in the other. No free hands to do anything else. And this is when it all clicked. The spider couldn't catch him\u2026so long as he successfully navigated via his map.

The player nervously rattled off directions according to his map. I checked them against my map. He found the stairs to the upper level. In my evil GMness, I let the spider close half the distance on the stairs since it could take some vertical short cuts. The player rapidly produced his map of the first level. He narrated a turn and then cursed. "No. Fuck." He'd taken a wrong turn. "Oh yes," I intoned evilly. He was heading down a passage that had two options: an outlet and a dead end. The player quickly reoriented himself toward the outlet. He headed through a door the group had previously unlocked. Slammed into the far wall, turned left and ran for his life. Ahead of him was a secret door that the group had spiked open when they were exploring the first level. He dashed through it and thence down and out to the exit. The spider didn't pursue beyond the dungeon. He's not that kind of spider. And it would have just been a cruel move on my part. This is our first adventure. There's nothing outside this dungeon.

Everyone cheered. "Shaking. My hands are shaking."
"I can't believe you're alive!"
"Me neither! You bastards! You left me!"
"It was that map. The map saved you."
Everyone agreed. Everyone looked at the map with renewed interest and understanding. And I certainly understood at that moment.

Without the map it would have been a total-party-kill. With the maps, two fell fighting but four survived.

You enter the dungeon and begin the orderly process of mapping. You try to maintain that order for as long as possible. Eventually, chaos creeps in and your friends start to die. That map is the only thing between you and a horrific death in the pitch dark corridor of some forgotten corner of this hell hole. Exploration of the dungeon space is an attempt to bring order to chaos, an attempt to glean knowledge from information, separate signal from noise. But unfortunately, exploration brings you in contact with many unforeseen elements, some of which can only be survived via the map.

    \n The caller is pretty natural, actually. I imagine that the rule is an outgrowth of the natural tendency of the players to select a leader for the enterprise. I mean, we don't have a caller as a systemic mandate, but one still emerges with practically any mix of players. It's just the nature of the practice that a hard core of two people forms within the party: one is the nominal leader while the other is the mapper, and these two debate the choices the party faces. (Sometimes there are three, in which case it's leader, aide, mapper.) The others listen, and interject with their ideas, and tell their opinions when asked for them. And when the GM wants to know whether any decisions have been made and actions prepared, he turns towards the naturally emergent caller/leader to ask: "OK, are you going to do anything?" Or, as happens often after the deliberative bit, the leader strings out a terse flow of orders and contingencies that everybody at the table takes for granted; he has authority born of natural social interaction.

    This party leader role is procedurally all but equivalently important to the GM, for this player may, should he wish, mandate both direction and pacing. Anybody who's played modern drama games knows that these two things alone are like two thirds of the entire imaginary space. The GM is often for long periods of time a secondary star in this constellation, when compared to the party leader and his trusty sergeant/mapper.

    In yesterday's session our group for some reason swapped the traditional leader and mapper - or rather, Timo took on both roles himself. This probably had something to do with Sipi's main character being stuck in a mushroom forest in another dungeon, so he played the decidedly laid-back and sassy "Mandrage the Magician" instead. This had decided impact on the effectiveness of the party, as Timo's certainly a fine player, but he's not at home as the caller: the point of debate gets lost, decisions do not get made and the GM has to request "your actions, gentlemen" several times before the party comes upon an acceptable plan.

    \n When I was a lad, I took one look at the AD&D caller rules and fell back, aghast with horror. How could such a thing be legal? What depraved fascist would ever perpetrate such a crime? How dare these dull game designers try to impose their will on my self-expression!

    Now 30 years later, I think the caller is one of the best rules in D&D. The caller is like a player-side GM, or even a union rep for the players. When everyone is screaming and howling, it's the caller's job to get things in order and pass on instructions to the GM.

    Since Moldvay D&D is about dungeon exploration, the caller is a very functional role. The group is constantly making minute decisions about direction, rate, light, search, noise, etc. The caller ensures that everyone at table is acting in concert. As noted from my example, if everyone simply headed off on his or her own, not only would it be unmanageable table chaos, but the adventure would be short-lived (as would the adventurers).

    And the game is specific when it says the other players are welcome to give their input and roleplay their characters, but the caller is the one responsible for relaying info the DM. Also, in combat the role of the caller recedes and the DM is responsible for addressing each player as necessary.

    One of our house rules is that the group sits in marching order from left to right around the table. The front rank sits to the GM's left; if he's not in the front rank, the caller sits at the GM's right hand (in the back rank).
    Last night, the group was so rambunctious I couldn't even hear the caller giving instructions. This caused a number of miscues. But it's fun to say that it's not my fault if I can't hear the caller! Being absolved of the duty of polling every character about every decision is fantastic. It makes the intense dungeon exploration manageable since D&D DMs have to do so much heavy lifting.

    The game advises that the caller be the character with the highest Charisma and that he or she should lead from the front. It doesn't explain why. I can guess why, but we chose to go another route. We rotate caller duties session to session. This process gives each session a particular character. One caller kept the group focused and quiet. One merely fueled the fires of insanity. One was democratic, and another was capricious.

    As I noted above, playing with a caller is a new experience for me. I love it. I love both the functionality of it and its emergent properties.

    \n Hi Jason,
    At first, I alternated between the two groups turn by turn. Time tracking in this version of D&D is pretty easy. Their exploration rate was 60' of dungeon per 10 minutes. So once the lone fighter (X the Veteran) had explored his 60' I cut back to the group.

    As it turns out X got stuck in the web for four turns while the group took two turns exploring an unknown tunnel. They then got into it with the hobgoblins which took another turn. Then they ran. So by the time X was free, they had left dungeon. Regardless of the in-game events, we did have 30 minutes of one player reading map directions on his own. Waiting that long for one player is taxing on the group's patience. Some folks were bored. Some were listening. Some were making up new characters.

    As for the caller, we haven't developed a veto rule yet. So far the group has been fine with letting the caller make the ultimate decision. I think this has two components: 1) Everyone knows they're going to get a turn now. 2) Finding your way about the dungeon requires focus and communication with the mappers. If you're not paying close attention, you're kind of fucking lost. So at any given time at least two players have no fucking clue where they are.

    Six players is a massive group for a Burning Wheel game, but it feels right for this version of D&D. I certainly wouldn't want to do anything that let the players explore their individual agendas, though. That's when you get five players bored to death while one player says really dumb shit in a funny voice.

    \n Johnzo,

    There is no boxed text in this adventure. The details are spare (but often unintentionally hilarious).
    Descriptive conceits that I settled on:
    I used compass directions (north, south) unless the group lost its light or bearings at which time I used relative bearing (left, right).
    I described distances in feet according to the map. A 20' x 20' room, the wall extends 40' ahead.
    I only told the humans what they could see according to their lantern light. 30' from the light source.
    I described what the Dwarf could see 60' from his position.
    In rooms larger/longer than those directions, I described darkness at the limits of sight. Though I would often fuck this up and describe a lurking monster before it could be seen.

    According to the rules, I'm obligated to accurately describe the environs explored by the players. However, I am not obligated to correct their map if it develops inaccuracies. In fact, I'm instructed not to by the game. Which is MUCH HARDER THAN IT SEEMS.

    In practice, I answered the players' questions about a room until they were satisfied unless they were in the dark, running or in a fight.

    I am totally on the players' side. I want them to succeed and get all the treasure. My instinct is to help them. But it's vital to resist that impulse entirely. Let the map be drawn and the dice fall where they may! It's how this version of D&D is played. If I want player agency and character-driven drama, I'll play Burning Wheel.

    \n Hans,
    The mapping thing is AMAZING. In order to map, the game instructs that the character must be near the front of the party and must have hands free and a light source. So the game is quite clear: If you are mapping, your character is mapping. It's a direction correlation between player action and character action.

    But check this out: That's all the character does. He's not involved in mapping in any other way (unless a spell is cast but that's different). The character doesn't get better or more skilled at mapping. Ever. The player cannot test his character's Wisdom or Intelligence to see if he maps the room accurately or inaccurately. You don't level up and get better maps. And once it's established that you're the mapper, you never describe your character mapping. It's the player who maps. It's he or she who must listen to instructions. It's his or her skill that improves. The character is merely a thin constraint so it feels like you're playing a roleplaying game.

    While this version of D&D is most assuredly a roleplaying game, it is one focused on player skill. This game is primarily about mapping. Everything else is ancillary. And the players map, not the characters. The mapping character is just a mannequin holding blank piece of paper.

      \nIn the Expert set, wilderness maps were hex maps - players would advance from hex to hex in the same way that you'd move through a dungeon. There are rules for getting lost - your orientation gets messed up, so when you think you go one direction, you go another. I haven't done it, but the kind of chaos this would produce on a map sounds like absolutely no fun whatsoever.

      Untangling that mess would be really hard, unless hexes are distinctive. "The room with the crumbling stairs," is much more specific than "another forest hex". I think the only saving grace would be that the next sunrise or sunset would presumably reveal the mistake so (with 5-mile hexes) you wouldn't get too far before realizing you'd messed up. But, unlike a dungeon where you can just nip around a loop quickly, who wants to backtrack 30 miles to fix the mistake? Distinctive hexes seem like a must.
