\n I must share this event. Why? Because it made me laugh, and maybe it will make you laugh, too.

Thursdays is something of a workshop night for the Peoria group. This is when we playtest games, both from other people and our current projects. Lately, we've been testing Ralph Mazza's forthcoming game Blood Red Sands. Since we're constantly tinkering with the rules, it's not as satisfying as actually playing the final product will be, but I enjoy both the design work and the game play as it actually evolves.

I think that the rules are coming together, because last night's conflict was tense and challenging for both sides. Plus, it included some seriously over-the-top narration, which I feel properly demonstrates the necessary Metal attitude required to play Blood Red Sands.

The scene is set. Shard (the hero of the current ordeal) has rallied some of the men of the freehold, and they are fighting to recover control of the freehold from a band of sorcerous shepherds. (It's a bit complicated.)

I'm playing Shard, and it's my turn to be the Chronicler. So I say how we surround the building where Cam, the leader of the shepherds, is holed up. Then we set the building on fire. Then Cam comes out, hair and body aflame, and bows before Shard, yielding control of the freehold.

Gabrielle is playing Cam and the shepherds, and she cannot accept this narration. So, in proper form, she slams her hand on the table and says, "It shall not be!" And so we go to conflict.

Crystal is playing the formerly Unliving freeholders who were rescued by Shard and the freeholders. She decides that she wants to join the conflict on the side of Shard. Tactically, this may not have been the best option, as it's in Crystal's best interest if the shepherds win. However, Crystal wants to get in her shots to earn some VPs of her own. So, into the conflict she goes.

Conflict begins. I tell Crystal that she needs to make the attack for our side. So one of the former Unliving calls into the house, trying to get Cam to come quietly.

This doesn't work.

Instead, Cam leaps from the building, hair and body ablaze, to attack Shard. Shard flips Cam over his head and slams him on the ground behind him. Then he crushes Cam's face with his heel.

And that's when things started to go wrong for me.

Cam reaches up, grabs Shard's foot, and breaks his ankle. And not just, "Your foot doesn't work" but "Bones are protruding from your leg" kind of broken ankle. I didn't have the necessary dice to block this attack, so I had to eat it. I get handed a new Trait: "Debilitating Limp d10 black". Great.

So now Shard is also lying on the ground. I talk about a foot sweep, forgetting that Cam was already on the ground. The rest of the group is mocking me. Crystal points out, "You can't foot sweep; you need another foot to stand on." And Ralph comments, "Yeah, but standing on your bloody stump would be seriously metal."

Did I mention that the catchword for this game is "metal"? I mean, like Durham 3, horns in the air, spikes upon spikes upon spikes, uber-macho with umlauts Metal. Yeah, it's like that.

So, I'm thinking, "What would be suitably metal?" Then I hit upon it.

I say, "I stab you in the eye with my bone."

In my humble opinion, this was the high point of the evening.

(It helped that I could back this up with a d10 and d8, showing 6 and 5, both being yellow, which basically means that I could reuse both dice.)

Sadly, Cam blocked, but he had to refresh his dice to do so. This is a big deal! I explain.

Imagine that you're playing Dogs in the Vineyard, and all the dice that you use for Raising and Seeing (except when you Take the Blow) go into a pool of used dice. Then you can burn one of those dice to get all the rest back, rerolled and able to be used. That's what "refreshing" is. There are only three refreshes in any conflict, and you can't refresh if someone on your side was the last to refresh.

So, while Cam and Shard are duking it out, I'm counting on being able to refresh. However, my "ally" Crystal chooses to refresh early, just to stop me from being able to refresh. So now I'm running low on dice and have no way to get any back unless Gabrielle refreshes.

In other words, even though I didn't actually stab Cam in the eye with my bone, that move saved the day for me.

Gabrielle refreshed. Soon after, I refreshed. Despite further trickery from Crystal, I managed to prevail.

Bloody and battered, Shard triumphed over Cam, who was finally forced to bow before Shard and yield control of the freehold to him.

It was a good night.

Seth Ben-Ezra

    \n What made this combat especially gratifying for me was how it highlighted one of the key differences between BRS and Dogs mechanically.
    \n
    \nIn Dogs, you look at your dice and say..."huh, no way I'm winning this one" and you give, yielding the stakes.
    \nIn BRS, often times what's at stake is of secondary importance to the opportunity to score points (you score points for causing "fallout"). So instead when you look at your dice you're thinking "huh, no way I'm winning this one...I wonder how many points I can score before I go down."
    \n
    \nIn the fiction, Shard had allied with the Freeholders (me) who had been betrayed by their allies the Nomadic Shephards (Gabrielle). So when the Shephards took the Freeholder leader prisoner and tried to take over my town, Shard jumped in to take it back.
    \n
    \nAt the meta level however, Gabrielle betrayed me because after helping me get my faction's goal, I was trying to pump her for more before helping her get her faction's goal...she was having none of it and promptly stomped me silly. Seth saw the opportunity to score some points against Gabrielle (who having recently been fighting me was already somewhat battered) and so "came to my rescue"...for purely selfish reasons.
    \n
    \nThe cool twist mechanically was, that even though Seth finally won the knock down drag out fight, beat Gabrielle and claimed the stakes (taking over the town for himself)...Gabrielle scored WAY more points than he did...so at the meta level, she totally won that fight (I however, had scored few points against her, losing both in the fiction and at the meta level...which is all good, because last week I totally owned everybody)
    \n
    \nAnother nice thing that this session demonstrated is that I think we finally have struck a pretty good balance between hero and faction power. The Faction players have been able to keep the hero in check, wearing him down, keeping him from running off with a huge victory. But at the same time, the huge amount of effectiveness sitting on the hero sheet makes us equally willing to court him to help us acheive our goals.
    \n
    \nI'm looking forward to getting the niggling mechanical effects out of the way and enjoying a few sessions of pure play.

    \n For what it's worth, I agree with Ralph. It's only worth writing about if it actually affects the game. Now, I happen to think that it is indeed a fun dice game, and I'd love to throw down Hero vs. Hero for laughs. But, if it's going to be in the game (beyond perhaps a brief note that says, "Hey! Fighting Hero vs. Hero can be fun!", then it needs to be part of the gameplay.

    Personally, I'd want to see an effect that is useful to the victor of the battle without awarding VPs. Playing through ordeals should be the path to victory. It seems to be that side-duels should give a bump in effectiveness to assist in the next ordeal, but in some way that eventually ceases to reward ongoing fighting of side-duels.

    Here's an idea. Maybe a Hero can earn one colored die, based on his victories in between ordeals. It starts at a nothing. Each win increases the die size, while each loss reduces the die size. It can't be bigger than a d10. So, by battling four times and winning, you can earn a d10. Of course, any loss sets that back a die size. And, for fun, maybe the loser of the duel says what the color is, based on the narrative events of the duel. So, in essence, the last person that you defeat tells you the die color.

    Once you enter an ordeal, the die is locked in, and it goes away at the end of the ordeal.

    And the duel doesn't have to be combat per se. Maybe it's a foot race, or a competition to scale a cliff, or something like that. You could even include a chart with possible conflict arenas that gets diced on, or maybe you roll Legend and the lower roll chooses. Or something like that.

    This way, fighting side-duels matters to the game, but it doesn't become an exploitable advantage.
