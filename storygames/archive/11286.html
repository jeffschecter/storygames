    \n I will be at GenCon and will be working Games on Demand, as I did last year.

    Kira, at the last GenCon, Scott and I did bring one-sheet listings of the games that each of us was willing and able to run, and a listing of times that we were available to run them. We don't schedule individual games, though. That defeats the purpose of the "Games on Demand" thing, which works thus: People come into the GoD room, find one of the hosts, look over the table full of game books and one-sheets, and say, "Hey, I have 4 friends and we all want to play _____. Can someone run that for us?" Of course, we hosts help them with all of that. We help them understand what games are available. We help them recruit other people to play games they're interested in.

    If we were going to just schedule a specific game at a specific time, we'd be better served registering the event with GenCon properly. And then it'd fill up really fast and you wouldn't get into it anyway. Furthermore, as soon as we start trying to have a fixed schedule of games run at Games on Demand, I suspect GenCon will come down on us like a ton of bricks and cancel our event. Also, GoD does assure that we don't schedule a slot and a GM to run some game that no one is interested in playing.

    The randomness is a little less chaotic than you'd think. We do try to run games within some predicted "slots," which we put on our one-sheets. Show a little before 10AM to get into the 10-2 "slot," or whatever, and we'll try to accommodate your gaming tastes.

    Also, find me at the Embassy Suites around 8-9 PM and I'm generally looking for people to game with. Sometimes I'm looking for a game to get into (and know people and can get you and me and other folks into something fun) or I'll just run something. My main issue with Embassy Suites planning is a total lack of urgency (it can take an hour or two to get a game started).

    \n Dave,

    There is no sign-up for playing in Games on Demand. You show up before the slot begins, consult with the GoD hosts and GMs, find players with similar interests, and play a game. You need a badge and generic tickets to play, as usual, but there is absolutely no registration for events. This is the "on Demand" part, get it?

    You can volunteer for two different roles: room hosts and GMs.

    Room hosts set up a display table full of games and one-sheets and then greet people who walk over to find out WTF is going on with Games on Demand. Hosts answer questions about the games and help players find a game to play in. In some circumstances, a host might also try to run a game while greeting and consulting, but this generally means doing a half-assed job at both GMing and consulting. A dedicated host is best.

    Last year, we had room hosts scheduled for large blocks of time. This turns out to be incredibly boring, especially in the slow middle of the slot when no one is coming in. Still, someone needs to keep an eye on all the games on the display table. I recommend that we set up 2-hour (half-slot) room host duties, so that people can mitigate their boredom and spend the other 2 hours effing around in the Dealer's Hall or getting lunch or something. This does not stop people from doing two 2-hour room host duties, of course, if they really want.

    GMs commit to running games in the designated slots. I recommend producing a one-sheet like Scott and I did last year and bringing 50 copies or more. We put one or two on the display table and we hand out the rest to players or drop a stack at the Forge Booth. At the very least, the one-sheet should have a short blurb about Games on Demand with a schedule and room location, plus brief info (and pictures!) of 5-10 games you know how to run. Also, put your name at the top. When you're on GM duty, you or the room host can hand people your one-sheet and say, "You can play any of these games right now." They read the blurbs, pick one, and it's off to the races. As we get closer to the con, I'll post an example of a one-sheet and a Word template. Also, I'll be happy to make your one-sheet for you if you give me your name, the game list, and the blurbs. Edited by me, free of charge.

      \n Re: LARPs and GenCon.

      I have some personal preferences. Theatre-style, meaning no boffers, meaning no persistent characters, meaning players oppose one another. The ones I really like are highly multilateral, meaning that there are a lot of objectives in play, and lots of different sides to each issue, and usually no monolithic factions that all completely agree across all objectives. Big pluses in my book are a high reliance on player-player interaction to drive outcomes, low reliance on mechanics, and a high proportion of players who enjoy costuming.

      The 7th Sea LARP run by Plaid Chameleon has been consistently good for my tastes. (LRP1009716)
      Elder Entertainment has a short track record, but strong so far. (LRP1012271)
      BYOV is a group that is somewhat hit-and-miss, but has a core group of people who tend to share my aesthetic (LRP1008192)
