\n OK, I'm sort of polling y'all, so tell me what you think, please.

My goal is to minimize preparation and handling time. Originally I had all interactions revolve around a single deck of playing cards, with each card serving in turn as randomizer and currency in a very constrained, very elegant, very boring package. The game needs a level of tactical complexity that a single deck of state-tracking cards won't allow, so I need to add a companion element. Here are the things I'm thinking of - which represents the least imposition on prospective pick-up players?

One deck of playing cards and tokens. With this option, the tokens get turned back into cards at the game's conclusion, one for one. The card deck serves as randomizer and then, at the end-game portion, currency.

Two decks of playing cards. With this option, one deck consists of re-usable randomizers and the other consists of end-game currency.

One deck of cards and pencils. Mark down currency as it is accrued, then convert it into cards at end-game. Honestly, I'm trying to avoid having to write anything in play, although you'll need pencils to write some stuff down during prep.

Is one of these more of a pain in the ass than the other? That's all I'm asking. They all seem sub-optimal and fiddly, but I can't do what I need to do with a single deck of cards. I'm looking for 1,2, or 3, but if 4 occurs to you, let me know.

Thanks!

    \n Personally, I like tokens. Having a solid object I can see and touch makes an abstract currency real (reifies it, if you're being fancy). Also, tokens can sometimes be chosen to match the game's theme (poker chips for western games, nuts and bolts for mecha games, etc.).

    Cards from a second deck could easily be confusing: even with different backs the risk of mixing would always be present.

    Pencils can easily be used instead of tokens even when tokens were specified, if one were so inclined.

    Another counting measure I've seen used is dice: the player has a number of dice in front of them, and the total number of points is the total of all the upright faces.

    \n Jason, I don't know if you've played the card game San Juan, but they are able to provide some complexity with no tokens or paper and pencil.

    But the designer did a brilliant thing in translating Puerto Rico into a easy-to-play card game. The cards act as both content as counters. A card represents both something to build (i.e. a silver mine or a library), but it also counts simply as a card. So for example, you have a hand of five cards and you want to build a Silver Mine. You need to have the card you want to build in your hand, but you also need to discard a number of cards equal to the building cost of that card. So if the Silver Mine costs 4 cards, you will need to dump your entire hand in order to build it. And maybe there is another cool card in your hand that you don't want to get rid of yet. So what do you do??

    I'm probably explaining this terribly, so I hope you get the gist.
