  • Colin_Fredericks
    February 2006
    [Blatant Plug] I have to mention my own science-fiction games: Valence and Sufficiently Advanced. Valence is part culture clash, part space opera, part standard sci-fi. I like the culture clash aspects best, myself. S.A., when it's done, will be the highest of the high-tech I can come up with and still have it be technology rather than magic. The game rides heavily on the use of Plot Points to change or create story elements, and also uses them to balance low-tech characters against high-tech ones. [/Blatant Plug]

    For other peoples' games, I have to mention Tribe 8 (more post-apocalyptic, but excellent), and Decipher's Star Trek game (actually rather well-done). Trinity ain't half bad either, mostly for the setting.

    I think part of the disadvantage to science fiction games is that fantasy gives such an automatic reference point, and horror specifically depends on removing or warpong reference points. You can say "non-traditional fantasy" and everyone knows your game has no elves or dwarves, but what the hell does "non-traditional sci-fi" mean? Every sci-fi game has to dig hard for traction, to avoid having people just pull the nifty ideas out and use them elsewhere. Also, in fantasy you can always just say "It's magic, shut up," while in sci-fi you're expected to either leave social structures alone (which most people do), or actually consider the ramifications of things like brain jacks (which most don't).

    Hmm. I think I might have needed [rant] tags there. Oops.

  • Joshua A.C. Newman
    February 2006
    Awesome! Two votes for Shock:!

    For a damn fine definition of science fiction, you should check out the Wikipedia article on the subject.

    For Shock:, I was drawing heavily from the premise that science fiction is a genre in which human conflicts take place that can only happen in the environment of the story.

    Oddly, that excludes Space Opera, which is usually based on normal Earth-stuff: us vs. them primate politics, sword fights, naval battles. Which isn't to say that Space Opera isn't cool. I love Star Wars, but it's based in the Campbellian cycle, so it's for saying different things, mostly about the personal growth of the hero, rather than the society-level stuff that the best science fiction stories deal with.

    Interesting side-note: When I saw that wash of a movie Reign of Fire, there was one really awesome scene where these guys do this "exXxtreme dive" out of high-altitude helicopters, get chased by dragons, and at the last second hit their parachutes before hitting the ground. It was a really cool action scene, probably the most thrilling "chase" scene I had ever seen.

It ended up being Universalis (and an SF Universalis game at that) that helped me recreate that scene: We had strong identification with the protags we created (well, me and another guy ended up passing one or two back and forth, but still), and at one point there was a several hundred mile suited free-fall through a gas giant's atmosphere, being chased by patrol ships, stopping once a gas-mining-colony-base-thingy was reached.

    It was coooooooooool.
