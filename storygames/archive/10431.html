\n I've ran Dogs game where someone was gaming the system like crazy while someone else was just choosing dice randomly. Where someone described everything in first person while someone else used third person. Where someone asked me questions about what the environment smelled and felt like... they wanted to be immersed, to feel like they were there while someone else just wanted to get to the meat of the conflict and leave the flavorful details aside.
\n
\nWe play games with all these people at the same time. Often they are imagining completely different things. In some ways, playing almost different games. And still interacting. And still having fun together!
\n
\nDon't get me wrong, there are certainly many, many, many examples of these different styles clashing and causing many problems (maybe these examples are the majority!). The point of this post is that I've seen these styles blend together in ways that amaze me and work perfectly functional. RPGs can be neat that way.
\n
\nRock,

    \n I've seen them blend in positive ways. Sometimes, if one player is consistently driving for mechanical rewards and the other players aren't, that player's character can become the villain or the foil, because they're super effective at doing whatever earns the mechanical reward, which can create positive tension if the other players are ambivalent or even skeptical about the reward. Like in Dogs where one player follows the "escalation to more violence is good" reward, becoming an avenging angel of death, which forces the other players to decide how they feel about that. So even if the avenging player isn't interested in wrestling with moral questions, their character's immoral actions provide opportunities for the other players to wrestle with those issues.

    But I do think positive synergy -- rather than a cacophony of different desires and intentions -- more easily happens when people are familiar with the other players' ticks or preferences and can create spaces for those players to shine. Or when people are self-aware and flexible about their own personal sockets or behaviors.

    \n I think "In A Wicked Age" is also capable of this. From my experience, it accommodates many different styles well. But you do need to get significant player input in the form of Best Interests up front, so that can be a hurdle for someone with a really passive style where they want everything to come from the GM so they can smash it. But after that it works well and I even see blending of the styles because the PCs are often fighting each other in game.
