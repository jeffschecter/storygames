    \n It seems to me that "OSR in space" matters because you can read "OSR" as meaning "operating at a level of abstraction where a single action spans a whole minute of Douglas Fairbanks swashbuckling" and thus a useful rejoinder to "it will take us six hours of pre-play and two hours of table time to dice out a two-minute fight", and "in space" as "needing to handle systems so complex that even the level of abstraction in something like hit points doesn't even begin to register" and thus a useful rejoinder to "we feel obliged to do whatever thing the GM clearly spent hours prepping the details for, and avoid the thing that will require waiting for more such prep before anything awesome can happen".
    \n
    \nNot having my sights set on space right now means that SwF matters to me as the OD&D; cornerstone for this approach, which has also resulted in things like Red Tide and An Echo, Resounding that prove that "in space" can mean lots of other scales than "interplanetary". Check out this thread for a discussion of this lineage's top-down approach and ACKS' bottom-up approach whose integration is my wish list for what I want to be playing in 2014.

    \n I'm GMing a new group that just played our first game on Sunday, after a previous session of just chargen. And I GM'ed a short campaign of it last fall.

    The twitter length description is "basic D&D meets Traveller." It's a mashup of D&D's stats and combat with Traveller's skills.

    It's super easy and VERY familiar to just about anyone who sits down to play. It allows for a lot of the OSR "eyeballing" style that I have grown to like, somewhat the opposite of, say, Pathfinder.

    Andy, you could very easily "mod" it to Star Frontiers. Give Yazirians, e.g., -1 to STR and CON, +1 to DEX and INT, maybe. Battle rage would maybe be +1 to hit/dmg melee only, but difficult to kick in, just like in SF.

    XP by default is based mostly on credits earned, but that section of the book takes up maybe half a page? Hackability? Easy.

    There are also character goals, which highly resemble the side quests in D&D4, or perhaps the BITs in BW. You get XP for pursuing them.

    It's definitely NOT a heroic power fantasy, so teenage me thinks this game sucks. But grownup me likes that any fight is potentially deadly. Not quite as grim as Luke's account of Moldvay D&D, but in that neighborhood. Characters start out with crap HP just like that, and big guns kill things dead.

    Combat gets about a page and a half in the text (but I don't know anything about a whole minute of swashbuckling; rounds are 6 seconds). Spaceship movement, combat, travel and repair gets 5 pages, including an example. It's lightweight and fast.

    It's fun to watch any confrontation and see the players thinking, shit, this could turn fatal if it escalates, then awesome exciting when they decide to go for it.

    The existence of rare, ancient, advanced tech in the setting serves as "magic items" for the characters, so you can provide the fun of neat treasure and gadgets and all that. Some old laser pistol from the days before the Scream, which has +1 accuracy. Or vials of nanotech healing drugs which cure 1d4+1 HP right then and there, etc. Steal from your D&D books and make something up on the fly.

    Spaceships are easy. Pick a hull type and fill it with components that stay within available space and power. Ship combat, like personal combat, is something you really hope you don't have to do, but when you have to it's deadly and exciting.

    Hope that helps. John has played a lot more than I have and will probably have a lot more to say.

    \nVernon asks: "So what do the characters go in such a game? Travel the stars looking for space loot? Battle space pirates? Become space pirates?"

    Vernon, have you played Traveller? I think the conceits are similar.

    The XP for money default reward system encourages slightly unscrupulous characters who are looking for the easiest way to earn the most creds. Maybe space pirates, but that sounds dangerous. There's probably an easier way to make a buck, I would think.

    It's a somewhat post-apocalyptic setting (possibly borrowing a bit from Traveller TNE) where many worlds are "lost" or have reverted to a primitive status. So there's plenty of exploring to do if that floats your boat.

    One of the characters in our game has the "tribal" background, as he's from a world that reverted to stone age tech after the crash (the "Scream" in game terms). He's not even sure where that world is, as he was kidnapped by mercenaries and then sold into indentured labor. So that makes for a good character goal along the way.

    \n So! Stars Without Number.

    Man. There's so much to talk about. I love so many things about this game it's hard to unpack it. It'll take a few posts.

    1. The Classes
    The game has three character classes: Warrior, Expert, and Psychic. They're kind of standard D&Dish classes at first glance (different hit dice, saves, etc.) but then they have the best class abilities. A Warrior can ignore the damage from one hit per combat. An Expert can re-roll their dice once per in-game hour. A Psychic gets woogie psionic powers (which they can 'master' and use as much as they want, as Matt mentioned above). They're so simple and consequential -- it really matters which class you are.

    2. The Backgrounds
    You know I love the two-word character type, and SWN delivers it with your character Background and Training Package. You might be a Con-Artist Pilot, a Hermit Psychic-Researcher, a Bandit Space-Marine, etc. These give you some skill levels, but we also treat the fiction really seriously here. If you were a Space Marine, you were a Space Marine. That sometimes positions you to make tests you might not otherwise get to make or attempt to win outcomes that others can't or not have to make a save that someone else might have to make.

    [Example from play: Dylan's ex-Space Marine "William Independent Contractor" (that's his name, used to be William Hanjin but he quit that job) is trying desperately to save his crew-mates who are currently mind-controlled by the horrible psychic hellhole on Triton. He figures out that hurting them gives them a chance to snap out of the mind-whammy. So he pulls off his oxygen tank (this is all happening in the dark, zero-g corridors of an ancient space hulk, btw), throws it at some crew-mates, and detonates it with a shot from his mazer rifle.

    William is a space marine, so I don't call for a Physical save (yet) when he rips off his mask and air supply. He's a rough motherfucker. So he also doesn't have to roll to hit the tiny air tank using the dreaded d20, he gets to make a skill test instead. Even in the dark, in zero-g, shooting at a small target, with no air supply, William's space marine background means he isn't thrown into the chaos of a combat roll. This is day one of boot camp.]

    \n Here's a few paragraphs from the rules:

    Skill Checks
    Skill checks are susceptible to overuse. Some GMs end up forcing a skill check on everything more complicated than tying your bootlaces, or prohibit PCs from even hoping to accomplish a feat for which they lack the appropriate skill. Skill checks are not meant to be the sole way that a PC gets something accomplished in the world.

    You should be charitable about the things a character can accomplish without a skill check. If an Expert\u2019s concept is that of a crack spaceship pilot with a Dexterity modifier of +2 and Vehicles/Space-2 skill, you shouldn\u2019t be expecting him to make skill checks to operate the ship under normal conditions. He can take off, he can land, he can get to places in the system without bothering with skill checks. Even someone with Vehicles/Space-0 should likely be able to do as much, except the expert pilot can do it without a check even amid high winds and low visibility. Let the PCs be good at their chosen focus.

    Don\u2019t spam skill checks. If a character is trying to repair a fusion power plant while under fire from badlands raiders, don\u2019t turn it into a roll-each-turn extravaganza to determine how far he progresses in his attempt. Let him roll once, with a failure meaning either that he can\u2019t do it in the available time or that he\u2019ll need another 1d6 rounds before he can bring the defense laser power plant online. Give him player choices each turn, like deciding whether to bring a laser online early and risk a short in the wiring. Let a single skill check indicate the general course of success or failure, but then let the PC make additional choices rather than additional rolls. Rolls can only add so much tension, and tension alone does not guarantee engagement with a situation.

    Don\u2019t require a skill check unless failing it means something...

      \n3. The Faction System
      SWN has a simple but interesting Faction mini-game that the GM plays behind the scenes. Basically, each faction gets ratings in Force, Cunning, and Wealth, then some tags (like Savage, Secretive, Colonists, etc.) and assets. The assets are weapons that the faction uses in the mini-game, each keyed to one of the stats (Force, Cunning, Wealth). Assets are things like space transports, spies, assassins, surveyors, banks, etc. Each faction turn (roughly a month) a faction gets one move. This usually means activating an asset and doing its thing, or moving an asset somewhere, or making a new asset.

      Here's why this is cool:
      - Faction moves are resolved with a simple d10+stat roll, so they're super quick and easy. You can do them all in no time.
      - Using faction moves instantly creates a living world backdrop for the game. The status quo isn't maintained, things are always changing outside the influence of the PCs.
      - The random rolls mean you sometimes get surprising outcomes, so there's a built-in check against over planning future events.

      Let me tell you a story about how the PCs in our game got a nuclear bomb.

      The Psychic Academy faction has an asset (a Psychic Assassin) that they want to use against the Perimeter Agency faction. The Academy is about to open the dormant jumpgate, and when they do that, the Perimeter is gonna get all up in their business, so the Academy is making a pre-emptive strike.

      So, a couple turns ago, I move the Psychic Assassin asset out to Saturn (using their Covert Transit Net asset) so he's in range to strike the Perimeter. He is armed with a briefcase nuke, to destroy the Perimeter HQ.

      Before that happens, though, my super secret weird faction, [REDACTED], makes a move and due to the dice, a really crazy thing happens. This leads to the destruction of another faction's asset (a Surveyor ship) and an advantage for [REDACTED] that I hadn't thought of before. So, using the weird result of the faction roll, I wrote up a mission lead involving the "destroyed" (actually missing and scuttled) survey ship and posted it on the job board we use in the game.

      And wouldn't you know it, but the players chose the "missing survey ship" mission as their next task (from a pool of about 12 other possible jobs).

      To pursue that mission -- out at Neptune/Triton -- the PCs had to first go to Saturn/Titan and make arrangements with the Perimeter to use their slipgate so they could more easily make the long transit out to Neptune. And then it hit me: Wait, the Academy definitely would have known about the crazy thing that happened with [REDACTED] on that faction roll, and they have an asset right there at Saturn already. They would totally re-task the Psychic Assassin and send him out to Neptune instead to try to squash the craziness that [REDACTED] was up to!

      So, next faction turn, just as the PCs headed out to Neptune themselves, I made the roll for the Psychic Assassin's mission out to Neptune/Triton with the nuke. Result: utter failure. Whoops! To keep things neat and tidy, I decided to fold the two results together: The success of [REDACTED] against the surveyor vessel and the utter failure of the Psychic Assassin and the nuke would be one and the same. The Assassin got passage aboard the surveyor ship out to Neptune, then when the ship got ganked by [REDACTED], the Assassin was foiled too (and he was killed, in fact).

      (This is kind of hard to untangle and talk about, I hope it's making sense so far)

      Basically, at this stage, due to the faction moves and outcomes we had:
      - A missing surveyor ship, scuttled, drifting near Neptune
      - A psychic assassin, passenger on the surveyor ship, killed before he could use his nuclear bomb against [REDACTED]

      Then the PCs arrived on the scene.

      Long story short, after lots of crazy hi-jinks and all the PCs almost dying several times over in desperate struggle against the mind-controlling psionic hellhole of [REDACTED] on Triton, they managed to capture the scuttled survey ship. Then, being space pirates, they searched every inch of it for loot.

      And they found the nuke. So now the PCs have a nuke.

      So, the faction system! It's cool.

      Posted By: Paul T.Can you explain what you mean by top-down vs. bottom-up in this context? What aspects of it has this particular game pioneered?
      \nBottom-up is what classic Traveller does: what is the price of fish on this world? ACKS doesn't pioneer this but it does take it really seriously, and makes sure that that answer is consistent with all the other little details and their inter-relationships that together create a coherent entity. But because it's formed from the roots it can be hard to see the forest for the trees.

      Top-down is what you can see SWN doing in the generator above: does this world have the Seagoing Cities tag? If SWN didn't pioneer this it's sure what I associate with the approach. It's awesome because it gives you the salient gameable details of something as big as a planet and lets you hook up all the other slightly lower level things like of course that means there are pirate city captains and giant pearls. But because it's imposed from above it can be hard to reach down to the roots; you are guessing at the price of fish.

      The combination of the two would be building up ACKS-type detail until you reached the point where you could tag that whole thing as a Seagoing Cities system. This gives you the missing top-down piece - you don't need to delve into the detail until you need it, but it is there to be had, there is a seamless integration of detail and concept that doesn't crash into an abstraction disjunction - and the missing bottom-up piece, where your Traveller characters are like "why is the price of fish so low in this system we are thinking about trading with?" and you don't have to generate or assimilate all the data yourself to reach the conclusion "because it's Seagoing Cities".

      I learned these terms in my former life in neuroscience, where such combination is the holy grail. Having these approaches integrated would make statements like "he is depressed because he has high serotonin levels" more meaningful than saying "I like Shakespeare because he uses the letter E so often."

      Um... let's see.

      1) Helping.
      If your character can take an action that will help someone else who's rolling, you get to roll to help. It's usually a complementary skill, which I call out as GM -- "Test Tech/Astronautics... help with Tech/Post-Tech...." A helping roll of 7+ gives the other character +1 to their test. If you miss (6-) you complicate matters.

      Sometimes help isn't a roll. If an extra body doing the thing is beneficial, it just works -- usually reducing the time required for the project.

      So far, we've allowed more than one person to help (so several +1s are possible). Hasn't been an issue, but I'm thinking I might switch it to AW style: multiple people can roll to help, but you only get a max of +1 from them. I'm also considering setting a target number for helping based on the situation, instead of always defaulting to 7.

      2) Solar System Travel Rules
      Which you can find here: http://www.sinenomine-pub.com/?p=149

      3) Chargen
      Each player created three characters to begin: One level 3 character and two level 1 characters. I do not pull punches and the game is quite deadly so it's handy to have extra bodies around when they start dropping like flies.

      Thanks to the totally awesome psychics in the group, no one has died permanently (yet). A lot of them have one or more deaths on their punch-card though.

      4) Resurrection Costs
      Being brought back to life gives you 3 permanent system strain. Being brought back after really catastrophic bodily destruction (using the pre-tech bio sarcophagus that the PCs looted) gives you 6 permanent system strain.

      5) Rolling to Hit, or Not
      We judge the need for to-hit rolling on a case by case basis. If the target is not aware of you and actively fighting/avoiding your attacks, you don't have to roll to hit. Often, you just roll damage. Other times, you make a skill test.

      The d20 hit roll only happens in a full-on fight between two parties exchanging violence. Which does happen, but less often than one-way violence (especially with teleport ambushes).

      There are probably some more. Back later.

      \n As one of John's players, I can comment on the multi-character dynamic. The characters were created as the crew of our original ship, and are all "on tap" at any time, all though not necessarily "in focus" at any given time. When we play out scenes, we'll focus on those characters who have a reason to participate, typically the ones using skills, or who have some dramatic reason to be there. When selecting away teams for particular missions, or when "splitting" the crew across multiple locations in a situation that may result in simultaneous actions scenes there is usually attention given to having at least one character from each player in each location if the needs of the mission can accommodate that, so that every player has an active voice in all parts of the action. During resolution of long downtimes, we'll go around the table and determine how each of our characters are spending the time. We have a tendency to engage in lots of "science projects" during such times, so the characters will often group into "lab teams", and then we'll play out the resolution of those projects. Players can definitely have more than one character in a scene, and having one of your characters help another is allowed.

      Our style of player makes heavy use of "free and clear" discussion at the table, with all information uncovered by any character known to all players. The players take responsibility for determining what subset of those facts each of their characters are aware of and determining their reactions appropriately. This does keep a lot of play in "pawn stance", but we do have occasional moments of in character play as well. This typically arises in specific "focus scenes" and occasionally from at the table riffing, where two players suddenly hit on a moment they'd like to have pass between two of their characters and briefly play it out. It's certainly been interesting for me to try to make sure each of my characters has a unique voice and style.

      We've had a few alterations in the roster since beginning. One character who was conceived of as coming from a "tech primitivist" culture, and who had a relationship with the Perimeter (the tech police) was retired by her player after our first mission made it clear that we were going to be dabbling with a lot of semi-legal pretech. She was replaced by an NPC, a pre-scream super psychic researcher whose consciousness we found in an ancient computer system, and who got "decanted" into a clone body, with concomitant loss of memory/ability to scale her down to fit the party power level. One of my starting level 1 characters became integrated into our original ship by symbiotic nanotech, and so when we traded that ship away, he went with it, and became an NPC. I haven't chosen to "refill" that slot in my roster so far. The character "William Independent Contractor" mentioned up thread (who was originally "William Hanjin", Hanjin being the megacorp we originally worked for) never really got much screen time until very recently, when a crisis left him as one of only two characters in control of their faculties and in a position to rescue the rest of the crew. So he suddenly went from being a blank slate to being a focus character, which was an interesting process to work out.

      I've found this style of play very successful for the kind of game we've been playing. It's allowed the central "ship and crew" conceit of our game to work very smoothly, without having to bog the GM down with managing a bunch of NPCs, and allowed players to put their oar into all different kinds of scenes, be they talky, techy, or fighty.
