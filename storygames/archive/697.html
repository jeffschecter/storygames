    Okay, I'd start with some of the things Matt said here.

    Then I'd add to that the fact that many of the things he talks about are not limited to women, or to racism, but have class ties as well.

    Then, I'd pile onto that the fact that most of us (in North America at least, I won't speak for places I've not spent time) have a tendency to cluster by class. We mostly live in areas that are inhabited by people of close to our class level, and mostly socalize with people close to our class level. Even online this clustering happens (and yes, sometimes it happens by people of certain classes not being present online in the same vollume or way as others). So most of our close contact experience (the kind where you get close enough to talk about gaming openly with someone) is limited to people of our generalized class level.

    It's only when those barriers get broken that we get a chance to realize what's going on. I've known several game writers who've worked in adult literacy programs (from me in LA to Malcom in Ontario, all the way across the continent, and Jason wherever the hell he lives) who have been shocked to find that many of our students play RPGs. Many of them play without books, or with only one book that they've mostly memorized, by having established procedures of play that members of the group are taught by gaming, rather than by being given the manual to read.

    Many of them are surprised to find out the vollume of RPG writing that is being done, especially in anything outside the big names of RPGs. Heck, most of them that I worked with were playing some version of old boxed D&D or AD&D first edition, and were often not aware (and profoundly did not care if they were aware) that there were newer editions. The attitude stands in pretty stark contrast to the consumerist culture that folks like Ron are talking about, where the fetishization of buying new books and reading new material becomes the focus of play.

    Under a paradigm like that, imagine one of these guys coming onto RPG.net and looking over the discussion -- with the endless "what is new and cool" and "let us compare detailed knowledge of the Exalted setting books" or, for that matter, similar discussions here in which we're all eager to see the hot new indy release. The very fact that we have such a focus on consuming and aquiring new games, where many of them have not aquired a new game book in over 25 years, drives an instant wedge into communications.

    For the record, this isn't saying that RPG.net, or here, is bad for talking the way we talk. I'm not lionizing or condeming either group -- just pointing out one of the divisions of POV that can act as a barrier between groups that are already playing RPGs.

    Brand, and to a lesser but noteable extent Jason, laid a fairly interesting smackdown on my sorry posterior, hehe.

    I should also say I identify with the working class. That's me, Mr. Working Class, hehe. But my experience outside of where I grew up (being Las Vegas) was that most gamers were not working class, but college educated white people from professional backgrounds. This idea was strengthened by occasional threads on places like RPG.net where folks would go out of their way to point out how many Ph.Ds and engineers they worked with and how cool and hip their groups are. So, both by my own experience (which is, naturally, limited -- but I've belonged to gaming groups in Nevada, California, South Carolina and Maine and only in Nevada would I typify the group as "working class") and what I've seen on the Net (NOT representative of the general population) did seem to suggest that most gamers are white and middle class.

    That there is at least anecdotal evidence to suggest a larger working class presence that exists than one could see from the Internet and hanging out in game stores is interesting. I also find it plausible. I know too many of my working class friends who refuse to let go of ANYTHING until it is ABSOLUTELY BROKEN and are driving around in '63 GMCs as a result of this (salutory, IMO) attitude. I believe a similar "if it isn't broke, don't fix it" attitude might go to gaming. They LIKE blue box D&D. Why bother changing?

    But it changes, then, I think to whom we are reaching out and how we reach out to them. Or at least complicates it a little bit -- because not only can we reach out to people who haven't ever gamed but we can also reach out to those gamers who game in solitude and towards whom there might be a class and/or racial divide. I suspect a lot could be learned from them! I mean, to talk to people who have been gaming for years and years and still keep doing it -- how they do it, why they do it, etc., strikes me as being something absolutely fascinating to learn (and raises the issue, I think, of all this online gaming theory -- to whom, really, is it all applicable if an unknown number of gamers game in relative obscurity).

    Still, I doubt that making a game about regular people will be the thing to bridge the divide of class, race or gender -- like with gender, I think the way change will happen is individual outreach and organization. Both individually and institutionally, if people actually want to bring in these out groups, we'll have to find and encourage people to play. That's my analysis, anyway. Right now, I'm not convinced the problem is with the rules or the setting or the characters -- but merely that gamers are, perhaps, divided along classist lines.

    I also think that class is the undiscussed elephant in many American conversations. It is part of our national mythology that the US has no classes, even tho' the US has the greatest divide between the rich and poor of any industrialized nation (and a greater wealth divide than a number of developing nations). But we don't talk about class very often, and if a person does they're often attacked for bringing it up. I'm not sure this is the proper forum to discuss it in more detail, so I won't, hehe.
