\n It so basic, that I don't know if it a good question to ask. But I am starting to think that I don't really know how to play or to enjoy freeform scenes or freeform sections of scenes.

By "freeform" I mean the part were we freely roleplay or narrate stuff.

Is there some techniques, that help enjoying more those scene or to get the best out of them?

I ask, because often I feel like I am left hanging in empty space, with no real technique or guideline when a game ask me to freely narrate or roleplay a scene.

For example:

Remember Tomorrow: My character is car racing a other character, before using the dice we freely narrate the scene, but it quickly felt kind of silly or awkward. We were narrating stuff, but we dint really knew how enjoy it or what to enjoy. Same thing happened with a gun fight, after describing some action it felt kind of awkward.

Trail of Cthulhu: A player play a quiet scene to refresh his character abilities pool, to do so his character visit the zoo with her daughter. We all liked the idea of the scene, but even when keeping it short, we din't really knew what to look for to enjoy it. We narrated some stuff, felt unsure, and closed the scene. But it felt like a letdown, like we missed something, and as the GM I noticed that I din't really knew how to play this scene beside just describing/narrating stuff.


I know it kind of "natural" to know how to do this, like we take for granted that everyone know how to do to it. I agree, but how do we get good or better at freely narrating scenes? What should we look for while we freely narrate? Is there some fun things to do? Interesting way to interact? Techniques to employ, etc.?

(any good game text, articles, post, etc discussing this?)

    \n - Keep the scenes short and to the point. If you are out of ideas, bring them to an end.
    - Work out the purpose of the scene
    - Stay focussed on that purpose
    - Build on what the others are contributing
    - Be mindful of the other PCs in that scene. There may be clues in their owne issues and themes that you can riff off.
    - Use motifs from other scenes and build on them where you can.
    - Engage the other characters. Don't just sit back and narrate - interact.

    \nJust wanted to stress and reiterate these two (though those are all good suggestions.) Just because a scene is freeform doesn't mean it is purposeless -- a scene can seem like a good idea, but you have to figure out what it's 'about' or it will likely fall flat.

    So you have an idea for a scene where the PC visits the zoo with her daughter -- that's a great start, but it's going to help a lot to think it out a little further in terms of what this scene is going to contribute to the fiction, to the larger story. Is the scene going to help us learn more about the PC? Is it going to set up the daughter as a vulnerable, potential future victim? Is it going to provide contrast to the intensity of other scenes, or is it going to include lots of foreshadowing and ominous bits to attempt to build on that intensity?

    If you have a strong idea of the function of the scene in terms of the larger story/fictional world, that tends to make it a lot easier to figure out what to focus on once you're actually playing out the scene.

    I am actually pretty terrible at this, myself, due in part to a long history of playing in online games where 95% of scenes were basically pointless -- exercises in time-filling and showy writing. But if you can't figure out beforehand what sort of things a scene is going to reveal about your characters, or the world, or the story, it might not be a good idea for a scene.

    All of the above advice is actually pretty good. I'll toss in something that may be of use.

    Someone needs to be a Trouble Maker, even in a peaceful scene meant to flesh out a character.

    Despite some interpretations of the Czege Principle, it really can be the player of the main character in the scene. Sometimes that's exactly the best person to be the Trouble Maker.

    A Trouble Maker can introduce low intensity/low stakes conflicts. Sideline players should still be able to offer suggestions on trouble for main characters in a scene, and leave it up to the main players in the scene to take up or ignore or bring into play however they wish.

    The other thing I'd suggest is just plain having other players play up characters in the scene. A quiet, low conflict stakes scene is an opportunity to play up and portray your character even more fully, so do it.

    \n One word: Conflict. I third the advice that having a clear sense of purpose makes a scene stronger. In addition, ideally, all scenes should revolve around conflicts. You know the usual rundown--conflicts can be external or internal, interpersonal or collective or against nature, physical or emotional or what have you.

    If a scene sags, that's most likely because it doesn't have conflict or the conflict is uninteresting. For best results, the conflict should be ones that push characters that the audience (players) care about into a meaningful choice. So if there's a) no conflict, b) the characters are uninteresting, c) there is no build-up to a choice that matters to the character or story, the scene will feel unsatisfying from a dramatic point of view.

    It's kind of a tall order for scenes in a roleplaying game to fulfill these elements every single time. Heck, professionally written stories don't always hit the mark. But having meaningful conflict in every scene is a good goal to strive for, and is pretty much guaranteed to make the game stronger as a story.

    So, take car race: Who's racing? Do we know and care about the characters? What's the relationship between the racers? What's at stake, is it something one or more of the characters care about--ego, status, money, women, safety, life? Is there a meaningful choice to be made, character-wise or plot-wise? For instance, will the character have to choose between victory and the possibility of risking life and limb? Is there something else the character could be doing, such as date or a rescue, and which is more important to him, that or winning the race? What does the choice tell us about the character?

    Taking your daughter to the zoo: I love this kind of scene because it can be such a gold mine. What are the character and daughter like? What is their relationship like? What is the relationship between the character and the other parent, if there is one? What's the conflict--divorce, marital troubles, financial troubles, illness on the daughter's part, the father's (assuming male) own ambivalence about fatherhood? What's at stake with this visit--is the character trying to be a good father, carving out some time to talk to his daughter alone, preparing to leave her for a while, does he want to distract her from her difficulties, is he trying to quiet the treacherous inner voice that resents his daughter's existence? What choice will he have to make--will he be called away to a job or will some other reason to terminate the time with his daughter present itself? Will he run into his current lover, encounter people whom he doesn't want his daughter to see, will he have to reveal his real identity when the daughter is kidnapped? What will the choice reveal about him?

    From a dramatic point of view good scenes start with good characters, by which I mean believable and textured fictional people who want something that they are willing to work to get. Then it's just a matter of dropping them into situations where they have to make choices that matter.

    I'm in a campaign that usually starts in this crappy townhouse courtyard, the PCs tend to just chat and catch up in character a bit. That's my favorite part, even though my character is something of a black sheet. Actually, that's exactly why - butting heads in a low-stakes way can be really fun.

    \n

    A barbarian hollering and blowing cover in a dungeon can be aggravating to the other party members, but a barbarian hollering because no one will do his laundry and he doesn't know how... is hilarious.

    \n

    So in those scenes where you're just kvetching, know how your character reacts to not getting what they want, and find ways to make that fun for other people. You don't have to play a buffoon - aggrieved, sardonic, aggressive, shy... these are things from life. Find ways to keep your character's schtick intact and give it expression.

    \n

    Put another way, people who get told that all the laundry machines are full rarely resort to death threats.

    \n Personally, I don't worry about manufacturing conflict or incorporating goals or issues from elsewhere in these kinds of scenes. Just make sure that the characters involved have some kind of strong viewpoint, any viewpoint, and commit to playing everything through that. Have them stick to their shit, pay attention to what's going on around them and react strongly to it, and you'll be fine. If something interesting comes up, that's great, but if not, don't sweat it, call the scene, and move on.

    \n Whenever we narrate freeform scenes they are about explaining things that happened on other parts of the game. Say, in the middle of a combat, the paladin rolls a crtical hit. After the battle, he can narrate how at that moment he had an ephiphany and heard the voice oh his god calling to him, and saw where he had to strike. Or the technician rolls a fumble and later he narrates how he realizes his tools were totally uncalibrated, so now all that happened during the combat makes sense. Making sense out of the dice outcomes isn't only the work of the GM, you can also help a lot there, as long as you don't interrupt the flow of the story or completely change its direction.

    Freeform scenes can also be used to prepare your character for the challenges ahead, either in a physical or mental way. Like narrating how your barbarian is trying to keep his cool before the battle and starts to argue about religion with the priest of the party to keep his mind away from the upcoming battle. Again, it's something that doesn't change the direction of the story, that's something that -at least in traditional games- is left to the GM, the mechanics of the game or the group of players.

    Now, if the freeform scenes are meant for having the player come up with a chamge in the story, that's where te term "collaborative" comes in play. It means all the players are helping each other to make a story. You can't thus go and play safe avoiding conflict and expect everybody think that's fun because you're comfortable with it. These games are most of time about getting into trouble and then getting out of it or burning your character in the flames of glory. But then again that's when it's all about the story, not about the characters survival, but about roleplaying the attitude of your character towards the conflict and everything that it`s at risk.

    I agree with Scott, if the player avoids conflict, it's the job of the GM and the rest of the players to bring it back somehow, if the mechanics of the game don't force it back.

    \nI think there's at least two main options here: focusing on the craft, or focusing on the fiction.

    By focusing on the craft, I mean that one thing you could do as a player is try and push yourself to do a really good job at something, like: this scene I'm going to talk in character and practice my accent; or this scene I'm going to try and really demonstrate my character's emotions through acting and description; or this scene I'm going to focus on adding lots and lots of detailed description about the scenery; or I always seem to rush through scenes, so this scene I'm going to focus on slow, deliberate pacing. Or on really driving towards conflict, or focus on pushing other characters, or really on any single thing that you want to get 'better' at when it comes to playing scenes in general.

    There's lots of crossover with the second thing, focusing on the fiction, because of course the fiction is generally the medium in which you are practicing your craft as a player, and your contributions to the fiction are ultimately governed by the limits of your craft. But, the main distinction in focusing on the fiction is it's a lot more like the AW mode of asking questions: instead of picking something you want to get better at, as a player, you pick something you want to know more about in the fiction. So like: in this scene I'm really going to try and develop details about Victorian class structure; or in this scene I'm going to find out what this PC really thinks about her daughter; or in this scene I'm going to highlight my character's personal beliefs about God; or in this scene I'm going to try and really emphasize how poor most people are; or in this scene I'm going to try and figure out why my PC hates his brother so much. Basically, pick something to be curious about, or pick something you 'know about' as a player but want to see come into the fiction more.

    I think just picking one of these sort of focuses per scene can really be enough to keep it interesting for you -- and assuming you pick one with the rest of the group in mind, it should also make the scene more interesting for everyone. If everyone in the scene is driving towards something then that's going to help build momentum and enjoyment on the immediate, what-is-happening-in-our-play level, regardless of whether the scene was perfectly framed or not (and as you say, it sounds like that part is not a problem.)

    I mean, when there's no mechanics at work in a scene then it's pretty clear there's only two broad categories of things that are going to keep things interesting: the players and the fiction. So if you try and make yourself a better player, or you try and make a better fiction, that's going to make a better scene -- and by picking some specific version of 'better' that you want, it will help keep you focused on that goal instead of getting overwhelmed by all the possibilities.

    \n This phrase has become my best friend: "Tell me what that looks like." Oddly, when you put something in *visual* terms it becomes much clearer what you're NOT communicating.

    "I take my daughter to the zoo." "Tell me what that looks like."
    "I convince him he needs to go into Rehab." "Tell me what that looks like."

    See how it works for even talking scenes? It takes the pressure off improv-ing dialogue but still pushes the group to generate more engaging details.

    \nI have to agree -- interaction is where the fun is for me. I do something, you react to it, I react to your reaction, and now we're sharing a spotlight and having a great time. Plus, most of the time, we're both in-character for it, which is always a blast.

    Narration, on the other hand, isn't particularly fun. I know what I'm going to say and all I get to do is say it while other people watch. It's a spotlight just on me, no surprises for me. Booo-ring. I don't even really get to be in-character, which drops all the fun performance aspects of doing a soliloquy.


    I think it's narration, more than anything else, that made me not really enjoy Primetime Adventures when our group played it. It always seemed like scenes went from the really good part where things were happening and we were all having fun, eventually leading to an interesting conflict that we were all interested in seeing...and then someone would get stuck with having to narrate the conclusion to it, which even at its very best was never as much fun as the interaction that preceded it. And if I was the one who had to do the narration, then I didn't even get to enjoy seeing a good narrative conclusion as an audience. Honestly, it drove me away from participating in conflicts in that game entirely, just so I couldn't get roped into doing that.
