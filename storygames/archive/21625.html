    \n It's a perpetuated GMing technique issue that gets stamped on the players. Players get taught by their gaming experience that the GM is massively more responsive to concrete skill activation than he is to any other kinds of input you might offer at the game table. This is particularly so for the latter-age universal-resolution trad games with long skill lists and hardly any other mechanical buttons. (BRP and the like, I imagine you know what I mean.)

    Even in a completely GM-controlled railroad game this phenomenon persists in player perspective: a non-mechanical input gains you a brief nod from the GM, while hitting one of the magic phrasings that indicate skill activation gains full mechanical attention, such as it is: the GM stops what they're doing, declares the appropriate skill check procedure, asks you to roll the dice, lets you roll, looks at the result and pronounces judgement on your action with a brief description of what happens in the fiction as the result of what you did. That's much more interaction and "game-play" than what you get by saying stuff that doesn't trigger a skill check. They may even feel pressured to change the plot course due to the moral impact of your good dice roll, which is something that simply can never happen if you're not pressing mechanical buttons.

    The negative reinforcement might be even more important in training players out of creativity: if you do something that both demands an answer and is not a skill check, what are the chances that this results in a positive interaction rather than a debate or judgement against your initiative? Not very good at all if you're at a traditional table. The GM is implicitly unwelcoming of creative initiative, as that endangers their prepped plot - naturally any creativity players express will be targeted towards escaping the plot, that's just how character-player agency works. In my experience a trad GM ends up damping down on initiative much more than encouraging it, simply because so few of the creative inputs the other players bring "fit" into their scheme. When I'm being a player in one of these games, I'm constantly faced by a mild disapproval for nearly every type of thing I may say; the GMs tolerate it because they can't muster the authority to tell the players to shut up, but it's clear that they would prefer us to color inside the lines instead of being creative.

    \n Well, harm... I guess so, from a certain perspective. But that's cultural history, isn't it? People try to shape the things they do to the perceived desirable ends, and they don't know in advance how it's going to end. If I was gaming in 1981, I certainly couldn't have predicted that neither universal skill systems nor Dragonlance style adventure paths would lead to the gaming nirvana that elevates player skill to unforeseen heights and enables equal artistic relations between GMs and players. I believe that the people at the time expected that this would be the road to follow, they didn't wish to get stuck in this pothole of eternal infantilization where players only grow more passive as the GMs ramp up the literary ambition of their game prep. I know I certainly didn't when I was trained into this tradition during the '90s - we were trying to game in an egalitarian and literarily sophisticated way, in hindsight it just happens to be the case that these techniques were not the way to get there.

    It's harm in the same way that corsetry was and is harmful to humans: there are clear critical perspectives, but there is also much good reason to understand and empathize with people who supported corset culture in its heyday, or choose to wear corsets today. Cultural history in action.

    But yeah, I definitely agree that universal skill resolution did much to establish the "streamlined" system chassis that would later become the go-to solution for any GM who truly preferred to be in control. A game with simpler mechanical touch-points and more isolated task resolution is much closer to the ideal monadic existence than something messy like old D&D, with all sorts of inconvenient accountabilities between the dice and the outcomes. I don't think that Fudge, for example, has more than one rule that incontrovertibly demands a specific thing of the fiction in response to the dice. Nothing like e.g. the monster reaction roll in D&D, which simply cannot be circumvented without hiding the roll or dropping the rule.

    \n If I were to mechanize skill buttons to encourage fiction-first play (watch me knit different threads together here, ha!), I would say that each skill must be "charged up" before using it. You charge it up by tying it to a detail in the setting.

    For example:

    GM: You come across four bodies on the road. They're ripped to pieces, guts everywhere, not just dead but spread out all over the place. It's horrible.

    Player: I circle around the mess, looking for bloody tracks or even droplets of blood leading away from the site.

    GM nods to the player, indicating acceptance of that charge. Maybe says, "You start tracking the blood trail," if it's not clear what the intent was.

    Player makes a tracking skill check now.

    \n I discussed this with another designer and ended up with something interesting, though still needs testing. Basically, skills and items in my design will give no bonuses for the roll, they will just enable the character to do those things.

    Except if these have a condition that must be fullfiled in order to use them. Then, depending on the condition, these will give from 1 to 3 bonus dice. The player rolls them all and keep the best. Of course, the more skills they want to use at the same time, the higher the difficulty, but they can still pile up some together to get all the bonus dice they want.

    It creates different interesting side effects: There's the Gamble, as players wil try to balance the risk of facing a higher difficulty against getting a bit more sure about the result, though still without getting a deterministic one. Things can be slowed down to action resolution or accelerated to cover the player's intention, since a few more things can be accomplished through a single roll if the player wants to risk it. Players will get a bonus by roleplaying around their character limitations. The more they do this, the more bonus dice they get after all. I get to keep the feeling of mixing several items and skills that I was going for in this design without breaking the game.

    And of course, players will have to argument/explain/describe more about their choices and actions to make the whole deal make sense, or risk giving the GM ground to make a move against them. Everything becomes a bit more RP and at the same time, everyone wins with it. Still have to see it at the table to find out what else is needed and what gets lost though. Hope it works as planned though.

    \n An assortment of thoughts on the matter.

    Description before intent. Player says, "I want to roll Investigate to find evidence of corruption." No. Bad. Player says, "I search the office for evidence of corruption." No. Not good enough. Player says, "I am checking his desk drawers for evidence of corruption." Yes. Very good, player. Excellent idea. Checking the desk drawers lets you find evidence if it's there. What if evidence is in the mattress? Now the skill roll comes into play. Roll Investigate. "You don't find any evidence in the desk drawers, but when you're done, you're pacing around the room wondering where this evidence might be. You're sure this guy's dirty. It has to be somewhere. You're looking around and you notice the envelope peeking out under the mattress corner."

    Character sheet layout. Players stuck looking down at their character sheets all session? Don't let them. One side has their skill ratings on it. The other side has their character. The who and what and why that makes the character who he is. Aspects, keywords, traits, whatever-you-haves. A little spot for character portrait. Unless the GM asks for a roll, you may not turn your character sheet over. It's in the rules, right here. Page 32, in big, bold, beautiful red letters.

    Pay the toll. If the players aren't sure what to do, they can say, "I want to roll X to do Y," and they can. But. There's a price to pay. A toll to cross that bridge. Maybe the GM makes a hard move, or maybe you pay him a Fate point, or maybe you make whatever skill roll you want to try a bit harder.

    \nMy pal wanted a "sneak attack" skill, which for me would usually meant that 1)you either roleplay or position to gain surprise or rolled some skill (stealth, feint, etc) and then make an attack which had some extra punch. Here we could have a sneak attack skill with the condition "gain surprise" needed to earn the bonus die. Player can mix it with his stealth skill, which wouldn't yield bonuses unless it had a condition on itself. Like "only when invisibility spell is activated". The player has to focus on two things, which rises the difficulty of the roll; but if he roleplays in order to fulfill this condition too, he can get another bonus die.

    However what we get in the end is a PC doing some things in preparation for that roll that could get him in trouble... if the roll fails. Then it would be easier to narrate "somebody spotted you when you tried to do magic and yelled "Kill the mage first!", as the player has established all these risks in the fiction himself.

    We went through the magic system easily with this too. We listed several types of magic and came up with 5 or 6 conditions needed to power up the magic. It doesn't mean you need to take them but if you take a few (we put a cap of 3 condicions) you get that amount of bonus dice to the roll. The conditions were simply Somatic Casting, Vocal and Material. We added "weak against opposite magic" for elemental and necromantic vs divine, and "weak when machines are present". Can't remember the other one right now, but you get the idea.

    \n Please don\u2019t get hung up on the specifics of the scenario I laid out. The player may as well have said, \u201cI sneak up on the orc,\u201d or \u201cI roll to remember my way out of the dungeon.\u201d The purpose is to demand a first person narrative of how the character accomplishes something.

    Obviously if you\u2019re running a mystery game don\u2019t rely on a skill test to provide vital clues. It also depends on if you want skills to grant narrative authority. Can the Investigate skill create evidence of corruption ex nihilo? How far does this authority extend? Alternatively: how much does the GM have to prep? Does he have to note whether every guard is asleep, alert, or awake-but-inattentive? And what factors does the die roll represent? Does a high roll on an Investigate check indicate that the sheriff left incriminating evidence in plain sight because he was called on an emergency, or does it indicate that the PC is a sleuth and knew where the sheriff would hide the evidence, or does it indicate a bout of dumb luck?     \n Interesting discussion here.

    I'm more or less with David and Eero on this topic:

    Players need a sense that doing clever fictional things is effective and rewarded, and making abilities both reliable and fictionally relevant is a great way to do that.

    "Roll Stealth to go unseen" is likely to lead to "button pushing" play. "You can disappear from any distracted person's immediate awareness by taking the shape of a nearby shadow" is often better, because it gets us talking about whether an NPC is distracted and describing the surrounding environment. The player knows to expect a reliable outcome, so they have strong incentive to follow through on those things - you end up with a more interesting dialogue between the player and GM.

    As Eero says, the key is largely in how the group reacts to various inputs, as that conditions players to act on a certain way over time.

    I see (even in this thread) lots of discussion of techniques to "trick" the players into "playing right", but, like Eero (if I'm reading him correctly), I think the group dynamics are really at the root here; players generally *want* to play, and if they're not, there's probably a good reason.

    You can see this in effect instantly with a generous GM and an unforgiving ruleset (like old-school D&D, for example). It doesn't take long to learn that, say, going to the dice gives you a 1 in 6 chance (and no way to improve that), but trying some more inventive approach gets you results and accolades.

    \n The interesting question, for me, in B/X is whether the roll is the default procedure or a fallback procedure.

    So, for instance, if someone is hiding in a good spot, good circumstances - a place they probably wouldn't be seen or found, let's say - do we roll, or not?

    In one school of thought, the roll is there for when that happens, so we've got to make with the dice. In other words, you CANNOT hide without succeeding at the roll - the odds of success are your maximum odds of success.

    "Ok, that's a good hiding place, but you have to roll to make sure you can pull it off."

    In another school, if your fictional positioning is strong, we follow that. If it's unclear or uncertain, then the player gets the roll anyway, kind of like a saving throw. In this view, assuming the action is plausible at all, your odds of success are your minimum odds of success.

    "Ok, that's not a very good hiding place, so you have to roll to see if you manage somehow, despite the difficulties."

    Those make for two very different games. Given how brutal odds are in B/X, I've seen a lot of people go with the latter interpretation.
