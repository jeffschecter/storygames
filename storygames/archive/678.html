    Waht are the things we can do to help people to go with the obvious. My first thougth is to limit the amount of time people get to decide. But that pressure sometimes makes people choke, which is not what we want.
    For me, this thing about being obvious is incredibly useful in roleplaying.

    The way I see this working is: when deciding what your character does, you should do the thing that seems really obvious. That thing that you think is obvious will seem incredibly creative to everyone else.

    For example, let's say we're playing My Life With Master. I'm a gravedigger charater and my master has told me to kill an old man. It seems to be incredibly obvious that I should entice him into the graveyard, creep up behind him with a shovel and whack him into an open grave.

    But that's actually quite a creative idea. Other people reading this will have different "obvious" ideas and, to me, their ideas will probably seem verycreative.

    Graham

    [Interesting wanky improv point, mainly for Jason. There's a major difference between the Johnstone style of doing improv and the Truth In Comedy / Chicago style.

    The Johnstone style encourages questions that provoke the other player to answer (such as "What's the flaw in the enemy's plan?"), on the grounds they force the other player to answer, and that makes them spontaneous.

    The Chicago style discourages questions, on the grounds that they make the other player do the work.]
