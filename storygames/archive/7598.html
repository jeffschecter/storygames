    \n The average is the same (0). 4F and 8F both produce a bell curve around zero.

    4F has a range of -4 to +4. 8F has a range of -8 to +8. However, the extremes on 8F are pretty unlikely (152 in 1,000,000 or 1/100 of a percent).

    There are definitely differences between the likelihood of you getting, say, +1 on 4F vs. 8F. I can run the numbers through Troll in a day or three if you want probability tables.

    \n ObThread:

    So here's another thing you can do with Fudge dice. When you start a conflict, declare three good things that could happen and three bad things. Rank them in order of desirability. Roll 3F. Don't "net" the dice. Count pluses and minuses separately. Ignore zeros for now.

    +++ means you get the most desirable good thing on your list.
    ++ means you get the second most desirable good thing
    + means you get the third most desirable good thing

    - means you get the least undesirable bad thing on your list
    -- means you get the second least undesirable bad thing
    --- means you get the most undesirable bad thing

    So you could roll -0+ and get the third most desirable good thing and the least undesirable bad thing on your list, at the same time!

    \n I use Fudge dice when I run Christmas Ninjas.

    In that game it's all about matching 3-sided dice. The outcome of the roll is the lowest match the Ninja's roll makes versus the GM. So if the Ninja rolls ++ - and the GM rolls a - ++ the match is a one, and the Ninja suceeds. But if the GM rolls 0 ++, the lowest match is a zero (as the Ninja rolled no 0). So the specific value of the dice don't matter, only that they arrive at 3 different ones.

    Christmas Ninjas is why I have three sets of Fudge dice, because I often end up needing all of them for a 6 player game.
