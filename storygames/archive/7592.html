    \n They're basically brief, well-written modules. I have several of them and they are excellent. The SAS itself is not so interesting. Basically it's a slightly more detailed version of the "For 3-5th level!" sticker on the front of D&D modules - it tells you approximately what level/proportion of mental, social and physical challenges the characters will face in each particular segment of the game. That is the root of something really cool (what if a GM only had a certain number of dots to spread around into that system?) but it doesn't really go anywhere special.

    The quality of the modules is uniformly good.

    \n I bought a couple of them, just to see what WW's system is. They are an interesting experiment in trying to figure out how to write modules for WW games, which is much harder than writing them for D&D. The problem with modules for WW is that, unlike d20 fighty stuff or dungeon crawls, you can't just throw down a bunch of monster stats and a map. In the end, though, I don't find the SAS stuff to be really satisfying, though clearly JD disagrees.

    Basically, the way they work is, instead of a dungeon with branching paths or whatever, you have a series of locations for scenes to take place in, with various NPCs and such in each location for the characters to interact with. Since you won't always be fighting these NPCs, each location also has mechanical guidelines for other major important things you might want to do in that location/scene, like investigation rolls or trying to get information out of someone or using magic powers to do various things etc. Each roll critical to the scenario is given a difficulty and some special guidelines. The locations are linked together by an overall situation. Sometimes there's an obvious order in which to progress from one to another, but sometimes the players have more freedom in which order to play out different scenes or go to different locations. Sometimes there's even mechanical or story-based consequences for not doing certain things or taking too long.

    Honestly, I would prefer WW to move more towards the kinds of situation generators that you see, for example, in the way towns are constructed in Dogs in the Vineyard, showing people how to construct interesting, open-ended situations instead of providing very specific modules and scenarios. Teach a man to fish, etc. But, as a way of adapting the traditional D&D module structure to WW, I don't think they're a bad attempt at all. They are certainly much more helpful than previous attempts. Still, I think if I was attempting to work off a module, I'd read it, grab some of the parts that look especially cool, and then just make the rest up off the top of my head. Maybe that's what WW expects GMs to do, since that's frequently the way they expact folks to approach their mechanics. I'm not sure.
