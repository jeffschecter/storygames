    \nHow not to lose your voice at a convention

    Brief summary: Drink water (starting early in the morning), don't shout, avoid coffee, warm up your voice by humming, stop talking if you feel a tickle at the back of the throat.

    Things I agree with from above: Play collaboratively (it means you talk less), play somewhere quiet (shouting over background noise can seriously damage your voice).

    Be very, very careful with lozenges. Some of them numb the throat, which means you still damage your voice, but don't feel the damage. You shouldn't really need them.

    \n I actually sell/install voice amplification systems, recording equipment and commercial sound systems. A decent voice amplification system can be as cheap or as expensive as you like, but when you start spending money, what you get goes up exponentially.

    My under $200 solution would be a commercial headset mic and a pair of Bose Companion 2 series computer speakers. You'll likely also need an adaptor to make your headset work with the RCA connection on the back of the powered speakers. If you don't have access to a distributor, Bestbuy and the Apple Store often run promotions on the Bose speakers.

    A real issue is that you might be projecting your voice beyond your table and disrupting other participants. If I took a $1000+ presentation system to gaming con and used it at its normal settings, people would only be able to hear me. Even with the volume lowered, the clarity would attract everyone's ear without fail. Companies like TOA and Bose have some pretty advanced sound wave bending technology that makes even a quiet whisper fill an area. I'm constantly replacing 300 watt speaker systems with tiny 15 watt speakers because of this technology.

    I really like the idea of a mixing board and sufficient head phone splitters to get everyone hearing you. I would also highly recommend getting enough 3.5mm headphone extension cords and taping them under the table that everyone has a handy place to plug in. You could also run XLR (or 3.5mm if you're going non commercial) cables to mic all the players as well.

    Another option would be to get a laptop with an extra usb hub and load up on usb sound devices and then use software to do the mixing. There are some excellent and cheap single ear headphone/mic combination headsets. If you already have a laptop, this would be the cheapest solution by far. You need a cheap USB hub, a USB sound device for each person and a combination headset for each person. You may also need USB extension cords.

    If anyone wants any help with designing such a system let me know. However, I won't be able to sell any equipment online as my distribution agreements exclude online sales.

    \n To get a USB hub with a bunch of audio devices working for real time playback, you're going to have to overcome a huge hurdle. Software. I use Linux to make it work. ASIO4ALL *may* get it working for Windows and I couldn't tell you what to do for a Mac. There's a ghetto work around using multiple headphone splitters and headphone extension cables to get around this problem.
    \n
    \nThe main issue with usb sound devices is them cutting out if you have anything but the latest drivers. So make sure to update.
    \n
    \nHubs will be a big issue. I have one where the devices on it cut out every now and again. You may need to spend a bit more here to get a quality hub.
    \n
    \nAnd this bring us to the biggest issue of all. Software. For some reason, Microsoft removed the feature that previous versions of windows had to control which device which program outputs to and now Windows 7 just plays to a single default sound device. I use Linux to make this all work.
    \n
    \nAlso, getting Audacity to record from multiple microphones is a bit of an arcane process. With Windows 7, the locking down of audio channels makes it impossible. Adobe Audition records from multiple devices.
    \n
    \nThe challenge will be taking the microphone input from all inputs, mixing them into one output and then sending that output to ALL of the headphone outputs of the USB devices.
    \n
    \nWith Linux, you have control over everything but the learning curve can be pretty staggering. Since I'm dealing primarily with commercial applications, we usually set up a dedicated Linux computer for stuff like this. And most of the time we'd just go with a mixing board and be done with it.
    \n
    \nIf you just want to record everything or are willing to splice everyone's headphones into a single jack, you can make it work on Windows.
    \n
    \nYou can probably also make it work with the ability to listen to microphones as they are playing through your default sound device.
    \n

    \n So here's how you do it in Windows 7:

    Good USB hub --> USB sound devices --> microphones
    Headphone jack on computer--> splitters --> headphones
    Right click on volume icon in task bar (bottom right)
    Choose "Recording devices"
    Choose each microphone and hit "properties"
    Go to the "Listen" tab and enable listening
    Tell it to output to whatever device is your headphone jack on the computer.
    Adjust the boost and levels for each microphone and your output volume

    If you want to record, get something that can record a sound output device like Audacity (free, just choose your main output as the microphone source) Messer (free but really old) or Total Recorder (costs money)

    So there we go, a cheap solution that will work on a Windows 7 computer.
