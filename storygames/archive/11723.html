\n Years and years ago, kids, there was a hot, new subgenre of science fiction that all the cool boys and girls were interested in. They called this subgenre cyberpunk and two major games came out of it: Cyberpunk and Shadowrun. They were both cool in their own way, though Shadowrun definitely had a more successful run that continues to this day.

Caught up in the writing of William Gibson, George Alec Effinger and Walter Jon Williams (among others), I was totally into cyberpunk as a literary movement and as a gaming environment. I ran both Cyberpunk and Shadowrun, the latter for several years. But both games had the same problem: a solitary figure that somehow had to be incorporated into a group-play situation. No, he wasn't the decker, though that "class" presented problems all his own. Rather, he was the rockerboy, and I could never make him work.

Anyway, enough with the "once upon a time" stuff. Let's talk rockers.

The biggest problem faced by the GM when trying to incorporate a decker into a cyberpunk group is that while he was doing his thing everybody else sort of got to hang around twiddling their thumbs. Similarly, in order to have a rockerboy doing rocker stuff, the adventuring had to come to a halt in order to accommodate the music.

In both my Cyberpunk and Shadowrun games I had players that wanted to play rocker-types. Which is cool because the basic idea behind the rockerboy type is a good one. Who wouldn't want to play a cool, sexy musician type with groupies and the whole thing? That's a fantasy just like being a hardwired street samurai. The problem of integration kept coming up, though, and I was repeatedly faced with the not-so-great options of either ignoring the musical aspect of the character in favor of treating him like any other adventuring type (albeit one with some useless skills) or having everyone else standing around cheering while the rockerboy played a blistering guitar solo. You see my conundrum.

Probably the easiest solution would be for everybody to have a role in the band. Sort of an ass-kicking bunch of musicians who supplement their income with a little shadowrunning on the side. But that has its problems when it comes to the players who don't want to spend skill points on things that don't contribute directly to kicking ass and taking names. The rockerboy archetype is attractive, but not to everyone.

I figure there are other, more nuanced ways to solve this problem so I'm curious to know how you \u2014 yes, you, the one reading this \u2014 would address the problem of rockerboy integration. Would it be from the story side or would it be a character-based solution like the one posited above? Have you actually had to deal with this problem in your own game? How did you resolve it? And so forth.

    \n Actually, I think the sample scenario in the Cyberpunk 2.0.2.0. book gave a fairly good example of how to integrate Rockers. IIRC, the characters needed to bust into an Arasaka building to rescue Johnny Silverhand's gf (Johnny was a pregen, but I think you were encouraged to sub in a PC Rocker if you wanted). So, Johnny plays a huge concert outside Arasaka HQ and uses Charismatic Leadership to cause a riot (yeah, the scenario is a little railroady - but that's a *different* problem) while the Nomad and Solo sneak in during the chaos.

    The general point is that, as a GM, you can work with Rockers so long as you provide them ways to contribute to the overall group goal. Yeah, there might be some cutting back and forth (that's the best way I had to handle Netrunners too - we used the more "realistic" Hardwired rules, so our net jockey mostly came along and hacked security terminals rather than spending a lot of time in VR), but it's not as if everyone is sitting back doing nothing.

    That said, I know back in the day when I played Cyberpunk we suffered a lot from not treating it as different enough from D&D. Specifically, I think a lot of the Roles suffered if you treated the characters as an adventuring party - Rockers and Medias just ended up being weird and hard to integrate, Nomads, Corporates, and Cops couldn't use their connections to full effect, etc. While "everyone is in the band" isn't a bad idea, either, I think the more general lesson is that to use Cyberpunk characters to full effect people need to come to some consensus about the nature of the group and tailor character concepts to fit it. If someone shows up with a Rocker and all your games are about corporate espionage runs, it's not going to work. Similar things apply if you're not actually playing Cyberpunk but trying to integrate Rockers with other character types, etc. But if you've got a group of players, one of whom wants to rock out on guitar all the time and the others who just want to kill stuff, you may just not have coherent preferences.

    Never played Shadowrun, though.

    \n Guys, the problems with rockers isn't that they're musicians. Playing music is just what they do when they're not shooting people. The problem is that the mainstays of cyberpunk games (deckers and solos/street samurai) don't do anything but shoot people or steal stuff. They're one-note character types, as presented on paper.

    The deal is, here's what you're doing and here's what you're risking and here's where your skills from one life can affect the other. Corporates as a character class? What, when they're not making 2x the same amount of money legally? Medics and techies as classes? These are the guys you go to before and after the job, not the guys you bring with you. Media? Oh yeah, that's a great idea. Broadcast my activities to the public.

    \n For Cyberpunk, one of my most beloved genres, I always go back to the source. Take what you read in the book (and the new one for that matter! the 203x version which is kinda cool with the updated info) with a grain of salt, and instead think of the movies and books you love and what the characters do in them.

    I think its a misconception that all cyberpunk characters do is fuck shit up with their uberhuge destructoid guns. I mean, that's definitely a fun part of the game. But those other characters definitely exist because they're a part of a really rich setting. In blade runner, there's alot of shoot outs, for example, but really its very film Noir in its psychology and detective fiction tropes. There's tons of downtime and relationship drama in Neuromancer, and the main character is horrible with weapons, so much so that it becomes almost a running joke. Johnny Neumonic is just an information runner who needs a badass to protect him... etc. I think its the weaknesses in cyberpunk characters that make them really interesting, and how vulnerable they are despite the amazing amounts of technology around them.

    Think of the performers that exist in other games and what they do when they're not performing, IE a bard character, a singing elf, a daeva musician. I think being a Rockerboy has the same dramatic elements in it as a character does with any other profession in an RPG. Find ways to tie it in! Maybe he's like Bob Marley, and wanted by the government. Maybe she's like Lady Gaga, and is a fashion icon who does radical performances to influence culture. Maybe its like Lestat in that Anne Rice book where he's giving out vampire secrets to the populace and the vampire population comes to kill him. Maybe rival musicians are out to get them.

    Really, the performance itself is always gonna take a back seat in RPGs, because for the most part, people don't pause the game to actually get out their guitar and larp it out, the same way people fade to black during romance scenes. Generally, you describe a line or two of the performance. Try to tap into the parts of the personality that reflect things related to the game.

    I dunno, that's how I would do it!

    \n I particularly like what Jared and Kira have said in this thread so far.

    As Jared and Brand pointed out, the rockerboy\u2014I'm speaking of rockerboys in general terms, not specifically the Cyberpunk game role; I have next to no experience with the Cyberpunk game itself, so I don't know how well it would actually support playing a rockerboy in this manner\u2014isn't really about being a musician, it's about communicating with others. Rockers are potent because they have connections, because they can make connections within the community, because they can rally the community spirit and get people working together, because they have the charisma and passion to lead the rebellion. One thing that still surprises and amuses me about music videos from the early '80s is the recurring theme of rock as rebellion against corporate/fascist oppression. There was a real strong sense, born from the folk movement and nurtured by the punks (hence "cyberpunk", naturally) that music could be used to rouse the people to challenge the status quo and make changes for the better. That's what the rocker role should be about. That's what keeps the group from being just another band of heartless mercenary shadowrunners.

    Rockers can also use their social and communication skills in many different situations: negotiating safe passage through gang territory (or even recruiting a gang for help), finding people with information the group needs, making contacts in the corporate world, maybe just bluffing the group past suspicious guards... As Kira and others indicated, a cyberpunk game should have combat but it should be pretty lethal, and finding ways to avoid violence but get what you need should be a common activity.

    A few years ago when my group played Prime Time Adventures, I pitched a series based on that concept of the rock band as underground rebels, but no one else was that interested. Reading this thread, I again really want to play that kind of game some time...
