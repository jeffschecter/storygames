    \n *D&D...Paladins have a moral code and must be a certain alignment. If they fail to follow it, they fall, losing they class powers. (Same goes for clerics and druids, too, to some degree.)

    *Vampire the Masquerade. Doing certain horrible stuff results in losing Humanity. Humanity and the actions that breach it are pre-determined.

    *Sorcerer is pretty much like that, too, except different levels of Humanity don't directly affect mechanical play, it's just a thematic gauge. Players collectively decide what Humanity means in their game.

    *Pendragon. Characters have values such as Chivalry that have some mechanical functions. I don't remember the exact specifics.

    *In Burning Wheel you can have Beliefs that are ethical and philosophical standpoints. Acting towards and against a Belief is reflected in the mechanics.

    *Keys as seen in TSoY and Lady Blackbird can function in a similar way.

    *Dogs in the Vineyard has that as the "fruitful void". The player always decides what is right or wrong for her character, and her character is constantly subjected to situations that require moral judgement, so play is all about that and the conflicts arising from those moral decisions have mechanical impact, but there are no direct mechanics for that per se.

    Uh, I could probably think of a bunch more.

    \n All of the White Wolf stuff has some variation of a Morality mechanic that generally works like this: Normal <-> [...] <-> Psychotic.

    D&D is the classic in my mind with it's alignment grid: Good - Evil ; Chaotic - Lawful.

    As for explicit player defined morals...

    Shock: has something called a fulcrum IIRC, where two terms are put into opposition. We talked a little about player-defined "dark side" count-down-clock as a tool for hacking Apocalypse World over at Barf Forth.

    Burning Wheel has Beliefs which could be closer to what you're looking for.

    \n Nobilis has moral restrictions that sort of function like Keys. When a flaw of any sort (including a moral restriction from doing stuff) restricts you from doing something, you get bonus Miracle Points. This means that broad restrictions are restricting, but get you lots of points, while minor inconveniences only come up occasionally and only get a few points.

    Nobilis also has a couple of other ways of mechanically addressing morality. Each faction has a moral code that they live by, which has mechanical effects.

    \n Smallville uses Virtues and Relationships as the core stats. So every single roll you make is done in relation to a virtue.

    It doesn't rate you on whether you act good or bad per se, but by framing every conflict in relation to virtues, it helps the game focus on "doing the right thing" as a theme. It also helps frame the perspectives of "bad guys" by helping to describe how they feel about the various virtues.

    \n The Rustbelt's got 'em. They're set up so that actions related to the morality-thingy in question prompt a choice from the player, and each choice has a mechanical consequence, but the authority over that choice is entirely in the hands of the player. It's part of the overall Psyche system, which is an abstracted simulation of human psychology. Basically it's a system for keeping characters emotionally and psychologically compelling, but without taking away the player's control.

    \n There is a whole game that's kind of a bolt-on morality mechanic for traditional RPGs, called "Power Kill". It's fascinating from a design perspective, because of its extreme postmodern-ness, and its position with respect to its "host" game. Playing it is hard... it has a valid point to make, about the sociopathic power fantasies that underwrite D&D and such, but it's kind of anvilicious about making it. I enjoyed it a lot, but your mileage may vary.

    kill puppies for satan keeps track of how much evil you've done, and how many people hate you (including your mother). They connect in an interesting way, and I'd like to actually play the game at some point to see it at work.

    \n KPFS is really a game of one-up-man-ship and moral chicken. It's a great game to teach you to bring the pain as a game via purely descriptive content. I found it fun at Cons. Way more fun than DitV but less satisfying.

    Honestly, it's not so much a game about mortality but a GM training exercise: Can you influence the players without mechanics? Can you turn their desires back on them? Where are the limits and 'fruitful voids' of morality play? I remember a con game where a dad and daughter team (say, 35ish and 13?) brought the nastiest shit but I stymied them with Gray's Anatomy account of a gun-shot wound to the knee. I also think it's where Vincent finds his voice. And fuck, that's important.

    \n As Gregor mentioned, I think of Pendragon as one of the archetypical games here. Characters position themselves on each of a set of sliding scales, and must make rolls to act against their dispositions. Whether they succeed or fail, their dispositions gradually change to reflect their actions. That's as best as I recall it, anyway - it's been a while. But it can produce some fun situations and nice character arcs.

    \n Albedo has a S.P.I. tracker, which can get busted down for doing inappropriate actions, and ratcheted up for fulfilling your duty. It's a military defined moral code, but your S.P.I. is affected by all your actions, and it affects what jobs you can get and sometimes what planets you can visit.

    Are you looking for mechanical consequences for the character (like, take the easy road now, and things get harder later) or mechanical consequences for the player (like the unspoken "you're being kind of a dick to the other people at the table, so here's some penalty to make things harder for you.")

    I've seen both, but I'm not sure which you're looking for.
