Authoring Characters, Interpersonal Connectedness and Dramatic Quality

\n
\n
\n
\n\nJan \n\n\n

\n\nSeptember 2009 \n edited September 2009 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n One line of thought in the discussion about Role-playing Characters vs. Authoring Characters circled roughly about the following argument: it's a little bit boring when a player is just authoring his character without advocating the character's interests. Why is it boring? Because a crucial dimension of the whole fun about RPGs is missing, namely the moments of personal revelation by one of the players which gives raise to a strong personal connection between the players. So one of the main advantages of role-playing a character seems to be basically an issue of the intensity in which the players connect to each other on a personal level. Role-playing the characters intensifies this kind of connectedness, whereas authoring the characters leads to a detachment between the players.

Firstly I would like to say that I think this line of thought conflates authoring a character with a detached way of participating in the mutual creation of a story. As long as someone is emotionally involved in the ongoing game and as long as his decisions reveal his emotional involvement it is perfectly possible to experience all the personal connectedness between the players that you can ask for.

But apart from the question whether authoring a character decreases the possibilities of a certain kind of personal closeness or not I am really puzzled by a further step in the aforementioned line of thought, namely that the fiction created by authoring the characters is less affecting than it would be if the characters are advocated. In my opinion the quality of a piece of fiction is independent of the relation it bears to its author. If someone creates a great moment while authoring his character I can really enjoy the fiction without my fun being spoiled by thoughts about the way he has created this moment. How could it be different? A cool moment in the fiction is completely encapsulated in the fiction. It does not matter what's going on in the real world (at the table).

In addition (coming back to the level of personal attachment between the players) I can feel really close to the player which came up with a beautiful idea. I think something like: \u201cYeah, great, we have a complete understanding of how to create a great enjoyable story.\u201d A player reveals a lot about himself through the ideas he puts forward for advancing the fiction.

So to summarize: The dramatic quality and the enjoyability of the fiction created during playing an RPG is not in any way directly dependent on an antagonism between the players that is fostered by advocating the characters instead of authoring them. Furthermore the way a player authors his character opens the road for an intense personal connectedness between him and the other players.

This said, I won't deny that introducing an antagonism between the players might be an additional source of emotional involvement and intensity. However, it has it's own costs as well, since as long as a player strictly advocates his character he might throw away great opportunities for making a breathtaking moment in the ongoing fiction.

[I'm not sure whether I should have posted this in the thread about Role-playing Characters vs. Authoring Characters. Of course I comment on a lot of the ideas that have been exchanged there. But I wanted to focus mainly on the effects that different ways of guiding a character have on the personal connectedness between the players and on the dramatic quality of the fiction \u2013 and since the discussion in the other thread has wandered to different places it seemed justifiable to start a new thread (I'm new here and not yet familiar with your policies of discussion, so please excuse me, if custom has demanded to post this as an answer there).]

    \n I'm not sure I completely understand all the dimensions of this debate, but it seems to me that there are some parallels to the divide in fiction writing between "organic" or "discovery" writers and "outline" writers. For the discovery writer, the idea of figuring out the story ahead of time robs them of interest in the story, because what gets them excited is the in-the-moment surprises of seeing how their characters would react to situations and how those things spin the story in new directions. The outliner, on the other hand, wants to make sure there's a good, engaging story happening for their characters to experience.

    \n As a counter point to the One True Way-ism point I'd like to point to Ron Edward's own Spione which I think address the dynamic I've been trying to get across perfectly.

    During a phase called Maneuvers the two players playing the Principles are specifically freed from having to advocate for their character's interests. They can contribute as much to the hardship of their own characters as they want. You're a KGB operative? It's totally kosher to narrate how a CIA agent just blew your cover. Similarly non-Principle players are free to help your guy out. They can totally say something like, "But the CIA agent decides to stay quiet about it for now."

    However! Once a conflict is established the game moves to different phase called Flashpoints. In Flashpoints, Principle players MUST narrate how the Principles advance their interests and non-Principles MUST narrate how external forces work against those interests.

    All anyone, has every been saying is that it's kind of boring when all the players are standing on one side of the fiction which is kind of an inflated version of the Czege Principle. Something, somewhere, in a conflict, must "stand up" for the interests of Party A and someone, somewhere must "stand up" for the interests of Party B.

    \n Robert,

    The GM advocates for the things that are not the PCs. This is how games like Sorcerer and Burning Wheel work. The way those games become about the characters is that we establish the PCs first. Most specifically we establish the things the PCs value and want. Then the GM simply establishes what all the NPCs want in ways that intersect with what the PCs want in interesting (not necessarily adversarial though it can be) ways. Then once that establishing is done, we hit the "Go" button and it's advocating all the way.

    However, I should point out that, that establishing phase can be returned to, even on scene-by-scene basis. This can get quite chatty and collaborative. Advocacy only kicks in once we've hit the "Go" button. Okay, now that we understand what the here-and-now situation is what do the characters *do*, here-and-now. Is that a conflict? If yes.. what do the *characters* want out of the conflict? Okay, roll dice to see if they get it.

    That's why I'm not very keen on Pauls original word "authoring." Because to me, this IS authoring the character. We've made giving the player's the opportunity to author their character's actions and decisions over the things those characters value the primary activity of play.

    The more important distinction is this. The above, to me, is "authoring the character." The thing Paul was talking about is more accurately described as "authoring at the players." In the former the player is merely demonstrating the thematic energy of the character and reserving emotional impact to the judgment of the observer. In the later the player is deliberately trying to provoke specific and calculated emotions by attempting to control key plot points in the fiction or by deliberately heaping on soap operatic levels of "stuff" to provoke a reaction.
