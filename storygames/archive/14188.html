    \n Socializing, Creativity, and Friendship.

    It's a form of entertainment where you sit around a table for hours with a group of people and talk to each other.

    People are naturally creative but as we grow older, many don't practice their creativity unless it is part of their profession. Roleplaying is a great excuse to be wildly creative.

    It's an easy way to make friends.

    \n Ry, what's teenblogging?

    I'm an intensely extroverted person, so having a captive audience have to talk with me for a few hours is really energizing, so that's probably the immediate, emotional reason for it.

    However, on a more cortex-dependent level, I really enjoy being creative with friends and making stories together. In fact, I think I actually have some roleplaying-induced brain damage to my creative faculties, in that I find it way way way more difficult to be creative and interesting alone than I do in a small group.

    \n I really enjoy the social engineering / "game design is mind control" aspect of tabletop gaming. I feel like it helps me better understand human behavior on both the micro and macro levels. In that way, game design, hacking, and having really good theory/practice discussions scratches a similar itch to doing or hearing about serious social science analysis or theory. I was at a China studies conference last weekend and got a similar "high" to what I get hanging out and chatting with the indie folks at a game convention or reading a new game that really pushes things forward. It's like, "Damn, what we do is IMPORTANT and MEANINGFUL." Both design and implementation (i.e. play) provide us with opportunities to say real things (and also express our personal beliefs) about how human beings behave, the choices they make, the world that they live in, etc. AND we have to put that same understanding of human behavior to work, in order to structure player interactions at the table and create meaningful game experiences. I feel intellectually and emotionally stimulated by this process and immensely grateful for the strong community we have that supports each other in these explorations. Gaming makes me a better thinker, a better researcher, a better writer, and, really, a better human being, more aware and sympathetic to the social interactions and situations happening all around me in my daily life.

    \n I like the transgressive aspect. I don't mean this in what I would describe as a banal way. However, I had thrilling experiences playing manipulative individuals. I also played an ex-Nazi in a game of Cold City who was determined not to be discovered. There was an unpleasant depth to the character. He was motivated by fear, arrogance, insecurity. I wondered how much any of us, myself specifically, could end up like this individual. Powerful stuff, although a friend accuses me jokingly of emotional masochism when I play.

    In terms of food, I prefer a meal to a snack. The pub where the group I run meets serves reasonably decent food including calves cheeks. I'm not brave enough for this yet.

    \n It's partly the (possibly illusory?) feeling that what we come up with at the table is better than the crap on TV. Why not just write fiction, you ask? Well, it's also being each other's audience. Why not bring your fiction to a writing workshop, you ask? Well, it's the magic trick. I still can't believe that you can put a handful of people down at a table and have them make shit up for a few hours and come out with a solid story on the other end. Every time it happens it's a small miracle.

    But all that is rationalizing / intellectualizing - all I know for sure is that once I finish a session I immediately start looking forward to the next one.
