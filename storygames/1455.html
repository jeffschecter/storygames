What's the Damn Rush?

Jason_Morningstar
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Play Advice
I want to discuss a trend I'm seeing of accelerated game development. I'm not privy to anyone's schedule, but this isn't a concern about time as much as it is a concern about care - games are being released that aren't fully baked, and that worries me a little. I'm not talking about layout errors, but rather mechanical errors or omissions that directly impact game play.

Are there specific pressures that are causing people to truncate playtesting, or to rush to market?

If you recently produced a game that took less than, say, six months from idea to glossy binding, I'd like to hear your thoughts. I think it's telling that the games we consistently fall back on as bullet-proof fun often took years to perfect. Anyway, let's discuss.

EDIT: I'm as guilty as the next guy, banging out Drowning and Falling in like four months. So I'm not scolding, I'd really like to see what this is all about.

    The beautiful circumstance was that I could revise the text and not have 1000+ customers angry that I had done so. I had probably fewer than 100. People -- including those first customers -- were very accepting of my newer edition that came out 1 year later.

    I've got to disagree Matt. Yes, willingness to revise texts is commendable if they need it. That's great. But we have the ability to get it right the first time. All it takes is playtesting. Playtesting and the willingness to NOT release at Gen Con, which is the road that you chose for Dust Devils Reloaded and Matt W. has taken for Galactic. I think you both made the right decision.

    Revising and releasing again the following year should be a last recourse, not a standard strategy.

    To get back to Jason's core point, if you've got a good game, not having it at Gen Con when it's first released is not going to hurt it. Your dedicated fans will pick it up whenever it's released. They'll play it and talk about it and that will create buzz. And when the next Gen Con rolls around, you'll kick ass.

    Here's what I suggest:
    1. Set an ambitious schedule. Go ahead. It will keep you motivated. Ambition is a good thing.

    2. Leave at least several months for playtesting. Play it HARD. Get others to play it lots, without you there. Make sure the playtesting covers the full reward cycle at least once, and preferably several times over. In other words, a bunch of one-shots won't cut it if your game is intended to play over a longer term.

    3. Leave yourself about a month to edit and copy edit the text (though I suggest an editor do a concurrent read during the writing and a second read during playtesting) and another month to layout the text (obviously you'll need less for a small text) and a month to get your stock back before your deadline.

    4. If there's not enough of step 2, screw your deadline and delay. It's better to get it right the first time and be late than to deliver a shoddy product on time.

    Jason, I think the reason has been alluded to but a finger hasn't been pointed at it: GenCon. Somehow, the indie design community seems to have hitched itself to a annual schedule that rotates around GenCon. New releases are expected at GenCon. We ask what this year's batch of new games is, and by that we mean what's being released at GenCon. Designers -- and especially new designers -- see all of this and decide to shoot for GenCon. And not any GenCon they're shooting for next GenCon, which gives them less than a full year to race through a full development and production cycle. Things inevitably fall by the wayside, and the first thing is a rigorous playtest.

    I know I felt some profound pressure to get FLFS out the door for GenCon. The only reason I didn't is because I'm too damn cautious to travel across the country to a con I've never been to and release my first big game at the same time. I'll release my game in my backyard when GenCon SoCal comes knocking in my neck of the woods.

    I suspect there's also a perceived pressure to get your innovative game out the door before your innovations are no longer innovative. I know Situation Engineering in FLFS would have been way cutting edge had I released it last year shortly after I wrote it, for instance. Since then lots of folks have been doing lots of interesting things with situation creation, and now my situation creation mechanic will be one among many rather than some kind of lone pioneer. I'd like to say that the trade-off for the solid game was worth it, but in all truth I won't be certain that it was until I start seeing actual sales numbers and AP reports.

    Fred (Hicks) mentioned somewhere else, that a game needs a critical mass of people to play to spot the errors. When a person is playtesting a game with his core group of friends and maybe one or two other groups, he is going to get the false sense of security. This whole phase gets passed right over to focus on other concerns (like art) because the designer simply does not have enough people to critically look at his creation. And lets face it...playtesting takes the longest part of production if done right, and not enough people are doing it right.

    And speaking of not being able to find enough players...how many of us always look for help to playtest our games, but rarely come through for others in a similar need? Sure, a con game here or there, a one-shot with the our regular group...but long term playtesting? It's rare, because honestly, most of us are trying to playtest our own games.

    Not to go too far off topic, but it would be nice if we could organize some kind of playtesting roundtable. You know...sign up to a list with your game, and when your turn comes around, everyone else on the list plays your game as one-shot during that month. Next month, everyone moves on to the next game. You wouldn't get long term play, but if you had a dozen or so groups giving your game a one-shot in a given month, it could provide some valuable feedback. Just a thought.
