It's official. We are artists.

Matthijs
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Story Games
Every year, the Norwegian Department of Church & Culture (it's a wonderful name, isn't it? right up there with Tobacco & Firearms) grants approx. 20-25 million dollars to artists in a variety of fields - music, painting, dance etc. Anyone can apply, and have their application processed by the appropriate committee. There's a separate committee for applications that don't fit in any of the standard categories.

This year, I applied. Mostly as part of a political program: Norwegian game designers are trying to get role-playing games accepted as a valid cultural expression and art form, and are using different channels for this purpose - mostly, bureaucratic and media channels.

I wrote an application based on the following: I've written two RPG books, published by a Norwegian publisher. I want to reach an international market. I want to finish We All Had Names.

I received the grant.

That means I get paid half a year's salary each year for the next two years in order to make "We All Had Names". There's no strings attached - only that I have to finish it, and do it well.

I'm stunned. Happy. Apprehensive. It's the chance of a lifetime, and now I have to not fuck up. It's hard for me to convey just how momentous this is, both for me personally, and for the status of role-playing as an art form in my country.

But I'm not just writing this just to say "Hey, lookit me, I'm a lucky bastard!" I want people to know that we're moving towards the point where we don't need to discuss whether role-playing is art - because the art institutions are already saying that it is. If you want this to happen, make it happen. Apply for grants, talk to journalists.

    "It is better to be ignored than to accept impotence for recognition."

    The fine arts are a social development which protects society at large from potentially dangerous cognitive technologies. It provides members of that society a potent psychological defense, namely the stratification of the arts. In essence the classification as a fine art removes nearly all of the potential to affect outside of carefully controlled circumstances, which largely negate the effects. The exchange is a place of respect and honor in society, often this honey is enough to distract most participants in these arts.

    The point is that RPGs are a potent cognitive technology, with more potential than most of the ones which have been turned into fine arts. It would be a tragic day if RPGs shared their fate. If you are one of the people who argues that RPGs can be so much more than just a game, realize I am saying they can be so much more than just art.

    To ground things back to the situation at hand, I would encourage you to consider the potential of a Holocaust RPG, not as pathos, but as a means to give its players a mental schema for actually retaining the importance of the Holocaust. RPGs can give you new ways to think, exploit that fact.

    - Mendel

    RPGs should not aspire to be art or fiction, they should aspire to transcend such things.

Heck, it goes to fostering that public sphere.

    A question I'm pondering - I don't expect anyone to have an easy answer, but any thoughts are welcome:

    I'm currently reading the Kovno ghetto diary. One thing I don't understand: The jews know other jews are being exterminated in neighboring provinces and towns. They know there's a death camp right outside their town. Pits are being dug, crowds of people are led there, never to return. They know the German occupants are lying, and any deal with them will be broken at the occupants' whim.

    Still, the Jewish leaders always try to use papers, documents, appeals to the civilized side of their tormentors. They appeal to rules and agreements.

    Why? Is it a case of clinging desperately to the last straw of hope? It can't be simple naivet; these leaders were wise and experienced community leaders. Were there no alternatives? Mass escape? Attempts at sabotage, attempts to strike back?

    The basic question: Did they act this way for cultural, psychological or tactical reasons?     I think, Matthijs, that you need to talk to actual survivors to get that question answered - and even then, you're likely to get different answers depending on who you talk to and where they were when it all happened.

    I'm from a family of Holocaust survivors, myself, and from my family stories I can tell you that there was a sense of "this cannot be real." We have gotten so used to the trappings of civilized life, the idea that our laws and customs are 'the real world', that when someone comes along who is willing to completely ignore them it can be almost impossible to believe. My mother's father escaped Germany in the late thirties, and he occasionally talks about how everyone thought he was crazy, that they figured it would be a time of legal oppression but not of throwing laws and morals to the wind.

    When all the solutions you've learned to problems are 'civilized' ones, it's very hard to learn how to deal with people who don't play by your rules.

    From my grandmother, an Auschwitz survivor, I heard very little about her experiences - but one of the things she told me is that there was a sense of 'there is no choice.' Mass escape would be useless - where would they go? Who would take them in? Even if they could get more than ten miles, which was unlikely, what would happen then? What would they eat? How would they survive? Few countries would take in the Jews, and those had strict quotas. It's not as though they had much of an alternative.

    Also, there were attempts at sabotage and resistance (the most famous: the Warsaw Ghetto). Even in the work camps, many workers would do a shoddy job on the goods they were producing for the Nazi army as the only form of resistance they were capable. However, you should also look at the failed escape from Treblinka to get a sense of the problems with this approach.

    That said: seriously. Ask survivors. Don't ask us.

    --Jess
