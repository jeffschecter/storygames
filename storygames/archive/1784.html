Do you play what you buy?

Matt Snyder
November 2006 edited November 2006 in Play Advice
Paul Czege states actual play as his ultimate goal as a game designer and publisher. I share that goal, though Paul and I do have slightly different methods in terms of sales and business (they're not really that different).

Anyway, it got me thinking about actual play and the indie scene. Or, really, about RPGs in general.

How many books did you buy, say, this year? (I bought about 15 or so.) How many did you actually play? (I played exactly ZERO of those new games, more's the pity. My situation is not as good as others; wish I could play these games more so.)

Were those actually played games really designed for short or one-off games? Or, did you devote some time to them? (No and no.)

If you are a game designer, or have inklings to be, how many of the books that you bought did you buy because you consider them interesting or good design influences? (Yes.)

Does the number of games you own compared to the number of games you actually play bother you? (Yes, greatly.)

What keeps you from playing more games you wish to play? Is it a stubborn or reluctant group? Not enough time? (General disinterest among my play groups, limited time.)

My responses to these questions lead me to some troubling conclusions (troubling to ME at least -- this will not be universal). Specifically, I think there is way too much emphasis on new designs and way too little emphasis on playing exising designs, marketing existing designs, and supporting existing designs. We all get a hard on for the new cool thing, when in fact some of the "old" cool things haven't even been explored very much at all.

In short, I think the indie scene suffers from designers -- myself included -- who love design so much that they'd rather create and publish Yet Another Game than support the solid designs they already have. I certainly do this, to the detriment of my own games.

Some of that, I believe, is the high one gets working on new designs, getting something new out there, and go go go! Some of it is the fact that many of us just don't know HOW to support a game very well, and/or that we find this more "boring" than designing something new and cool.

The end result is almost a Top 40 style hit parade of the New Hotness in gaming. At worst, it'll lead to a kind of fad-ism, which I find troubling. At best, it means we've got a diverse selection of tightly focused games, which I find exciting.

But, what really troubles me most is that we have a broad, shallow market of many games that, in total, very few people have played. I argue we risk confusing and fragmenting hobbyists who feel overwhelmed by choices, have too little support for making buying AND (more importantly) playing decisions.

I'm arguing we risk it, not that the Titanic is sinking. I think it's something publishers should address. But, then, there's nothing I can do to make a publisher do that. I can point out my observation, and the publisher can take it or leave it. Either way, I think it's something worth discussing.

    Jon, to clarify my position (which is maybe unnecessary):

    I'm not suggesting that purchasing games-without-play is wrong, per se. I'm raising the question, and encouraging each publisher to answer that for himself or herself (or, possibly, themselves!). I mean, I do it myself. I have a whole bookshelf full-to-burstin' with indie games alone. I've played maybe 3-4 of them in 3-4 years.

    But, what does it mean to a publisher? Does it mean including in the business model the fact that some significant percentage will not be played? Is play, in fact, one's goal?

    And, this thread has raised some other interesting questions. Jason Morningstar suggests deliberately designing games to be played in one night as an option and/or default assumption. I'm sympathetic; I'm currently trying to design a game to do just that.

    But, I also publish a game that would suck as only that. To see Nine Worlds in action, you must play it for more than a session or two. One of the most universally praised games 'round these parts is Prime Time Adventures. Wouldn't it suck if Matt decided that, due to the nature of this cottage industry, PTA should ONLY be played in one night, rather than 12? Boy, that would suck!

    So, what I'm getting at is that we're seeing a lot of assumptions take place that effect a lot of things we shouldn't take for granted -- like, how many copies to sell, how to design for a single-sessions, how to better market games, and so on. I'm not seeing a LOT of discussion about this stuff. I'm seeing some. I think more is really important.

    Well, then, let me hasten to disappoint you. Aaaaaaand let me apologize for the length of this post. And for the fact that it's mostly just impressions and feelings, as opposed to useful data points.

    I guess I kind of ran out of steam on that last paragraph there because I'm remarkably ill-informed, and will probably need a Bud Light to wash my foot down if I ramble on about the scene for too long. But, you know, it does seem a little insular sometimes. I think there's a lot that's being done to expose mainstream gamers to what's going on, and I think that's great. But it appears to me that we can do more. I'm not sure what, yet.

    In a way, the indie game scene is a little daunting. I still feel like an outsider a lot of the time, because I read a post about someone talking about Game X, and I have no idea what they're talking about. Not like, I don't know the game. I mean, I don't understand what language they're speaking. They're using terms I know nothing about. The thing is, a lot of game sites (and PR materials) are crafted similarly. It's like one of those scenes where if you already know, then you know, and if you don't know, you never will. And I realize that's not intentional -- but designers speak their own language, which is different from the language of the user. I make a lot of comparisons to the video game industry (far too many, I'm sure), but in the game biz, we talk about games one way when designing/developing them, and another way when marketing them. In the indie game scene, the line appears to blur. I have no idea if any of the aforementioned made sense. I guess I'm saying that the terminology is a barrier to entry, and it's not just on the behind-the-scenes discussions. It's a lot of the public presentation. I go to a game site, read the text, click through a few pages, and I still have no idea what I'm about to order via PayPal.

    Another issue that I see is that these indie games deliver a user experience that's remarkably like the Wii in some regards. The Wii is garnering a substantial buzz for Nintendo this year because it offers an experience with a low barrier to entry -- perhaps lower than the DS, in fact. I mean, who doesn't know how to swing a tennis racket, or cast a fishing rod? It's a toy, in some ways, as opposed to a traditional video game console. And the conventional wisdom holds that its appeal will result in a crossover with audiences that are normally wary of video games (due in some cases to their complexity or unfamiliarity).

    Story games have the potential for a similar crossover, in my mind, because they are easier to grasp (mechanically), and because they are familiar (who doesn't know how to tell a story?). Maybe I'm oversimplifying here, but it seems like a lot of story games would go over well with those who are curious about tabletop gaming, but who lack the experience.

    Personally, my story goes something like this. I had a couple of buddies, typical nerds. But! They'd never done tabletop. They always wanted to, but didn't know enough about it, didn't have any friends with books or dice, yada yada. One night, we're hanging out drinking beer, and one of 'em says, hey, is that a D&D book? Few weeks later, we play our first game. Roll! I miss. Roll initiative. Which die is that? Low or high? I miss. Can I jump off the roof and land on his back, then stick the dagger in his ear? Which one do I roll if I want to do that? Wait, which one's the d8?

    So, I reconsidered. The game was too complex, they couldn't do the cool stuff they wanted to, et cetera. But, there were other games out there, and I discovered the Forge, and the next thing you know, we're doing Star Wars Paladin. It works great. Aha, I think. Just perfect. They grasp the game mechanics easily, they get to do cool things, and they don't feel like ineffectual rat-killers. (Now, don't get me wrong, I still love D&D and would play it at the drop of a hat, but it just didn't work out for this gaming group.)

    Now, these dudes are probably not alone. There's a veritable army of geeks out there in similar circumstances. Not as much free time as we had during adolescence, so those five-night-a-week gaming campaigns are not so likely these days. Maybe once every couple of weeks, or once a month. So, is a game where you start off fighting rabbits, then build up to kobolds over the space of a year, really going to work for you? I'd guess no. If you're a complete novice gamer, and you want to just play a game and do something cool, then I think you could do a damn sight worse than a game like Sorcerer.

    Here's the other thing. While playtesting my game, Dread, I once ran a game session with four women. These broads had never played an RPG, except for my wife, with whom I played Sorcerer. And they really got into it. But they didn't seem to be aware of the limitations that more experienced players seem to worry about when I run games at a con. Experienced gamers are concerned about repercussions -- What if we get caught? Who's watching the back? I look up, do I see anything?

    But these novice gamers were pretty much playing for fun. Why worry about getting caught? Obviously, we're the stars of the show, so we can't go to jail.

  • Caesar_X
    November 2006
    If I understand Matt's basic premise, I think the real point here is that game designers naturally prefer to spend as much time designing games as possible. The farther away you get from that point (such as playtesting, editing, publishing, selling, marketing, etc.), the less interesting the task typically is to a game designer, so it's easy to choose not to spend as much time on it. That's why a one-man show is so hard to pull off in any kind of game development. You really need a team of several specialists working full-time on different parts of the development and publishing process to consistently put out product that sells well.

    Right now the indie games scene is selling mainly to itself and to a very small subset of curious players from other gaming genres. But it's really hard to move past that and still keep it as a "fun hobby". It takes a commitment of time and focus on things other than interesting mechanics and themes. And it also takes a commitment to making games that a wider audience is going to want to play. The current indie story games are typically very focused in nature. This makes logical sense since they are appealing mainly to a group of people who already like indie story games and want to see innovation. But the end result is that when looking at the customer pyramid, these games keep bumping up against the sharp (and small) tip of "hardcore" first adopters instead of aggressively moving towards the fat end with all of those non-gamers and lite-gamers.

    This trend can certainly continue, but it will always be a niche market with little room for category growth. So instead of five titles vying for those thousands of potential customers, in not too long there are going to be fifty good-quality titles fighting for those same people. You need to greatly expand the overall customer base in order to fully support the increasing number of titles being released.

      It can be difficult to find players for long-running series of some off-beat games.       And finally, how to better market games:
      My recommendations for getting more people to play: first, put in the effort to organize a club -- and then the additional effort of keeping it alive after that. That lets you reach the ready market of RP gamers that would otherwise not try your games. Then try to market your book like any other book: contact bookstores and highschools in your area to organize meet-and-greets with the author (you), run demo games at sci-fi or comic cons and (non-RPG) game day events, etc. That gives you a chance to get in touch with new potential markets.
