\n In honor of Alexander Cherry's* speedy recovery from surgery, I have put FoundryMUSH back up. The Foundry is a chat server with a "room" feel rather than the "channel" feel of IRC. It's built on TinyMUSH 3.1 software, which gives every account access to its built-in building and programming language, though it's arcane and bizarre.

In the past, folks have used The Foundry as a place to hang out and chat. Occasionally, people run games there. We have built code for generic dice and cards, and there are additional tools specific to certain games (like Dogs or Roach, for example).

Most people will want to use a mushclient program to connect. I recommend SimpleMU for Windows, TinyFugue for Linux, and Atlantis for Mac. The Foundry web site should have links to these free programs. You can use "raw" telnet to connect, if you really want. The game is on foundry.legendary.org port 7777.

* Alexander was a regular member at the Foundry club.

    Posted By: Dave CleaverI think that would be something really interesting to experiment with.
    \nYeah, I was actually struck recently by how many of my recent game designs (specifically Geiger Counter and Transantiago) borrow a lot from my time playtesting for Skotos and work very much like small-scale MUSHs, with players defining various "rooms" and features as they go. Of course, both Geiger and Transan benefit from players being able to see, visually, the layout of the entire space, which MUSHs have traditionally not done, I think (though Castle Marrach did). Honestly, it would be interesting to design not so much a small-scale MUSH itself but, um, rules for playing in a MUSH environment. Like, y'know, you can only create a new location if XYZ conditions are met and then you have to connect it to location B and create PDQ objects with certain kinds of traits/description. More like the rules for PTA or Universalis or Mist-Robed Gate than the rules for a D&D module, y'know? Where the ability of players to alter the environment was taken for granted and strongly incorperated into the guidelines for play.

    \n That'd be cool. It'd be pretty easy to build your "personal sets" as rooms. As you started scenes, you might need to have everyone relocate to the next set, but that's not a huge deal. I recommend organizing it like a movie set. Have a central area for the director and crew, and then have specific sets attached to it. You might even set up a conference room to get people into the mindset of planning the stuff before and between episodes.

    Use character descriptions (@desc) to record information about each character (stuff usually on the sheet). Also, feel free to create extra characters for games and stuff. You don't have to try to recycle your normal player account. I'm really not worried about the database size.
