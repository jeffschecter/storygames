    \n Unfortunately, Ryan, none of us (aside from Justin) are lawyers, so I'm not sure how much we can help you here. Infringement, unfortunately, if often in the eyes of the IP holder, and they may send you a cease and desist no matter what you do. I basically have 3 suggestions:

    1.) Draw all the graphics yourself, rather than copying or tweaking rips from the actual video games.

    2.) Count on the fact that you will make very little money and are in a very tiny market, so most likely nobody will care what you do; or

    3.) Give your game away for free, in which case the likelihood that anyone will care will be even smaller.

    \n The easy answer without involving lawyers is that if you want to own your own work you have to make your own work (or comission it). I have no idea how to answer your question since I don't know what those images are from. However, the point of infringement risks occurring anytime you use anyone else's existing material.

    I'd check out the Pixel Joint for discussions on the art form. Both the Pixel Joint forum and Pixellation forum (also linked in the Community pulldown - yeah, it's a little confusing) are pretty active.

    There's another old joke: "How do you convey emotions like a puppet in a 16 by 24 pixel frame?" "Practice, practice, practice."

    If you don't want to teach yourself, you can probably find people to collaborate with at these forums. I'd also suggest poking around deviant art if that's the primary goal.

    \n Another option is to look around the programming forums for public domain and free-usage sprite sets, then build your pictures like a jigsaw. A good many of the older programming languages that were aimed at games design came with sprite sets like this that were open source and could be used by anyone, which is largely why no-one did.

    Alternatively, maybe you could approach some of the programming teams making freeware games like this and ask them if you can use their resources? If they give their games away for free anyway, it's possible they'll consider doing the same with the graphics.

    \n For another method, you could find an icon editor, push a graphic into it, let it make the 16x16 (or 32x32 or whatever) and then use that for the larger-scale model (basically, extract the BMP from the icon file and blow it up to the scale you need). Then, your graphic sources could be nearly anything, because the iconization (new word!) will render them unrecognizable. You might have to tweak a pixel here and there, once blown up, but that's easier than trying to draw from scratch--it's an art form, and a LOT harder than you'd imagine (I've pulled my hair out for hours just trying to get ONE icon for a web site favicon "perfect").
