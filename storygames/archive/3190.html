\n
\n This other thread on GMs got me thinking on a more general level about the positive qualities of GM utlization, which is also relevant to my development of Bullseye. I am operating under a Benevolent President-Judge model for Bullseye (but that's a topic for another discussion). These are some general thoughts that have been brewing for me:

1) Limitation on Improvisation: Yes, utilization of a GM is inherently, at some measure, a limitation on player involvement. However, this involvment is itself inherently improvisational. Giving players the power to, e.g., build the game world, requires them to improvise those elements. One of the fundamental aspects of a traditional GM is significant preparation, e.g., at the most classic level, mapping out the dungeon. This concentrates pre-game effort into the hands of a single individual, which can be helpful--or even necessary--for many play groups. Not all players like or are even able to engage in the improvisation demanded by GM-less games.

2) The Labyrinthine Plot: When players build large parts of the story in play, it necessarily limits the complexity of the plot. It's far harder to spontaneously and collaboratively generate, e.g, an intricate web of courtly intrigue, backstabbing, and lies, than to script the framework of same ahead of time. I recognize that this can devolve into the functional equivalent of everyone sitting around while the GM reads the players his latest bit of fiction, but play styles should not be judged on their worst examples. While it may not be for all play groups, this sort of experience can generally only be achieved by utilizing a GM.

Thoughts on these topics? Other benefits of using a GM over GM-less play?

    \n Justin, I'm highly skeptical of your #2. In my experience, I find the labyrinthine plot is more likely when you have five cooks for the soup improvising what goes in as it boils away. The resulting plot may not be the elegant construction that a GM can plan, but I also have not seen those elegant GM plans actually come to fruition without significant in-play modification.

    Others:
    3) Coordinated Logistics. Often, the GM is also the host or player-wrangler. "Hey, we're playing on Saturday."

    4) Coordinated Spotlight Time. The GM serves a very foundational role of directing the attention of the table to different players. "Okay, what are you doing, Jim? Now what are you doing, Sally?" And so on.

    5) Meting out Approval. In any game where the GM either (a) awards XP/Plot Points or (b) can disregard mechanical resolution in favor of "a really good idea," the GM is distributing approval of player contributions. He's saying, "Yes, that was a good idea" and by consequence, when something doesn't work or get a reward, "That was not a good idea." Note this is hardly a criticism: there needs to be some way to sort between good and bad ideas and what is and is not a good contribution; making that one guy's job is a very functional and very easy way of providing that.

    \n The GM plays the world to the player's character. That's the general benefit.

    If you play a Choose Your Own Adventure game book, you get the same thing, with one enormous drawback: the options available to you the player are severely restricted.

    Same goes for the old "chit-pull dungeon" games like Deathmaze.

    On the other hand a few games like Magic Realm offer a pretty wide range of options and variety to play--at the cost of being pretty damn complex to learn, let alone master. And it still doesn't offer the detailed narrative coherence that you have with a GM, and you're still working at a fairly high level of abstraction. (You don't feel your way through the dank caverns, chamber by chamber. You roll on the LOOT chart, hope your HIDE action is successful, and that the monster chits on your tile are ones you can handle if necessary.)

    With a GM, whatever happens to the character happens to "you", "you" the character act on the "the world", and "the world" takes care of itself (through the GM).

    But (a) I feel my perspective on this is if not a minority among roleplayers in general, an extremely rare one on this forum, and (b) talking about it in general isn't necessarily relevant to your game at all. If you can sketch your game or provide a link, and then ask what benefits or drawbacks people see in your use of a GM in your game, that would be much more productive.

    \nIt's a matter of rights. As a crappy analogy, it's the difference between you saying:
    \nIt's against the law to steal. Police have a job to enforce that.
    \n
    \nAnd me saying:
    \nWe don't steal because stealing is wrong.
    \n
    \nIs the role of the GM that of police? That is, is the job of the GM to limit players, or do the players limit themselves? The problem with the former attitude is that it assumes that the GM knows better than the players. This attitude has arisen from problems due to conflicts in player interests in early RPGs. The games incentivize "abuse" so the GM must be there to limit what the player can reasonably do, using the argument of "This is what he would reasonably do" as the benchmark.
    \n
    \nIt's unenforcable, and harmful to play. Possibly the single most damaging problem in RPGs ever, and has resulted in the death of innumerable groups of RPG players. I'm not saying this lightly.
    \n
    \nIf the system in question incentivizes players to have their characters act like, well, interesting characters, then there's no need for the GM to limit the players. This is not part of his role. Quite simply I haven't done this in a RPG for years now.
    \n
    \nTruely abusive players are everyone's problem, not just the GM. It's a social level problem that has to be resolved outside of the game, and not by some game-given responsibility. Assuming that the players are playing non-abusively, there is no problem that requires a GM to solve. Unless the game is badly designed, and promotes bad play and requires the GM to fix it.
    \n
    \nThe real harm is that it puts in the GM's mind the notions like "players will never do something as productive as making plot, so I'll have to force one upon them." Basically in casting players as people who need to be controlled, you create an adversarialy relationship instead of one in which the players and GM are actually working together to produce something in play. And not in the sense of competitive, either, but more like adversaries in a courtroom.
    \n
    \nNow, if by "limit" you mean that in reserving certain rights for the GM that this automatically denies them to the players, and that this sort of limitation is good in that it gives players something to focus on? OK, sure. But, again, that's not the purpose of a GM, but an outcome of the split of the real duties. You're just saying what Elliot and I are saying in 6. The GM does something else.
    \n
    \nCall me touchy on this issue, but it's a biggie.

    \n Justin, you could certainly unpack #1. You've got

    improvisation
    preparation (back story and secrets)
    area of responsibility (character/world)

    Consider how these could be mixed up in ways other than traditional.

    Can players prep the game world? Sure--but if they're not collaborating during prep, there's a good chance the results will be inconsistent.

    If they do collaborate during prep, secrets go out the window.

    Neither inconsistency nor lack of secrets are game killers--depending on what you're trying to achieve.

    You also might try a clever method of dividing game world prep among players while still having "hooks" to interconnect the elements. Like, "Okay, I've got an NPC named Ugly Wat, the groundskeeper. If anyone wants to define his secret, go ahead. Also I have two minor and one major secrets in the ch\xc3\xa2teau; I can't tell you what they are, but if you'd like to connect them in general terms with some elements you're creating, go ahead."

    Sort of like object-oriented setting design to allow team prep without forcing the teammembers to look inside the "black boxes" of each other's area of responsibility.

    I'm sure the above would be nontrivial to work out systematically but if you can do it, wouldn't it be neat?

    \n...because I was just about to jump in and say something along the same lines. One of the things our group talked about when we did the postmortem on our PTA games was how some players really didn't like what Elliot refers to above as "inconsistency" and "lack of secrets." (And I should say that it didn't kill the game for us all on its own, but it was definitely part of the other mismatches between our group and PTA that did kill it.)

    Our more traditional GM-driven games play out more like what Mike talks about earlier: the GM creates plots and at the same time accepts (and occasionally actively solicits) contributions from the players. When combined with what Joshua described as the "meting out approval" role of a GM, this is hugely popular in our group...especially with certain GMs who have developed a reputation for being particularly good at using player contributions in unexpected but satisfying ways.

    I suspect, though no one ever said it out loud, that part of the reason why some of our players strongly preferred the usual GM-driven games to our experiments in collaborative narration has to do with that "meting out approval"/impartial-judge thing. We've got a few players who are genuinely good at improvising and developing plot, adversity, and ideas, but for various reasons (shyness or whatever) won't do it if they feel like they're being put on the spot. For them, I think the thing that they appreciate most is being able to use the GM as a filter, a proxy between them and the spotlight: they use some back channel like e-mail or lunchtime conversation to get their idea to the GM, who then stands up in front of everyone else and incorporates that idea seamlessly into the game. They're also putting their trust in the GM to use their idea if it's good, or to revise or even ignore it entirely if it isn't. It's win-win for those players, because they they get to focus on the face-to-face element of gaming that they enjoy (typically playing their character/"avatarism") and get to have input on the other elements of the game without the pressure of the spotlight on them.

    \n This is probably a subset of one of the already mentioned things or a collection of some techniques, but:

    GM can deliver his vision of the game (* by setting the mood, theme and play style. He defines what the game is about, where it's set, who's it about. This sets the borders of the fiction and frees the players to work within these borders, explore and create content within. Easiest way to do this is to use popular culture references: "Imagine Twin Peaks; a quiet little town in which just about everything is a little off from the contemporary setting and odd things seem more normal. You're playing a group of investigators".

    *) Game = Scenario or a campaign.

    But yes, having one person planning things behind the scenes makes this kind of stuff easier, and it comes as more of a surprise.

    \n The presense of a GM can contribute to group cohesiveness -- not quite on the level of being a common enemy, but along those lines. The GM provides the conflict, the players work against it, and as such, are often "forced" to work together.

    Also, the GM is the designated "mean guy," who can screw over other players in the game (through conflicts and so on) without them taking it too personally, in a way that could be problematic for individual players.

    A GM isn't right for every game, but if you want more group cooperation, one way to get it is to have a GM.
