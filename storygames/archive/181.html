Elsewhere, Kuma says, of "'system' is whatever a group does to keep consensus"...
Sure, that'll get the job done, but first of all it leaves those groups out in the cold who maybe need a crutch for consensus-building at first - assuming that the point is to help groups who aren't already masters of the form; and secondly I feel that it divvies up the responsibility unequally between the designer and the players.
The real intent and implication of the LP is upside-down from your reading: all the LP means is that designing rules IS designing tools the players will use socially. The rules can't make things happen in the game except by the active assent and participation of the players themselves.

On the questions of how you design rules, what you should design rules for, what rules make for good design, all your concerns, the LP is totally silent. It's a very small-brained, stupid little principle! It really doesn't have much to say, and what it has to say is head-slappingly obvious.

Leaving the LP aside, then, any designer foisting design work off on the eventual play group is a crap designer. Anybody using the Lumpley Principle to defend doing THAT, sucks. I agree with you there. But I don't know anybody but you who reads the LP as defending that.

-Vincent

    Just so's everyone knows, BTW - I work 3rd shift, and so I'm rarely on the PC until late evening when my daughter is safely tucked in bed, or I finally get to work. I'm not silent - I'm just most active online at 4am. :^)
    The real intent and implication of the LP is upside-down from your reading: all the LP means is that designing rules IS designing tools the players will use socially. The rules can't make things happen in the game except by the active assent and participation of the players themselves.
    Let me explain a bit where I'm coming from - my model that I've been formulating over @ Elsewhere is concerned with all of gaming - not just roleplaying games. In the model, there are four 'spaces' that a game game has: the gamespace, which is the set of all the legal moves as denoted by the rules (and modified by the players); the emulationspace (emspace for short), which is the equivalent of the SIS (shared information space); each player carries an individual information space (called the aspace) - which is essentially a mix of their perspective on the SIS combined with the rest of their prior knowledge; and the playspace, which is the social arena in which the game takes place.

    The thing that I've been fighting with all day has been this: the Loody Poody (love that) assumes that the playspace and the gamespace are in unity, and affect the SIS; my model suggests otherwise - that there are parts of the social sphere which don't impact the SIS directly, but which are nonetheless important to the function of the game. Was I wrong? Does the playspace not exist?

    And I've finally figured it out - yes, the playspace does exist as a separate entity - it's just that in some game forms, it's relationship with the gamespace is much more removed, and it is a much more distinct thing. My exemplar of a game with very distinct gamespace and prominent playspace would be poker.

    Poker is highly social - bluffing, tells, the whole nine yards. There's a very large social component to the game. However, this social component, while it influences each player's individual information space (aspace), and thereby changes the shape of play, it doesn't change the gamespace (the rules of poker don't change according to social mechanics), and it doesn't affect the SIS because there really isn't one. So in poker, the social negotiations (being driven by a desire to hide information) are separate from the rules of the game.

    And so what I've come to realize is that the relationships between the the spaces in my model change according to the game being played. In roleplaying games, where the SIS is, well, the crux of the game, the playspace and gamespace are very near unity - there are fewer social interactions that don't impact the SIS or gamespace in some fashion.

    I'm working on formalizing this a bit more, and kicking the tires a bit. But the upshot is that the Lumpley Principle holds true(r) for roleplaying games than other gameforms, and for narrativist games most of all. But for some gameforms, the only consent and consensus are to agree to the rules of the game - everything after that may affect individuals and their course of play, but it doesn't affect the game itself.
    Leaving the LP aside, then, any designer foisting design work off on the eventual play group is a crap designer. Anybody using the Lumpley Principle to defend doing THAT, sucks. I agree with you there. But I don't know anybody but you who reads the LP as defending that.
    Well, let me give you an example of what I mean. I have a copy of PTA in front of me (not that I want to pick on PTA, but I consider it an axiomatic narrativist game. In the section on Creating a Series, p. 16:
    Every show has a setting, which includes a place, and usually a time period. [...] If you have a premise in mind, this part is probably pretty easy to figure out. But before you go any further, everyone should make some notes and speak up about any important details that the do or don't want to have in place. [...] Just as important is to make clear what you don't want t address in the game. (Emphasis added.)
    So here's what I see as a piece of what the Lumpley Principle refers to as System which is non-systemic. For any number of reasons, there are going to be times when someone doesn't feel that they can speak up about their wishes; there are a whole spectrum of group game dynamics ranging between pure democracy and GM-as-god. What PTA is doing here is assuming that this sort of decision-making - consensus-building - is readily present in the group - that the result reached will be wholly satisfactory for all concerned; or that any compromises in satisfaction will be redressed.

    So what I'm really trying to get at with my critique of the Lumpley Principle is that by putting 'everybody sit down and talk about it' in the same basket as the (much more impressive) mechanics for Fan Mail, for example, the LP may lead a designer to leave groups who don't have these consensus-building skills in the lurch.

    I'm also not advocating a complete structuralization of the RPG experience from top to bottom - I'm not calling for 'Creating the Series Randomization Tables A-Z' or including a copy of parliamentary procedure in every RPG. What I'm saying is that we, as designers, should fabricate tools to meet groups halfway to consensus - and if they don't need the tools, then they can just toss them out.

    This also goes back to the whole 'damage' hoo-ha. If groups are damaged, then I see games like PTA as a great way to help fix them. But there needs to be a bridge between the two experiences that can lead a horse to water, if you get my drift. In the end, it can only be a good thing for all concerned.

I don't think there's a single best formula for how much (and what) the rules in a game text are supposed to do. In some cases, the game text provides a skeleton and leaves the participants to flesh it out. In some cases, the game text provides a bunch of flesh and leaves the participants to structure it.

    Kuma said: What I'm getting at is that even if the LP was a retroactive defintion of System as 'system (little s) + consensus', what do you get when you work forward from the LP?

    The LP doesn't say anything about how consensus is reached. It only says that whether you reach a decision through mechanics or unwritten social rules, it all constitutes your system for making decisions. If the group vests all power in the GM to make decisions about what can or can't enter the game events, then they have reached consensus, and the GM will make all such decisions without deferring to the will of the group.

    The spectrum of possibility within the LP is anything from a game with complete GM fiat, to one with no GM in which all the players get an equal vote, to one in which most decisions are made by the rules.

    Given that, I think Tony's question ("Will you resolve X by consensus or mechanics?") is spot on.

    Hmm. Well, maybe I have a bit more radical reading of the LP than I thought I did. Because I do see the LP as saying, essentially, that the act of play IS an act of design. "Resolve by mechanics" is only possible if we have a consensus agreement that we will resolve by mechanics - an agreement that always can (and sometimes does, even in games like Chess and Monopoloy - though I'm more comfortable sticking within the "collaborative, thematic play" box ) get rescinded and/or renegotiated. "Resolve by mechanics" is a tool to create more interesting (and/or coherent, and/or speedy, and/or etc.) consensus agreements than if we just tried talking until we acheived consensus.

    Now, I don't think that excuses "foisting" design work off on the play group - give 'em tools, and good ones, or you're not doing much of a design job. But I do (did?) think the LP told me the play group is ALWAYS the final designer. The "real" game designer is crap or not-crap based on how easy he or she makes that final design process, in the context of the particular focus of the particular game.
