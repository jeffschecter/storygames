\n Well, anyone who has played Paranoia knows that 5 to 6 players brings the best results. Unfortunately, sometimes people are working, travelling or are just plain lazy. Sometimes, because of that, you end up with 2 or maybe just one player. Should the GM send this worthy gamer back home? 'Course not! The question is, how to make that game interesting without losing the paranoid "watch your back" feeling? I asked Allen Varney himself for some tips and he came with good ones, but it seems that I'll be experiencing this low-player scenario for quite some time, so the more ideas the better! Anyone have been already through this or just happens to have an insight?
\nBy the way, this is my first time here so if I'm posting in the wrong place, please let me know.

    \n Oh, and the other logical choice for Paranoia... execute one clone at the start of the next session they play. Make it a running gag for the campaign. Let the players suggest how their clones died... if truly twisted, award them as you activate their next clone. An extra mutation, a promotion to higher security clearance (or the far more rewarding removal from position of authority), a nifty experimental device to test... let them know they're hosed regardless, but they can count on a little something special for giving you a chuckle.

    \n Kafka's book, The Trial, is Paranoia-like, and that's got one protagonist.

    I think you could do a one player Paranoia game like that. Have the character arrested for something. When he asks what he's accused of, tell him the information is above his clearance. Have him descend through increasing layers of bureaucracy and show-trials, until he eventually reaches his execution. At any stage, if he refuses to confess or go along with the process, tell him he's being disloyal.

    Have you seen the scenario Hunger, in WMD? That's the sort of thing I mean. That'd work fine with one player.
