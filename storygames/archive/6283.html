    \n The issue lately is the leaching of chemicals in plastic water bottles. i.e. Nalgene and other polycarbonate bottle. These are the bottles that have a 7 inside the recycle symbol. I suspect that we'll see these bottles pulled from shelves at some point.

    So, the Sigg bottle is a good idea if you buy into all the research showing that the chemicals in these plastics are doing your body harm. I've had a Sigg bottle for years. It's dented, paint is coming off, but it keeps going. Worth the money.

    You can also find stainless steel for about the same price and same weight.

    \n I've got a Sigg traveler, just a plain old blue one, and it's taken abuse pretty darn well. Closes tightly, and the caribiner hoop on the top is nice.     \n There are plastics you should worry about reusing, but to the best of my knowledge none of them are in Nalgenes. (Edit: anymore? I noticed Frank's comment about the "old-style soft Nalgenes," and who knows what those were.)

    "7" is the "Other" code; it covers too much stuff to be useful as a guide to anything in and of itself. I believe my Nalgene at home is either a 2 (High Density PolyEthylene) or a 5 (PolyPropylene), though I'm not 100% sure which, both of which are safe.

    Stay away from reusing anything that's a (1?,) 3 or a 6, as far as I remember. Stay away from reusing anything that's a 7 without further info as to what that 7 is. Nalgenes should be just fine.

    (references:
    http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plastic_recycling#Plastic_Identification_Code
    http://www.nalgene-outdoor.com/technical/bpaInfo.html
    and an article from the National Geographic's "Green Guide" that isn't freely available online)

    \n Soda bottles are 1 (PET or PETE, usually PETE). The "soft" Nalgene bottled are 2 (HDPE). Part of the problem with HDPE is that it absorbs flavors and passes them off to the next contents (which is part of why early in the days of recycling there was a lot of blather about milk bottles if I'm remembering right). Water in HDPE just seems to get funky much faster than in PETE or PC.

    \n According to the studies,Nalgene bottles are the ones among those to be concerned about. They are polycarbonate (Lexan), which has been shown to leach bisphenol A, which has been linked to chromosomal aberrations in mice. Nalgene begs to differ.

    SIGG bottles have aluminum and stainless steel. The aluminum bottles are lined with a "secret formula" to stop any leaching.
