Posted By: Mike HolmesBut, yes, a lot of the original COC ethic is gamist. That is, the idea is that you're trying to find the bad-guys, and destroy them, and your abilities as a player are being tested in the process. It's a Lovecraftian Dungeon Crawl, because that's all anybody knew back then. Note that COC is a massive failure at this, as well, because your odds of succeeding are nil. Worse, there's usually no player tactics allowed in the vast majority of the situations. If the GM calls for a climbing roll, and you fail, fall, and die, that's hardly something that the player could have prevented through good tactical play (which leads to the rope-climbing problem, which I can address elsewhere).

This is all covered in Bryan Bankhead's rants on this precise subject over at The Forge (three long essays).
\nBy the way, here's the links for Brian's rants (from late 2003). I had replies on the first and third.

Drifting to R'lyeh:Facing the Problems with Call of Cthulhu
Cthulhu's Clues: Why failure is not an option
Hot Lead and Hypocrisy: a rant on guns in Call of Cthulhu

Frankly, I think Brian shot himself in the foot from his first post, in that he constructed an argument based on GNS as to why Call of Cthulhu in practice is so unpopular -- that everyone hates it. To anyone who is familiar with the larger picture of gaming, this is blatantly wrong. CoC is enormously popular. In 1998, 8% of all gamers were playing it monthly, and it was the #8 among all RPGs -- this from a game 17 years old with no external market force.

I agree with Mike that the original design was more concentrated on flavorful challenge -- i.e. gamism. I'd also agree that it is high lethality -- i.e. PCs will die often due to die rolls. What I'm puzzled by is the idea that death in general and random death specifically mean that gamism is a failure.

For example, huge numbers of video games are built where the chance of succeeding is virtually nil. They are deliberately designed to kill you off, and if you do well then they just try harder and harder to kill you off. Yet people enjoy them. People will often enjoy them even if there is no point score displayed -- they'll play to see how far they get or how many opponents they can take out before they go.

From my view, Call of Cthulhu as designed is a strategic, mystery-based game. It appeals to people who are interested in more strategic, investigative thinking as opposed to ticky, rule-based tactics. In a well-designed scenario, you are automatically provided with enough information to get into real trouble and be killed. Good rolls and a clever approach (especially in where you go and what preparations you make) will give you more information to help you survive. Still, you should expect casualties regardless. i.e. This is more like poker than chess.

There were and are many problems in it as originally published: Handling of longer time scales, the whiff factor, and critically the art of scenario design. However, the lethality is part of the appeal of Call of Cthulhu, not a flaw.

    \nI don't think CoC particularly supports the idea that you have to make a climbing roll or die, or any other single roll for that matter. The game is quite explicit that players should get a warning, albeit not necessarily much of one, and if they proceed they do so at risk. That's part of the appeal, playing smart so as to avoid instakill situations. Mike's quote does not reflect the game as written IMO, it reflects the game as filtered through a whole load of later advice which is not present in the actual book.

    But to be honest, Bryan's rants for me fall foul of a classic error. If a game has has survived for years and has seen tons of play and is much loved by many, then the odds are it is a good game. The fact that one may not oneself have had those results with it simply suggests it is not a good game for you, not that it is per se a bad game.

    The alternative is silly, if a game (D&D, CoC, whatever) has years of widespread play behind it then it seems to me the simplest hypothesis is that it is a good game which for many delivers fun play. Any other hypothesis seems to me to fall foul of Occam's razor. We end up with hypotheses that people aren't really enjoying themselves when they think they are, which is a far more extraordinary hypothesis than the idea that a few people just don't get the game.

    CoC, like D&D or for that matter Vampire, is a conspicuously successful rpg. I think if one wants to understand it, one can't just ignore that fact.

    \n jenskot hit hte nail on the head. I would never consider BRP a "good system." What was popular was Lovecraft and that particular brand of horror. It has spawned all sorts of merchandising and sustains a gaming subculture. It's not because rolling percentile dice was brilliant.

    CoC is not gamist. Nor is it's predesessor in GUMSHOE. Death and instanity is not there to make a statement about how your characters' succeed, rather to really make a game about how your character's fail. The game is ABOUT going insane. It reflects HPL's fiction rather well in that your character is not really expected to get to the end in one piece and really becomes more like the zombie addage about running faster that the guy behind you.

    Now designers and players over the years confused this intent. I like GUMSHOE and I think it fits the genre well, but in using it for CoC specifically I think one misses the point of CoC - to lose. I think CoC is all about "does your character see the end of the world." Otherwise it becomes Hellboy - "sure we see it and we're there to stop it." CoC is a sort of nihilist play. It's not about finding clues and stopping the cultists.

    I can not stress this enough: CoC is about the gotchas and the character's dealing with their compatriots dying and going insane. Look at the text. I am in the 2001 20th Anniversary Edition. The lionshare of the book is about setting - lists of spells (and how they drive you insane), lists of critters (and how they kill you or drive you insane), lists of books (and...well you get it). Out of a 320 page book the rules go from pg 51-88 (37 pages). Of that 10 are about killing one another and 11 are about going mad.

    Now I agree that when put into the hands of 15 year olds and people innundated by dungeon crawls it can easily revert to a gamist perspective - a lot of games can because they were all pretty much written in the same manner back then. It was a matter of momentum. But I would say that if that's the case then they weren't reading carefully enough or weren't savvy enough to understand the goals here.

    As many of us have, I ran CoC for years and years. It was mostly a series of one shots to fill in holes for other campaigns or if I ran a campiagn it was a ensemble cast with a stable to draw new PCs from.

    Now could CoC have been done better mechanically to drive it's themes forward? No doubt! In fact I wonder if I could hack Heroes Banner to create a CoC game... hmmmm.

    \n I think the justification for the BRP combat system in CoC would be something like this. We're not emulating a Lovecraftian story, only the world in which those stories are set.     IMO preserving characters from death where it isn't part of a player chosen sacrifice fundamentally undermines the concept of the game, the story the game is designed to help you tell. Moreover, I don't think it reflects the underlying point of the stories.

    At any moment we may die, even as I type this a blood vessel could burst in my brain and it would be as if I never was. HPL's fiction is a dramatisation of existentialism, a realisation that the universe is indifferent to us and our concerns. CoC helps manifest this by making the game system, the rules of the game universe, equally indifferent. I think if you change that you lose the essence of what HPL was seeking to achieve.

    \n Max, no worries about the tone of your post, which seems fine to me.

    I'm not sure I agree with the content, however. This idea that because Lovecraft describes an uncaring universe, the game mechanics should somehow reflect that, is often touted, but seldom fully justified.     To a degree, it depends how much we are interested in recreating Lovecraftian stories, and how much simulating the world they describe.       \n I think these are all good questions for nearly any game (answers no doubt differ game to game).

      But what's your aim in setting up the situation?
      I aim to accomplish two things in a session: hard choices and escalating horror.

      I will list four or five hard choices that the PCs are likely to encounter, mostly in the beginning of the game. This accomplishes two things. It allows the players to feel like they have some control over the situation and to establish moment of realization that they sometimes made their own beds, so to speak. Other hard choices are made extemporaneously.

      Escalating horror means that the adventure is a string of events designed to inspire SANITY checks. It is done within the context of a storyline and starts small and builds to more dangerous checks. I start with mundane events (screams, animals behaving oddly, murders, interviews with the insane, etc), go to more supernatural oddness (hints of critters in the periphery, cultist ceremoneis, scraps of mythos research, odd insects or unexplained marks on a body) then it culminates to the blatant supernaturals for those that have thus survived to experience (cultists with supernatural powers, mythos tomes, minion critters, sighting of old ones in mirrors, etc).

      On what basis do you call for dice rolls?
      My criteria for anyone rolling dice is this:
      - each player generally only rolls once or twice per scene and narration happens based on that roll.
      - players declare their intentions based on the situation. I either create conflict and have them roll or narrate according to their intentions.
      - if conflict arises then I set the consequences for failure (sometimes secretly but more often openly) read the dice and narrate based on the whole picture.

      When people start chasing the PCs, swinging Khopesh or firing Tommy guns at the characters i will usually reveal wounds and/or deaths in dramatic moments. I will set a scene so that they realize what's happening when the action lulls or they realize a character is slowed or dead when it critical that they get away fast.

      What's your overall approach?
      I frame most everything as if we were watching a movie. I think about things as storyboards and frames, stills to start the action out in and then the game itself and the players interactions get the film rolling.

      Almost everything is on the table. I have a few secrets I keep from the players but almost everything is laid out for them to experience to build suspense. They are privy to cultist's conversations, kidnappings or a flashbacks to how someone went bonkers.

      What makes a session good or bad for you?
      Smiles, shudders, gesticulating. Are we having fun? Are their lulls where we're just looking at one another? Is the story clicking? Are they talking to one another in character? When it's over are they excited to play the next one? Do they recall great moments in the game? Do they talk about decisions they could have made and lament the fallen?
