    \n The obvious one to start with is Bacchanal. You roll the various dice in your cup and use them to inspire your narration; also, the result tells you whether to add or remove dice.

    Example from the text: "If Pluto is the high die, add a Soldier die to your glass. And describe how
    the scene resolves itself with the commission of a crime.".

    Note how the text is vague: "...with the commission of a crime" rather than, say, "...with someone present being murdered". I think that's important.

    Another game to look at is Terra Nova, by one Robert Poppe. You draw cards, which inspire a scene of hardship between polar explorers.

    This game, I think, is less successful. I personally feel it's because the rules are too restrictive: every scene is a hardship, revolving around either Discipline, Hope or Strength. For me, that'd be OK for a few scenes, but then things would start repeating. You don't see Robert Poppe around much these days, so perhaps he gave up after this game*.

    Also, and this is totally irrational, I like Bacchanal's dice rolling, which seems elegant, as opposed to Terra Nova's card drawing, which seems too random. This preference of min makes no sense.

    \n Sorry guys,

    Fortune = using some random element in resolution (as opposed to drama or karma or whatever). In my case playing cards bid by players.

    At the beginning = before the conflict is initiated or played out. This is pretty rare, which is the point of the thread.
    In the middle = usually after a decisive point of opposition has been reached mid-conflict. Stake-setting systems often use this.
    At the end = after the action has been completed, just determining outcome. This is the traditional approach, I think.

    I may be using these terms incorrectly, who knows? But those are the definitions I'm using for the purpose of this conversation.

    So in Medical Hospital, it'll go like this: Me and Joel have a scene together, which means we'll have a conflict. We each put forward some cards from our hands. It's a drama scene, I put forward more spades and win. The specific cards I put forward point to situation elements. Joel has the highest total card, so he uses those elements and frames the scene. We roleplay it, knowing that my guy is going to get what he wants.

    \n OK Jason, thanks for the clarification. Getting back on track:

    >>1. Games that successfully use fortune-at-the-beginning
    So, if I got the terms right, you'll want to look at Pretender by Kirt Dankmeyer. It has either FiB or early FiM. You basically go and play until you come to a point where you want to do something. At which point you roll a bunch of dice (2, 5, 12, or 99) and group the dice into sets any way you want, then assign them to 7-8 categories: Stuff like "Did you succeed? Did you take Damage? How 'cool/spectacular' was your success? How much did it influence hidden plot agendas? Who gets to narrate the outcome?" I can't remember all the columns, just that they were cool, and I pretty much stole that system wholesale for an early draft of The Ghost Killers ("Flip Mode", which I ended up recently discarding because even though I like it it just didn't fit what I wanted).

    I would go as far as to say "it's stupid-easy to run: Find a copy and play or run it as a one shot some evening". That will show you some of the potential pitfalls.

    2. Your thoughts on the perils and opportunities of completely front-loading conflict resolution

    That if you don't think about how to bring in influence of other people, or actually inject "roleplay" into the conflict, then the conflict can probably end up looking like "The Die Roller's Story Hour":

    "OK, so I succeed. So my guy says to your guy "Gimmie the disk", and your guy is all "No way!", but then I wrestle him to the ground, and then Jake comes in but sees what's going on and says "Shit!" and dives for cover. But then I elbow your guy in the face and make off with my disk, jumping on a snowmobile and riding hard back to the military base. You get in another snowmobile and try to follow me, but I've already shot out the bridge behind me yadda yadda yadda."

    You're roleplaying, back-and-forth, character interaction, until conflict goes down, then pretty much the narrator takes over and tells a story: A defined story where they can't take liberties or cock-block other characters, but still one person yapping on for a long time while the rest sit patiently, listening.

    3. Techniques for making this cool, building tension, etc.

    The end result is defined, but what is sacrificed/done along the way is not defined. That would replace the normal tension of "did I succeed or not?"

    Making sure that one person becomes the Card Lord, with control over how the card are interpreted, so that you don't have a lot of back-and-forth bending and derailing by players. And make sure the others all roleplay it out, in character, even though the end result is revealed.

    -Andy

    Fortune-in-the-Beginning is kind of problematic, because it almost always could be argued that there's something happening before the Fortune part. In the extreme case, Vincent argued that there is no such thing as Fortune in-the-Beginning, because you have to make the decision to roll the dice before actually rolling.

    \n

    I prefer to define it differently. Here are my summaries of the three methods:

    \n
      \n
    • FatE: set up the conflict, choose your strategy and modifiers, then resolve it. Works best for task resolution systems.
      • \n
    • FitM: set up conflict, roll, then make strategy decisions on how to apply your dice. Works best for conflict resolution systems.
      • \n
    • FitB: roll the dice to establish resources available, then set up conflicts, maneuver, and resolve based on this. Works best for systems that require random inspiration.
      • \n
    \n

    I've tinkered with FitB in most of my designs, although again some would argue that, because I have people state what they are trying to do in general before rolling the dice, it's FitM. Plus, I like doing the "skills and advantages = rerolls" thing, so that kind of makes it look more like FitM. I define it as: FitB to set the stage, Karma in the Middle to resolve the conflict, possibly leading to FitM rerolls.

    \n

    Jason, it sounds like you're starting out the same way I do (Fortune sets up the scene,) but actually resolving the conflict then, too. What the scene actually resolves is what the details were. players might get the feeling that it's really a card game plus some talking. Is there an opportunity for the loser to reduce the loss, by trading away some of the details to the winner? Is there an opportunity for the winner to sacrifice getting some of what was won, in order to veto some descriptions or add elements to the scene?

    \n Yeah, Orx is about one of the only actual FitB games out there. It's a very strange concept. Basically you have to decide to have a resolution before you know what it is that you're trying to resolve. The only methods I've seen for this are typically scene-based. Like "At the beginning of each scene, roll the dice" then figure out what conflict is going on in the scene, then resolve it from the combination of the two effects.
    \n
    \nOne I could imagine is something like, "A player can spend a token to cause a conflict to occur. First roll the dice to see who wins. Then decide what it is that was at stake, and resolve."
    \n
    \nFitB exists pretty much because people discovered the existence of FitM as a response to FatE. But that's like saying that the McDonald's small fries must exist because there are medium and large size fries. Sure, one can conceptualize what it's like. But that's far from saying that it's a really useful idea.
    \n
    \nWhich is to say that I find it odd that people persue the idea as a design element just because it's been proposed as a fill-in on a spectrum. For instance, if they had come up with Fortune Before for FitM (refering to it being done before other mechanical manipulations) and Fortune After (refering to it being done after the other mechanical manipulations had occured), then we wouldn't even be having this discussion.
    \n
    \nThat's not to say that people shouldn't design such games. But it is to say that working off a terminological oddity is no way to come up with all of the possibilities. For instance, this spectrum also assumes things like narraton of the resolution comes after everything else. Maybe that can be turned on it's head? Or any other part of how resolutions may occur in a RPG.
    \n
    \nBut, largely, the two major paradigms here work just fine.

    Mike:

    \n

    To a certain extent, I agree. I think the obsession with new ways of doing things, which includes the desire to create FitB, is in part a hangover of an old attitude of "I need to do something completely different!" Thing is, dice mechanics or other randomization methods are the least important part of any game. The truly exciting new territory in RPGs is play structure; there's plenty of room for novelty there.

    \n

    But on the other hand, sometimes you need to do something more efficiently, or need to do something that hasn't been truly explored before. In those cases, you should explore new techniques if, in theory, they seem to fit the needs of what you need to accomplish.

    \n

    I think that, if you are talking about resolution, you are completely correct about FitM and FatE being sufficient for everything. However, if you are talking about rolling dice/drawing cards for something, like generation (as I suggested,) FitB is perfectly fine and may in fact be a better fit.
