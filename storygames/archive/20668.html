Brawl: Unarmed, light blunt weapons. Basically anything bruce lee would use.

Blades: Swords, Daggers and Axes

Heavy: Maces, Morningstars, Polearms, Cloud's Buster Sword. All heavy weapons have some degree of armor piercing.

Reach: Semi-ranged weapons, as throwing knives, chains and whips. Most ninja weapons.

Ranged: Anything that shoots projectiles, from bows and slings to flintlock guns.

Is there anything I'm forgetting? any ideas of how could I word the "brawl weapons" without resorting to the Bruce Lee comparison? Makes sense from a realistic point of view to separate the Brawl and the Heavy blunt weapons?

    \n From a realistic pedagogical perspective (as in, how and what people learn when they master the use of weapons) these categories do not make much sense, I think. I want to emphasize that this may not be a problem for you (you may have other overriding reasons for prioritizing these categories), but if it is, I would recommend an entirely different starting point in that case - perhaps something like unarmed/melee/shooting/exotic (with the specific expanse of the latter bought separately to cover whatever exotic forms of fighting the character might practice).

    Specifically, as you asked about the separation between heavy and light blunt weapons: in real life the fighting techniques involved in using a mace are more similar to those used with a sword than they are to those used with a polearm; a sword is likewise more similar to a mace than a dagger; finally, a dagger is more similar to boxing than it is to a sword. Thus, from a realistic point of view I think you'd be best off splitting the "Blades" category between "Brawl" and "Heavy", and renaming "Heavy" to reflect the fact that it covers basically all combat with melee weapons. Maybe split "Heavy" into two if you think that the special nature of two-handed polearms merits a separate skill.

    (Think that "Melee" is too broad? In that case, on realistic grounds, you'd probably want to split it into three categories: one-handed weapons, top-heavy weapons and two-handed weapons. Despite all of those being reasonably suited for general melee, they each involve certain broad principles that differ from the other categories.)

    As for the coverage, I suppose a net used as a weapon would fall into "Reach" because it's not anything else. That would go for most exotic weapons, I suppose. I think you're covering most things here.

    I don't know about how to improve the description of "Brawl". If the conceptual definition of the category really is "light blunt weapons", then I think that pretty much suffices as a description as well - I understand what it covers and what it doesn't, at least. Maybe you could explain the reasoning further for the reader, as they may come to wonder what makes light blunt weapons so special as to merit their own weapon skill category...

    \n Ancient weaponry was all about range, where the advantage in terms of weapon vs weapon is first about the distance and then about the material. After that you have "control of the tactical situation" as a main factor to decide which weapon is more useful in determined circumstances.

    So, gaining experience in the use of a weapon gives you a better grasp of your reach vs the enemy reach, as well as giving you more options to gain or recover tactical advantage when you're in reach disadvantage.

    But that's just another viewpoint into the subject, it would also make sense that once you learn how to wield a blunt lever-powered weapon you could wield anything as long as you have enough strenght to lift it. Swords share some of the principles but offer other advantages and difficulties, as well as daggers. Having practiced martial arts myself it makes sense to me to give them a separate place, as they compensate a lack of range with the extra mobility, tactic and knowledge about how and where to hit an opponent to maximize the damage.

    Perhaps you could use mixed categories? Like an axe would be heavy+blade, a dagger light+blade, a tonfa would be light+blunt, etc.

    \n What are you going for with separate categories and improvement tracks? Is this a strategic balancing game, where being good at different things confers different advantages in different situations? If so, then your desired categories depend on how you differentiate situations. If play is about the differences between fighting spear-wielding dudes and knife-wielding dudes, then cool, your rules and categories should reflect that. But if a spear fight and a knife fight are basically the same, then you should probably focus elsewhere. Do you have inspirations for what sorts of fights and encounters you want your players to go through?

    Or maybe my guess is off and it's not about tactics, but about flavor? With just a teeny bit of practical difference thrown in to reward specialization and reinforce niches?

    Without knowing your general play goals and any specific technical goals you may have, I'd propose these weapon/unarmed types: grappling, striking, ambush, punishing, demon-bound, elemental. I enjoy the differences between those situations. I can also enjoy a good spears vs knives combat, but only if the rules for maneuvering in and out of range are interesting.

    \n I agree with David, above: what are your goals for creating this taxonomy?

    That will determine what kinds of answers would be most useful to you. Are you going for niche protection, game balance, stylistic unity, simulation of historical training, colourful styles, or something else?

    I have two suggestions, just off the top of my head:

    * Perhaps, rather than talk about the type of weapon used, look at a cultural/background of familiarity. A knight might be equally comfortable with a sword or a lance (even though they're very different weapons), whereas a Mongolian horse archer might be great with a bow (from horseback, no less!) or a short chopping sword. A street brawler could use a table leg without problem, but might feel out of sorts with a sword.

    So, for example, during character creation, you might pick your background/training and that would come with a list of familiar weapons. For other weapons, you could simply default to "unfamiliar" (with some appropriate penalty or whatever), or you could have a brief rubric, like:

    - Is the weapon of an unfamiliar style or construction? -1.
    - Is the weapon similar to something you are experienced with? If not, -1.

    So, a familiar weapon is best (no penalty), a sword from a different culture is a -1, and a weird weapon you have no experience with is at -2.

    Then you don't need a strict taxonomy; you could either have a list of weapons ("here are the weapons used in Southern France during the 12th century") or make an educated guess on the fly ("Yeah, this would be familiar to an Orcish warrior from the North, but it's made by Elves, so -1.").

    * When I've attempted this kind of thing in the past, I've tried to separate all the possible weapons into strict categories - this one belongs here, that one belongs there. After some thought, I decided this was unproductive (too many weapons were borderline cases). So I would advise that, if you do this, you have provisions for overlapping categories and weapons which fit in several categories.

    That will make it cleaner and easier, and means you don't HAVE to cover every possible weapon with a set number of categories, as well. (Which, otherwise, will force you to place some weapons in categories that don't QUITE fit, and take them out of others where they arguably could. Would an archer be better with a crossbow, or a rifleman? I don't know.)

    \n You could think about this the way that wargamers do. As an example, WRG6 classified weapons like this:

    Melee weapons:

    SA sidearms, e.g. swords, daggers, clubs, maces, etc.
    2SA Two sidearms, one in each hand
    2HCW 2-handed cutting or concussing weapons, e.g. great swords, great axes, 2-handed clubs
    2HCT 2-handed cut and thrust weapons, e.g. pole ax, glaive, halberd
    LTS Long thrusting spears, one handed; can be grounded against cavalry charges
    P Pikes, two-handed long thrusting weapon; can be grounded against cavalry charges
    JLS Javelins & Light spears, one-handed short poking spear that can be thrown
    L Lances
    HTW Heavy throwing weapons & swords combination, e.g. pillum, etc. used just before entering combat.
    IPW Improvised peasant weapons

    Missle weapons:

    B bow
    LB longbow
    CB crossbow
    Sl sling
    SS staff sling
    D darts

    The categories are based somewhat on effect but mostly on method of use, I think.

    \n I like Eero's idea. The only downside I see is if multiple players get inspired to make weapons that are too similar to each other. To avoid that, I think you could list a number of specialist categories, and then have some bidding or dice or whatever determine who gets first, second, third, etc. pick of their specialist category.

    These categories could be the five you suggested, or they could describe stylistic niches instead, like overwhelming, polished, athletic, sneaky, and vicious. Sometimes the difference between an elegant fencing sword and an armor-cleaving sword describes two characters better than the difference between a sword and an axe.

    If I were running with the basic idea of your five categories, I guess I'd do:
    close - fists, bass knuckles, knives, tonfa, blackjack
    flexible - whips, chains, nunchucks, flails
    crushing - maces, spiked maces, clubs, a log with a nail in it, warhammers, giant swords & axes
    blades - swords from pretty small to pretty big
    reach - spears
    staves - staves
    thrown - knives, shuriken, hand axes, darts
    missile - arrows and slings

    To me, these all have distinct flavor, and having 8 means no one's likely to get stuck with one they don't like.

    Any left unused could get folded into the ones that are used. If no one takes "thrown" then the "missile" player gets thrown weapons. If no one takes "staves", then the "reach" player gets staves... unless the "crushing" player wants it more and it fits their concept better.

    Or maybe just use Eero's idea and tell the players "nothing too close to each other."

    Here's what I was thinking; not sure how well my category names express it:

    overwhelming - max effort, relentless, too large or strong to be resisted, impressive, scary, possibly sloppy or sometimes out of control (example: huge muscled guy with huge battle axe)
    polished - deft master of skills, wins via perfected technique, fights without apparent exertion, can probably be suave or charming or talking while fighting (example: master fencer)
    athletic - uses acrobatics, spins, jumps, strikes from odd angles, props, furniture, environment, superior balance and quickness to gain advantage (example: a lot of Tony Jaa and Jackie Chan stuff)
    sneaky - master of stealth and surprise, great shot, good with quick and quiet weapons, can set enemies up by misleading them (example: classic ninja or D&D thief)
    vicious - maximizes harm to opponent with poisons, acid, fire, dark magic, etc., expert in called shots to eyes, groin, pressure points, etc., could either be calculating pro or unhinged animal type (example: Sabretooth)

    \n Sharp, Heavy, Quick, Long, Ranged, Weird. Each weapon has one or two (or more!) categories.

    Sword: Sharp
    Huge club: Heavy
    Staff: Long
    Dagger: Quick and Sharp
    Spear, polearm: Long and Sharp
    Throwing stars: Quick, Ranged and Sharp
    Battle fan: Quick and Weird
    Whip: Long and Weird

    This way you could get a +1 for each category you master, and -1 for each you don't. So if you're good with Sharp weapons, you get +1 with a sword, +/-0 with a knife and -2 with a battle fan. If you've mastered Sharp and Quick, you suddenly get +2 with the dagger and +/-0 with the battle fan.

    Genre-appropriately, the stranger the weapon, the more difficult it is to handle, but the higher potential bonus you can get.
