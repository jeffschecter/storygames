\n Long story short, I am in a position to host a panel on "indie" games, focusing on techniques / what we can learn kind of thing.

I'm excited about this! I think it has potential.

I'm looking for shortcuts to my understanding, not things I will repeat verbatim to the audience or anything, just organizational tools for me.

So i'm trying to come up with what "insert game here" can Reveal, what it Allows, and what it Exposes.

Essentially, Puppetland "Reveals" our dependence on out of character dialogue. It highlights that flow of information that we take for granted, and shows us something about how we play all games.
It "Allows" us to stay in character all the time, and let the Storyteller officiate with rules we all know and understand. We can safely push and pull on the story without risking on dice.
But it also "Exposes" how we don't often share the same understanding or interpretation of things. It can happen, by this I mean, that Jeff believes one thing about the setting or the scene, based on what has been said, and that the Storyteller or another player can shut Jeff's plans or ideas down because he misunderstood. It's particularly harder hitting in Puppetland, because the Storyteller is the final word. Also it exposes us to judging one another on how eloquently we speak. It's just a danger we accept when playing this game.

If you have a game that I should expose people to in this panel (you wrote it? you just love playing it? you never played it but you read it and thought it was cool?), and you want to help me understand what it does, please hit me up here (or whisper).

It doesn't explicitly have to be a Reveal / Allow / Expose list, again. This is just my learning tool.

Thanks in advance!

    \n I'll try to start:
    Burning Wheel reveals (ugh, already don't like the verb... pushing on) that characters in fiction are driven by Beliefs and, more narrowly, goals reflected by Beliefs. It allows a player to assume an authorial role over their character while still "doing roleplaying" as we expect it to be: acting the part, declaring actions, reacting to GM content, and so forth. The Artha system ensures that Belief-driven play is rewarded; and in doing so, it exposes character designs that are not driven by Beliefs or goals pointed directly at the current fiction (i.e., encourages rewriting to better "ping" them).

    Is that sort of what you wanted?

    \n Think less in terms of "what you do" and more in terms of "how you engage media," Monsterhearts:

    Monsterhearts reveals how relationships work in teen monster drama, it allows you to deconstruct and subvert the genre, and simultaneously it exposes why the genre is valid as it stands (or, put differently, it allows you to validate the genre).

    "reveals" and "exposes" are very similar verbs, are they not?

    \n Hmmm. There are a handful of approaches to this, but here's one I think fits Dungeon World.

    Dungeon World reveals how the fictional outcomes of a game (in this case, dungeon crawl adventure) are not tied to the system in an inherent way. It allows you to play out traditional-style dungeon crawl stories without having to engage traditional mechanisms. It exposes that there are many paths to the same story, in the end.
