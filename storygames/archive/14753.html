I'm looking to create new out-of-the-box ideas, crazy fun ideas, and rules that become primarily simple and elegant in play.

I'm also looking at the various other supers RPG systems out there, not to mention other action oriented RPGs, for fun ideas - of what's come before.

I'd love to open a thought here for anyone with anything unique and cool or ideas from other past game engines. Anything you'd like to share for a collective inspiration fun to brainstrom and spin off of!

Anyone? Any ideas?

    Here's a supers game:

    \n

    CHARACTER CREATION

    \n

    Name your guy. Your superhero name first.

    \n

    Come up with a word for your powers - 'super', 'bat', 'speed', whatever. Just one word.

    \n

    Make a list of people/places/things that you don't want to get hurt. You can be all Peter Parker about this and just name like your family and girlfriend, or you can be all some other guy and name things like "Justice," or you can be Batman and pick "My pride." I don't care.

    \n

    Now name your guy, regular name.

    \n

    PLAYING

    \n

    Take turns. When it's your turn, say that something terrible is about to happen to something someone else has listed on their guy's list. Now the heroes get to intervene. It doesn't count as intervening if you don't use your power word. The person who intervenes awesomestly wins.

    \n There's a lightweight supers game in the form of a comicbook: The Supercrew. (I think this has some potential for your purposes. Some other RPGs: Hearts & Souls, With Great Power (OOP), and Sage's work-in-progress, Powers for Good. (Which is in-progress, but still a cool project.)

    Comics mean a lot of things to various people. I'm curious which comics you want to model.

    ...This is part of why I like the kernel of Shreyas's idea here. It's a fiction creation engine that adapts itself to the whatever superheroic zeitgeist when it comes to the player. Improvised fictional position needs to be grounded *anyway* by players being on the same page (in terms of cultural references), so we needn't rely on rules to impose on that positioning. Instead, focus on whatever issues are the ones that drive play. (There's also some neat gamesmanship here: if you want your character to matter more, you have to care about more things that are at risk in play.)

    A boost to the haptics of ShreyaSupers: these are clearly better suited as index cards as part of the content engine.

    1. Each player picks their own color of index cards.
    2. Each player writes one issues on each colored index card. These are added to the event deck.
    3. Each player takes 1d6 index cards and simply writes the word "ACTION!" on them. Add these to the event deck. This is for some randomization and to interpolate the action.
    4. Shuffle the event deck.

    On your turn to frame a scene, draw from the deck. Scene frame and you're ready. You can adjust the number of action cards to fit the kind of comicbook: 1d2 index cards if this issues was written by JMS, 4d10 if it was inked by Liefeld.

    \n Chris, I can't speak fork ev, but yeah, there is definitely uncovered ground in those. Mostly in how they approach the heroes. Where's the game that does superhero stories, not superhero 'simulation,' in a fast-playing package, which actually encourages play as superheroes, not just guys with superpowers? I don't think any of those create anything like Morrison's JLA, which is pretty much by benchmark for the subject (I've written a bit about it).

    My own take on it is that:

    a) Characters need to mostly be about how you describe your character's actions, not relative power. Batman takes down four superpowered aliens that can each beat Superman, for ex. Neither Supes nor Bats is "stronger," they just have different current resources and different abilities they channel those resources through.

    b) The team needs to be a solid thing that influences the game

    c) The game needs to be tightly focused on one type of superheroes. The game that does Captain Britain and MI-13 is badly-suited for Runaways.

    d) The GM needs real, concrete, moment-to-moment tools to create opposition and threats with.


    I've been working on the game that does these things, Powers For Good. Expect more on it soon.
