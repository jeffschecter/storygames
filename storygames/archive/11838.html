\n It's time for another "Why Weren't You at PAX?" thread on Story-Games! This time, the question is directed at the East Coast design/publishing community.
\n
\nPAX's first East Coast event was held this weekend in Boston at the Hynes Convention center. It was like having Gen Con in my backyard.
\n
\nWhy weren't you there pimping your game?
\n
\nI was there pimping your games. I had people begging me for your games. I didn't have nearly enough product to sell to everyone who wanted some.
\n
\nAnother crazy phenomenon in the PAX community: "Indie" isn't a dirty word. You say "indie" to them and they get it -- "small, scrappy, weird and different." That was an eye opener!
\n
\n-Luke

    \n Selling at PAX was a completely different experience! The crowd is super friendly and interested. Many of them have little experience with tabletop RPGs but are interested and open to the experience. Many of the ones who are experienced are not fixated on the idea that there's only one way to play them. They're open to new ideas and ready to embrace them.

    And because video games are the primary draw, they're used to games costing $60 or more. To them, our games are inexpensive.

    The con staff and volunteers are also incredibly nice and helpful.

    We started the con on Friday with a table overflowing with indie games and ended with only 17 books of various games by the time we closed up shop on Sunday.

    We severely underestimated the amount of product that we brought. We sold out of all Luke's books (Burning Wheel, Monster Burner, Magic Burner, Burning Empires, Blossoms Are Falling and Jihad) by early Saturday afternoon. Joshua sold out of all his copies of Shock by Saturday afternoon and all his copies of the Human Contact preview edition by Sunday morning. Fiasco, gone. Dread, gone. Shab-al-hiri Roach, gone. Reign, gone. Wild Talents, gone. Plenty of other things too.

    People were standing in line for 30 to 45 minutes, just to jump into the demos we were running.

    \n This doesn't surprise me. I have found that "indie" games as Luke describes them above are actually much easier to explain to "gamers" who focus on games as games. That is to say video gamers, board/card gamers and a certain brand of hardcore D&D player. That's because they are used to thinking of games purely in terms of an abstracted organized activity with an agenda instead of some of the crazier rpg-gamer think about "realism" and "modeling" of various kinds. So they look at some of the "stranger" rules of an indie game and barely blink an eye. They look at the rules and ask themselves, "does this look a fun set of levers and pulleys to play with" rather than, "does this accurately portray my personal ideal of an RPG "should" do."

    Jesse

    \n It's about curiosity, in my experience. Newer/modern* gamers who self-describe as gamers are often willing and eager to try out games in general \u2014 they have less interest in aligning themselves with one game for a long-term relationship. They're able to identify as gamers, first, and as fans of particular titles or milieus second. And they're building a repertoire so they can stay in the conversation. I think it's a healthy way to approach gaming, personally, and I'm thrilled to see conventions growing that embrace not quite fandom but curiosity about new games.

    I say this without having gone to PAX, though, so maybe I don't know nothing. I'm speaking more about the communities that PAX attracts, as I know them. Correct me if I'm wrong.

    *(I know this may be loaded or imprecise language, but it's all I've got in my kit right now. I'm just looking for a positive descriptor to use.)

    This is a blanket "you."
    \n(edit: cross-posted with walkerp, who asked the same thing with a bunch more brevity...)

    Ok, I'm gonna drift the thread a little bit because I'm just a game player. I'm not a publisher/designer, so I am not the person Luke is talking to. But I do live within a 10 minute walk of PAX.

    First, it's super awesome to hear that there's lots of new gamer energy there, but I don't need new gamers or new games! I need more time to play the games I know with the gamers I know but see frustratingly little of.

    So, what's the vibe like for someone like me? How easy is it to just show up and play rpgs? I'm not into RPGA at all and I get all the D&D I need in my regular group. Do I have to plan my experience and deal with scheduling and signups and tickets and other crap? Is there any space for unscheduled events? Is there a critical mass of free floaters like me there? How loud is it in there?

    Should I expect that if I want to play a particular niche-y game--IAWA, say--I'm also going to have to facilitate it? Should I expect that I'm going to have to teach that game to the majority of the players as well? I don't mind either teaching or facilitating, but if I were really into those things, I'd find a volunteer gig as a demo dude and help cut down those 45min lines that Thor was mentioning. (Actually, I might do that, if a game I really really love wants demo help.)

    Will I have to hustle for players and pitch games repeatedly? I'm not real good at elevator pitches for games -- I had a bunch of trouble pitching Montsegur this pass weekend. And when I sell people on playing games with me, especially people who don't know me well, I feel kinda responsible for making sure they have a good time, and that can drain energy fast.

    Are all my friends going to be treating PAX as a working con and not a playing con? If I'm there to hang out and Ogre's there to sell Combat Diaries, I won't hang with Ogre unless it's in a professional capacity -- like buying or demoing Combat Diaries. Am I better off just crashing the PAX afterparties, when all the publishers I know might be into playing some games?

    These are unreasonable questions to ask anyone since they're so much about my personal preferences, but that's how the go-to-PAX thought process plays out in my head.

      What do you mean?

      \n

      We were one of, what? a dozen? vendor booths. We weren't one of hundreds. It's good to be a big fish in a little pond, I guess, particularly when you're right next to another pond, full of big fish that don't want to get in your pond at all.

      \n

      Here's a very typical interaction, in the form of another playlet:

      \n
      \n

      SETTING: A couple sidles up to the Compleat Strategist booth, looking furtively.

      \n

      Me: Hi! How ya doin?

      \n

      Dude: (suddenly friendly and moving closer with his partner) Great! I'm having a great time!

      \n

      Me: (facing woman in the couple, of which there were many) What are you guys into?

      \n

      Woman: We're looking for something different.

      \n

      Dude: (smiles sheepishly)

      \n

      Me: Huh! Different from what?

      \n

      Woman: Well, like everyone [multiple people said this to me], we play a lot of D&D, but we want something with characters and a story.

      \n

      Me: Cool! What kind of fiction are you into?

      \n

      ... and from there, the interaction was unique.

      \n
      \n

      We were literally offering the different thing there. Person after person came up to us to find what a new, fun thing was. Indie video games were making a big splash there. I think folks were excited to learn that there are indie RPGs, too.

      \n The first few years, PAX was the last weekend in August, while Bumbershoot is always Labor Day weekend. In 2008, the two weekends coincided, because Labor Day was Monday September 1. I thought that was just a fluke, but the following year PAX was clearly scheduled for Labor Day weekend, and they've continued to schedule that way. When I mentioned this last year, someone pointed out that it was probably a better/cheaper weekend for them to book the convention center, not to mention that being a holiday weekend it's easier for people to attend.

      There's sure to be a little overlap in audience, but overall PAX and Bumbershoot are very different events catering to different crowds, so the only potential drawback I see is how the overlap affects hotel availability.
