\n Or least, I hate the fact that the ongoing campaign is held up so high as an ideal in the RPG hobby.

Don't get me wrong, I love the idea of being able to come back and revisit great characters and situations.

But truthfully, I detest the way it encourages a lax approach to forward momentum of events and its delayed gratification.

I also dislike the way it encourages lengthy chargen and situation+setting creation.

I dislike the way it encourages and justifies the creation of involved and time consuming game mechanics.

I dislike the way it requires a stable player group to meet multiple times to accomplish much of anything.

I dislike the way it encourages the enshrining of an Alpha Player (whether GM or not) to keep things going.

What I do like, and what I'd like to see more of?

Games that can be explained in 10 minutes, even to non-RPGers/S-Gers.

Games where, after 3-5 hours of play, the group has completed something, and even if they game is never touched again, people have had a really great experience.

Games where a lead-gamer/GM can spend, at most 20-40 minutes reading and have an idea of what they're supposed to do.

Games that are not meant to last for more than one session in connection, except for the really fanatical. Two seessions should be an accidental outlier. 3+ sessions? It shouldn't be the norm at all for a single story/series of events.

Games, and designers, that build systems based on the idea that stuff for the really enthusiastic players can wait! Put it in a supplement. Put it online. For the love of god, don't put it in a core product!

I also want games where the characters start as competent individuals, whatever that means in context. This is actually the only one that old B/X D&D really fails on for me. One adventure as noobs? Nifty. Several just to get to the 3-5 level sweetspot? Blargh!

I want games that assume the players will customize/hack/modify the thing if they really love it, and that the designer doesn't have to cover every base.

    \n I like campaigns that have endings. Ten sessions, twenty sessions, forty sessions, whatever, just as long as there's a point at which THE END arrives and we can look back with satisfaction on it; I love going on a trip whether it's a short or a long one, but I hate walking on a treadmill. The old school ideal of the neverending campaign, where you're playing the same characters over years and years...yeah, that sucks. There's too much stuff I want to play for me to want to be chained to a single game in a single genre forever and ever.

    I like campaigns when someone else is GMing them. My interest in GMing a game waxes and wanes over time, but mostly it just wanes, and it feels like an awful chore. My interest in playing, however, is a constant, so as a player I can stay fully engaged for week after week without any trouble. Fortunately, we've got folks in our gaming group who are the opposite of me: they thrive on GMing and get restless and unhappy if they spend too much time playing. (Shared narrative authority games were a huge flop with our group -- just not fun at all for over half of us, and the only two people who actually enjoyed them get to scratch that itch elsewhere, so we're all cool with sticking to a standard GM/player arrangement.)

    We did nothing but one-shots for a few months, and it was okay, but after a certain point I really missed being able to spend more time on things. I missed being able to do longer-term character and plot development. I missed character advancement, and being able to plan for character advancement. And the steady diet of one-shots was limiting: when you have to wrap things up in 4 hours, there are some things you just can't do properly.

    Also, sometimes I don't really "get" a game or a character right off the bat; if we ended everything we played after one session, I would have missed out on some awesome gaming experiences that only came four or five sessions in. One-shots don't give anyone time for a slow burn: I would either have to ignite immediately or stay cold, with no other choices. And sure, it'd be nice if everything caught fire for me right away, but that is not actually how the world works.

    We tend to do campaign-length games regularly now, with occasional one-shot or two-shot "vacations" thrown in to suit various whims. Because short games ARE fun! They definitely still have their place at our table, but they'll never be the only thing on the menu again.

    \n Because it's pretty directly relevant, and a good article all around, here's Joshua BishopRoby on the Campaign Fallacy:\n

    What other activity requires you to invest so much effort over so much time? Hint: marriage and childrearing don't count. And we're talking coordinated effort, here.     \n As the Voice of Long Campaigns (woogy woogy woogy) let me attempt to sing their praises.

    Note: I am not telling you that you are wrong. It's against my religion to call anyone's fun wrong. However, it's not a discussion if there's only one side, so here I go.

    Long campaigns give a great sense of achievement. You can look back at an awesome story that took months (or even weeks) to tell, and think "damn... I made that?" You look at a character sheet bloated with well-earned experience points and think back on how you deserved every god-damned point, every extra dot, every additional level, every new Sphere or Arcana or Art or Rote or spell or whatever. You can look at your character and think happily of what happens next. Is she still alive? Does she retire? How many of his personal issues are resolved? Is he happy now? Sad? Ready to grow? Ready to die? Maybe you share your thoughts with your fellow players, or maybe you just keep them happily to yourself. Or, maybe you even grow a new chronicle in the fertile soil of the old.

    Long campaigns give you something to look forward to. Not just in the "I can look forward to playing this game every week for years" sense. You can also look forward to your character increasing in power a great deal, achieving his goals, overcoming his personal issues, and becoming integral to the setting. Now, I know you can design short run games to do the same thing, but in long running campaigns it feels more organic. It feels like you earn it every step of the way. It also feels like you can take your time with it. Even extremely character-focused short run games like, say Flowers for Mara, which center on characters changing and overcoming issues, feel rushed compared to the experience of coaxing a character through overcoming issues in a long form.

    Finally, long running campaigns let you tell very big, very long stories. Stories with casts of thousands. World-hopping stories of epic proportions. You can't do stuff that big with short games, or if you can, I haven't.

    I agree there are lots of things short games do better, but I believe there are some ways in which long games are superior.

    \n Me, I like games that are both long and short.

    That is, I like games that can go long if you want them to, and get at those long term benefits like Josh was talking about, but also have some smaller structures that keep momentum, process, and engagement current and ongoing.

    Episodes and seasons in PTA, towns in Dogs, chapters and novels in the Swashbuckling game I'm playing now, cycles and arcs... these things make me happy. They lead to long term immersion, character and setting detail and complexity, emotional investment and ongoing build in verisimilitude. But at the same time they allow people to have a thing they're doing now, with a focus on the good part being right here and right now, and give structure and understanding to the development on an ongoing basis. Really, it isn't accidental that most ongoing fiction in most media and genres has methods of breaking itself up into chunks.

    As far as the center of design and play philosophy goes... I sometimes wonder how true that is these days. I mean, I still know a lot of groups that want to play forever and always, and some few games designed to do that. But most games, and especially most Story Games, coming out these days seem more focused on shorter term play, or at least on cyclical play with adventures of limited duration building into a campaign over time.

    I guess that's just a perception thing, but when folks talk about the ongoing, low-structure, more than a year long campaign as being the assumed default I get a thing in my head that's like "Sure, that was probably the case in the mid and late 90s, but I'm really not seeing it as being the case these days."

    :;shrug:: Maybe I'm cut off from the mainstream or something.

    \n To pick up the film/TV metaphor, consider also literature.

    In a short story, you get to plot really tightly, do really intense but closely circumscribed character development, and sketch the setting in broad but clear strokes, if you're good. You need to be good; short stories are hard to write.

    At novel length, you can plot a bit more leisurely, but you still need a structure, a beginning/middle/end. Your character development can be more in-depth and you can have more contradictions and more change. Your setting can also take on more depth and scope.

    In a series of novels, you get the chance to build up a real depth of setting and character - which means your initial concepts need to be such that they can bear some weight. You can have an overarching plot, but usually it's more like reincorporation of elements and exploration of themes. Each individual novel still needs to work in its own right. Often, it's not until the third book that it starts to get really good; often, it's about there that something really significant changes that affects everything from then on. The tone shifts, even.

    Eventually, though, if you don't have an overarching plot or some kind of major resolution, these series can degenerate and become weary pastiches of themselves.

    I'll leave drawing the parallels with the different lengths of RPG play as an exercise for the reader.

    \nI totally agree with the above statements, taking "fully resolve" as "we never complete a final objective for the game".

    I love long, long, long campaign games. But I can't stand Adventure Paths that are somehow some thing we are supposed to follow. The only thing that matters in gaming is the "Here and Now". (Another theoretical concept by Moreno who then lent it to Buber).

    I think I understand campaign play versus adventure play as folks have outlined them. I think it's easier just to call one objective-based and the other open-ended. That the objective in a finite game is to climb a mountain or examine a social situation like having your car stolen. Open-ended play is a potentially infinite game. The only objective is to play one's role. Players decide (often in character) on what to just as we decide what we will do in real life. It's just the decision is based on what we know of the game world.

    Why I like potentially never ending games is that a continually growing game world simply gets richer and richer as people play (and really, the only one who needs to consistently be involved in this game world's games is the GM. Players can come and go enjoying their contributions of success and failure.)
