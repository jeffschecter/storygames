\n
\n Lately a couple of things have been triggering my wince reflex almost as much as "anyways" and "nukUlar" do. When someone says something about the people they game with and refer to them as "my players" it makes me a little dizzy. It feels slightly creepy to me, the ownership and hierarchical structure it subtly implies.

I have a similar reaction when I hear my friends who are game designers talk about their fans. It seems to set up an artificial separation.

A disclaimer: I do not hate or disrespect people who use any of these terms. I get why they're effective.

    \n The problem is not the word fan. I'm a "fan" of, say, Jared Sorensen. But, he's a friend of mine. We talk about stuff. Not like every day, but we chat it up sometimes. We've hung out. We had a good time.

    Jared's a better designer than me. That's ok. But, he's into this hobby. He's on my level. We're peers, not because we're both designers (and other people are not). But, because we like each other, hang out, and have fun with each other in the hobby.

    The minute Jared expects me to be his fan where he just assumes he's better than me, and I'm a consumer of his creations and thoughts (rather than a peer with whom we can dialogue, for example), a switch will go off in my brain that says "Ok, he's an asshole. I don't need him."

    This exact scenario has gone down in my brain with real people. It's a big reason the Forge booth has been a hit, because it made people realize there wasn't some special fucking social club to "be" a designer. This presumption was and is prevalent among designers and publishers. They literally had jag-off secret pal hang-outs where they made fun of each other, and especially of consumers of their product.

    Fuck. That. Shit.

    There is no fucking "secret club" of designer-dom. It's a socially maladjusted construct created in people's heads. It's really, really, really stupid to think "Hey, I've arrived because So-and-so acknowledges me now, and I've lapped up his shit for years thinking he's more special than he really is."

    There isn't one fucking So-and-so in this hobby who's that special. Not one. Every single member of the hobby is just a hobbyist. Some are in business, too. That makes them not special at all. Fuck 'em.

    The level of social partitioning in this system is loathsome. The whole idea of "fandom" here is disgusting and insulting to regular people who either are in this hobby enjoying it (and sometimes not enjoying it) or to people who peer in from time to time, wanting to play, and quite rightly avoid it thinking "That's crazy." It's crazy. It really is.

    Next time you think So-and-so is hot shit, think again. Just think, "Hey, he or she is a cool person, and I like their ideas.     \n Rob, I understand where you're coming from... to an extent.

    \nWhile Peter is correct, as are many others, that the use of "My" in other contexts doesn't imply anything, it does, in fact imply something when you use it to talk about group relationships. Simon had it pegged from the first, and nobody's contradicted him. It's a paternal thing like a coach.
    \n
    \nIf I'm a player, no, I don't say, "My players." It would be wierd to do so in that context, because, for whatever reason of historical usage, doing so would indicate heirarchical ownership... in this case. It just does. If, in fact, "my" is always ambiguous here, you'd find every player saying "My players." But, in fact, only GM's do it.
    \n
    \nConsider that it would even be slightly odd to say "My group." Less so, but people are as like to say, "The group I play with. Yeah, even if it's grammatically incorrect, it's what people say. If you want to be grammatically correct, you'd say, "The group of players with whom I play." But people shortcut that because they want something shorter, and the grammatically correct "My group" just seems wrong.
    \n
    \nJustin... no. You would't say "My setter did X..." unless you were the coach. As a player you'd say, "Our setter." Or, perhaps if you don't want to indicate possession, you'd say, "The Setter."
    \n
    \nSee, there's this other possessive pronoun that indicates group onwership. Even of a group. "Our Group." So, tell me Justin... why don't you use "Our Players?" If you're not trying to imply ownership of the group in some way, why aren't you using the pronoun that indicates that everybody owns the game?
    \n
    \n
    \nNow, all that said... I'm not a sensitive type. I understand the argument that subtext like this in language can be informative and theoretically damaging. I'm of the opinion, however, that two things are true:
    \nA) We're capable of understanding the accurate truth about something, even if the language used is lazy or inaccurate.
    \nB) I don't know that you can change these habits, and to the extent that it is possible to change them, I'm not sure that it will change the underlying paradigms that you're seeking to do away with.
    \n
    \nI'm not "anti-PC"... if you want to try to get the language use to change, be my guest. I just don't agree that people who are using the language in this way are trying to oppress anyone. At worst it's accidental.
    \n
    \n
