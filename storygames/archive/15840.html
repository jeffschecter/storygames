\n Hey, folks. After a few people urged me in another thread to attend Dreamation, I took the plunge and got my room and registration and all that good stuff, so I'll be there. Since this is my first time, however, I have a question about how getting into the available games is done.

From what I've been able to gather, at some point in the very near future the Dreamation site will offer up a complete list of events for which one may register. Obviously these have limited seating, so I'm going to guess competition to be the first come and first served is fierce. Though no one's said so, I'm further guessing that registration happens via the site somehow. Pointing and clicking may be involved.

It was recommended to me that I have a couple or three backup games in mind for every slot. Which makes me ask an etiquette question: do I just register for a whole slew of games, hoping I get one of them, and then let the other ones slide when the hour arrives? If I end up in two games in the same slot, is it seriously uncool to choose one over the other, thus possibly cutting out someone who really wanted to play in that game but couldn't register because I took their spot? Is this scenario even possible, given the set-up Dreamation has?

Anyway, I have questions. If you have answers, please do feel free to put them here.

    \n You actually just send in an email once the schedule is sent out, with your preferences in ranked order by timeslot and by the event's code number. (The indie RPGs all have the prefix "R", except some of the Jeepform LARPs, which have "L.") So you'll say something like:

    Saturday 9 AM: 1st choice R209, 2nd choice R212, 3rd choice R211.

    And do the same for all the slots that you are attending.

    Once Vinny compiles everything, you will only be pre-reg'd for one game in each slot. But, if something else that you wanted more in a particular slot opens up, it's not a big deal to switch\u2014most of the time the game you're switching out of will have alternates who will step in to take your place.

    \n So, it's your first Dreamation, or maybe your first game convention. What should you expect?

    Finding Stuff

    It's a big, sprawling hotel. You should seek out at least three distinct areas:

    1) Registration. The place where you get your badge and shit. It's usually not far from the exhibition/dealer's room. Go there first, get your badge and welcome bag. Then proceed to #2 to sign up for games.

    2) The RPG area. Where the rooms for the games are, and where the sign-up sheets for the games are. The hallways usually have papers covering the walls. These are sign-up sheets. They're usually organized by category and number. Find games you want to play and add your name to them. If you preregistered, your name will be typed on the sign-up sheet already.

    3) The IPR booth. This is where indie games are sold. It's usually a cramped little table or two near the RPG area -- NOT in the dealer's hall. Story Gamers also tend to hang out in this area.


    Meeting People

    By and large, story gamers are a friendly bunch, but we often forget that there are new folks around. A lot of us already know a bunch of people there, and we can't walk twenty paces without seeing someone we haven't seen in a year. Do not let this intimidate you. We'd love to meet new people.

    A good way to meet folks is just to say hello and tell us who you are. Remind us how we might know you ("I'm Sam! with an exclamation point on Story Games"). Don't be surprised if you get confused looks at first, and then that moment of recognition a bit later ("Oh, hi!"). There are a lot of people at Dreamation and it can be overwhelming to remember who everyone is. But it's awesome to put faces to names.

    The best way to meet people is to hang out in the hotel lounge in the evening and at night. People start getting too tired to game, but we're too stubborn to go to bed when there are all these cool people around, so we hang out, maybe have a drink, and kibitz. Find a group of people, ask if it's okay if you join them, and they'll probably say yes. By the evening, you'll probably know a few people there anyway.

    Food

    People need to eat. It's a great time to get some face time with new friends. Often people don't mind if another person tags along. Find out what people are doing for lunch or for dinner.

    Some of us regular attendees get brave and walk a few blocks to local restaurants. There's a great burrito place not too far away. There's a fantastic Japanese place within walking distance.

    The con has food, too. The con staff tends to feed the GMs. I think the hotel sells relatively cheap, edible fare like hot dogs and nachos. Sometimes you just want to grab something quick and take it with you to your next game. I think they usually sell bottles of water, which you'll want. (Better, bring your own refillable water bottle--yay, environment!--or buy one of theirs and refill it yourself from the tap.)

    Gaming!

    I suspect there will be an overwhelming number of fantastic games to play in this year. Hopefully, you'll get into the games you want. Even if you can't, do not despair. It's possible (though often tricky) to find people interested in playing games that aren't scheduled or slotted. Just find an empty table in one of the free gaming areas (make sure you're not in someone's slotted table) and game with new friends.

    There are a couple different approaches to scheduling. I used to schedule every single slot and play until I had screamed myself hoarse and could barely keep my eyes open. These days, I tend to schedule a far less busy con for myself, spend as much time socializing as I do gaming, and get a solid eight hours of sleep. Occasionally, I'll take a refreshing, one-hour nap during an open slot, mid-con. You get less gaming in that way, but the gaming is better (especially towards the end).

    Other awesome stuff

    Dreamation has a lot to offer beyond role-playing games. Every kind of gaming is represented there. You can probably play poker, if you want to. There are LARPs and wargames and card games and strategy games and fun family-type games and everything under the sun. I think there's usually a place to watch anime and other videos. There's a room full of chocolate for a couple hours on one evening. Check the schedule, look around, and try new things.

    At some point, someone (in the past, it was usually Jason Morningstar) may come to you with a video camera and ask you to say, "Hi, I'm ____. One cool thing I saw at Dreamation was ____." If you're like me, you freeze in the moment and make a fool of yourself. You will call it GenCon instead of Dreamation. You will forget to say your name or maybe say someone else's name. You will forget what was cool. No one really cares. They just want to hear that you had fun.

    Less awesome stuff

    Confluenza. Hundreds of gamers. Small, cramped spaces. Table spaces that don't get wiped down much. Winter cold season. Shaking hands with new friends. Wearing oneself down with constant activity, poor eating habits, and little sleep. Very likely, you will catch a cold or something worse.

    Do whatever you can to reduce the chance of that happening (for yourself and others). Wash your hands often. Cough into your sleeve or shoulder instead of your hands, if you can. Rest as much as you can. Drink fluids. Get plenty of vitamin C and zinc. I believe in the power of Zicam as a cold preventative, but you may not.
