\n The D&D round is an interesting phenomenon. It stands in contrast to story-focused affairs because it has an emphasis on granularity and task resolution while remaining abstract. Hit points, Armor Class, "an action" - it's a strange balance between detailed crunch and narrative.

The Setup
Combat procedures begins with establishing surprise. Those who are surprised are unable to take any actions on the first round of combat. Procedures then move to initiative: who goes when.

Both of these are matters of determining fictional positioning (something that has been ever on my mind). A strong fictional position: not surprised, high initiative. A weak fictional position: surprised, low initiative. This becomes even more finely sifted when one considers permutations of surprise and a 1-20 initiative roll. "Not surprised" is always the best proposition, but high initiative in a drawn-out fight is desirable.

A strong fictional position offers considerable advantages in risk mitigation and resource conservation. Acting twice before the enemy can act once can very well end a combat before it truly begins. On the other hand, those with weak fictional position are more likely to use up their precious hit points or be forced into expending resources (spells, per-rest abilities, magic items) to decisively end a battle.

It should also be noted that fictional positioning is also influenced by distance between opponents. Sadly, I do not know of a procedure in modern D&D (5e) to determine initial distance when encountering others.

Swings and Misses
In the typical combat round, characters will attack, cast spells, and perform other actions. Everything done in the combat round has an opportunity cost except for movement (in 5e): it costs an action. First, let's talk about the to-hit roll.

The attack roll in D&D has always been simplistic at its core: roll to-hit vs. AC. In contrast to the typical storygame, the consequences of failure (in this instance) are lower. A missed attack roll is merely a missed opportunity to deplete a monster's hit points. Come the next turn, a character has another opportunity to attack. Interestingly, the stakes of multiple failures are cumulative. Cumulative misses (or related failures) narrow the window of a character's success. ("Success" being defined as "eliminating the enemy's resources while preserving his own.") Missed attack rolls grant more opportunities for the enemy to strike at a character's resource cache, whereas attack rolls that hit do the exact opposite.

Spells
More punishing is the use of spells, cantrips excluded. Every spell cast is a semi-permanent resource expenditure, making them far more valuable than the standard attack roll, but this has the side effect of a higher cost for spell failure (when the enemy's saving throw succeeds). As most spells having a casting time of "an action," spell failure wastes an action and a spell slot. Compounding this is the potential for a spell slot to be useful outside of combat. Spending a spell now could very well result in greater hardship down the line, up to and including death.

Hit Points
Death is the ultimate "stake" set in D&D combat. Character death is the total failure state. Hit points are a barrier to this failure state. In one sense, hit points exist as a measure of a character's ability to defend against his enemy's intent - and thus his ability to reduce hit points is his measure of his ability to achieve his intent.

Armor Class
I cannot believe I forgot about this iconic D&D aspect. Armor Class is a brilliant piece of game technology that represents the ability for armor to deflect blows and protect the wearer from lethal harm. It's almost a shame that Gygax created this, as it would preclude any number of debates about the nature of hit points and the role of armor within the game. But nevermind what could have been. Armor Class sets the difficulty for task resolution (to-hit rolls) in D&D. In an interesting twist, an escalating to-hit bonus causes Armor Class to become less relevant over time, transforming it from a first line of defense into a form of damage reduction. Over a period of time, Armor Class works out to less damage taken, but in the short term, it may not provide much protection.

Additional thoughts are welcome.

    \n To me, combat has always felt like the weak link in a traditional RPG. It lacks the crunch of a wargame (unless you play with minis and grids and super-simulationist rules), but it\u2019s slow and has player downtime and dissipates the momentum of the surrounding story.

    On the other hand, reducing an \u201cencounter\u201d to a die roll or two, as pioneered by Tunnels and Trolls, feels like an anticlimax in a genre that\u2019s largely about fighting.

    Fate Core and the Apocalypse Engine do a reasonable job of merging fiction into the action, so it\u2019s not just \u201cmy dude hits the orc with his sword\u201d over and over, but I\u2019d still like to see some more mechanics involved that get you more physically involved than just rolling 4dF or 2d6.

    Some recent board games seem to be looking at this from the opposite side. Gloomhaven, which I haven\u2019t played (I don\u2019t even have the STR to lift the box!) has a kind of deck-building engine where one deck is your possible actions \u2014 which you literally exhaust during the fight \u2014 and another deck is your randomizer which shows how well you did. Both decks get customized as you gain experience. Old School Hack has a deck of actions too, only different. Another thing I like about cards is that they let everyone choose actions simultaneously, which helps speed up combat and reduce downtime.

    ValyrianSteelKatana said:

    Sadly, I do not know of a procedure in modern D&D (5e) to determine initial distance when encountering others.

    \nWe are houseruling a kinda wargamey simple procedure for this, as an alternative to marching order when this matters less: Areas for the players and the GM to place their minis are designated (1/4 or 1/5 of the area in opposing sides, unless surrounded) and then everyone places their minis on reverse initiative order, wherever they want inside the limits of the area designated. Players who rolled the higher initiative can also choose to place their minis before other players if they want.

    The effect is that whoever gets higher initiative can obtain a better positioning.

    Works best on outdoor locations with some obstacles and cover.

    Distance in general terms is still somewhat defined by the narration (2 turns worth of normal movement top, melee at worst, more if the terrain and conditions allow), but this adds a little bit more of fairness and strategy to miniature placement.

    \n Here is the B/X (Moldvay) combat sequence for reference:

    COMBAT SEQUENCE (this is nested inside a broader procedural sequence)
    A. Each side rolls for initiative (1d6).

    B. The side that wins the initiative acts first (if simultaneous all actions are performed by each side at the same time):

    1. Morale checks, if needed (page B27).

    2. Movement per round, meleed opponents may only move defensively (spell casters may not move and cast spells).

    3. Missile fire combat:
    a. choose targets
    b. roll ld20 to hit; adjust result by Dexterity adjustment, range, cover, and magic
    c. DM rolls damage

    4. Magic spells(roll saving throws, as needed: 1d20).

    5. Melee or hand-to-hand combat:
    a. choose (or be attacked by) opponents
    b. roll 1d20 to hit; adjust result by Strength adjustment and
    magic weapons
    c. DM rolls damage; adjust result by Strength adjustment
    and magic weapons

    C. The side with the next highest initiative acts second, and so on using the order given above, until all sides have completed melee.

    D. The DM handles any surrenders, retreats, etc. as they occur.

    \n It's funny to me that 5e doesn't have concrete encounter distance rules, as they are pretty crucial in any variety with wandering monsters in particular. But\u2014it is very informative which versions are missing which bits. For instance, OD&D basically doesn't have initiative rules at all.

    Which is part of why I think that Rafu's description of initiative as outside of fictional positioning is not really correct. Another reason is that some versions of D&D give characters bonuses on their initiative roll based on stats or feats etc. I've specifically embraced it as a piece of fictional positioning by preferentially determining it by weapon reach, movement (flying or horseback), height, or formation before ever rolling the dice.

    (In OD&D the entire initiative system is \u201csuch things as speed, ferocity, and weaponry of the monster attacking are subsumed in the matrixes\u201d.)

    Much the same I lean into the surprise roll\u2014the winner of surprise gets to choose the encounter distance, either by the players navigating the fictional space, or the referee-as-Dungeon-Master advocating for the monsters to the best of their ability. This kind of close examination of each moving part is one of my favourite parts of the tradition!

    WarriorMonk said:

    I have played other games where initiative is defined by who stated first their reaction when the GM asked for it, and either comparing the stats or rolling for initiative when discrepances arise. Has anybody tried this approach with D&D? Do you think it would break the game?

    \nI used this approach with 5e for over a year. It worked perfectly, and was much more natural than rolling initiative. No more, "I attack him!" "Roll initiative!" "Welp, guess he attacks me instead."

    To avoid rolling initiative entirely, after you attacked an enemy, the initiative passed to that enemy, or an ally of their choice if they'd already acted that round. If you didn't attack or take any other aggressive action\u2014if you cast a healing spell or went into full defensive stance or whatever\u2014then you instead passed the initiative to one of your own allies.
