"I like these calm little moments before the storm.
It reminds me of Beethoven.
Can you hear it?
It's like, when you put your head to the grass.
You can hear it growing.
You can hear the insects, bzzzz..."

- Gary Oldman's Character from Leon or the Professional

I was reading this recent post of John Kim's LJ and he quotes Robert (Scorpio Rising) talking about Bang-driven play.

It just seems like there is this view of those who play Nar-heavy games that we sit in a circle and kick each other in the nuts for four hours yelling "BANG!" with each kick, pat each other on the back and go home to write AP threads about how awesome it is that our metaphorical testicles are sore.

Example:

Player 1: Then the demon humps your PC's head!

Player 2: Dude! I have that trait about having my head humped!

Player 1: I know!

Player 3: Aw man, and this now triggers my trait about getting cold nut-kicking vengeance upon anyone who allows that demon, who I'm in love with, to hump their head!

My favorite part of the techniques, like Bangs, Ron mentions in the Sorcerer books is that they put names to effective practices that I was bringing to the table. Identifying them meant that I could watch out for them and instead of swinging in the dark, my GMing became more precise.

But Bangs weren't the only things in the books.

Crosses, Weavings, Openings and Bobs from Sex & Sorcery get used by me a whole lot but they are odd little moments and difficult for me to remember or really parse out for an AP post.

I can recall one L5R game where our stalwart samurai PC's were really getting hammered by oni and cultists and conspiracies and I really wanted a number of scenes, before the final big ole battle, where we could just chill for a bit. I wanted that calm before the storm and when we didn't get it, I was bummed.

I just wanted us to chill in a bath-house for a scene, talk about what we had discovered and let the epic nature of our discoveries sink in. Those quiet moments make the powerful bangs more meaningful.

So, anyway, some things to go back and re-read for me and I'm going to try to look out for them in play and mention them in future AP posts right along-side the BANGS and the kicks to the groin.

    I think perhaps the term for the kind of thing you wanted for your Samuri is a Sequel. A Sequel is what happens after a Scene(s) where a character(s) responds emotionally and intellectually to the fallout from the last scene before setting sights on the next scene. It is that kind of calm before/between the storms. In writing novels they are used for pacing and building character empathy. I personally think they have the same kind of job in role-playing.

  • Brand_Robins
    February 2007
    Bobs are simple -- they're a break, a rest, or a delay. Heroes does this to us every fucking week. In game its where you say, "Okay, but before you can kill the evil wizard you have a day of travel. Why don't we play out a brief scene at camp the night before as you all face the prospect you could die tomorrow" or anything like that. Its pretty much just taking a moment to reflect, build investment, and do some shit that isn't punching someone in the cock.

    A weave is moving two different plots, especially two different kicker based plots or major NPC goals closer together. So say you've got one PC who has been chasing his long lost father because he's needing money and another who is closing in on the asshole mob-boss who raped his sister so he can kill him. The two then get clues to the identity of dad and mob-boss, and as it turns out its the same guy. Now the PCs are headed towards each other and the NPC at the speed of blood opera.

    OK, example of a weave from actual play:

    This one guy totally falls for the beautiful wife of an Austro-Hungarian spy. He and Tatiana Subacic have a brief moment of illicit passion before tearfully parting ways, being from different worlds and all that. Later this guy and his pals (the PCs) get her husband sort of killed in a fantastic plane crash.

    At the same time the characters are chasing the villain, the insidious King Lung, master of disguise, mind control, and making cool bombs. King Lung mocks and thwarts them, and issues villainous challenges right and left. He's a real bastard and they can't wait to sink their teeth into him. They chase King Lung across continents, across years. He tries to blow up New York with acid bombs. He's kick-ass.

    Four years later they've got him on the ropes. They chase him to Cuba. They kick in the doors of his secret base and kill some of his dudes, and he barely escapes. Weirdly, King Lung leaves behind a three year old orphan. WTF, King Lung? And weirder, it makes him desperate - he kidnaps Theodore Roosevelt and offers to trade him for the anonymous kid. The players start to make the connection.

    The PCs are on King Lung like a hydrophobic dog, all except one guy, who is a little reluctant now ... but to hell with him, they shoot down Lung's airship. They bust in and there "he" is - King Lung, Tatiana Subacic, the revenging widow, tearfully dying.

    The point of attack for inter-connection is pretty late, so maybe that's not a textbook weave.

    Weaving, also mentioned in Sex and Sorcery is something I do with the other players' help a whole lot in the Dictionary of Mu con games. I tell the players that their pre-written kickers would normally be written by them but for the con, I go with pre-written stuff. So, if they want to meet another player, they need to angle their character towards them, if not out and out talk about it a bit and talk about a scene they'd like to see.

    So the Khan of Khans begins play after being crucified on the desert floor to show his ability to be the Khan of Khans and meanwhile, the Primite Prince lets his human slaves go in a glimmer of conscious, realizing that these humans have souls. Naturally, the people run into the Khan of Khan's horde and naturally the Primite realizes that without his help, the people he just let go will be run down and killed without his help.

    Boy. I really dislike the terms "Bob and Weave". It is a boxing term (and I've heard in fighter pilot jargon alongside "juke and jive" and it implies action).     It seems to me that refreshment scenes, from The Shadow of Yesterday, are a perfect example of System-enforced Bobs or Sequels.

    I do a similar thing in my game The Camel's Egg, where character need to play to their Key Traits (kind of like their Issues) to regain resources spent in conflict. The idea is that if you're stuck in prison, you might need to tell your cellmate about your lost innocence or your goal of marrying so-and-so before you're ready to play through the daring escape.

    I've had a lot of success with mechanically enforced Weaves, as well, in several different games: Land of Nodd, which Dave describes earlier, and a storytelling game which makes storylines based on the interaction of two important story elements. Simply recombining those elements in new permutations tends to draw the plot together and create interesting climaxes in the story. For instance, one storyline might be about how "the King" plans to rescue "his daughter", and another might be about how "the criminal" wants to steal "the crown jewels". Well, in the next phase of the game, we might have the criminal trying to become King, and the daughter trying to seduce him into telling her where he's hidden the jewels.

    This sort of thing is fun and works really well!

One of my favorite scenes was Judd's bastard Wizard in BW having breakfast over at his beloved's manor trying to impress her rough hewn, just barely noble father. While there was a duel of wits conflict, it was so low level, so mundane and gosh.... cute.... it became one of my favorite scenes of the entire campaign. So dice hit the table, but it wasn't Sturm and drang at all.

    But it was in a game of political ambition, so many conflicts around that small scene were big and weighty.

    So hearing Judd talk about the lack of such a scene in his L5R game really resonated with me. I've felt that way before too.
