\n This thread is a spin off of Graham's concern over judging long Game Chef Games.

Basically there are a couple of assertions that have been made that appear to be drawing further discussion. I will try to distill the topics as best I can.

1. Is there an appropriate/maximum length for games that you expect to be peer reviewed (specifically in the Game Chef scenario)?

2. More generally, in a design sense are short games better than long games? Should flavor text and other "non-essentials" be included as part of a game design contest... or should the focus be exclusively on the core mechanics? Is there a middle-ground?

Discuss, as you will. As there does seem to be some vitriol here, lets keep it civil

    Posted By: DirkGently1. Is there an appropriate/maximum length for games that you expect to be peer reviewed (specifically in the Game Chef scenario)?
    \nIf there is, and I'm not sure if that's even the case, it would be a combination of word count and page count -- for the sake of reading time (word count) and printer usage (page count). Certainly *I* prefer reading short games, but I won't fault a designer for taking up a lot of my time & paper if I feel it was necessary in communicating the game's idea/play/essence.\n
    2. More generally, in a design sense are short games better than long games? Should flavor text and other "non-essentials" be included as part of a game design contest... or should the focus be exclusively on the core mechanics? Is there a middle-ground?
    \nMy answer is going to be rather unhelpful: whatever is necessary for conveying the play experience you want to evoke. If that means flavor text, as in Shreyas's games, it's very much needed. I think examples are crucial for any game, and don't think they would go amiss (as I'm in the "game text is first and foremost meant to teach" camp). I felt that the pages I devoted to the palace rooms in Know Thyself were necessary for giving an example framework for how to use the game -- they weren't part of the core mechanic, but I think without it the game would have been less complete. And recall that completeness is one of the criteria used in judging.

    Which, I suppose means this: if you think that the game shouldn't be able trying to stuff text and text in a document -- text that may not be "necessary" -- in order to fulfill one of the contest's goals, perhaps there should be a discussion about changing that goal. What people think is a "complete" game differs heavily, and you're going to see that when people submit entries and with others judge/critique them.

    Hey Adam, there's this thing that Jon and I have batted around, the game-chefish game. I'll see if I can get him to unpack it.
    But basically the idea is that, uh, one looks to the current frame of reference for content and structural standards, not outside it.
    \nFair enough. Let me counter this by asking "who sets the current frame of reference"?

    If it is the organizer (as I believe) and no length is specified as in Game Chef... then it seems pretty clear that you should write as much as you feel is necessary. Will writing too much dilute the overall quality of your work? Probably. Will writing a long document make it harder for people to give good feedback ? Maybe (I would argue that clarity and ease of reading is the operative variable here). Will writing "War and Peace:the game" ensure that less people read and review it? Almost certainly.

    But the choice to do it and accept the possible consequences, is left to you.

    If, on the other hand, you are arguing that as a universal rule, expecting people to read documents beyond a certain length is cruel in a Peer Review scenario... I completely disagree. If you enter a 'short story' writing competition and someone gives you 300 pages, you would rightfully be miffed and probably read little (if any) of it. If you enter a 'write a novel' contest and complain that you have to read something more than 20 pages long... I think you would be foolish to complain. If you enter a contest that says 'write a story' and doesn't tell you how long it is supposed to be... at that point it comes down to personal preference.

    I think a Game Chef where participants were required to create a game of 15 pages and under would be a lot of fun. It would probably provoke more detailed reviews of everyone's games and certainly would encourage everyone to read more in toto... but unless Andy (or whoever runs the contest after him) wants to set that specification (and you should certainly suggest it to him)... then I do not presume to enforce such a limit on others.

    Part of the 'length' problem is that, for good or ill, allowing 2 weeks (rather than 1) lets people add in some non-essential stuff that was not present in previous years. *shrug*

    Thomas,

    \n

    I'm very uncomfortable with your and others' dimissal of "flavor text" here and in Graham's sister thread.

    \n

    Yes, it's the case that some designers do write games where this is a real thing, in the M:tG sense - it has no bearing on what the game is or means - but it's also the case that some designers take a more holistic tack, and with such designs, you are missing important things when you skip text that isn't full of rules and commands. I'd point to Jon Walton and Ben Lehman as people whose designs I read this way (Polaris, for instance, is full of non-accidental stories and details), and I feel that when I do, they are better designs. And you can't ever know without reading an entire game where on that spectrum it falls!

    \n

    Similarly, there are designs where particular mechanics aren't really important to the game, like (I would suggest) the particulars of any given loresheet in Weapons of the Gods, or the details of any singular feat in D&D.

    \n

    So I don't think that it's very smart reading to choose to privilege one kind of text over another just because some of it is implicit and some of it is explicit. Either way, you are discarding important awareness about how some games work. I think the best thing to do is to be aware of this and try and figure out how a game was designed after reading the whole thing, but if you don't choose to do that, then at least understand that you are creating a blind spot for yourself, and be aware that it has effects.

    No, I'm not criticizing your game. I only started reading it recently, and it seems to me that most of the length is from examples. More examples are always fine. There's also a design notes section, which isn't strictly necessary, but it can be skipped -- but it's worth it not to skip it (I read part of it first.)

    I'm not really criticizing anyone, but I'm warning some people, mostly people new to design, that they should focus more on their game and save the bulk of the supporting material for later. There's always plenty of time to flesh out the background.

    \n

    The key questions, I would say, are: how much does the reader have to read, and how much information does the reader actually gain? Ideally, you need a couple pages to convey your setting concept, your rules, and your examples. Examples can be skipped, if you understand the rules. So can flavor text, if you feel you get the idea behind the setting. So can many other kinds of text, as long it's easy to find the rules necessary.

    \n

    I'll use my own entry as a bad example. People liked the hippy voice I used when I wrote my game. However, the rules are embedded in this flavor text. Many people did not understand how to play the game, as a consequence. If I had repeated every rule as a pullquote and included a rules summary at the end, as well as more examples, it would have been a better entry. The problem wasn't the length (it's pretty short,) it's the way the text obscures the rules, which makes reading it a chore and makes it seem longer than it is.
