\n One of my guitar students and his girlfriend hung out with us on Christmas Eve, and since they read my blog, they were interested in this "story games" thing I do. They aren't gamers, but they are very artsy. Musicians, drama-types. Will was over and we decided to run a one-shot of PTA. I didn't think they had such a good time, but today they told me that they tried to play the really cool story game (without documentation, mind you) with some drama-geek friends of theirs and they just didn't get it.

So...I invited them over Saturday for dinner and a story game. I want to broaden their experience a bit without a) getting the geek-stink on it, and b) confusing them with all sorts of weird mechanics and high-brow Forge-y concepts.

Level 1 for non-gamers is definitely PTA.

Level 2 is what?

    \n I have played In A Wicked Age with non-gamers, and I think it's ideal. You can really start playing without having to explain any rules. Just say what the game is generally about, then have the players choose the oracle and the story elements and then brainstorm on the characters.
    \n
    \nSo *then* you explain the rules for character creation, but by that time, everyone's already having fun and feeling creatively charged and invested in the game. You can approach character creation very easily, because the players are already kind of interested in the characters.
    \n
    \nAlso, I'm a total fanboy for this game. I'll just go on and on at the drop of a hat.

    \n I think that's something that's easy to say, but isn't necessarily helpful for Brennen.
    \n
    \nYes, it's very easy to over-think and geek out on a question like this. Point taken.
    \n
    \nBut there are real variations in games in terms of complexity and level of investment. So it's not crazy to put some thought into what specific game might make the evening the most fun. I've set up game nights like this, and there are some games I simply wouldn't have played, because they require a little too much effort for a first-time gamer.
    \n
    \nIf I had a gamer friend who wanted to play "something like, you know, D&D, with wizards and shit", I'd loan them a copy of Burning Wheel, or offer to run a session. But if a non-gamer friend said the same thing, I would probably choose something else. Not because of geek shame, but because I don't know if they would enjoy that game so much on their first time out. It would require a lot more explanation and rules discussions, and I don't think it would be as much fun.
    \n
    \nYou might not agree with that line of thinking, but it's unfair to call it "idiotic". After all, Brennen knows these people. Surely he has some idea what they would enjoy and what they're up for.

    \n I believe, perhaps foolishly, perhaps as a snob, there are two tracks here...

    Geek might be one concern. But in the first post we were told these people are "...very artsy. Musicians, drama-types."

    I think this matters.

    Some folks what games that feed the geek. There are plenty of games that feed the artsy. In my experience some of these games cross over... and others are at cross-purpose to tastes.

    In other words, don't think "non-geek." Think, "What tools (or games) will be let a group of artsy, musician, drama-types have a blast?" Which is a quest completely off the geek/non-geek axis.
