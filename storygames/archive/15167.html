    \n The game gets published and the writer is proud of his work, and also happy at the thought of people playing it. When the writer imagines it, people are playing the game as written, and it's wonderful.

    Then some bozo (me) buys that game, and immediately starts yanking it apart and making it do things that the writer didn't intend. All kinds of changes, small and large, get made to the setting, to the tone, even to the rules. Around the gaming table, this new, collaborative game is being played, and only parts of it look like what the writer imagined...the rest is what the nonconsensual collaborator did with it.

    Posted by: Ben RobbinsCollaboration where you don't get to give consent to how people collaborate with you? Dibs on Microscope.
    \nYup, I think Ben Robbins got it right on this one. "Nonconsensual" doesn't mean that there are opposite positions (how could that be collaboration, really?), it means that a consensus is not sought out.

    Pretty much every game where you don't have to agree 100% of the time (i.e.: all roleplaying / story games) yet where you tell a tale within a common narrative space ("this fiction") has aspects which are nonconsensual collaborations.

    I guess it could be interesting to dissect games to determine what parts of their collaborative element are consensual and what parts are nonconsensual.

It was used whenever he framed an scene and asked players for their reactions and suddenly somebody made a joke about his character doing something stupid or something happening all of a sudden. Then the DM yelled: "You roared!" and proceed to make that joke happen in the game to the dismal of all the players. Of course nobody was happy about that happening and yet we engeerered that situation ourselves.

    So there you have: Nonconsensual collaboration.
