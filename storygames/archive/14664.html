    \n First, the document itself is beautiful, with evocative bits of setting that appeal to anyone who knows fantasy or sci-fi (or westerns or pirates or romance). People want to read this. Then they come across the initial situation and pre-gen characters, which allow them to jump straight into the action, no planning necessary. The ruleset is also very simple, not a heavy tome like D&D, and manages to include a handful of features that keep players engaged in the storytelling; keys encourage players to think about how their characters would act, and traits and tags encourage players to narrate their actions creatively.

    Combine all these features and you've got something approachable and engaging, which you can plop this down in front of a group of newbies and be playing five minutes later.

    \n Let's not forget the user interface design - putting the rules right on the character sheets - you can learn the game as you go. Plus the brief examples - these are the kinds of things you should be saying.

    Some popular games lately have done away with the "GM sets difficulty" thing, but I've realized it's a really powerful way to make sure your SiS is in sync on a moment-to-moment basis.
    "GM: Difficulty 6.
    Player: Difficulty 6? Say what now?
    GM: You're scaling this sheer wall without equipment?
    Player: Oh, I thought it was kind of a rocky cliff face."
    It requires understanding & negotiation of the SiS in a way that "Yes, and..." doesn't.

    Players can't lose: not only can they not die, but failure leads to success later.

    The die mechanic is sneakily in-your-favor: when I hear "difficulty 3" it feels like I need 6 dice for a 50% shot, but really I only need 5 dice.

    "Ask questions. Don't plan." Helps to make sure a GM who is new to improvisational GMing doesn't get stuck. Kind of the same point Todd was making, I guess.

    \n One of the things I use when running the game for newbies is to say, "When we figure out how many dice you get to roll, you start off with one, just for trying." It becomes this mantra, sometimes more than just the player taking action will say it, "One for trying..." It encourages you to get up and do things. Same thing with taking XP. It opens up the game. I often will remind players to take XP, then they will start to do and then ask if its okay (XP = chips on the table, I always use chips so I can visually see that I'm helping everyone hit their Keys). Then by the end of the session, they reach for it and at most give me a head nod and I head nod back.

    But the previously suggested reasons of "Steampunk", beautiful art, compelling male and female characters, and all of the rules you need to know on one sheet of paper are huge.

    But I think another factor is that it screams ROMANCE and ADVENTURE instead of COMBAT. That's been a big call for newbies at my table.

    \n I ran this game yesterday with 2 experienced gamers and 1 new (to rpgs) player. The experienced players chose Kale and Naomi, and the new guy picked the Captain. We had been playing a couple other games earlier in the day, so he was starting to get the hang of it, but he was a little unsure of how to play the captain at first. The Kale player immediately started to take charge, when I remembered the suggestion in the rules about asking PCs how they feel about the actions of the other PCs. I asked the Captain player what he thought about the first mate taking command, when the player surprised me by saying he wanted to drop the Key of Command. That took us all back, and after thinking about it for a second, I strongly suggested that he reconsider. I figured that losing that aspect of the character would make it much more difficult to play, especially for a new player. In the end, we convinced the player to keep it, but gave him this advice: anytime anyone makes a suggestion, immediately shut it down, but then say it again as if it were his own idea, and let Kale say, "That's a great idea!" That helped immensely. A better GM than I would probably have found a way to let the player lose the key and still be interesting... what do you guys think?
    \n
    \nIn the end, we found our way to the Pirate King at the end of the session, and right as the game was about to end, with the NPC (and really obnoxioius) Lady Blackbird about to run to the arms of the waiting King, the Captain player surprised us all by declaring his love (that he had hidden/forgotten about until now, haha). He ended up defeating the sorcerous Pirate King and usurped his throne. I can't wait to run this game again!

    \n I don't think there is a single thing about Lady Blackbird that makes it perform so well with newbieish players, but several.
    \n
    \nThe first is the basic plot of unrequited love, dashing pirates and the wild blue yonder. You have to have a heart of stone or a neckbeard of iron bristles not to immediately want to leap into the world.
    \nThis gets the player into the game.
    \n
    \nThe second is the ways the keys are presented.If you don't remember playing an RPG for the first time, most of that time can be spent worrying about whether or not you are doing it right, or what you should be doing at all. The keys are a basic instruction manual as to what to do in play. In most situations the keys present one or more clear courses of action that the player can take. I discussed LB with my partner and a first-time rpg'er and what they pointed out was that they didn't have to worry about doing what they thought their character would do. Also, the immediate reward of pool dice or experience, means that the player doesn't have to sit and wonder "am I doing it wrong", their behaviour is immediately rewarded by the game. This way the new player feels that their in-game actions are approved by the game itself, and make them feel more comfortable about playing.
    \n
    \nThe simplicity of the rules help, but I think the keys and the basic plot are more important.
