Non-Commercial Roleplaying

J_Walton
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Story Games
So when Mike Sugarbaker interviewed me for the OgreCave audio report, I had no idea what I would end up saying. I managed to surprise myself by talking a bit about the possibility of "non-commercial roleplaying." I'm not entirely sure where this came from, but I'm going to hazzard a few guesses.

If you've read Eero Tuovinen's contribution to Push vol 1, you know of his concerns that English-language (and, typically, American) roleplaying publishers are dominating Finnish tabletop and the tabletop communities of many other European countries where English is widely spoken. I think Japan is probably insulated from a lot of this, partially for lingusitic reasons (I should ask Andy). In any case, one of the major points that Eero made is that the Nordic larp movement is, in many ways, a reaction against American commercialization and commodification of roleplaying, because you can't really make good money off a larp, though Mind's Eye Theatre certainly tries. Nordic larps are generally not published commercially, but exist, in my understanding, mostly in the notes of those planning the larp and in the memories of the people who participated in it.

This, to me, is fascinating, and definitely indicates a community that's centered more around play than publishing, which is a weakness of the Anglo-American indie tabletop community, in my mind. Despite the Forge and Story Games' attempts to focus on Actual Play, we are largely about publishing and, more importantly, selling printed game texts. While we may wish it was different, games published for free (or for cost) online do not get as much attention or play as those sold as print products. Many people still don't seem to consider free or PDF games to be "real publishing," which is a damn shame. Here is a place where the indie community could be really subversive, I think, by breaking down the idea that a $50 tome has more value than a free PDF or something Kevin Allen Jr. photocopied at Kinkos. Or that more expensive products are necessaily better one. Or that games you pay for deserve to be played more (which is often what happens).

I'm aware that more and more people are looking to beat the mainstream publishers at their own gamebook-as-artifact game, as products like Nobilis and (especially) Burning Empires clearly illustrate. That's cool and certainly subversive in another way. It's cool to show people that you don't need underpaid creators and/or the backing of Hasbro in order to make beautiful books.

However, I am personally worried about the increasing entry-level costs (as well as time commitment) to be involved in roleplaying. It means that roleplaying will even more be the domain of people with lots of disposable income who are already interested in really dorky things, which is a needlessly limited market. Also, it limits people's ability to get to know a whole host of different games and play styles, because they have a limited amoutn of funds to spend on gaming.

Even more damaging, there's the pervasive idea that if you want to be a better player/GM, you should buy more roleplaying products, which is the biggest, stupidest fallacy I can possibly think of. Roleplaying should not be thought of like a damn CCG, where buying more or better cards makes you more effective. Roleplaying should be accssible to anyone with the inclination and an overactive imagination. Hell, you shouldn't need to buy printed texts about roleplaying in order to roleplay. There already are awesome free games available online, but most of us are saving our best work to be released in print where we can sell it and make a little extra money on the side.

So here's a subversive idea. None of us are really in this for the money. It's a nice bonus for putting out a good product, sure, but it's not necessary to our daily survival (except maybe in Ben's case) and we could certainly do without it. What if we began releasing really awesome games either 1) for free or 2) really cheap. If you need an ideology to get behind, here's the "Cheap Art Manifesto" of the Bread & Puppet Theatre.

    (cont.)

    Finally, here's an even more subversive idea, once which I don't think we're ready for yet. What would tabletop roleplaying look like if it stopped being text-based in the way it traditionally has been? What if it was more like the Nordic larp movement, where games were just notes on scraps of paper and the important thing was not owning them or reading them but participating in them? As an example: imagine a world where Meg Baker and Paul Czege came to GenCon and instead of selling us copies of 1001 Nights and Bacchanal, they just taught us the games, transmitting them as oral traditions. And then we went home and taught the games to the people we knew. What would it be like if the practice of roleplaying was transmitted as a practice instead of in the imperfect medium of the written word, something that often fails to capture the power of actual play?

    And that's what I've been thinking about recently.

    To reign in my initial overenthusiasm a little...

    I'm saying this partially because I get to go to work every day and research Chinese cults (Ken Hite would give his right arm for my job), before coming home to work on games. I don't ever plan on having game design be a major source of income, much less a full time job. I already love what I do. If things were different, if I was working as a wage slave, I might feel very differently about it.

    I'm not trying to be One Size Fits All, One True Way about this. Obviously some games are not going to work as well transmitted orally. But I think I'd be cool if there were more that could be. I'm not trying to tell people they give away their blood, sweat, and tears for free. That's obviously their own business. I'm trying to lay out the advantages in approaching things differently or planning different products with different distribution tactics and audiences in mind, instead of always going with the "let's print some books and sell them" approach.

    One thing to consider: free or easily-taught games see more online and casual play. My experience with playing in online chat games is that we played 100% more of Otherkind and Shadows than we would have if we were playing face-to-face.

    Likewise, one of the reasons that freeform dominates online play is because of the low barrier to entry. When I was organizing and running In Nomine PBeM games (which I did for the 2+ years before I found the Forge), there were always lots of people who were interested in playing, as long as we assured them that they didn't have to own/read the books in order to play. They just read the descriptions of In Nomine on the website and went at it. Some of them bought the books later, but nobody bought In Nomine so they could play in an online freeform game. I would argue that we are missing our chance to reach this audience by offering mostly print products. This is not an audience we could make a ton of money from, since it's generally a non-commercial audience, but it's more sizable than the indie gaming audience in lots of ways and one worth considering for some projects.

    And Ben's right. We've had a small number of influencial free games: Fudge, The Pool, Fate, Risus, Engel Matrix, Active Exploits, etc. Those are awesome. We should do more of that, from time to time, instead of pushing towards print as the default. Clinton's doing some valuable stuff in that department, but there are definitely other ways of making things freely available.

    I guess I was really inspired by playing Bacchanal and 1001 Nights. I feel like, with the right kind of demo and some pneumonic devices, that I wouldn't need to consult the text at all in order to play those games. And that's POWER. Think of the sheer transportability! I would be able to play Baccahanal or 1001 whenever I was in a place that had dice, with zero preperation or forethought. And I could teach it to others and spread it like a virus (or a meme). It's like designing a cult instead of a game, designing a practice that will replicate itself because it's addictive and cool (like the way Jungle Speed totally p0wnd the indie game community at GenCon this year).

    What differentiates this from sneakernet is that I want the recipients to be on the same plane as the original authors, in terms of their ability to rework and redistribute. One of the big disappointments for me was that the OGL never fulfilled its promise -- that you could take OGL stuff and rework it as lightly or deeply as needed for your application. This didn't pan out, basically because far too many game publishers were paranoid and did their best to put out stuff that technically complied with the license but put as many roadblocks as possible in the way of actual reuse (mostly via the "product identity" clauses). That kind of paranoia is destructive. In the case of the OGL, it arose from the fear of competition sucking away sales. But it can arise from noncommercial motives as well, such as a belief in the moral right of an author to prevent modification to his or her work.

    If you look at the way Lexicon is played, you'll notice very quickly that most games take the whole text of the rules, post it on their site, and then revise it to suit local taste. This is awesome, because it means that the game rules are a living thing not frozen in stone by some broken concept of artistic integrity.

    Posted By: JonathanWaltonIn any case, one of the major points that Eero made is that the Nordic larp movement is, in many ways, a reaction against American commercialization and commodification of roleplaying, because you can't really make good money off a larp, though Mind's Eye Theatre certainly tries. Nordic larps are generally not published commercially, but exist, in my understanding, mostly in the notes of those planning the larp and in the memories of the people who participated in it.
    I haven't yet listened the audio report or read Eero's article, but here are few points which spring into my mind.

    Nordic larps are usually tailored for the occasion; they are not commercially viable because there's very little to sell, except for the experience. You'll invest a lot of time and energy to great that one larp, but for the players, that's just a one-day or a weekend long event, from which they pay a small amount of money which can - though usually does not - cover the organisers expenses. There can be some rerun-value for the event, but generally this is not done. I'm not sure if it's really a counter-argument for US-lead commercial roleplaying; perhaps it's more based on the fact that when live-action roleplaying started, there wasn't any commercial material available and people had to learn everything the hard way. And when the first products became available (like MET), it was quickly found out that they didn't really suit the already evolved Nordic way of larping at all. Later on, some of this attitude/difference can be seen on tabletop scene as well.

    There are very few published "rules" or larp books in general, since the larps are usually built from a scratch, based on previous experience and the mentioned oral tradition. You don't run games as such (with the exception of MET which has been used), you create events and tailor everything to suit that particular event.

    One might be able to make a commercially succesful larp, but no one really tries to do this because there's some kind of "everything should be free or at least as cheap as possible"-mentality in Nordic gaming circles. Plus, players usually invest in props and costumes and whatnot. This is slowly changing, though mostly so that players are willing to invest more into a larp event but simultaneously except more bangs for their bucks. So the events are more elegant and provide better, say, food than they used to - but they still manage only to cover their expenses.

    And it's not a bad thing, I think - it's a hobby, something that people do because they want to, not because they expect to make a living out of it. Some of this can also be seen in tabletop playing circles, especially with folks that have been doing larps for a long time. A lot tabletop games - I don't know if it's a majority or not - in these circles are built and run in the same way that larps are; they are tailor-made (and usually freeformish in style, since there's little reason to design mechanics - also a social contract issue) and not based on any published game, except maybe shamelessly ripping of the setting and some themes.

    About the oral tradition; I've never visited a, say, US gaming convention, but I've been lead to believe that they are quite different from conventions around here. Nordic conventions are not a place to sell anything, with the exception of some hobby stores and few indie-publishers. There is gaming, of course, but a lot of the effort goes into organising programme; panels, lectures, workshops and the stuff. The oral tradition is strong and this is where the knowledge moves around. Also, the unoffical, enlightening discussions happening in pubs is a major contributor, here.

    I've been thinking about writing something about how's and why's of designing a "traditional" Nordic larp and how the why's (and how's, probably) have affected the tabletop playing as well. I just haven't figured out where to start and how to make it approachable.

      Oh, man, the upset is totally reasonable. The problem is that the _reasons_ a game might be free are so varied. Look at the three big reasons to offer a game for free:
      1. The creator considers it "undone" or a minimal effort, and offers it for free because she's not going to do anything further with it (or more generally, if I just don't want to bother).
      2. The creator has a philosophical (open source, say) or tactical (Offer core for free, sell supplements) reason why they feel the game should be free.
      3. The creator doesn't feel anyone would pay money for their product.

      1 and 2 have in common that they recognize that the creator's work has intrinisic value, and that this thing being offered for free is an abberation. In the case of #1, it is free because it is incomplete or lacking compared to other products which would be worth somethign because they're complete. In #2, there's a tacit acceptance that the product has value, but the creator is actively choosing to set that value aside for some secodnary reason. Honestly, if all free products were free for reasons 1 and 2, I don't think there woudl be any issue of consternation.

      The problem is that #3 is poison. #3 devalues the work of the creator and, implicitly, all other created work. #3 is a declaration that the work _is_ being offered at a fair value, and that value is _nothing_. Not only is that incredibly self destructive, it is destructive to anyone else who pursues the same sort of creation, because it devalues that _type_ of effort across the board.

      Now, outside of free, these same questions come up when it comes to pricing a game (or other creation) but those questiosn are less clear cut because as a hobby, our pricing model is incredibly crude, so things may end up under or over priced for any number of reasons seperate from these 3, though #3 (I don't "deserve" to charge that much) is still pretty freaking destructive when it shows up.

      All that in mind, here's the rub. I, as a creator, am entitled to release my product for free, even for very bad reasons. Frankly, if my reasons are bad, a certain amount of self correction is likely, and if my reasons are good, they will support me in this decision. However, I must always be wary that my freedom to do so does not necessarily create an expectation on the part of my audience that they are entitled to my work for free or cheap, and folks who release things for free for reason #3 feed this expectation. Similarly, as I promote the virtues of free product, the burden is on me (to my mind) that I am _not_ undercutting or condemming those who have chosen to charge a fair market rate for their material. If I am not careful about that, everything else comes crumbling down.

      In the end, I dont' think anyoen objects to free games being out there, I think they're (reasonably) concerned about games that are free or cheap for bad reasons, or any thinking that results in free being taken for granted rather than recognized as an exception.

      -Rob D.

      Mike, you're not disagreeing with me. I'm not talking about the $$$ cost of participation being the barrier to entry. Let's face it, a group can play with one $0-$50 book and a few other incidentals. I'm talking about the only kind of costs that matter in an information economy (which is what gaming publishing is swiftly becoming): search costs. When there are 1,000 free RPGs, each existing as an intersection of orally-transmitted culture and shifting hand-me-down texts passed from gamer to gamer through informal networks... how does anyone ever discover quality? How do you find other people right here in your town who are playing the game you want to be playing? Exchange value, market value, is a signaling mechanism. It tells me what other people are willing to pay money for. It points me at the kewlness.

      Without that, you have reputation economies. Reputation economies are wonderful things, but they fundamentally only work when you have large amounts of social capital to invest in them. Indeed, "indie" scenes start to fail (as art-creation environments) when they become more about Who You Know than What You Do. I think the indie gaming movement is teetering right on the edge of one of those critical moments. It can turn in on itself and become self-referential and clique/rockstar driven, accessible only to those "in the know." It can "sell out" and become commercially successful, but probably lose the open-ness and mutualism that have characterized it as an artistic movement. Or it can thread the needle - and really change the gaming culture.

      This is like, so intimately tied into the Kerfuffle and Ben's social context posts. What I see Jonathan talking about is one possible social context for game design. I don't think it's new - it's the original. It's the cargo cult all over again. This isn't an automatic negative! There is a reason that model is so strong and beloved. But it's not revolutionary. The really revolutionary model would be to build something that's as broadly successful and accessible as possible but based around a different paradigm of What Gaming Is and How It Is Done.

      Posted By: Mark WI'm talking about the only kind of costs that matter in an information economy (which is what gaming publishing is swiftly becoming): search costs. When there are 1,000 free RPGs, each existing as an intersection of orally-transmitted culture and shifting hand-me-down texts passed from gamer to gamer through informal networks... how does anyone ever discover quality? How do you find other people right here in your town who are playing the game you want to be playing? Exchange value, market value, is a signaling mechanism. It tells me what other people are willing to pay money for. It points me at the kewlness.
      No, this isn't true. The market price (not value) tells you where an upward sloping supply curve meets a downward sloping demand curve, and that's it. If there are thirty idiots are willing to pay $100 for the Big Fat Book of Garbage, but to get to a thousand buyers the price needs to be cut to $2, then the market price will be $100, and not $2, because that's the profit-maximizing price. But a price is not remotely the "true value" of the good; that's simply not the information market prices reveal. If you decide to pretend the price is the value anyway, well, that's you engaging in commodity fetishism right there, and that's your fault, not "the system's". Moreover, you already know all this -- if I asked you if Ptolus ($119) is 8.5 times as much fun as Dogs in the Vineyard ($14), you'd think I was making a joke, because it's too ludicrous an idea to take seriously.

      The way you find genuine quality is exactly the way you find quality now, by playing games, talking to people, posting on bulletin boards, wikis, and weblogs, searching the Net, writing and reading reviews, showing novices how to play, and all the other real work of creating and maintaining a culture. The Internet has made this a lot easier to do than ever been before, too.

      EDIT: I should add that there's nothing particularly wrong with selling games for profit. Just don't expect me to take seriously any argument that it's particularly right, or that it's wrong but mandated by The Man.
