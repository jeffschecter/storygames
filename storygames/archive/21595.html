\n People have asked me to talk a bit about how I fight railroading, GM fiat, illusionism, etc., in the moment when I noticed them occurring in a game in which I am a player.

The truth is, in recent years, I mostly just disengage from games, make excuses, etc., if I notice a game going downhill. (I'm sufficiently experienced that I can usually tell in the first 10 seconds after a GM opens their mouth if their game is going to be any good.)

But if I *am* going to try to redirect a bad GM, there's only one way I've found that ever works with a minimum of collateral damage. It's to insist on enforcing the rules in ways disadvantageous to the players.

Because why do GMs often get so up-in-arms about players insisting on fidelity to the rules? Ostensibly, they don't want players to get some sort of unearned advantage. So if I'm sitting there, going, "No, you can't use that Feat right now because X..." to another *player*, suddenly that narrative starts to shift, and it's clear that my intention is simply that we should *all* follow the rules. That then, sometimes, when the stars align, gives me enough credibility later on to insist on some player-empowering rule being followed.

    \n Again, this doesn't mesh with my own experience of why GMs railroad. They aren't openly speaking about player's gaining an advantage. They want to wrestle control back from the gloracle, and, they claim that better flow&drama&'fairness' is the reason ("no-one should die unless they choose it so I'll fudge in deathly situations" is often heard, as is "they're so optimized that I've stopped using monster HP, I just pretend to, so I can make the fights more dramatic").

    Rules lawyering, regardless of who it's favoring, goes against both their real reason (desire for control) and their stated reason (better drama) and doesn't go over well.

    I've also tried the opposite, "if they as GM can make hidden rolls, then so can I as player make hidden rolls and fudge" as some GMs have said in debates that they're OK with. But in practice the ones I've played with haven't been OK with that at all.

    My main strat when encountering railroaders is to try to steer them to a story game instead. Usually I do go with an emergent narrative like Fiasco or Microscope over a scripty one like Dread or bomby one like Sorcerer. But I'd rather them pre-script a game meant for pre-scripting, or bomb a game meant for bombing, than playing in some 90:s style morass.

    \n I'm pretty much going to skip right over the fully loaded OP here and say this: if the game I'm playing in is a railroad and I'm the only one not enjoying that, I'll make my apologies and leave. It was my error in not understanding the assumptions of play, which everyone else was on the same page for.

    On the other hand, if it's a railroad and it's really not meant to be (Primetime Adventures, virtually any PbtA game, etc) then I'll ask them GM/MC why they are running it that way when those specific instances come up ("Hey, you're skipping the 'Investigate' move and just giving us all the answers, but my playbook is all about the investigating, so what gives?") I'm not a behavioral scientist and the GM isn't a lab animal: I don't have to use rewards & punishments to get them to behave the way I think they should, we can just have a talk about the situation and try to reach an accord or go our separate ways without hard feelings.

    \n When you folks asked how Matt handles these situations, I had to laugh and shrug and think, "Y'all just don't know Matt, do you." Because folks who know him, know that he just doesn't tolerate a lot of bullshit, and he can be pretty direct and vocal about it.

    I tend to get out of convention games if I'm not having fun. If it's "eh, this isn't bad, just meh," I might sit through a full session if I think my leaving will inconvenience other people at the table. If I sit there for 45 minutes listening to the GM explain the backstory, or the GM says "It really doesn't matter what you roll on the dice most of the time, as long as you're entertaining," or I get 5 minutes of spotlight time in the first 140 minutes of play, or we're literally in a "rocks fall, everyone is trapped in the cave, fight monsters!" railroad ... I just leave. I make nice excuses and go.

    With my friends, I'm more direct. I'll stop the game and explain what isn't working for me. Sometimes we abandon games, even abandon whole campaigns.

    I'm not sure that railroaded games are much different than other games that aren't fun for me.

    \n I really like Sandra's post here, which describes how railroading instincts are more about creative control than anything else. That matches my experience.

    I also agree with people who say that "they just leave", but, in practice that can be stymied by two things:

    * Railroading practices aren't always immediately apparent. Many of the techniques are hidden from the players (e.g the GM fudging monster hit points).

    Even in retrospect, sometimes it's hard to spot subtler forms of railroading - and if there is a lot of doubt we're reduced to a "he said/she said" situation.

    * The Mystery GM Cult is so strong for some people, that it means that we're simply *not allowed to even talk about the possibility*. In other words, me asking a GM whether they would change an outcome or force a certain event in play will always be met with a strong refusal (regardless of what the GM actually does) and taken as an offensive accusation.

    Worse yet, for some people it's so strong that they don't even admit it to themselves. That's when it gets really tricky.

    As an example of the former, I recently played in a convention game which was definitely railroaded, complete with a very specific pre-scripted ending where the players had no valid choices to make and ended up dead.

    However, the nature of the game was not at all apparent until the very last moments of play. There was no way I could have simply left the table early, because it was very hard to tell that anything like that was going on. (The scenario revolved around a big mystery underlying everything, but we had no idea about that. In practice, it meant that we couldn't tell whether events happening were being scripted or not - and I don't think they were, on the small scale. It's only in the retrospective provided by the final reveal that we could see that various actions and choices we made had no real impact on the final outcome. In play, however, without knowing the Underlying Truth of the scenario, that was impossible to judge.)
