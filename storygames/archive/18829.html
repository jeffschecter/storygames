\n Every single time we've played Primetime Adventures, it's gone: maybe one or two conflicts in the entire game, and mostly just free-form scenes. This is with different groups. I'm thinking the issue might be me!

In our games, we have pretty vivid show creation. Scenes vary between introspective scenes with little interaction between characters (others aren't present) to a few of them together, interaction a-plenty. But every time we go to do conflict, it feels unnatural. Like, we can just not do it and the scene is the same. Because of this, there's little incentive for Fan Mail. I mean, we're doing it unofficially: "that's awesome!" - "that was really funny!" - "I'd really like to see the next episode of this," - but we're not doing it mechanically, because we don't have a reason to.

And this keeps happening! We do one or two conflicts, and we just abandon it because it feels like it's getting in the way. It's a solid system, but it feels like it's restricting us making our show. Everyone I've played with has agreed - different groups, different years.

In fact, this most recent game (just this night) people were saying we didn't even need the Producer in an official capacity. I was the Producer, but I could've just been a player. Mechanically, it could work: the Producer can go from 1 to 6 cards, budget wise, and so you could just roll a d6 to decide how many cards go down. If it's more likely the budget would be big, roll two dice and pick the highest. But we don't, so really it's just us doing scenes one at a time.

Has anyone else felt this way? What could we be doing differently - I wouldn't say "wrong" - that's encouraging this kind of play? I don't think anyone has a problem with the rules, we just fail to engage them when they've shown up.

    \n I don't play PTA often, and I'm not about to say that anyone's doing it wrong unless it isn't working. And conflicts by-the-book in PTA are tricky; they take a lot of mindbending to get right and the result may not be what you want.

The whole point is to mark good play at the time it happens; to go "yes, that!" without being as obtrusive. This is why people used to fan-mail will often make the "fan-mail" gesture even if there isn't any fan mail to give.

You don't have to stop roleplaying after the conflict even if you play by the book, but by mechanically passing around that section of the "GM" duties it lets everyone flavor resolution.

    \n For starters the game, as written, won't work without fanmail flowing. If you "take it out" you've taken out the currency that allows the game to continue. At that point you are sitting around a table telling each other a story, which is a fine thing to do but has no relation to PTA.

    Simon, you're right that the mechanical impact of fanmail is pretty minor in resolution, but the ecosystem it forms half of is the engine that drives play. It is explicitly non-diagetic, which is super interesting, because it is the only component of the game's design that really is (you can play PTA from a deeply metagame perspective, and people do, but you don't have to and I never have).

    So you have to give and receive fanmail if you are enjoying the game and want it to continue. If you don't share it, the game stops, which is a graceful interface between rules and social contract. When that happens it's time to go do something else, and it is a nice reminder.

    How and when you give fanmail is something each group hashes out on the social level, so it is possible to do it in a stupid, ugly way I suppose. When you play with people you trust and respect what seems to happen is that fanmail becomes a metatechnique for apportioning not spotlight but attention - it ends up getting shared more or less equally, and if someone hasn't caught any recently that is a flag for the whole table to put the deserving character in situations where they are likely to. And since it is the entire table's responsibility (and the Producer is locked out of that currency), it is sort of a carrier wave constantly reminding everyone to actively listen and care about each other.

    \n FWIW, the reason I (and, I believe, Rickard) dislike fanmail isn't that it doesn't work as an economy or anything similar, it's for the well-documented psychological effect of people enjoying things less if they are rewarded for doing them, assuming the activity is intrinsically rewarding. Basically, extrinsic rewards undermine intrinsic rewards. However, this only applies to economical, and not social, rewards. This is why I think it might be important how mechanically "desirable" the fanmail is. If it's mostly symbolical and not very important, it becomes a social reward and all is fine. If it's something obviously valuable that buys you important things like screen time or success in the game, then the overjustification effect is likely to kick in. Not having played PTA, I'm prepared to give it the benefit of the doubt and I'm sorry if I criticized it unjustly.

    I have the same beef with keys, by the way. My one session with a game that had keys (not TSOY or SS) felt hollow because the XP reward took the fun out of doing awesome things with my character. Again, it might have been the implementation in that specific game, or badly written keys or something, but I have no doubt it's a real effect. I'm prepared to accept that it's not an issue with how many people play PTA, however, because of mechanical, social or even cultural reasons.

    \nI know what you mean, it's the "points for good roleplaying" thing. In Sweden we call this "poodle show" (pudelutst\xe4llning) or "poodle points" (pudelpo\xe4ng). You put the poodle on display and the judges look at it and decide whether or not it's a good poodle. Anyway, the phenomenon I was talking about is something different, more subtle. Did you look at Rickard's Wikipedia link for the overjustification effect?\n
    two meta-analyses found that, for high-interest tasks, intrinsic motivation is diminished by expected, tangible rewards in both children and adults, especially when the reward is given for simply performing a task, regardless of the results. Nontangible rewards, such as verbal praise, and unexpected rewards do not undermine intrinsic motivation. In fact, praise may actually increase intrinsic motivation, as long as the praise is interpreted as sincere and a valid signal about the actor's competence.
    \nSo the important thing here is whether the reward is seen as "tangible" or "praise". If fanmail is percieved as a thumbs up, it can increase motivation. If it's seen as a reward, a resource that you need and want, it risks having the opposite effect. Interestingly, the effect here is stronger when it's given "regardless of the results", like with keys, and weaker when linked to performing well, like with fanmail.

    EDIT: Note, though, that if the player thinks fanmail is given just for trying, even if her contributions aren't as great as the others, the praise is no longer percieved as "sincere and a valid signal about the actor's competence", which will not have the effect of increasing intrinsic motivation.

    \n I played freeform for many years (and still do). There's distributed narrative authority, chronological play, no planning ahead or editing past passages, etc. It can be fun and functional.

    The question about PTA (or any game product, really), thus becomes: What does it add to your fully fun and functional play?

    If collaboratively deciding the outcome works for you and drawing cards doesn't add any value, then this particular group doing this activity wouldn't benefit from using those rules. And that's cool. But there might be benefits you haven't seen or considered.

    So! I think the most useful direction for this kind of conversation is to examine the potential benefits your particular group could get from these rules. I'm going to list a couple, and I'm sure others have their experiences to share as well (some of which are up there, just not formulated this way).

    Disclaiming decision-making: when I run freeform games, I sometimes come across a situation where I'd rather disclaim the decision of a conflict to dice or cards. AP example: ancient Egypt heroes run out into a swarm of locusts to save a priest. Are they getting hurt? How much? Each equally (which would be fair but feel off)? Let's draw cards to spare me from agonizing over this decision and move on quickly.

    Exiting the comfort zone: when we control the characters' fate, it's easy to be nice to them. Or harsh, depending on what you want. Use the cards to allow the possibility of something unwelcome to occur.

    Audience experience: one of the things setting RPGs apart from other creative endeavors is that the creators are also the primary audience. Let yourself be surprised, the story taken in unexpected directions, by letting the cards decide branching.

    Pacing: in PTA specifically, the budget provides a mechanism. By using cards and fan mail and the director's budget, everyone can feel which act of the show you're in and when it's time to start wrapping things up.

    Again, this is not exhaustive and these might not appeal to you. But this way you can think about whether or not using the conflict rules makes sense for your group.

    \n This overjustification effect is being used as a point of authority here, so I thought it would be useful to take a moment to think about this. I personally suspect that the effect is being overextended here, thanks to different uses of the term 'reward'.

    In common parlance, game reward mechanisms are things that exist within the game system*. XP helps you level up which provides you with fun toys to play with. It's rare that you talk about a game reward system that asks you to collect $10 off each player (although now I've mentioned it, someone will design a game about it). Fan mail is a game reward mechanism that affects the system, and so we might think it's the same kind of reward that psychology research talks about.

    But I don't think that's true. Psychology typically refers to a reward to mean something of extrinsic value that follows an event: in operant conditioning, where the word got sharpened for purpose: peck the button, get the birdseed. Birdseed isn't an extension of buttoning, it's just a good thing that follows an event.

    It's my understanding that all the research into overjustification follows this meaning of reward.

    * solve puzzles, get money
    * build a model volcano, get a badge

    Even the counter-findings mentioned are examples of rewards that follow the event and sit outside of it. Solve a puzzle, get praise.

    I'm not aware of any instances of the OJ effect where the 'reward' is a component that extends or changes the original task/activity.

    * solve puzzles, given trickier puzzles to solve?
    * build a model volcano, get given fizzy lava to put into your volcano?

    And with it laid out like that, I'd be personally very surprised to hear that kids are less likely to make model volcanoes if they are promised cool fizzy lava to put into it.

    Maybe fan mail isn't quite fizzy lava. But it's more fizzy lava than a valid gift certificate. So I'm not sure that overjustification tells us very much.

    *I know, the Forge definition is a bit more complex. Not sure it's pertinent, though.

    \n John and Simon, good points, which have helped sharpen things for me.

    The overjustification effect, as I understand it, is the consequence of presenting an activity as instrumental (important because it achieves something else) instead of presenting it as worthy on its own terms, which is a better way to motivate.

    what I'm trying to get at is that any 'reward' which is part of the original activity, rather than apart from it, is less likely to trigger the effect. If I reward making stuff with cash, then I'm doing stuff for cash (instrumental). But if I'm making stuff for the opportunity to make more stuff, then the original activity is still framed as valuable - because the pay-off is the activity.

    Of course, there is always a risk. Fizzy Lava could certainly instrumentalise volcano-making if it is portrayed as the cool activity you are trying to win, rather than a cherry on the cake to make your awesome volcano even more awesome. But this is also true of verbal praise, or non-verbal feedback: there is some non-zero risk that someone will think 'you know, the only reason I'm using this silly voice is because everyone seems to like me when I do it'.

    So I think it's fine to point to the overjustification effect as a point in principle, but less so to point to metanalyses and argue that this provides evidence that we shouldn't have reward principles. I just don't think the science extends that far: all the significant effects I know of all involve rewards that sit outside the activity, therefore instrumentalising the activity. Fan Mail, unless it buys you benefits outside the activity, shouldn't be considered under the scientific demonstration of the overjustification effect, even though sometimes, for some people, they will find it instrumentalising (because individual differences are huge, and people can react many ways to many things)

    I could be wrong, which is why I asked for examples before. It's not exactly an idle question for me as I've taught this topic before and am involved in putting together course materials for university students that reference this finding, so I'm interested in understanding this properly.

There is (or seems to be) no research that suggests there would be a difference between rewards within an activity and rewards external to it. You might be right, and you might be wrong. The research we have says that there's a difference between verbal praise and token rewards, and between unpredictable and expected rewards, but that's it.

    I'm not sure that the person saying "the only reason I'm using this silly voice is because everyone seems to like me when I do it" wouldn't still be motivated. These effects are seldom conscious and logical. I think the effect is a consequence of misattribution. There is plenty of research showing that if you talk to a person and feel good, you will think that you like that person, even if the reason you feel good has nothing to do with that person. I think it's also related to confabulation, where a person will make up a reason for an action they undertook when they don't know the real reason (for example, because it was subliminal). It is clear to the observer that the reason was constructed after the fact, but the person still believes it.

    I suspect the overjustification effect works much in the same way. I'm doing this thing, and when I'm doing it I get rewarded. Therefore I draw the conclusion that I'm doing it for the reward, not because I like it. If I'm doing the thing and I don't get rewarded for it, I must like doing it, because the only thing I get out of it is the activity itself. Compare with hazing, where I'm doing this thing (joining a fraternity) and I get punished and humiliated, but I still do it. Therefore I draw the conclusion that I must really want to get in. That's how hazing strengthens group loyalty.

    This of course doesn't explain why it doesn't happen with verbal praise, but social interaction has so many other processes and effects going on that I suspect it's just interference from a different process.

    (Oh, and I'm an engineer with no formal education in psychology. I'm just an interested amateur, so feel free to correct me when I'm way off.)

    So I think it's fine to point to the overjustification effect as a point in principle, but less so to point to metanalyses and argue that this provides evidence that we shouldn't have reward principles. I just don't think the science extends that far: all the significant effects I know of all involve rewards that sit outside the activity, therefore instrumentalising the activity. Fan Mail, unless it buys you benefits outside the activity, shouldn't be considered under the scientific demonstration of the overjustification effect, even though sometimes, for some people, they will find it instrumentalising (because individual differences are huge, and people can react many ways to many things)
    \nI would still quibble about what tangible means, but overall I agree with you that the science doesn't extend that far to really drive game design. However, conversely, there is a common idea from earlier behaviorist psychology that any desired behavior should be rewarded to motivate it. A lot of people have designed based on this principle. I don't think we should feel a need to put in a mechanical reward principle like Fan Mail for what we want. It's fine to try, but one should also try without it. My experience is that many games in practice get great results from behavior with no mechanical reward.
