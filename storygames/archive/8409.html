\n Simple concept, really: Our community (at least in my experience) is very wary of saying "X is a better way of playing than Y." This is all well and good and tolerant when you're talking about (for instance) "Deep drama isn't a better way of playing than crunchy power-gaming," but there's a dark side to it too. If you don't say "X is better than Y" than you can't say "X+Y is better than X on its own." You can't, in short, say "Hey, here's a tool that can help us play better."
\n
\nAt best, you can say stuff like "Well, this tool has helped me and maybe ... blah, blah, blah." What other recreational activity could possibly flourish under such mealy-mouthed refusal to advocate for techniques? Are multiple gears something that has helped me with bicycling? No. They are a critical tool for all but the wackiest of serious bicyclists, and if I came upon someone trying to cross-country on a one-speed Schwinn I would feel no compunction whatsoever about telling them to get a bike with some gears before they hit the Rocky Mountains.
\n
\nDo you guys have any good ways for talking about these things? Because I get easily stumped. I can see the problem, but can't see solutions. I mean, seriously, how do I say to someone "Y'know, you'd be a much better player if you just X" without being an utter twit?

    \n Tony, I think there are two extreme positions here, which are:\n
    • Whenever someone says "This way is better", they are merely expressing an opinion
      • \n
    • Some ways of playing are objectively better than others
    \nNeither extreme is entirely satisfactory. To the first extreme, we can respond that some methods of play seem objectively better: for example, listening, or not hitting other players in the face. To the second, we can respond that many methods of play that are touted as objective are actually subjective: for example, "The players should not read the adventure before playing it" or "You should push each other's boundaries".

    We're likely to end up with a postmodern position, where people do argue that "X is a better way to play than Y", and different people argue different ways to play, but we accept that as a good thing. We understand none of the explanations are objectively correct, but we learn something from the variety of different explanations.

    Even then, it's not entirely satisfactory. Are any of these explanations actually better? Should we treat them all equally?

    (This means I agree with Jonathan, above, and I imagine he'll explain it better than me).

    \n In my opinion, there isn't "better" or "worse" in gaming, which is why better/worse discussions don't make very good progress. Gaming is about "more fun", "less fun", and "not fun at all." Play advice is, I think, all about better and worse ways to get to the fun.

    Talking about fun is harder than talking about better, because when I say "this is better", I've got the power. When someone contradicts me, I can easily challenge them to back up their statement. When I say "this is fun", you've got the power. If you say "I tried that, and it's not fun". I can't baldly contradict you. I have to back up my statement now. That's the core challenge of talking about gaming, at least as I see it.

    If we talk about better and worse in gaming, it needs to be in the context of a better/worse way of describing fun; a better/worse way of achieving a certain goal on the way to fun; a better/worse way of getting the fun out of a particular system, theme, game, or situation. And we do need to talk about what's better or worse, we just need to do so carefully.

    \n Wow, this discussion got abstract really fast. Tony, can you help bring it back to some of these concrete "better" things? Here's my "walking the dog" thoughts: I feel that the key problems with improving player behavior in a significant way are

    1. Can the behavior actually be changed without disrupting other behaviors that are important to the play experience? (i.e. how do you change just a small part of the interconnected whole)

    2. Context: "Better" how, for whom, and under what conditions? (specific games, play styles, group dynamics, when playing with specific individuals)

    For example:

    You could say, "Jonathan, you aren't very good at games in which you have to compete tactically in order for your contributions to be valued by the group or in the fiction. You're always trying to be expressive through the mechanics instead of just playing hard to win." (This is true.)

    Or you could say, "Jonathan, when you GM, sometimes you consistently restate other players' contributions in your own words, stripping some of the spontaneity and energy from play, as well as robbing players of feeling like they are directly participating." (This is something I keep an eye on and like to think I have gotten better at.)

    Or you could say, "Jonathan, when you are organizing or GMing a game, sometimes you facilitate a play environment in which players compete to do the 'most awesome' thing instead of following the natural ebb and flow of the fiction, creating an awkward social/narrative dynamic." (Something I try to avoid, but sometimes still happens.)

    However, not all of these are universally bad things that should be avoided if my play is to get better. They're mostly bad in specific circumstances. Maybe I should just avoid competitive games entirely, if I don't enjoy that kind of play, instead of trying to play them in a way I find unsatisfying. Honestly, I'm not sure I could change that part of how I approach games without destroying my overall play priorities. Also, sometimes it's important for a GM to restate player contributions to validate them or work them more effectively into the fiction, as long as it's not overused; plus, there are entire styles of play (which we may consider less functional, but some groups seem to love them) that thrive on seeking GM approval and validation. Finally, there are some games and play styles in which the escalating awesome thing is totally functional and great.

    On the theoretical side, aesthetic relativism in art comes from, uh, three main revelations, I think:

    1. High and low art were traditionally defined by the idea that some people have good taste in art and some don't. Good taste was thought to be cultivated through education and regular appreciation of great works, which was, of course, limited to the upper class. This was later found to be arrogant bullshit. So I'm personally really wary of assuming that study and personal reflection necessary leads to play that is better or more fun. Certainly, it's changed the way I play, but it's also made certain kinds of play, which I remember once really enjoying (relatively passive participation in GM-authored adventures), completely unsatisfying.

    2. Descarte was really into subjectivity, right? Nobody can know what goes on in anybody else's head, which makes it really hard to judge other people's aesthetic enjoyment of something and decide whether it's better or worse than their own enjoyment. All we have is what people say they enjoy, and we pretty much have to take their word for it. If folks claim to really love play styles that we consider to be based on dysfunctional relationships and full of ridiculous power fantasies, who are we to say that they don't really? Or would enjoy something else more? We have no idea.

    3. The "institutional theory of art" was really influential, arguing that good art is basically determined by a group of influential people agreeing that something is good, meaning that value and quality are socially determined. Took us hundreds of years to figure that out, crazily enough. This holds true in roleplaying, but on a micro level as well. The only people who can determine the value of a specific play experience is the people at the table, which makes universal declarations of "better" really difficult and contingent on who's sitting across from you.

    Hopefully some of that is useful to this discussion.

Remember those movements to have strong criticism of games that were terrible or lacking? Yeah. Honestly, your post reminds me of that in some ways. You're saying that we've constructed a social environment in which it's not okay to say the really harsh things you want to say, about how other people's play (or games) are no good. Honesty would make everything better. Would it? I'm not sure. I feel like the existing social environment of "play and let play" (or "publish and let publish") does a lot of good. Is relativism really preventing us from making our own play better and better? I'm not sure. Do you really want me to tell you all the things I find annoying / less good about the way you play? Maybe not. Would it make playing with each other more enjoyable or just make the social situation between us worse, such that we might never play together again? Who knows.
