    \n a) Rough math: if you shoot ten shots, you'll hit four times, on average. Nine shots, slightly less. So, around 50/50.

    a.5) Using AnyDice to roll custom "die" that have 10 sides, four of them with 1 on the face, six of them with zero (i.e. 0.4 probability). Each time a 1 comes up is a hit. You need four or more hits. Roll nine of these dice at once. AnyDice says chance of >= 4 hits is ~52%.

    b) It's slightly easier to figure out the chance of failure, then take the complement. To fail, your nine shots need to generate either zero, one, two or three hits. To figure out the chance of exactly a certain number of hits r from n trials with success rate of p, the
    raw formula is C(n,r) * pr * (1-p)n-r.

    In this example, it is always the case that n=9 and p=0.4

    Chance of exactly zero hits (r=0) = 0.01
    Chance of exactly zero hits (r=1) = 0.06
    Chance of exactly zero hits (r=2) = 0.16
    Chance of exactly zero hits (r=3) = 0.25
    Total chance of failure (summing the above) = 0.48
    Total chance of success (1 - chance of failure) = 1 - 0.48 = 0.52

    c) Since gamers will never answer b (and probably not a, either), they will ask an infinite amount of questions.

    Posted By: timonkeyAre there many autistic gamers?
    \nIf you're counting all forms of Autism spectrum disorder...then I'd say yes. Probably more autistic gamers than the general averages across the population. Asperger's Syndrome is a high functioning form of Autism (with a number of symptoms that could be the basis for the typical "Nerd" stereotype), such individuals often have a fascination for numbers, patterns, systems and rule structures (while often lacking in areas such as social skills or emotional development). Roleplaying offers these individuals a way to focus on their patterns and rule structures while engaging in social play at a limited and more predictable format. I'd almost be inclined to posit that around half of all "rules lawyer" types are autistic at some degree on the spectrum (especially those who are busy searching for all those little +1 bonuses in the obscure pages of their hefty gaming books, while the rest of the table gets fed up with them).

    I know that I have Asperger's, and I know a number of Asperger's support groups that use roleplaying as a means of socialising some of the more "socially inhibited" members, gently coaxing them out of their shells through structured encounters. Some of these groups also make regular forays to Sci-Fi and Comic conventions, but that's more to do with a common interest in the group and plays back into my beliefs that Asperger's is probably behind the "Nerd" stereotype.
