    \n Yeah, I think we are probably in agreement then. Like, whenever someone says "I want to play This Game, but using the rules from Other Game," I think that one of two things is happening.

    1: They really like the premise ofThis Game, but the rules aren't facilitating the themes or situations that come up at their table. So they think about the other games they own and pick one whose rules are a better match for their table's style. Then they get to use the setting but with a different playstyle.

    2: They really like the rules of Other Game, and they want to use them more often. They wish Other Game's rules applied to a different genre or playstyle that they really want to run, because the rules are just so awesome but the premise isn't grabbing them, or they're burned out on it.

    The second scenario is the one I see more often and it usually leads to less compelling games, it's true. There's a million and one ApocWorld hacks because people want to make 7-9 lists for everything ever. But usually they don't really want to hack AW, they just want to keep using that ruleset for other genres.

    So... if you've been playing Delta Green a lot and the rules are fighting your playstyle, and you've thought about what's missing and it's definitely DitV, then like HELL YEAH go for it. But if you've been playing or reading DitV and love the rules but want a fresh setting, then it'll probably be much more difficult and maybe not really what you're after?

    \n So, I have some thoughts on game-hacking and its legacy (and the ethos of game design), but it doesn't seem heavily relevant to the main subject of this thread.

    I think for the "Delta Green opposes new knowledge" schtick to work, you have to find the gray areas where standard human knowledge starts crossing the line into something touched by the Old Ones, something that can be a gateway into eldritch invasion. Some areas where this might happen: paintings, mathematics, poetry, music. Like, there's a composer making stuff like this, and that way lies the path to the Old Ones entering their mind and entering this world.

    So, knowledge and expression that strays from the established norm, that's the sort of thing that lets the monstrosities gain a foothold in this world. And Delta Green operatives need to quash it. Maybe you start with missions where it's really overt (a professor has uncovered a mind-destroying theorem and is awakening an Old One), and then you move on to other missions where what they're doing is crushing human individuality and creativity in the name of saving us all.

    And that's the spark that drives games like Dogs.

    \nBut thats exactly what Delta Green is about! I think people are confusing Delta Green for vanilla CoC in here. If you read the DG books and, specially, the fiction anthologies (like say, Dark Theaters or Alien Intelligence ) you will see most stories are about DG agents dealing with communities, cults or individual madmen trying to make contact with unspeakable horrors for knowledge/power/curiosity, or simply out of fear. The agents moral dilemmas are in dealing with people (not monsters) and the personal sacrifices they make because of that. Even if it means sacrificing other humans, their friends, relatives or even themselves.

    Thats what Delta Green is about. Thats why I thought DitV would be better vehicle for exploring its central themes than its default, theme-agnostic, system.

      \n Having read through all of this there's a definite issue that a lot of us (me included) just don't know enough about the Delta Green world to gauge how well it would all fit.

      One thing that standard setting Dogs has going for it is the idea that the community is small. There's no surprise if you come in to a branch and find the person in trouble is your cousin, or your sister in law. It adds that twist of the knife. With the Jane example, in Dogs Jane wouldn't just be 'some pregnant woman with a possible demon spawn in her', Jane would probably be your sister, or the woman who everyone said you'd marry when you grew up.

      Is there a way to give Delta Green that level of attachment? Is the community of people who almost know small enough that the agents could have personal ties (a friend, a training buddy, an old professor) that would add that poignancy to the situation?

      Alternatively, is it something that could be built up? The other push with Dogs is that the choice you make today decides the choice you are going to be given tomorrow. So in theory you could start with less personal situations and then slowly ramp them up. You make this decision when faced with strangers doing it, what decision do you make when it's slightly worse, but it's people you know? What if it's people you know and they tell you they have a really good reason for doing it? What if it's people you know and the really good reason they give actually sounds like a really good reason to you?

      \n And here's another idea:

      How do deal with madness and sanity loss? Let's make it a bit like the Dark Side: something which changes you, but drawing on it potentially gets you what you want.

      I'd have to give a thought to when or how these things manifest, but, basically, losing some kind of sanity conflict (not too different from a healing conflict, perhaps) can have one of two effects, presumably as Fallout. (And perhaps these become an option as Fallout from any old conflict, as well, if it makes sense in that context.)

      1. You can take Madness Traits as a Fallout option. These are things about you which change who you are: delusions, transformations, various psychological issues. Maybe you gain something like, "My focused stare causes panic in animals and children - 1d6".

      First of all, these descriptive changes to your character accumulate and, over time, change who he or she is dramatically. They also form a bit of history of your character's psychic trauma over time. (And I like the idea of the Initiation being an incident which gives you your first Madness Trait.)

      You can call on these Traits in conflicts, of course, but, since they are somewhat monstrous, it means revealing your twisted or warped nature. If you keep them hidden, you don't get the dice. If you let them show, you're more likely to get your way. Being weird and scary now becomes a temptation. You might use your focused, otherworldly stare to convince some children to perform some task for you, for example.

      2. As a second option, the GM can take some kind of currency to represent you losing your grip on reality. This is not a change to your character that you're aware of and can control (like Madness Traits): this is reality slipping out of your grasp.

      Let's call these Madness Dice.

      The GM can spend these dice in play for various psychedelic effects. The bigger the die, the stronger the effect.

      They manifest as:
      d4 - a minor detail, sufficient to creep out someone noticing, but you could easily think your eyes played a trick on you
      d6 - a significant detail, something you wouldn't forget, and clearly out of the realm of the ordinary
      d8 - a minor episode or hallucination, likely causing a frightening altercation if noticed
      d10 - a major episode, including dramatic harm or change to anyone affected

      When the GM spends these dice, she *lies* to the player about what their character is doing, saying, or seeing. With minor details, it might be something innocuous, and the lie is just that the character is not aware of it. Perhaps some cockroaches have taken up residence in the character's coat, and occasionally crawl out onto his hands or face, only to disappear again. Other characters will see the roaches, but the character himself cannot - they do not exist in his reality.

      Perhaps the agent's sister-in-law has been leaving phone messages on his answering machine... but the character does not remember them. He listens to them and erases them, with no memory of having done so.

      More dramatic events or episodes manifest as character action. Perhaps the character is telling a story about something to a friend, or relating a weird dream, and doesn't realize that this story *actually happened*: they've made themselves forget that. (In play, we might have the character describe a dream, and then establish retroactively, when the die is spent, that this really happened.)

      More typically, the character will experience hallucinations. The GM might describe a dead person - a revenant! - entering his home and threatening his life: perhaps the agent fights back and kills the intruder. We later learn that this was his sister-in-law coming to check on him, concerned that he's not returning phone calls. Now the agent has to figure out what to do with her mangled corpse. (Or perhaps he still doesn't realize what really happened, but remains convinced it was a revenant!)

      In short, the GM can play with the agent's perception of reality by spending Madness Dice.

      If this happens in a conflict, of course, the die becomes a Trait for the opposition - or the agent - as the GM deems fit. A cockroach crawling out from under your hat might help you convince someone to get out of your way, but it could also cause the librarian to panic and call the police.
