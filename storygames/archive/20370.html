    \n So, for those of you familiar with Durance, here's how I'm looking at adapting parts of it to Barsoomian Chronicles [B.C.].

    Pass around a work sheet as in Durance (from the planet survey and colonial records) and players circle one thing which will be true and cross out on thing which is false - about Mars and its pantheon of gods.

    \t1. The gods reside on Mars [or 'off' Mars, e.g. the 'heavens'; cosmos; etc]
    \t2. The gods came down from the stars to Barsoom [or the gods originated on Mars].
    \t3. 'Spirit Races' were on Mars before the gods [or were created by the gods - 'Spirit Races' are lesser entities - think elemental spirits, etc]
    \t4. Mars begins its existence as an ocean planet. [or as a dry rock]
    \t5. The gods of Mars are strictly hierarchical. [or anarchic]

    Thus you can end up with Mars beginning as a rock in space, with the gods descending to it from beyond. Note, in this iteration, there would be no equivalent 'planet and colony codes of three letters'.

    \n Much appreciated, Ry. I think it's a great exercise in trying to clarify things.

    What we mean by a game about Australia would be a game about co-operatively creating Australia's geography and history.

    It is about Australia in that (the Australia of the straw man) is an Australia who's geography is limited to:
    1. only about a dozen major known cities;
    2. the Burraga Swamp and the Herveys Mountain Range;
    3. at the time of the Great Fire of Brisbane.

    And this straw-man's Australia has no history before the Great Fire, save for a few hints at what came before the fire, amounting to a few paragraphs of text.

    The assumption is that players who would want to play characters in this Australia (especially if they've never heard of a place called 'Australia') would have some questions about what it's like, and what came before the fire (especially if they are going to play native Australians).

    The common approach, it seems, is to include a chapter on the history of Australia in the RPG rulebook. This is fine. Another approach (the one I'm exploring) is to replace that chapter with a game in itself, which would aim to make 'sure that players are familiar with the history of the gameworld without having to bore them to tears with endless exposition or giving them a novella to read'.

    \n That's a good question, and one that is answered early on in the game by players creating a 'Menu' of what they agree can be included, and what can't.

    In the example you offer, one of three things would happen:

    1. your SkittleTM-eating rocket-cats would be overruled ab initio as they would fall under the 'not in this game' portion of the Menu; or

    2. a player who had final say over races (or technology) in the game would veto them as being inconsistent with the themes of Barsoom (themes previously decided upon by the group); or

    3. that player would allow it, as the group understands that, for them to exist, either (a) the poor cats will become extinct by the time of John Carter (when the RPG 'proper' will be played), as they are not recorded by ERB/JC or (b) the wacky cats will survive, but evidently as such a small population as to not have been noticed by the major civilisations on Mars (compare the kaldanes).

    \n Yes, indeed.

    So, in B.C., there are a few different ways for the group to come up with what they want in the setting. Drawing on Microscope, it would be that portion of the game when the group is listing what they want 'in' the 'Palette'.

    For B.C, one way is for the group to look over a list of suggested 'Ingredients', and choosing things they particularly want to see. Below is a short sample from this list:

    \u2022\tcold-blooded control of the common people by authoritarian jeddaks
    \u2022\tthreats to the corrupt establishment
    \u2022\treason versus superstition
    \u2022\tfanatical religious authoritarianism and devotion

    Each player chooses two of these, and then the group explores Barsoom's (pre-J.C) past to see how those themes developed through history until arriving at the stage they are upon the advent of J.C. on Mars.

    \nThere's no problem having a game with Barsoom and gods. That game is:

    "hi guys this is my game, it's based in a version of Barsoom - like the John Carter Mars, but I riffed a bit on the gods of the setting."

    "cool. Do I have to read the novels to get it?"

    "No, I made a 2 page handout and you can ask me the rest as game goes on. I'll fill you in if its important."

    "Cool."

    ------------

    The above is perfectly fine and great. But when you add microscope to that, it's really problematic because in Microscope everybody can add their ideas. But if we're playing Microscope, but
    1) one player knows the "real" setting
    2) that player has elevated authority compared to the other contributions
    3) what playes make has to conform to this other text which we have varying levels of access to
    4) it doesn't even conform to the text, but to one player's idea
    then we're really not playing microscope. In fact we'll be fighting with Microscope the whole way because there's this mother-may-I element that's been tacked on to it.

    \nI see this come up a lot, and my answer is to not say \u2018We\u2019re using rule blah from Microscope here\u2019 but to say instead \u2018We\u2019re using rule x here\u2019, i.e. don\u2019t mention the game where that particular rule or mechanic (or your interpretation of it) has come from. As soon as you start saying things like \u2018We\u2019re using such-and-such mechanic from game so-and-so you immediately lay yourself open to possible negative comparisons of your game with the game you\u2019ve taken that bit of the system from. You\u2019ll probably still get comments like \u2018Oh, but Game Blah does it this way\u2019, but you can simply say to that \u2018Yes, but this game isn\u2019t Game Blah\u2019.
