\n Could you give me a list of effective techniques for railroading? I need them for this thing.

I'm looking for examples of bad practice, but not too jokey. So not this:

Keep teleporting the PCs to alternative Earths until they get it right!.

But these are good:

Stare and sigh when they do the wrong thing.
Make sure that, wherever they go, they get to your thing.
If they go in the wrong direction, tell them it's "pretty boring" there.

You know? Stick close to reality and things you've seen.

    \n -Keep saying no until they give up.

    \n - Use the phrase "Your character would know..." a lot.
    - Make only one thing at a time have any level of detail.
    - Take one player aside secretly and tell them what's supposed to happen next. Give them a bribe for helping you steer things that way.
    - If the players are discussing options, wait until they mention the right one, then immediately jump in and behave as if they're acting on that one.
    - Increasingly specific prophetic dreams.
    - Boil down the question of what happens next to three or four options. Describe them to the players, then roll dice behind a screen. Announce the correct option.

    \n Simple one is to just make them really want to do whatever it is that you want them to do.     \n Listen to the players, figure out their deal, and play to their motivations.

    Example from REAL LIFE - I'm playing a druid:
    GM: "You hear the sounds of some kind of hurt animal ... and something happily singing in a gutteral voice about how he's having bear for dinner tonight."
    Party leader: "Are we going to go save that bear, Jamek?"
    I shrug. "It's the circle of life."
    GM thinks for a split second, then: "Now it's singing about how it's really going to enjoy making the bear suffer."
    Me: "That's it. I'm going. I shapeshift into tiger and plunge into the woods."


    Also - let your players know ahead of time it's a canned module or old-school dungeon crawl - then they'll understand that it's partly their responsibility to not fuck things up.


    Also - you can try enlisting the player: "So, this session you're going to be investigating the fortress of Al Akzah. Why are you doing that?"
    Hopefully the player will come up with something like: "Because the fucker has my sister in his slave warrens!"
    But then maybe it wouldn't be a railroad anymore...

    \n Let players do whatever they want.

    But when they go off track, don't use any mechanics or pretend they might be on track. Just describe what they do in a series of quick third person narration scenes to clearly indicate that they have gone off track or hit a dead end. This way no time is wasted exploring anything that won't ultimately matter.

    It's also really effective to follow that up with, "time is running out" and "the stakes are now higher" since you spent time off track.

    If I know I'm playing this type of game, I don't mind it at all. It's only when I don't know I'm playing this style, we go off track, roleplay and use mechanics as if what we are doing matters\u2026 and it doesn't.

    Posted By: JDCorleyScene frame.
    \nThis is what Smallville does (and some Jeep Form). The book essentially tells the GM to act as a literal director and Scene Frame hard. I had to warn my players that it may feel like railroading but all the scenes will be focused on the character's dirves and relationships instead of the GM's plot. Plus Smallville gives you mechanical tricks in case a player doesn't agree with the Scene Frame so they can later flash back to tell us what they were planning to do and how it helps them later.

    I don't personally consider Scene Framing railroading although I can completely see how others might. For me, it doesn't feel negative because once the scene is framed, I have complete freedom to do what I want. The Scene Frame is more about setting up situations quickly and clearly. But how I resolve problems is completely up to me and there isn't 1 way to win.

    That said, I think Scene Framing could be a useful tool in a railroading GM's arsenal.

    \n I was playing in a game of Dread hacked for Robotech by a Call of Cthulhu GM who had a reputation (when I asked around later) of being extremely railroady, to the point where I was told people come to his games to passively listen, so I guess he's a master railroader. I learned a lot.

    * Take a lot of time preplanning the presentation, keying it to moments you've already scripted, so that you can feel justified forcing people to go on your rails. E.g., develop a soundtrack for the game.

    * If the rulesystem doesn't really permit railroading, break the rules. When this fails because the players complain, tell them you're doing it right and rely on social discomfort to prevent any of them from pursuing the matter further. E.g., if a player has something on his sheet that says he can pull one less block, you change the facts you presented after-the-fact so you were always right anyway.

    * Have a failure to notice something be a key ingredient to the next part of your story happening, then throw out details that people have no reason to pay attention to and punish them for missing them. This has the benefit of letting you feel like you've been fair, and to feel free to mock them for missing it. E.g., the pilots are guarding 12 trucks and you keep talking about how there are 12 trucks, then at some point say there are 8.

    * When a player goes off script, never ever tell her that you're not prepared or comfortable. Instead, find of in-fiction reasons why she really ought to know better and if she continues to insist on doing something that seems perfectly reasonable, then cut her out of the game and don't go back to her for a few hours. Remember that punishment is as important to a good game as killing her independent spirit. Why not join them?

    * Crucially, DO NOT pick a game system that allows people to get around your blocking of them by possessing other independent skills. This, sadly, was the one area in which the master railroader of my example failed to utterly lock things down. Next time, maybe.

    \n Not rolling dice:

    The GM knows the desired result and makes it happen. Not to be confused with the "Say Yes" part of "Roll the Dice or Say Yes"! Frex: my character is trying to convince the king to lend him a unit of soldiers and the GM doesn't have me roll dice because that is what the GM wanted.

    This can also be used to block a player's desire when it conflicts with the GM's desired outcome. Not to be confused with the GM saying no because the player is being a dick.

    \n Only techniques used in games I actually played in (sometimes, Heaven forgive me, I was the GM). If I had listed all the things I heard about, this list would me endless...

    - If the PG don't do what Elminster said, they get caught and impaled in the public square (this was in a official AD&D2 module I did run when I was learning how to GM. One of the PG was impaled)

    - If you don't do what your god tell you, you will lose your powers (many, many times, with different classes and games)

    - There is only one way to solve this adventure, every other way will fail. You have to do exactly what I said (I used this many times and saw this used even more times.       \n The one technique out of all of these that I am aware of is the first on the list. The rest I reckon should work given the right group and context, if not pushed too far. Some will engender a long-standing antipathy between you and your players. Some of these are only "horse to water" techniques, but after that why not booby trap the party into submission?

      FORESHADOWING VIA TIME-TRAVEL
      The party see their future selves trying to fandoogle the McGuffin. It is clear from the situation that this is of vital importance. Shortly after they stumble accross a time-portal. If you can get them through the portal, then you have them eating out of your hands from then until the McGuffin is finally fandoogled.

      FORESHADOWING VIA WAVING PRETTY STUFF AND PROMISES AT YOUR PLAYERS
      Let's say you have a super cool picture of an ancient Aztec temple with live sacrifices, a lizard king, a handful of white slaves in feather bikinis and battle harness clad dino-riders. Wave it under your players faces at the start of the session and tell them that this is where they are going to end up. They would be rude not to follow your rails after that.

      LOOK AT WHAT I SHELLED OUT FOR, DON'T TELL ME I'VE WASTED ALL THAT MONEY
      Expensive unless you're on very good terms with the local game-store owner, and as long as your players fail to notice the book is still in its shrink wrap. Should work well in conjuntion with the previous item.

      THIS IS WHAT YOU SAID YOU WANTED - MAYBE WITH JUST A TOUCH OF BAIT AND SWITCH
      Ask the players what the would like to do in the next session. Try to do this with leading questions, such as what sort of monster they would like to fight. If they say 'dragons' then you know that any kind of plot lead that hints at dragon involvement will be leapt on. See how long you can string them along before they start getting suspicious.

      MASS TRANSIT METHODS
      Probably more effective with ocean liners or spaceships than trains. Make sure the best way the PC's have to get anywhere is by some sort public transport. They are then captive for the rest of the journey, without feeling they need escape or turn on their 'captors'. You can bung plot hook charlies at them like confetti, and if this is all done in club class the players are less likely to slaughter the poor blighters out of hand.

      IN SPACE NO-ONE CAN COMPLAIN ABOUT AN EMERGENCY STOPOVER
      Ships can be forced to make emergency stops at the port of your choice. They can be forced to make a diversion to a more out of the way port of your choice. They can be mangled up by a gigantic [space] octopus leaving the PCs stranded on the desert island [or planet] of your choice.

      YOU'RE IN THE ARMY NOW
      It is probably best as a premise for the campaign as a whole. A lot of games have this hard coded into the ruleset. Timemaster had the PCs going back in time to foil evil four armed shape-shifting aliens and their time-meddling schemes. 3:16 and Requiem inserts them directly into a no questions asked "follow the plot my little underlings" chain of command. As noted already, if you try to lay this on the players during the game, then their immediate superiors tend to become their most immediate adversary, which spoils the whole point.

      TAKE THEIR TOYS AWAY
      Take away one of the characters most prized possession. They will go chase it to the ends of the earth. Of course your players might be a little more grown up than that, but compare and contrast this technique with the previous one.

      TAKE THEIR CHARACTER SHEET AWAY
      This is seriously the most desperate. If a player consistently breaks out of their straightjacket, tell them that they have a perfect right to do that, but that their character is in danger of becoming irrelevant to the plot. Maybe they should think about creating a new character that would fit better in with rest of the group. Do the best you can to make out that your hand was forced on the issue. If you are enough of a bastard you might want to try and marginalise them socially as well, until they knuckle under or agree to create a more compliant alter ego.


      DISCLAIMER
      I am very disorganised, and don't know how to prepare sufficiently that railroading could ever be my thing. In fact I positively exude unreliability and very rarely run games at all. However if I did I expect I would do it as described.

      (Andy Morris, UK edition)

      Well that exactly describes my issues with GMing and the reason why I'm not playing anymore. I just got railroaded by players :-) (rant off)

      To be usefull in the thread.. (I'm kind of ashamed what I came up with, didn't know I'm such hidden railroader)

      Support our intentions by praising, rewarding players for doing so.

      Take away their stuff, a lot (makes them weaker and more vulnerable. also with previous point make usefull "stick and carrot" scenario)

      Support weak players (makes you more important)

      Make them know you are dissatisfied (with the play, with their choices)

      Whenewer they ask for/on something make it small, unimportant if it is not in your focus. If it is, describe it graciously and with details.

      Don't tell players what is your intention, make the players create/invent it.

      In case of making a choice make..
      - the way that is clear to you. other ways unimportant/non passable (make the players want it)
      - the other way that would lead your way or back to the point of decision, after some sort of unimportant complication
      - after you let them play their action, urge players to get to points where they have to choose one of first two ways

      Trade with players realization your intention for realization their intention, but only if theirs intention are not in contradiction with yours.

      Make players action unimportant.

      If you want make play interesting, describe in full details two choices, both of them would lead in the same ending.

      When in doubts, say "I don't think this is important".

      When in trouble, say "I have non of that prepared".

      Prepare your NPC, places, situations to be playable at most of the situation you can imagine. Make them floating-able and flexibile.

      When PC would take initiative in way you did not planned, make the NPC/events to have major role in the play.

      ...and, always pick your seat first! :-)

Funnily enough when these are deployed by a reasonable GM with a good awareness of what the group wants they're evil but when they're decided by a die or a pre-written rule, they're fine. Go figure.
      \nIt's the intentionality behind the action. When the dice come up bad it's luck, when a rule says you can't it's a system you've bought in to, when the GM tells you no it's arbitrary.

      Three similar examples: If I want magic flight powers but the game says I can't because we're playing Dogs, I suck it up and not fly. If I'm playing something where I can fly but I have to succeed in a roll to pull it off and the dice come up bad, I suck it up because sometimes it works and sometimes it doesn't. If I'm playing a game where I have always-on flight powers but the GM doesn't want me leaving a place so he declares my flight power unusable, I've just been screwed.
