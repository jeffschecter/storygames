\n I'd like to try an experiment here.

- I'll give give you the frame for a game (see further down).

- You may design the game and post it here (or link to it), or you may post a brief of how you possibly would make the game.

Here it comes:
_____________________________________
The Mob
It is a game for adults.
It is about sticking out, and getting it hard.

The game is in three stages:
Initial game - the object is to find someone who sticks out amongst the characters.
Middle game - the object is to enlarge his/her faults by gossip and slander, but being polite whenever he/she turns up.
Endgame - the object is to confront the character, to bully him/her in a "humorous" way, this being "necessary" to defend against his/her bad influence/charma/smell/whatever. The endgame should go towards flight, murder or suicide.
The player of the ostracized character should be given some kind of extra attention after the game.
_____________________________________

Any game-posts may be discussed, of course, and the game-frame as well. Be my guest!

    \n Initial game - players frame scenes where they try and fit in and then roll 2d6. 1-2 their "Stand-outness" grows, 3-4 their Stand-outness stays the same, 5-6 they can either decrease their Stand-outness or increse the Stand-outness of another player. Rense, repeat until someone reaches a high enough level of Stand-outness (say 2 plus the number of players).

    Middle game - the most stand-out player starts and trys to do something to fit back in. Roll 2d6 and the higher number is the number the other players must meet or exceed in on their turn (rolling 1d6) in order to further embaress their friend (add one to that player's Stand-outness). Dice that roll lower go into the Stand-out player's pool. When it is the Stand-out player's turn again if they have not reached some number (say 12) then they can roll the dice they collected this round to see if they can lower their Stand-outness. Every die that meets or is lower than the number they rolled on their last turn lowers their Stand-outness by one. If they lower their score to the number of other players then they successfully re-intigrate with the group. They get to keep any dice that roll 6 in a seporate pool.

    If they manage to reintigrate themselves then they get to roll an extra die to try and push other players out until thier pool runs out.

    If they get over 12 then move to Endgame

    Endgame - the Stand-out player rolls all of the dice in their seporate pool to determine their end. 1-2s run away from their problems (let each of the other players describe where they ran off to and what they are doing now and the Stand-out player says which one is true), 3-4 suicide (the Stand-out player's body is found and the cops are vague on the details of her death; let each of the other players describe how the Stand-out player's life ended and then the Stand-out player says which story is true), 5-6 the Stand-out player chooses one of the other players and murders them describing how they do the deed.

    That was just my initial thoughts. Numbers might not be just right but I thought they would work for a game like you described which seemed to be focused on a one shot experience. As I was writing it I kept thinking about the movie Mean Girls which had alot of this feel. I could also see raising the numbers and lowering the end consiquences for a Desporate Housewives sort of game. That would require some more "session level" mechanics for what happens in each episode. For campaign play you might also want to add more abilities and qualities to each of the characters that allow them to alter their rolls in some way or to alter others rolls to their advantage. Another campaign level mechanic could also be a way to gain new abilities or qualities, improve existing ones or impose abilities or qualities on other players.

    > The players then, via anonymous ballot, vote on who is 'Red' (two-three votes makes you Red, depending in the group size). Everyone is secretly informed of his or her new designation and interaction continues but the aim is to provoke potential Reds into implicating themselves in Un-American activities or communist sympathies.
    > In the final part another ballot is held (or open denouncements, depending on taste) and the Commie is outed! The FBI arrive and drag the traitor away for interrogation, the True Patriots may congratulate themselves and wholesome American citizens can sleep safe knowing their minds are no longer being polluted.
    > Epilogue: The Commie reveals whether he or she was indeed a Red. Were your paranoid accusations justified? Is democracy to blame? Are you a monster? Those True Patriots who are actually Red should now unmask themselves.
