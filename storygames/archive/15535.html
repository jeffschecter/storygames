\n I've been playing more games where I give up narrative authority to the players. I use a fairly standard technique of providing them with a starting situation (usually In Media Res) and a "long-term" goal.
\n
\nI let the players figure out how they get to that goal and all the good stuff that comes between. I let them fill in the gaps using something similar to the InSpectres method of successful die rolls allowing players to create clues that lead to the next objective. The problem comes with pacing. Sometimes things work well and the group has a grand adventure trying to complete the goal, coming up with several of their own objectives that must be met in order to progress towards the end goal, each objective complicating their own lives but making a much better story (and they even feel that way after the game). Sometimes the group feels like they should be able to solve the goal in one scene, leading to a less than satisfying gaming session.
\n
\nI was considering implementing something whereby the players must complete X number of smaller objectives before reaching and resolving the goal. The objectives would be left up to the players to create themselves and drawn from the narrative, but it gives them a guideline on how many milestones they must reach before the story reaches its climax. This controls the pacing a bit better, but still leaves control up to the players.
\n
\nOnce they choose the next logical objective to progress the story towards its goal, they would make the InSpectres roll to add details about the people, places, and things that would help them reach the objective.
\n
\nAny thoughts? Is there a better way to achieve what I'm trying to do? Is this a terrible idea?

    I find that pacing can be really hard to get right in an automated way (i.e. using rules and no human judgement), especially if you want it to work every time.

    \n

    Have you considered using GM fiat to say, "Ok, you guys have gotten far enough, you can now figure out the mystery. " ? This way you can judge if the suspense is at the right level for the players. This is more-or-less how it works in my game Muse after I spent years trying to figure out a good mechanic.

    \n

    If you really must do this with a rules-based mechanic, have you read Geiger Counter? It's got an interesting mechanic where the players get closer and closer to figuring out the monster/mystery, basically every time the monster reveals itself it gets a bit stronger and when it reaches full strength (at about 8 appearances) the PCs can now try to kill it. I think somethig similar might work for you.

    \n Is the object of the game to challenge the players or to make a cool story (which challenges the characters)?

    If it's the latter, I think you should *let* the players control the pacing. They'll automatically calibrate the pace of the story to what they're interested in, zooming in and out of areas of the story. If I'm more interested in this part (the wedding ceremony), I'll set small goals ("Can I carry the candles to the altar?"). If I'm not interested at all in another part of the story (the fight on the mountaintop, I can name a goal at the scale that interests me ("Can we defeat our enemies and make it to the Eastern coast on time?").

    The only restriction I would set would be to make sure that everyone realizes any named goal must be believably achievable in the current scene. So if someone says, "I want to fly to the moon!", you respond, "OK, how?" And that probably generates some other smaller goal.

    But if they're sitting in a rocket on the launchpad, and they want their goal to be "landing on the moon", it tells you that they're not all that interested in playing out the journey to get there. And why would you want to make them play through something they're not interested in?

    \n Hi Bobby,

    Your investigation method sounds pretty close to how SotC works already, so I think you're probably on the right track. Where you say "they create and add facts" I'd say "they declare new Aspects and assess Aspects that the GM created." You declare with knowledge skills and assess with perception skills (see p. 83). I think it's worth keeping the declare/assess distinction, so those skills feel distinctive in play.

    One tweak we've done that works well: Instead of assessing and simply getting an Aspect from the GM, the players can ask questions about the subject, and get an Aspect from that. Like, "does he have anything that involves his history in the Crimson Brotherhood?" "Yep! He has the 'Champion of the Brotherhood' aspect." Making it a Q&A lets the GM add an aspect on the spot if the players ask an especially cool question.

    \n I've been playing a lot of Technoir lately, which has me thinking about vectors.

    By way of explanation, Technoir features a mechanic that allows a player to push an adjective onto another character or object. (For example, you might push "crippled" onto Bill's character. That then becomes part of the fiction, has to be respected, and also has mechanical consequences.) The important part of this is that you can't always "skip to the end"; you have to be able to establish a proper vector.

    Similar thinking applies to the situation you're talking about, I think: You've got players who are skipping straight from "there's a murder" to "I catch the murderer" and that's problematic for you. The solution is to look at the vector they're using to achieve "I solve the murder". And the question is, have they actually positioned themselves

    So, for example:

    "I catch the murderer."
    "How are you doing that?"
    "I go to where he is."
    "Okay, well first you need to determine where he is."
    "I go to where he is."
    "How are you doing that?"
    "I figure out who he is."
    "How are you doing that?"

    And you've established the vector: You need to figure out who he is, then you need to figure out where he is, and then you can catch him.

    Of course, this conversation could just as easily go:

    "I catch the murderer."
    "How do you do that?"
    "I set a trap for him."
    "How do you do that?"
    "I'll announce to all the party guests that the victim has actually survived and ask if anyone knows shorthand so that dictation can be taken in the Master Bedroom."

    And that, obviously, gives you a different vector.

    In practice, this is similar to playing a traditional RPG with a flexible GM. Where the players get narrative control, however, is dictating that a particular vector will work. (For example, in the traditional RPG the murderer might not be at the party or he might not fall for the bluff or the plan might fail for any number of other reasons even if the GM is perfectly open to the possibility of its success. With narrative control, the players can either dictate or determine whether or not the guy is at the party.)

    I don't have play experience with InSpectres, but my understanding is that it does something pretty similar with its Job dice: You collect Job dice through successful checks, but you can't actually complete the mission until you've collected the pre-defined number of Job dice required. If the players end the mission prematurely, then they get penalized half the Job dice. You'll notice the mechanical incentive to avoid "jumping to the end": The game is forcing the players to improvise vector paths of sufficient complexity.
