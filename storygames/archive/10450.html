\n I was flipping through the advice in the DMG, and it seems like the GM has basically unregulated power to determine surprise and encounter distance. Am I missing something?

What I'd like is something with inputs like this:

1) What is the terrain / visibility? (Dungeon / Fog / Hill / Clear Plain)
2) What is the PC party's disposition? (Stealthy, Wary, Normal, Obvious)
3) What is the NPC party's disposition? (Stealthy, Wary, Normal, Obvious)

The middle would be a few checks (Perception vs. Stealth) or other guidelines, depending (like, just looking at the passive perceptions).

and the outputs would be
A) Surprise, if any
encounter distance

Does anyone have this already?

    \n I've been using @ side's Perception vs Stealth, but with Players rolling active vs Passive in both cases.

    E.G., from one encounter looking for group of monsters preying on travellers in area (thus each group is trying to avoid detection and detect others):

    PC's make perception (vs foe Stealth) and stealth (vs foe Perception) = surprise for party: You see a harpy flying into a stand of trees ahead by the trail, and spot small reptilian figures vanishing into same trees ahead, where sounds of voices come. [speaking draconic] 20 squares [a good range for missile fighters, ability to come at site from different directions, ability to avoid encounter, longer not sensible in the wooded terrain]
    - from that point, moving up etc. we go into combat time actions and that may require more stealth checks

    PC's making perception & failing stealth = even footing in encounter, no surprise round: There are small reptile-men moving into place around you, though they\u2019re not quite yet at \u201ccombat range\u201d (by not being stealthy they didn't get chance to spot the harpy, who is 100% concealed further back in woods) - they\u2019re difficult to spot among the trees. 10 squares (a respectable distance, can back peddle for more distance, close to engage, lots of terrain features around in between)

    PC's fail perception & make stealth = close encounter, You realize there are creatures in the woods around you, that you have blundered into even as they blundered into you. No surprise; encounter starts at close quarters. 5 squares.

    PC's fail perception & stealth = bad ambush, just a bad ambush on the road if failure not by 5+, if failure by 5+ the monsters trail PC\u2019s till they make camp and attack on the 1st watch (which coincidentally is in an area battle map wise much like the first 3 even if narratively someplace else), You have just fallen asleep when an attack comes! Individual perception checks for the PCs on watch (PC who failed originally doesn\u2019t reroll); monsters surprise from ideal positions. 5 squares

    I have started using the "everyone make Stealth, you don't fail unless majority do" instead of having the poor stealth dwarf PC be the reason they never surprise anything.

    Exceptions for terrain just as dungeons or situations where you know the attacking goblins are coming up the valley from that direction and it's just a matter of when you spot the next wave.

    I apply general logic of:

    20 squares + is great for PC's (who are usually better at distance than monsters)
    10-20 squares is a respectable distance for getting into formation you want, taking cover etc., usually still lets party use its optimal versions of each (defenders to fore, wizard behind rock, ranger up a tree etc.)
    5-10 is "lets' get it on" initiative matters alot, with it the controller and striker can scramble back to cover/concealment, without it they are getting monsters in their face.
    0-5 is advantage suprise range "PC's caught with pants down if surprised, but ideal if surprising as it's within charge and hit 'em in first suprise round range".

    Would be interested to know other's experience, thinking.

    \n Ryan, I don't think I would use a generalized system for starting encounter range, because...\n
    the outputs would be
    A) Surprise, if any
    encounter distance
    \n...those outputs need to become inputs to the battlemat setup. My battlemats are special snowflakes. I want them to be big fun drivers. Having their design be partially driven by some generalized encounter distance algorithm seems like a reversal of priorities to me.

    Like, say I design an enormous battlemat intended to provoke a slow maneuver / approach phase to the encounter. I'll probably just dictate the starting positions of all figures, to ensure that all the design work I did around the approach phase isn't blown away by some rule that says the encounter starts at sneezing range.

    If I think the encounter would benefit from the possibility of sides ambushing, slipping away from, or surprising their opponents, then I'll use something like what Ralph Rob does.

      4. \n
      1. If party wins both rolls: DM sets up opposition, then party sets up as they like, anywhere on the battlemat. If they're not in a fighting mood, the party may opt to bypass the encounter entirely.
        2. \n
      2. If party wins one roll: both sides blunder into each other. party is placed on a logical entry spot by DM, in their marching order / formation. Monsters are placed in their standard spots per the encounter. Roll initiative.
        3. \n
      3. If party loses both rolls: DM sets up all combatants and does not need to respect marching order.

    \n Here are some relevant bits from the DMG:
    \n
    \n
    Encounter Distance: For outdoor encounters, start the characters 10 squares away from the monsters. If terrain or visibility suggests a short-range encounter (dense forest or thick fog, for example), use 5 squares instead. In wide-open areas, such as rolling hills or farmland, use 20 squares. In open terrain, characters might see monsters at even greater distances, which gives them a chance to try to avoid an encounter entirely (especially if the monsters don\u2019t see them). Don\u2019t start an outdoor encounter with either the characters or the monsters at the edge of the map. Leave room for everyone to move around, which for some characters often means getting away to a safer distance from the monsters.
    \n
    \n
    \n
    An area about 10 squares by 16 squares is a good minimum for parties of seven or more characters.
    \n
    \n
    \n
    Room to Move: Make sure everyone has enough room to move around. For most encounters, the minimum is an area roughly 8 to 10 squares on a side (which happens to be the size of the largest D&D; Dungeon Tiles). For an important encounter, consider a space as large as 16 or 20 squares on a side (two of those 8-square-by-10-square dungeon tiles). A postersize map like those included in Dungeons & Dragons Miniatures starter sets and in D&D; adventures covers an area roughly 20 by 30 squares and makes a great area for a climactic battle. Folded in half (at about 15 by 20 squares), it also works well for other important fights.
    \n
    \n
    \n
    Bigger Creatures Need More Space: Large and Huge creatures need more space. An encounter that includes Large monsters needs at least 16 squares by 10 squares. With Huge monsters, the encounter area should be at least 20 squares by 20 squares (or about three large D&D; Dungeon Tiles). Gargantuan monsters work best on poster maps.

    \n What I do in my game when I roll up a wandering monster:
    \n
    \nI consider the terrain and sighting distance.
    \nI consider what the monster is doing; flying, stalking, just wandering about, lurking in ambush, etc.
    \nThat defines what kind of check (if any) is going to be made. Sometimes it's PC Per vs. NPC Per, sometimes PC Stealth vs. NPC Per, sometimes PC Per vs. NPC Stealth. If circumstances are right (dark night, no moon, fog on the moors, combined with low Per on both sides), both groups might wander into each other and be surprised.
    \n
    \nWhen I rolled up the bandits who prey on the King's Road from the bluffs overlooking the road, I used the PC's Passive Perception check vs. the NPC's Passive Stealth. The bandit was stalking the PCs and they dealt with it.
    \n
    \nWhen I rolled up a bunch of griffons, the PCs spotted them flying from a distance away; since the PCs had no horses, the griffons ignored them. The PCs were able to spot which mountain top they roost on (Perception check).
    \n
    \nWhen I rolled up the forsaken shell who was patrolling the entrance to the Keep on the Shadowfell, it was lurking, so I compared Passive Perception vs. its Passive Steath; the PCs missed it and it followed the PCs into the Keep.
    \n
    \nGenerally I'd use Passive checks, but if the PCs want to be "on the lookout" I'd reduce their movement and allow them to make a roll. That means Aid Another is in play, so the result is almost always going to be better than Passive Perception.
