\n Is anybody else reading this book? Although the historical coverage is a bit exhausting, most of it is really interesting and it may spark further reading on the indicated sources.

One of the things I've wondered is if one could assemble a D&D-like game by copy-pasting from earlier sources. Here's an example from 1879 on the role of what would come to be the DM:

"[The Referee] should bear in mind the principle that anything can be attempted. The advisability of an attempt is another thing, and one that it is the object of the War Game to make evident to all concerned by results."

"The decision in all such cases devolves more or less upon the Referee himself, but should seldom be so rendered as entirely to foreclose the question; it should rather be formulated into a ratio, in which both sides receive due consideration, and under which, by suitably consulting the dice, a decision can be arrived at without prejudice to either side."

"Table I will be found of great convenience for reference in these premises. It by no means covers all the cases liable to arise in action, but, to say the least, it is suggestive. In all such cases a large discretion must be exercised, and, when tabulated data do not exist or are inadequate, the Referee must originate."

"It will thus be seen that upon the Referee, in particular, will depend the successful realization of very much of the vast amount of interest and information which may be derived from a study so comprehensive. And it should, of course, be needless to state that the players themselves should enter into the spirit of the game, and supplement to their utmost every effort of the Referee to perform his arduous duties."

- Charles Totten, 1879, \xabStrategos: a Series of American Games of War\xbb: http://catalog.hathitrust.org/Record/006111870

    \nWhile Playing at the World exhaustively analyzes the mechanics of the first version of D&D, remember that the central thing that set the Blackmoor and Greyhawk campaign apart from their immediate progenitor was the fact the players could attempt anything their character could do even if there wasn't a specific rule to cover the action. This cause subsequent campaigns and slightly later the D&D rules to rapidly become something different.

    With wargames you play the game. You play chess, you play go, you play Chainmail. However you experience Blackmoor, Greyhawk, and Dungeons & Dragons. The wargame rules that lie at the heart of many RPGs are there to make resolving certain types of actions (commonly combat and magic) fun and interesting. But they are not the point like it is in a wargame. This crucial shift in focus is what caused tabletop roleplaying to spin off into its own branch.

    \n Michael Korns would inject himself into this thread and remind everyone that his work brought wargames down to the individual level, to turns that lasted only seconds where you made decisions for a specific character in a military situation, back in 1966. And that both the Twin Cities and Lake Geneva crowd knew his game intimately.

    It's futile to try to draw a line in the sand and say that Arneson's Blackmoor or Gygax's Greyhawk transmuted gaming from being about wars to being about roles (or characters). People pushed the envelope of wargaming in the late 1960s, and plenty of practices we would happily call wargames "presented experiences" or involved playing characters. People played characters in Midgard, in Coventry, in Hyboria, in Braunstein, in Diplomacy, in Fight in the Skies, in countless other games we should label wargames. The revolution, if there was a revolution, occurred after D&D came out, when both the authors and the fan communities took the game to places that were not obvious in its original formulation. When they started playing at worlds, say.

    This is indeed one of the major arguments of Playing at the World, that the line between wargames and role-playing games is hard to draw.

    \n I hear what you're saying. Maybe we could put it this way. Wargames are about approximating the experience of command in times of war. Wargames in the Hellwig tradition (which include more or less all of AH and SPI) do so by letting players directly manipulate pieces representing their forces located on a visible board. Wargames in the Reiswitz tradition (which include Totten, Korns, Braunstein) do so by letting players act as the character of a commander. We know that RPGs emerged from people playing games in this latter tradition. They emerged when the referee - and those games always needed referees - allowed the focus of the simulation to drift away from issuing commands to military forces. Players could always "attempt anything" in this tradition, but the duty of the referee is to keep the game directed at the "general idea." Wesely's Braunstein, Arneson's Blackmoor and some of Tony Bath's experimental wargames showed that new avenues of creativity were opened up when the referee let things slide. Arneson's Blackmoor in particular provided a focus for these games other than winning a war: personal progression, the increase in power of a character. The dungeon helped too, certainly. Games that combined these principles very rapidly adopted play elements that looked little like the precedents of wargames.

    My point was that while we can create tidy explanations in hindsight, it wasn't tidy at the time. When D&D was published, these ideas were at best half-baked, and it took the community some time experimenting with the game for these distinctions to become rigid enough to draw genre boundaries. Some people picked up the D&D booklets and tried to play a wargame with them - and it worked, because the tools in the box could still be used to make wargames.

    \n It was about 1982 when my best friend and I started playing a game we called "Win or Lose?" In the game, one person (the "GM") asks the other person if they want to win or lose. The other person answers. The GM gives them exactly what they asked for. End of game.

    I realized that these games could be strung out in series. The name was changed to "Succeed or Fail?" and I counted each question as a "scene goal" in a larger narrative. While the player could choose for the character to fail in the fiction, of course there was zero chance of player failure in the metagame, which was rather the point of the exercise.

    It was parodic on purpose, but philosophically it presages the drive toward abstraction in mechanics that we have witnessed across major regions of the industry over the last ten or twenty years.
