    I'll answer those questions separately and out of order, if I may.
    4) What is broken about Rewards?
    This is the biggest problem I have with the rules-as-published. The reward system is very straightforward: the longer you survive, the more Kewl Powerz you get. I want the players to be rewarded for addressing their thematic agendas, instead of for showing up to play and for keeping their characters alive.

    The power level of the characters restricts the content of the story. It doesn't take all that many XP before any number of mortal beings with conventional equipment are no longer an interesting source of adversity. If the group isn't interested in taking on major governments single-handed, the system shouldn't sucker them into building their characters up to the point where what they do want to do becomes boringly easy.

    (Switching to question 5 for a moment, I'd be happy to toss the XP rules as written, and probably the training-time rules as well, and just say that characters gain dots and Charms by group consensus.)

    I want the players to be able to declare a thematic agenda using explicit, rules-endorsed cues, and to be rewarded for taking actions that address that agenda. I want the currency of that reward to be short-term advantage (bonus dice, say), rather than long-term advancement.

    3) What is broken about Virtue[s]?
    Let's do a cost/benefit analysis. The benefits:
    • You can channel them for extra dice. That costs Willpower, which is the most precious resource on your character sheet, and gives you a dice bonus that is tiny in comparison to what you can get from even the simplest of Charms. Furthermore, you can't do it very often.
    • Temperance and Valor improve your character's resistance to certain magical effects that come into play fairly rarely.
    • Conviction determines the rate at which you regain Willpower.
    The costs:
    • They give you Limit and drain your Willpower when your character behaves in a manner contradicting them. Having a high Valor is downright suicidal; high Compassion gets you in trouble around slavery, which is ubiquitous in the setting.
    • They increase the severity of a Limit Break.
    A sensible player will always buy up Conviction (especially) and Temperance (to a lesser degree), and leave Valor and Compassion at exactly 2. So what if you don't want to play a character who's ruthless, calculating, stoic and ascetic? Doing anything else incurs heavy penalties.

    The particular selection of Virtues, along with the economy of Limit, appears to be intended to encourage characters who behave like the heroes of pulp fantasy and Greek mythology. However, the rules just don't work.

    2) What do you see the problem about power and responsibility now? Can't a good GM/group just work this into the game as is? Why not?
    The current system is the standard White Wolf incoherent Gamist/Sim mish-mash. It punishes players who build characters that don't go in one of a few specific directions by making them less capable of doing cool stuff than their fellow players. Because of this, the system imposes a judgment on the players' choice of themes. That's a Bad Thing and must be corrected.

    Yeah, a group that's committed to a Narrativist agenda can accomplish it in spite of the published rules, by drifting the system. That is, in fact, what I'm trying to do here—but systematically, in advance, rather than piecemeal over the course of play.

    what do you suggest if you're expecting a transitional period where you are balancing the demands of amassing greatness and being the master of your world?

    A two-tiered system with transition rules! I mean, fundamentally we're talking about a long-term game that's meant to be played over the course of many sessions. The early sessions will be about survival and amassing power; the middle sessions will be about amassing power and confronting global/universal conflicts, and the last sessions will be about confronting those massive conflicts and dabbling in transhumanism.

    So you'd need to embed some metric into the characters/campaign that decides what sorts of rules you have access to. Think of like, way-old D&D where the upper character levels had you picking up a castle and retainers and knights and shit. A very clumsy example, but you get the idea. Set up the system so that, at the start you must or are only able to focus on survival and amassing power, then the survival eases up and you're able to address larger-scale problems (that probably drove your survival conflicts before) while still amassing power, and then when your power-amassing infrastructure can basically work on automatic (if that needs to be unpacked, tell me), you finish off your confrontations and look forward to what sort of world it will be once you've vanquished the big bads.

    Exalted as printed notably only has rules applicable to the first stage where survival and power-mongering are important, begins to break down in that middle range, and offers hardly any support for the last stage. If you recall Mage, it had the same problem, and the Masters of the Art book attempted and failed to address it -- they didn't want to face the simple fact that the game was about playing neophyte-to-middling-experience mages.
