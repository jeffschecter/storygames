Is the Lumpley Principle incomplete?

Adam_Dray
February 2006 edited February 2006 in Story Games
So, over in [TSoY] Wings of Shadow Climax, I posted a bit about how I didn't think the GM had done anything wrong.

To summarize what he did:

The GM told them their entire TSOY campaign had been a VR simulation gone bad in a Shadowrun game. He even handed them Shadowrun characters based on their TSOY characters. He thought it was a trippyfun thing to do, and at least one other player thought it was the cool shiznit, too. The rest of the players were all WTF and got pissed.

My wife Steph and I talked about this, and my initial reaction was WTF, too, and Steph beat it into me that the GM did nothing wrong.

Certainly, when players disagree about something in the fiction, System steps in, right? Lumpley Principle. System is how players agree on changes to the fiction. Or "shared imaginary space" or "SIS," if you don't like "fiction." The problem here, as I see it, is that this was not only a change to the fiction but also a change to the System.

Should the Lumpley Principle say: "System is how players agree on changes to the System"? Gah, I hope not. Circular definitions aren't terribly useful.

If LP doesn't contain changes to System, what does? Is that just Social Construct outside of rules? What if you write rules that tell you how to modify the rules? Is that part of System (subject to LP) or not?

    Whether the GM was "wrong" or "right" has nothing to do with System. System is what happens, not what is supposed to happen.

    So, LP-System-wise, the GM dictated that he was making massive changes to the game. In the absence of any formal means of discussing the matter, the players did not stand up and walk away from the table, and so the gigantic change was ratified. Further, by saying "we are no longer playing according to TSOY rules" the GM removed any and all authority tools that the players may have had access to under the old system -- what intrigues me is that it was assumed that the GM retained his authority.

    Whether or not the GM did something "wrong" is entirely up to the social contract -- the mores of a specific group are idiosyncratic to that group -- and it sounds like that wasn't very developed in this situation.

    I did a very similar thing to a group many years ago. However, I think the importance here was that the players were aware there was something wrong.

    In short, the Kult game I was running was, in effect, the security system for a program they were trying to crack in a cyberpunk game of my own invention. Above and beyond the strangeness of a normal Kult game, there was an awareness that some was indeed not right with the situation: recurring experiences, identical people, 'blips' in reality (pre-Matrix days as well). There was an expectation that the game would 'change' at some point and the players were expecting this.

    I guess it all comes down to 'permission'. If the GM does not have the permission of the players to seriously alter the very game they are playing, then that is, in my mind, bad for the shared experience they are trying to create. In my case, I would argue that was: 1) expectation from the players that changes would take place and 2) permission from the players to allow these changes.

    It's not the kind of game I would run now, as it's way too much the "you woke up and it was all a dream" kind of thing. However, in the context of this discussion, I think the important difference is permission.

    I'll have to review some things, but I believe that in the strictest theorectical sense (at least according to Forge theory which the LP is apart of), mechanics don't constitute system per se, they represent authorities. In other words, when trying to decide what to do, or when there is disagreement, players can call upon the mechanics as an authority to boost their own credibility in the matter.

    So in other words, changing the game mechanics (changing around authorities) wouldn't *neccessarily* constitute changing the system, according to the Lumpley Principle. I know, I know---totally swapping games would probably shift the social dynamic enough to constitute a changed system, but in the strictest theorectical sense I'm not sure it does.
