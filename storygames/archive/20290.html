This is "The Warring Woods", my very first story-game. I've been working on this for over a year now and it's become a totally different creature from the pseudo-D&D that i started off with.

Now, it's simply a game about making stories together, without any human characters. Aimed mostly at (aspiring) writers and actors, but meant for everyone who loves a good tale. You start by pulling 1 card from the following decks: Clade (your species), Stature (your position in life/job) and Numen (your magical gift). This allows you to write a character out of your comfort zone, be it protagonist/antagonist/sidekick/... . Plus NPC's can also easily be build up from this template. Your history, gender, etc. are all chosen by you, but a sort of "native american name" will be given to you by the other players, based on your appearance, deeds or family. So this "moniker" can change throughout the story.

From there on out, it follows the "8 sequences" that make up most stories, whatever the genre may be. During each sequence, you each pull a card from the Ambition deck. This is your personal motivation during that sequence. This can span throughout the entire story or can simply be resolved in a couple of turns. As a group, you also pull one Objective card. This is the main "goal" during that sequence. A binding element that drives the story forward, if you will. Many of them can be interpreted in their own way, so you can give it your own twist.

I've left out a lot of details but so far, so good, right? Well, not really, i'm afraid that it lacks an element that drives the story forward. And well, i have no idea how to solve this. Maracanda, a friend of mine, has recommended me to avoid using a win/fail mechanic. I've also considered using a sort of democratic system to vote on ideas, but frankly, that's just a disaster waiting to happen. And trying to turn the Maslow Pyramid into a game mechanic hasn't seemed to work either.

So i'm turning this over to the experts. Any advice that can be given is more than welcome! And if you want to know more about "The Warring Woods", be sure to let me know.

    \n The design and presentation look awesome!

    Do you have anything more to show us? Pages, rules summaries... I'm especially looking forward to seeing more of the layout.

    In my opinion, win/fail mechanics don't pull the story onwards. On the opposite, 'failing' on something (or even 'winning' in a way) removes you from your creative role and ransoms the story. The challenge is to create procedures that create and reinforce the kind of story you want to be played. I think your decks work quite well to inspire the story and pull it forward.

    A mechanic for fairness is something else. This is usually what win/fail mechanics and gamemasters function as. I don't believe in heavy-handed systems much. In Microscope or The Extraordinary adventures of Baron Munchausen, for instance, narration mandate is hardly diluted, except for a very conditional veto. In a bunch of games, players need to work together to overcome a challenge, so there is less need for inter-player fairness.

    Psychological research points out that when players sit down at a table, there are already social expectations and roles between them, or they are quickly improvised and exerted. This unspoken 'social contract' is what your rules are up against. You can only challenge this insofar as the players want to roll with it. That's why only minimalist interferences can puncture this network of social performances. In Monsterhearts gender performance is pierced by tiny sub-rules and tone (for instance pc's can be turned on regardless from orientation and gender). In Alien\xf2r the powers of the narrator are centrifugally balanced by the performer of Alien\xf2rs character. I don't believe the performance of social roles out of the game is natural or beneficial. Imo, games should teach us to challenge them (and learn better ones).

    I also think you'd have had more luck with your thread of you had a different title... But... show us more please!

    \n So, I've mostly encountered two problems in my designs that seem a bit like what you're fishing for.

    1: What should I do next?
    One problem can be that when it's a player's turn to make something up, a simple "Do something interesting" is not enough. In my games, the answers to this question has been things like this:
    * Look at the Friction Meter. If the characters are fighting, threaten them with something. If they are not fighting, find something that can get them to fight.
    * Look at the relationship map. Find two characters who have a conflict of interests and find a way to escalate that conflict.
    * Look at the list of questions. Set a scene to either answer one of the questions, or to ask a new one.
    It seems the answer you have is "Look at the Sequence list. Set a scene where the things that should happen in this sequence happen". That's pretty good, but maybe it's not enough? Mechanically going through the Storytelling 101 sequence doesn't guarantee a good story. Maybe you need to make the players explore the characters more, for example? Maybe we need to make sure that there's some uncertainty, so that it's not obvious from the beginning what the threat is and how the characters will eventually overcome it?

    2: The Czege principle
    the Czege principle is basically "It's not fun to make up a problem and then solve it yourself". Maybe you need to make sure the players are giving each other creative challenges? "Here's a threat that looms over the characters, now you make up how they respond!". I've found it's very challenging to make a GMless game that has a group of characters facing a common threat. If there's no specific player playing the threat, the group will make up the threat and then make up how they face it, and that's not a lot of fun. For a game where characters are in conflict with each other, there's no need for a GM, but for a "party play" game, it's hard to go without. There are ways around it, though. One way is to make sure challenges are more individualized, facing one or two characters at a time. Another way is to have the players battle against the game itself, adding a layer of mechanical obstacles, but that's probably not what you're after here.

    Just some loose thoughts from my side. The game looks beautiful!

    \n If each player characters has his Clade, his Stature, his Numen, his Ambitions, while the whole group has one Objective dangling in a vacuum and nothing else, the design clearly tends towards very limited interactions between independent personal stories.

    To encourage involvement of other characters (and, indirectly, the creation of a single coherent story everybody contributes to), I would add to the character descriptions something the character can do or use for the benefit of other characters (it might be magic, skills, favors, habits, objects, anything that fits the setting) and make characters "succeed" in their story if they involve another character's traits in their machinations.

    For example (I hope I don't misunderstand the setting too much) the Mouse might need either the Bear's attitude for physical intimidation or the Eagle's hunting skills to reclaim his wife who has been kidnapped by the Snake. Convince either animal to help, and she's back (unless the Snake gets help...).
    It would be a better story, and better roleplaying, than the Mouse attempting a stealth rescue mission on his own and getting a random outcome.

    \nI'm planning a new playtest next week. My previous playtest did provide some interesting insights though. The process of creating characters proved to be very easy (even for two non-RPG players) and by sharing the background during the naming process, a sort of status quo was quickly established by the group. So by the time everybody got named we knew what kind of story we were going to have and how we each filled in certain roles.

    In this particular example we had an ancient yet strong tortoise who was a kindhearted diplomat, a corrupt bear councilor, a raccoon scolar looking for forbidden wisdoms and a crazy snake herbalist. From there we each pulled an ambition card and compared them. The tortoise had the disaster card, while the councilor pulled the miracle card. We quickly agreed that what might be bad for the good guy, might seem like an chance for the bad guy. So again this seemed to work in the games favour. It had turned into a Game of Thrones-esque situation with the Councilor and his protegee, the Scolar, having poisoned the King with a potion from the Herbalist. And then, after interpreting the objective card, the story begins with the Diplomat being called back to the courts after the murder.

    But that's where it grinds to a halt. As lorenzogatti points out, from here on out it's just every individual player spitballing ideas. They each have their own subplots going on in the form of the ambition cards. These are supposed to help drive the objective in that chapter (a single turn) or sequence (a couple of turns that are bound to narrative rules) forward. But it needs something more binding. Something that pulls it all together.

    But i think the following quotes from Simon_Pettersson and lorenzogatti might prove useful in solving that:\n

    If there's no specific player playing the threat, the group will make up the threat and then make up how they face it, and that's not a lot of fun. For a game where characters are in conflict with each other, there's no need for a GM, but for a "party play" game, it's hard to go without.
    \nand\n

    The Mouse might need either the Bear's attitude for physical intimidation or the Eagle's hunting skills to reclaim his wife who has been kidnapped by the Snake. Convince either animal to help, and she's back (unless the Snake gets help...).
    \nFrom what i've noticed, the players quickly pick their own side. During the last session there were 3 antagonists and 1 protagonist. So there is already a source of friction ensured. But even within the group of antagonists there were separate loyalties (for example, the Scolar was loyal to the Councilor). And that's something that might improve both teamwork and/or the creation of challenges.

    A possible solution thus might be to quietly pick an ally after you've heard everyone's story, the story wherein their (alleged) background and skills are revealed. Or you just pull a name randomly (preferably from a hat, of course!) You do not reveal this person but in need you can provide him/her with aid. However, you can just as easily be on the other side, only aiding in the effort to fool you later. So an antagonist might reveal to have been on the protagonists side all along or vica versa. Only you know what side you're really on.

    It might also be more interesting to see what happen when you don't reveal your personal ambition card to the other players. This way it is up to you to fulfill this task without being able to rely on the rest of the group. And then you can reveal it upon completion.

    How does that sound?
