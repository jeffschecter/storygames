\n I don't really like putting my damage on my character sheet. Whiteboards are cool for this, but we don't even have one, and sometimes the table is crowded and your health gets rubbed off by accident. I went through a bunch of options, and came up with one that's probably been done before, but I haven't found an example of. I made a doll from cheap fabric and drew my character on it, and then used different coloured pins to record the wounds. (Our system has 3 wound severities: minor, moderate, and serious, so one pin for each.) If the wound damages the limb or gets infected, I tie a black or green ribbon around the limb. When my character takes a hit, I hand the doll and the appropriate pin to the GM, and the GM sticks the pin where it hurts. If it's my own doing, I have to stick the pin in. It's a visual/tactile reminder of how pain hurts. The pins also help me remember where my injuries are so I roleplay them properly.

Here is a photo of a new one that hasn't been drawn on for someone else's character:

As you can see, I am not experienced at sewing. I found it really easy to do while watching TV, though, and the materials are very cheap from the local fabric shop. The last one I made, despite being of even poorer construction than the one pictured, survived the previous campaign without much damage. I imagine that as long as you're not using a HP BIGNUMBERS system, it's adaptable to other health/wound/harm systems.

So, yeah. What y'all think? Has anyone done something similar? Was it done better by someone else twenty years ago in something I've never heard of? Do you know of (or have you made) RPGs that use similar tactile game components instead of writing things down? I know of Dread, of course, and imagine that SG knows of many others.

    \n Adding items to it would be cool, but would require effort. We have a system of history-having magic items in our fantasy setting and it could be cool to make them in felt or something. Not that I know how to make anything in felt.

    The Voodoo thing is, I think, the response of nearly everyone when they first see it. My sister was like "oh, so you made a voodoo doll." There's some resonance with that in how it works. Pin goes in, your character gets hurt. Because the pins are longer than the doll is thick, I handle the doll of a more hurt character more carefully because I'm concerned about getting pricked.
