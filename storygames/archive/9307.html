\nI don't know how it came about but I've been using a really great technique in my current game in which I do something extremely unexpected - I have NPCs give in under threat of death and I have it stick. In other words, I have NPCs choose to be controlled rather than killed, and marvel openly at the stupidity (or passion?) of those that would go the other route.

It's really gone a long way towards helping players not dig in their characters' heels. "A1 steak sauce instead of Heinz?! NEVER! I'LL DIE FIRST!!!" They see what others are doing, and gaining by it, and they don't want to be left out of the action.

    \n I'd rephrase it as "Death Before Some Other Player Can Put Words Into My Character's Mouth That Make Me Squirm"

    Or, more shortly, "Death Before Line-Crossing;" and one line is losing control of what my character thinks and says. "Surrender or die" (how I read your NPC example) isn't mind control: the player still has agency. "[hidden die roll] OK, you're mind controlled; you surrender and fall in love with the mind flayer" fucks with people.

    I don't see the former (compulsion) as much of a problem... in the context of games which have the characteristics spelled out in that other thread (character embodies player's ethos and ideals; major investment to even begin play or due to continuing long-term play; being frozen out of play for inordinate periods of time; etc).

    I'll add another wiggle, from my own LARP rules design: not every player is wise or kind. Shit like seduction and mind control can make folks quit a LARP, if it is even slightly abused; and losing folks = not affording play space (even if the abuser is punished, the trust is gone).

    And ANOTHER wiggle, from LARP experiences in general: I am a "DO" LARPer, not a "STAT" LARPer. That's why I play boffer not MET or Cthulhu Live (though I dig the latter, for con games or other places where a contact LARP is impractical or dangerous). As DO LARPer, I think winning friends and influencing people is a real world skill which can and should be exercised, in play. JUST like firing an Airsoft or swinging a boffer (the former can make one a VERY good .22 marksman, and the latter is basically escrima). And, in my own system's design, if it can be done safely, affordably, and without inordinate ridiculousness... you DO it, dammit. So making a "mind control" ability is just... lame. (Note, I've always had a "fear"-like ability that can only make someone leave your presence, if you use it A LOT on them--more a monster ability than a viable character ability: you'll get wacked once it wears off, because folks don't dig being told to leave a cool scene.)

    That said... I succumbed to the lameness, during the last design discussion. I now have "Compel" and "Forget" Abilities which are VERY expensive, have the Bomb Icon (which means "GMs WATCH OUT!"), and is basically REALLY weak--last minutes, at best; and if "low powered" probably won't work at all. hey, what can I say? Fucking peer pressure.... (Though I'm not out of Gold Candidate Testing, yet; and if I see *one* abusive or uber use of them, they're GONE!)

    Sorry for the Friday afternoon ramble... I should just leave work.

Lie to me makes you a liar, fool me makes me a fool. NPC's being liars is just fine, PC being fool (except at Player's choice) not okay in this view.

    \n Yeah, what Krippler said. I'm really proud of Rustbelt's "Influence" system.

    It works like this: I state my intent to influence you, and describe how I do it (i.e. we still "roleplay out" the conflict). We roll dice and compare. Whoever is lowest has the choice to Give or to Push. Giving means taking your failure and liking it. Pushing means losing some resources, but winning anyway. Of course, since Influence is a diametric thing (only one person can win), the other guy can Push Back and create a Deadlock, in which we can go back and forth for several rounds, shedding resources as we go, until one of us Gives.

    In other words, it's all about resolve. Just like the real thing, if you ask me.

    \n I agree with the empowerment angle. In combat, you're inherently in a contested situation. If you're beaten, there's a consequence, but it doesn't seem as disempowering as losing agency--even if you're killed (paradoxically).

    Put another way, taking away or reducing a character's capabability to affect the fictional world seems to differ from taking away the character's decision-making capacity. I've had players with mind-controlled characters react very strongly, almost violently, against being controlled, to the point where the only way I would ever contemplate it in a game would be (a) the doppelganger gambit, with the "real" character spirited elsewhere, or (b) the bennie/reward approach for roleplaying the control.

    I'm talking about outright control of the Charm Person sort, mind you. I haven't had the same problems with social influence or (non-supernatural) seduction. Once there's a hint of paranormal sway in the mix, folks bridle.

    \n Thinking aloud here ...

    First; a wild shot at rephrasing the core content of this discussion; Player initiatives are the foundation of role-playing games. To accommodate player initiatives is good.

    The player may take all kinds of initiatives in a role-playing game (not all games allows for all initiatives, but still). How the game supports any given initiative varies, of course, but the general rule is that any initiative may create a situation, which in turn may lead to a consequence (change something; a relation, a course of action, the atmosphere, a material object).

    If there is conflict in the situation, the consequence may be something the character don't enjoy. This is mostly ok. There is nothing principally wrong with the character being challenged.

    A consequence may be something the character enjoy, which at the same time makes the player squirm. This may, or may not, be ok. There is nothing wrong with the player being challenged through his character (walk in strange shoes, etc.), but most players will be put off with an overload of squirm.
    ---> A little squirm will make the player feel challenged; he will face his own shortcomings/neuroses/feelings/preconceptions through the condition of the character, or he will feel his character being threatened, but will be able to play on and explore the condition, being loyal to the demands of the interaction and/or fiction. This is an interesting position to be in, as a player, and may open up for exploration with mind-changing qualities.
    ---> An overload of squirm will make the player feel bad, and detract from his game-joy. What makes an "overload" varies. How the game-motor supports the players in various situations, have a lot to say in this. Lack of support often leaves the players with little or no options for initiatives, and that puts the game in danger of "squirm-overload". So a broad-themed game need to have general ways of dealing with situations that may leave the players with their characters in unwanted conditions, to help them see acceptable venues of interplay.

    "Interplay" may be any of these three;
    - playing with his own character-concept
    - playing with the situation
    - playing with relations to other characters

    This leads me to a rephrasing of the first paragraph in my post: Player interplay are the foundation of role-playing games. A game needs to create venues of interplay for the players at all times.

    I hope this has spurred some thoughts in you. Feel free to pluck the feathers of this goose/eagle/penguin. ;-)

    \n Hey, getting back to my OP, here's an actual play example of what I've done.

    The game is nWoD, the players are part of a Southern Arizona/Northern Mexico crime family of immigrant smugglers.

    A branch of the family wants to get back into dealing and transporting drugs. The PCs, for various reasons, oppose this. They fear a bloody civil war that the pro-drugs side of the family is likely to win because they have access to a LOT more money. So they are going around trying to cut off support for that side of the family, open up new "business opportunities", some legit, some shady to keep everyone busy, and isolate the leader of that branch of the family so that if a war happens it will be a one-bullet war.

    The other branch of the family is doing the same.

    They are trying to subvert a shady owner of several crappy motels, since he can provide work for immigrants as well as help with temporary safe houses. A rival syndicate threatened him, extorted him and he backed down. I then depicted him as being happier and more relaxed. He doesn't have to fight. He isn't happy about paying the money but it beats fighting all the time and constantly worrying. (The PCs stepped in and did the fighting for him without him asking, which he was not super-happy about but which he also accepted.)

    The lesson here is that in the world I've established, threats work, and they stick. The guy wasn't plotting against the people that controlled him, he was cool with it.

    That gives the PCs this strategy to use on others in the game world and gives them a strong demonstration of it.

    I've done the same with bribery and blackmail, too. People stay intimidated or bought. People who resist to the point of being put in danger of their lives are mocked and reviled (sometimes posthumously). "What a fucking idiot, they COULD have just given in and made a pile of money."

    \nWell, after demonstrating what works by having NPCs target each other, and giving PCs the chance to target NPCs, I then put PCs in situations where they can give in, be intimidated, be controlled by bribery or threats, and I find the players are MUCH more likely to voluntarily cave, because they see that in this game world, you're not rewarded for digging in your heels and screaming "I'll die first!", indeed, you're punished for doing that.

    Which makes the times when they (or NPCs) really DO want to dig in their heels that much more significant.

    Example:

    A PC was trying to convince a NPC to help him destroy this corrupt branch of the family, and she was resisting, he thought it was because he hadn't convinced her properly, so he marshalled more arguments, insisted on reasonable things, laid out the advantages and disadvantages, but the NPC wasn't resisting because she thought it was a bad idea - she was resisting because she lost a husband the last time the family had a war, and it broke her heart forever. I finally had her flip her lid and scream "I AM NOT GOING TO DO THIS, I AM NOT GOING TO BE THE ONE THAT STARTS THIS SHIT AGAIN." and it was really powerful because the players knew that this made her vulnerable, that they could use her intransigence to destroy her. And it was also very, very sad.
