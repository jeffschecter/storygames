\n I've been reading "Killing Monsters: Why Children Need Fantasy, Super Heroes and Make-believe Violence", an excellent book about the misinterpreted data regarding violent media and play. It keeps bringing me back, in a sort of roundabout way, to my opinion that RPGs are Imaginative Play of a sort largely or completely denied to adults, or even children older than, say, ten years of age.

One interesting section of the book discusses a child psychologist who, in the wake of the Trade Center attacks, experienced a high incidence of violent play among the children she works with. Rather than being concerned about this, we should encourage it, the book opines - these violent fantasy games and such are a method by which children attempt to make sense of senseless violence and killing in the world. They also serve to release stored-up tension, anxiety and aggression, and without such a release valve these children might be more prone to serious violence, etc.

I don't doubt that our play (mine, yours, and everyone here) has at one time or another been of use to us in this way. (Bad day at the office? Beat on some Nazis, kill some Orcs!) But I wonder, with focussed design like we work with now, if the therapeutic applications of our play won't become more apparent? Where do you see gaming at in ten years, with regards to personal development, therapy, and imaginative play? In what ways does gaming serve the aforementioned needs in your game life?

    \n How can I explain how I feel about this?

    I tend to believe that, like traditional RPGs have given rise to Story Games, so Story Games will be changed enough in the future to be considered something else. I don't mean to suggest that one is superior to another - I'll be the first to say that I believe all these games (Trad, Story, and what comes after...) have important things to teach us. Perhaps like Story Games seem better, to me, at addressing theme, so future games will address it more fully, or address something that Story Games have missed? I don't know, it's hard for me to be eloquent when attempting to define something not-fully-formed in my mind.

    Maybe if we take GNS as a basis, Trad games primarily addressed Gamist play, Story Games Narrativist play, and these other ones Sim? Or would that be LARP? I'm seriously just spitballing, because clearly I made a statement that baffled someone, and I wanted to elaborate upon my thoughts.

    And, yes, I know about therapeutic play. But it's not something adults are as often encouraged to engage in as children. I was trying to say that I think there's an imbalance between the Play we allow children and the Play we allow adults, and that causes an issue or block of sorts in many adults - something that might be better addressed rather than ignored.

    It's late, and I'm possibly ranting now. Sleep time.

    \n That book is amazing stuff. As I father, I read it and went "damn straight!" It flies in the face of all of today's aggressive prevention of violence in children's play. You know, like you're not allowed to play cops and robbers on the schoolyard and such. The book is mostly about boys and their need for this kind of aggressive play to help them define what growing up to be a man is all about. However, it does touch on girls and their need for play, both of the same variety, but often addressing different emotional and developmental issues.

    I know none of that addresses the topic directly, but I have often thought that he should have written a sequel entitled Why Adults Need Fantasy, Super Heroes and Make-believe Violence.

    \n I had some very interesting experiences when I roleplayed A Faeries Tale with my girls for the first time. They understood the roleplaying side of things easily and the dramatic editing side as well. However they were very reluctant to get involved in any violence. My girls are big for their age, head and shoulders above most of their classmates and they have had 'thou shalt not smack anyone' drummed into them. Even when they got it into their heads that they were allowed to enact violence on the nasty goblins, they still tended towards the clever, non-violent solutions. They quite liked bopping people on the nose and Emma (the younger one) carried an axe with great glee, but never actually used it! The games as played created a strict sense of right and wrong in them and brought out quite a number of issues for the girls which we talked about afterwards. It was very illuminating. The APs are here:
    \n
    \nhttp://neilgow.blogspot.com/search/label/Faerys Tale
    \n
    \nThey've now been absorbed by their Nintendo DS' and I'm seeing some similar patterns emerging. They don't play against each other, they work together. Lara (the elder) is better at some tasks than Emma. They swap their handsets so that they can overcome different parts together. They got a Submarine in some Sonic the Hedgehog variant last night and the joint celebrations were tantamount to my WoW guilds whooping and hollering when we downed Ragnaros for the first time.
    \n
    \nIt vexes me that some people can bemoan these styles of play as inappropriate when they are really speaking from an ill-informed point of view. Play keeps me sane in an increasingly insane world and gives vent to a creative streak that is still running strong in me. I see no reason why children cannot enjoy this as well. I can feel myself revving up for a long and quite snarly 'hoodies without sports fields' rant so I'll stop to save the boards karma..
    \n
    \nPlay is good.

    \n I agree with JD, that role-playing in psychology is already in place, and does what it's intended to do, and that psychologists will continue to do the most for psychology in the future.
    \n
    \nBut I think that psychology sorta misses the soul while focusing on the mind. Put in more rationalistic terms, psychology remains dead neutral on issues of "what we ought to do" or "what we ought to be," letting the patient supply those on their own. And, as such, its our myths that fill this role of putting meaning in our lives. If those are lacking, I think that psychology doesn't help much.
    \n
    \nI think that RPGs already do serve a role as a way to have a discourse on mythic matters of meaning. But I also think that in the future they'll serve a somewhat important role in getting to a place where meaning is more part a meaningful part of certain parts of public discourse. To put it fliply, when everyone in the world is playing World of Warcraft one day, the world is going to disintigrate fast if there isn't some method for inserting more meaning than basic survival and power.
    \n
    \nTTRPGs development could be seen as the way to which we give individials a framework for personally empowered thematic statement. Sure, a director of a movie has that power now, but that's a small percentage of people. RPGs could be the seed of the democritization of spiritual expression.
    \n
    \nJust a thought.
