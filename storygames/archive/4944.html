\n Hi, I thought I might spin this thread off of the angry thread, because I think there's an interesting concept that deserves further discussion. There was considerable discussion of bad games diluting the market, and Ben Lehman gave an example from his own experience of being turned off to new RPG's by the experience of buying an exciting new game and discovering it to be broken. (I'll quote him on this thread, if he gives me permission).

I have had a similar experience: a few years ago, I was on a quixotic mission to collect all the crime-themed games and supplements that were easily available. I found some neglected jewels (i.e. the Ghost Dog RPG). I also discovered that having the "D20" logo on a product was highly correlated with that product being crap. It's wasn't a perfect correlation, but high enough that I stopped buying D20 stuff unless it had very strong reviews.

    \n Disclaimer: I'm no economist. I'm trying to use these terms correctly, but please feel free to chime in if I'm getting something seriously wrong. Also, this stuff will probably be blindingly obvious to some, though it wasn't to me.

    This seems to me to be a case of the Market for Lemons phenomenon. There's asymmetrical information in the market for games. In particular, as in Ben's story, a game can have an interesting theme, setting, and attractive layout and graphics, but have lousy or actually broken rules.

    It's hard to judge these things without a playtest, which usually is not possible without buying the game. Even if you are able to return the game after figuring out it's a "lemon", you've still lost out because nobody will repay you for the time and energy you spent figuring out that game sucked, the opportunity cost of not playing a game you would have liked, and your lot credibility among your gaming group for "making them play that game that sucked." Given all that, it's pretty rational to be hesitant to try a new game without some added assurances.

    I think this has some implications for both consumers and producers. It's noteworthy that some of the marketing techniques that are often recommended, such as playing demos at cons and stores, being a helpful presence on the 'Net, getting some good Actual Play reports out there -- all work strongly to address the information asymmetry.

    This may also help explain why the indie game scene seems somewhat personality-driven -- beyond whatever cults of personality exist, people are using the famous name as a marker for quality. It also implies that for the indie game designer, quality control is of paramount importance. (See this for an analogous concept in manufacturing.)

    \n Thanks, Ben. Here's the story, and I think it shows that this is something for game producers to worry about.\n
    Posted By: Ben Lehman
    Let's see. It's Christmas of 2000. I'm an avid RPG player (I mean it, seriously, AVID) but I haven't bought a game book since ... two three years I think? Most of my games have come from used book stores, anyway. I'm not in touch with the market. I don't go to cons, I don't read about games online, I'm not in any way tied to the greater community.

    A friend of mine buys me a new game for Christmas. (Game will not be mentioned publically). I'm like "holy shit, NEW role-playing game?" I barely knew that they still made those. The color is really cool, too. It's super-exciting. I get very jazzed up about it and decide I'm going to run a one-shot on New Year's Eve. I pre-generate the characters to my player's specifications, I make a whole little scenario that showcases the cool setting without needing the players to know the world background, I do the whole shebang.

    New year's eve: We're playing, we're having fun, everything is great. Then ... we use the system to resolve something. Eh. It's flat and unexciting. Okay, whatever, we roll over it and keep playing and it's a good game. Then we come to a place where we need to use the extended conflict system ... and ... it doesn't work. Not as in "it doesn't give good results" it doesn'twork. I did a back of the envelope calculation and figured out that it would have taken nearly 1000 repetitions of the resolution for it to have a decent chance of resolving. It's clear, abundantly clear, that this was never playtested. Not "never blind playtested." Not "never played in the context of a real game." But simply "the guy who wrote it never even sat down to try it out, and that was abundantly clear as soon as we did."

    We ended up handwaving it ... but the rest of the game was just ... flat. We spent the whole time avoiding conflict and danger because we didn't want to have to think about using that system anymore. Yuck.

    The whole experience soured me on new games. Why should I pay money for someone else's unplaytested crap? I mean, seriously, they clearly didn't even have the simplest concepts of game design, whereas I had a pretty together head about most of these things. I didn't buy any new games until Riddle of Steel in ... late 2002?

    If the game had been good, fun, exciting, I imagine I would have been buying a lot more games. It soured me on theconceptof new role-playing games, and it wasn't until much later that I decided to give it a shot again.

    So there's something to that. The market can get diluted and flooded and even deflated with bad games.

    Which is totally different from "games that you bet your life savings on." The game in question was produced in large quantities and sold through distribution. I would put Polaris up against that game in terms of making customers excited any day of the week.

    \n First something of a rant, and I'm no economist either:

    Games are not like cars or other manufactured product. They're much closer to movie DVDs or music CDs. Or video games might be the best analogy.

    If a game is broken, all of the copies are broken. So, one person can get hold of a copy and report the status and it's true for all copies. There is no assymetrical information here at all.

    Unlike a Lemon Market, you do have the ability to assess the sale before it's made. You can talk to other people who've purchased it, or look up reviews, or arrange to get in on a game at a con or with a friend who owns it. Sellers with a high quality product do have a reliable way to get the information out there. They can hand out review copies, they organize demos. It's not like a used car where you can't tell what's a lemon or isn't, even when you're looking at two identical cars of the same model and year. Even Consumer Reports might report that the model is the best of the year. You still might end up with a car with a cracked axle or leaking oil pan.

    And no amount of information will prevent someone who, line in Ben's case ~7 years ago, has absolutely no connection to the flow of information (information being reviews, playtests or demos at cons, discussion on the internet, whatever). There's just no cure for that except to tell the consumer that it's their job to be informed if they want a satisfactory experience (and it's pretty rude to tell someone that who just gave you it as a gift anyway).

    But you mention that it's not always possible to get a playtest, and I'll add sometimes games don't have any reviews or any discussion. Just because you can't get reliable information on one product, doesn't mean you can't get reliable information on products in general. If you really want information, you could always try to make yourself a part of the flow by asking for a review copy.

    Market dilution doesn't hinge on quality. And let me reiterate what I was concluding above: it does not effect the buyer in significantly negative way when they have access to fair information - which we do.

    You could flood the market with nothing but high quality games, and the sellers would still get hurt.*

    But buyers still have access to the same information. Whether the market is flooded with good games or bad games, if the consumer doesn't purchase until they satisfactory information, the only difference is how long it takes them to find something to purchase. Aside from time, the other threat to the consumer is it could cause a market crash. If the market is too flooded so that no one can make enough money to stay in it, then suddenly you'll have no one left in business (we actually saw this to a degree with the D20 boom and the first generation game consoles). But then an equilibrium is reached. It might cause those with the most knowledge and experience to "get real jobs" somewhere else, so whether or not its a bad thing depends on how it recovers. Market crashes are a healthy part of the cycle. And in such an event, the downside is mostly on sellers than the buyers. The buyers just get inconvienienced.

    I said video games would be a good analogy. All the above can be read considering video games or RPGs.

    *Unless the market grows - but if the market grows, you don't have market dilution (or at least, not as much, depending on the math). Watchers of MMO's have been worrying about market dilution, but every game that has been released so far has increased the total size of the market overall.

    \n That said, I do see a potential big problem of market dilution. And one big one that's it's effectively free to publish a PDF or a Print on Demand product. When you have to print up a product into a hard copy form, that costs money for each copy. Potential sellers are going to be wary because that's up front cost that they're going to want to see back. Even if they don't care about a profit, there's only so much money they're going to spend on the endevour.

    But when that bar is lowered, or even free, a seller can keep pushing out product without concern (or less concern) for the upfront costs. And if they aren't as concerned about the upfront costs (or have none outside of time), they don't have to be as concerned about recouping those costs, so there's not as much need to ensure the product is something that anyone would want to buy or enjoy.

    Although this does strain the information path I mentioned, it doesn't make it assymetrical, it just makes it harder to get information about all the products, so it doesn't really increase your choices.

    But if you're a seller, and are in it to make money, then it becomes difficult to break in because you have to differentiate yourself from the pack. The larger the pack is, whether or not the quality is there, the harder it is to stand out. Basically what King Turnip said in his second paragraph, except whether or not the competition is crap, it's still a dilution.

    \n A couple of things, related to this topic, that I think are important to note:\n
    Posted By: Alvin FrewerAnd one big one that's it's effectively free to publish a PDF or a Print on Demand product.
    \nNot really true. The only cost that is removed is the cost of printing, which, admittedly, is usually the largest chunk of the budget -- but you still have to pay writers, artists, editors, pay for delivery (if you're using a commercial service, like Lulu or RPGNow), etc. (And that's not getting into the non-inventorial costs, like marketing, advertising, etc.) It's not free, or even "effectively free" -- it's just cheaper. \n
    Posted By: Alvin FrewerBut when that bar is lowered, or even free, a seller can keep pushing out product without concern (or less concern) for the upfront costs.
    \nThis is also a huge factor when dealing with folks who are publishing as a hobby -- all they really care about is getting the game out there, and they're often willing to take a loss to do it. They're not concerned about selling enough to maintain full operations, they're just wanting to make enough to eventually (and on no set schedule, so that "eventually" is effectively a perpetual state) crank out another product. They'll underprice, they'll stay in business long past the point of solvency, etc. Competing with someone who doesn't really care about running a business makes it damn hard for actual businesses. (Not too affectionately called the "pissing in the pool" syndrome. )
