    \n If we're looking at price alone and I've got a gaming budget, I'm looking at combinations that will get me to that $35 mark or slightly over that mark. That $22 game can be paired with a ton of $14, $15, and $16 games that will get me slightly over the mark, which I'm comfortable with.

    At $25, I'm looking at the $10 books and there isn't as much there I can impulse buy.

    Looking under Books at IPR, I see five games at the $10 price point (two of which I'd consider purchasing on impulse) and eight at the $11 to $12.95 price point (only one that I'd purchase on impulse). For your $22 game, I'm looking at one at $13.95, three games at $14, seventeen at $15, and six at $16. There's about nine books in that group I might impulse buy. (There's nothing at $13 for this skinflint.)

    So initially, I'd be happier with your $25 price point because I know that the majority of the games are at even dollar price points and that there are a lot of games at $15 and $20 (twenty-nine offerings at twenty bucks) I can combine to get my order to $35. Upon reflection, I see that there's fewer options at the $10 mark to balance a $25 book.

    So here's my order at both price points:

    Grey Ranks alone, USPS priority shipping: $27.80
    Grey Ranks plus Cat, free shipping: $37.00

    Grey Ranks ($25) alone, USPS priority shipping: $30.80
    Grey Ranks ($25) plus The Secret Lives of Gingerbread Men, free shipping: $37.00
    Grey Ranks ($25) plus Cat, free shipping: $40.00

    So with this, I actually see a larger pool of impulse purchases at the $22 price point than I do at the $25 price. Psychologically, I see with my $22 + impulse purchase, a total in the thirty dollar range; $25 + impulse is in the forties.

    Having taken the time to review IPR's offerings, if a game is below $20, I'd be more likely to order it if the price is a multiple of $5. Once we hit games over $20, it's harder to pair up games at IPR to get that magic cheap bastard number I'm looking for. I'm neither more nor less likely to purchase Grey Ranks at $22 than I would at $25.

    As a customer that wants to save as much as he can, I'd be happier if Grey Ranks was $22 than $25, but the difference isn't that much. As the price point and matching impulse buy products to get free shipping doesn't bother me as much, if I were Jason Morningstar, I'd strongly consider selling my game for $25.

    Now if I were Joe McDonald with Perfect at $18, I'd be more likely to buy that game at $20 because there is only one thing at $17 I can pair that with or several things at $20. And I'd much rather buy something on impulse if it's less than the thing I went to IPR to purchase. If I were Joe, I'd raise my price to $20 or drop it to $15 so it would be picked up on impulse by shoppers that went in to purchase a $20+ book. But because there are so many things at the $15 price point, there's more chance of someone picking up Play Dirty than Perfect to round out the shipping deal; I'd go with $20.

    This is also why I believe Clinton's The Prince's Kingdom would sell more if it cost one cent more.

    While I was looking up prices and products at IPR, I came across two examples of pricing combinations there, one good and one bad for the gamer on a budget.

    Cold City is $23 and Cold City Companion is $12, which is a perfect pricing match for someone trying to get free shipping.

    Conspiracy of Shadows is $20 and the CoS Companion is $14, which means I'm going to have to eat the shipping costs or scrounge for a low priced game that I don't have. If I'm on a budget, I'm less likely to pick up the CoS games because the Companion isn't $15. CoS winds up costing me about $6 more than it would if it was priced one dollar higher.
