    \n If "incoherent" means "mixing 'creative agendas' and not neatly focusing on any single 'creative agenda'" then the simple reason why "incoherent" games do so well might be that by the very fact of being "incoherent" they permit players and groups to focus on different agendas to suit themselves. I leave aside for the moment the obvious point that G, N and S do not exhaust the possible agendas of play (and immersion in particular has been described as an experiential agenda rather than a creative agenda).

    I do not know why the writer conflates this so-called "incoherence" with a game being GM-led, even if many GM-led games are "incoherent" and vice-versa. There is no necessary connection between the two. The article is riddled with ideologically grounded assumptions and seems to miss the point about how GM-led, traditional games can, when done well, offer an experience that many (often passionate and/or cerebral) roleplayers prefer; sneering expressions such as "Mother May I" in relation to online, moderated narrative roleplay do not assist the writer's cause, especially since, from what I understand, those games generally have a very focused agenda (the writing of satisfying prose).

    The writer is probably right to observe that a game that depends on one energetic and knowledgeable participant (who can shepherd the others if need be) has, in a way, a more surmountable buy-in requirement than a game that depends on EVERYONE being energetic and knowledgeable. Another feature of traditional, campaign-style games is that people can get to know characters and follow what are in a loose sense those characters' stories over time, plus with a campaign format not only are you hooked on the ongoing unfolding of events and eager to find out what happens next, but you also turn up each week ready to dive straight into play without having to relearn rules or setting details, as you are already familiar with them. So this style of play maximises playing time versus set-up time, in the medium-to-long term, meaning that you get a lot more bang for your buck in terms of playing hours enjoyed per \xa3 spent on the rules or per hour spent setting up the game (including negotiating what to play, learning the rules etc.). Whilst the rules can be criticised for their complexity, this complexity may be necessary... or at least helpful... in sustaining a varied campaign over the medium-to-long term, and in making it easy to differentiate between different characters and situations mechanically, so that they are not only described as different, but feel different to the more analytical players.

    To my mind D&D 5e can credibly be seen as a backpedalling from the (commercially speaking) excessively narrow, "coherent" focus of D&D 4e, and that suggests to me that whilst the fight for "coherence" managed to attract the attention of the designers of THE big RPG, they have now understood that it is not commercially optimal.

    Many of the people on this site will have a natural preference for "coherent" and "focused" games with a rapid build-up and efficient, narrowly focused resolution mechanisms, and for games that offer a compact exploration of themes with equally shared narrative contribution/participation, and that's absolutely grand. But if you think that a preference for that sort of experience is somehow more sophisticated or more mature or more rational than a preference for having a traditional GM or for campaign play with a slow build-up or for meandering rules choc-full of subsystems, you are absolutely kidding yourselves. What's more, that preference is not driven by some narcissistic need for "empowerment" (OK, I can't rule out the existence of the occasional narcissitic nutter GM, but broadly speaking):- there are all sorts of perfectly legitimate reasons for preferring a traditional game (even among people who are more than capable of playing and enjoying a more focused game in a way that contributes to satisfying play for all). This latter sort of game can also lead to a more thoughtful experience of play than the gonzo experience that "tighter" games with shared narrative authority sometimes (sometimes!) produce.

    Designers have already solved the puzzle of how to give roleplay maximum breadth of appeal, and its name is (regrettably) World of Warcraft. World of Warcraft as I understand it (never played it) has both roleplay servers and questing servers to choose from. Like a broad-church RPG, it can cater to a variety of tastes. The commercially speaking most successful "indie" or supposedly "coherent" RPGs (PbtA, Fate, BW) all have GMs and lend themselves to campaign play (possible exception that proves the rule: Fiasco?), and my one experience of a PbtA game was that it felt very much like a trad game and not much like the "indie" games I had also played. Neither that game nor Mouse Guard seemed to me to offer any impressive advantages over trad games. I did very much enjoy a Star Wars hack of Duty and Honour, which maybe did offer something trad games don't (though I still prefer the very best experiences of trad play over my experience of that game), but, ya know, it had Jedi. ANYTHING can be good with Jedi. Unless it also has mentally defective animal-people with racist caricature accents.

    You may be 100% right that for you personally there are few subjective attractions in going back to trad gaming now that you have tried "indie" gaming. But people's most fundamental preferences differ. And by no means all the people whose fundamental preference is for trad games have that preference because they want endless combat, or a massively detailed mechanical simulation of the game-world. Many people who play RPGs to engage in some way with the fiction, especially in character portrayal, character exploration and immersion, have a very thoughtful preference for trad games over "indie" games.

    \n I think that a lot of people (myself included) are coming from a background of playing lots and lots of "trad" games in a very specific context:

    1) There are a number of people playing the games because they have always played them, and don't know what else is out there.

    2) Some of them are just happy to hang out or play whatever. The rest are more active in making the games work and searching for better play. They are constantly dissatisfied, because they're not getting exactly what they want.

    3) At some point, different/more modern/progressive games are discovered. Whether individually or collectively, these games start providing tools to fix that dissatisfaction and improve the gaming.

    Sometimes the quieter, non-so-committed players are lost in the switch (they preferred being able to play more "passively", in a trad style, and the indie games are too demanding for them), and only the highly motivated, vocal parties remain. This doesn't seem as a huge loss, perhaps, or even goes unnoticed (maybe those people just got bored of gaming, in general?).

    In any case, after this experience, it seems like a no-brainer that the necessary step has been taken. Gaming has improved; why go back?

    I find it interesting that, even though I know there are lots of people out there who *consciously* prefer trad gaming (in a deliberate way; not just from a limited mindset) because I encounter them on the internet, I don't think I've ever met those people in real life. (Or, when I have, their games were a mess - in that case, it's hard to tell what's really going on.) The one exception is the OSR crowd, who have their techniques and approaches which serve their gaming agenda.

    I can see how someone coming from that perspective can't really imagine why the apparent majority of the gaming world is doing something they don't enjoy. Don't those people get it? They could go through the same transformation that I went through and have much more fun!

    Now, from online discussions, we know that's not the case. We have people (as in this thread) who are very consciously choosing trad gaming styles, presumably because they enjoy that kind of gaming more.

    But, somehow, there are a number of people who have gone through the experience I've described above. If you can imagine yourself going through that trajectory I've just described, hopefully you can see where the "rabid indie game zealot" attitude gets its wheels. I think that, to someone like that, it takes some serious shocking discussions and encounters to actually be able to grasp the reality of people *who don't feel the same way*.

    Ultimately, we all have to broaden our horizons a little bit and remember that everyone has different priorities and finds pleasure in different activities.

But this is good game design only if it was the trad designer's original conscious intention and not just the way trad game design has been previously done.

    \n There are two approaches, by and large, to innovation and improvement. The first is the incremental approach, staying true to the tried and tested, but slowly and steadily improving by trial and error. The second is to step back and look at things from a theoretical perspective, taking a broad, abstract point of view and trying to formulate a theory. The second approach (as per the Forge) can result in very sweeping, very fast changes, whereas the first approach tends to result in limited but safer progress. The thing to recognise is that the second approach is not only on the whole faster (an advantage) but also on the whole riskier (a disadvantage). Since the projectile is released with such force, a small error in the positioning of the sights can result in a grotesquely erroneous trajectory. There is the danger of slinging the baby out with the bathwater. It is all well and good to develop overarching theories of how RPGs and storygames work, but human nature being complex and human intellect fallible, these theories are almost inevitably going to be imperfect in some way, so taking them too seriously (rather than treating them as a potential rule-of-thumb needing to be checked against the experience on the ground) can result in going way off course. The ultimate example of this is possibly the way that "The Big Model" fails to understand or account for the attractions of any of "simulationism", immersion or "incoherent" gaming adequately.

    But even if by and large one would expect to find trad games developing more along the lines of the first approach and "indie" games developing more along the lines of the second approach, that is itself an overgeneralisation. Many trad games have introduced quite remarkable changes in the way the hobby works, and have involved re-imagining the hobby (dare I say re-imagineering it?) from the ground up - Amber Diceless being a case in point, or the questionable metaplot/railroading habit apparently ascribed to White Wolf games potentially being another (though that never really seemed to intrude upon my long experience of heartily and immersively enjoying V:tR MET-LARP). Likewise, many indie games have stuck to what is now a tried and tested pattern, the development of PbtA games from AW being the most notable example of this. Also, Sorcerer itself, by reputation (I have not played it), includes many elements borrowed from trad RPGs (so there was an element of the first approach there, i.e. trial and error).

    I like theorising. I like attempts to impose abstract structure on human reality. But much in the same way that religious believers should always check the ultimate logical consequences of their belief system against the basic impulses of the human spirit (otherwise they risk becoming extremists and forgetting the basics of what it means to be human), in the same way you always have to take any form of theorising about RPGs with a pinch of salt.

    \n Some more notes on this:

    * For some context, at the Forge the terms "coherent" and "incoherent" were technical terms.       \n I would agree with the idea that drawing a line is somewhat arbitrary. I do also happen to subscribe to a school of rpg theory that is capable of drawing the line very clearly, so I'll have to stand on that for myself, even as others are free to view the matter differently

      For instance, a LARP that just happens to have an audience is still a LARP because it is not being played for that audience, but for the sake of the participant experience. I think that this is theoretically entirely straightforward, even if ascertaining the matter one way or another may be difficult in practice (e.g. because the players might lie or be mistaken when self-reporting). Usually it is not: when my re-enactor friends are e.g. hired to perform gladiator combat at some local museum, it's pretty clear that it's not a roleplaying game of any sort, but rather a theatrical event. Likewise, when a tv crew comes to one of their larps to get some material for a newscast or whatever, the players do not generally cease playing and start performing for the camera.

      Similarly, a recording of a played game does not make that game into theater until the point where the recording becomes the purpose of play. Our S/lay w/ Me blog game is actually a pretty good example of hewing close to the line: if we had more readers, it's not that difficult to see how the project might cease to be a rpg and become a serial novel instead; all it would take would be for us to start viewing the rules and interaction of the game as serving the purpose of the audience, rather than serving the purpose of entertaining us, the players.

      There are other activities that can be defined against theater in similar ways. Religion, for example, could be viewed in this light: a religious rite is itself until the point where it starts to exist as a performance for an audience. This distinction seems pretty handy for determining whether e.g. the martial practices of a Shaolin temple bogged with tourists are religious or theatrical in nature.

      Or consider sex - pornography or merely sexual self-expression? Depends on whether you're doing it for an audience, would be my argument.

      To me all this seems pretty clear conceptually. I do agree that the material context of theater and roleplaying (or the written word and roleplaying!) can be practically identical, which may make the line between the two unimportant; it only matters for the design of the event, insofar as the creative agenda of whatever you are doing matters. Sometimes a LARP played well may be entirely serviceable as a theater-piece, and sometimes a theater production may be creatively just as liberating and satisfying and fun for the players as a roleplaying game would have been. The secondary outcome is accidental, but it is possible.

      \nIn line with posts I have recently posted on this site, I am perfectly happy with freeform tabletop RP. The reason why I personally prefer to have mechanics for combat is hard to pin down. There are probably various confluent reasons. I would hazard a guess that the main reason is that I find it easier to believe in the process and outcome of a fight (or other conflict or struggle), whether as player or as GM, if I know that the ultimate outcome is strongly influenced by factors other than immediate fiat, so that I am not second-guessing the GM (or the players).

      But whatever the reasons are for preferring to have mechanics, they are not strong enough to outweigh my objection to intrusive social mechanics, which is that they stifle and destroy what is for me (subjectively) the great joy of tabletop RPGs:- natural, flowing dialogue as the medium of roleplay.

      THE reason why it is acceptable to use mechanics for combat but not social interaction is that it is NOT possible in a tabletop RPG to use real-world combat to represent fictional combat, but it IS possible to use real-world dialogue to represent fictional dialogue. What's more, even with LARP, the approximation to real-world combat used with foam swords is very much an obvious approximation, whereas the real-world dialogue used to represent fictional dialogue in RP in general can be almost indistinguishable from the fictional dialogue itself. The closer the real-world process (dialogue, combat or whatever) resembles the fictional process, the easier it is to suspend your disbelief in the proposition that you are doing what your character is doing and that by extension you are your character. That is where the immersion comes from that seems to mystify so many people.

      In the same way, and even more importantly in a way, leading on to my next point, we can use real-world thought processes to represent the fictional thought-processes of our characters. The closer our real-world thoughts resemble the fictional thoughts of our characters, the easier it is to suspend our disbelief that we are doing what our characters are doing, and by extension that we are our characters. This explains why intrusive personality mechanics are rejected for reasons of immersion.

      In other words, combat mechanics are OK because it's impossible to get an optimal contribution to immersion from the representation of combat at the table in any event. Dialogue and thought-processes, in contrast, can be represented in a way that contributes to immersion, to self-identity with the character, and since the contribution they make is invaluable, from the immersionist perspective, there is no sense in jettisoning them.

      In other words you are looking at this conundrum entirely the wrong way round. You ask why is it not acceptable to freeform combat? The answer is that it IS acceptable to freeform combat, but there is no urgent need to, because mechanicalising combat does not kill an immersion-contributor in the way that mechanicalising dialogue or thought-processes does.\n

      Having said that, there's certainly nothing wrong with having a preference. I've yet to see an argument for such preferences being universally "correct", however. (Though I would welcome some!)

      You ask why is it not acceptable to freeform combat? The answer is that it IS acceptable to freeform combat, but there is no urgent need to, because mechanicalising combat does not kill an immersion-contributor in the way that mechanicalising dialogue or thought-processes does.
      \nThis part is pretty personal. I've met gamers with real combat experience who find die-rolling and action-choosing in round-by-round combat to be utterly ludicrous, and gamers who work in, say, high-stakes negotiation and find procedural dialogue or "thought-process" mechanics fun and immersive.

      I think familiarity and the willingness to suspend disbelief is at the center of it. Someone who claims to seek "immersive play" but enjoys D&D combat has clearly made the leap to accept its limitations and to enjoy it anyway. That same person, I think, could choose to place the same kind of trust in a social conflict mechanic and love that, instead (or as well). It's a question of accepting something as part of the medium and then enjoying that medium (much like how panels in a comic book aren't "realistic", but the mind can stitch them together into a continuous story - the trained gamer does much the same with "combat rounds").

      Occasionally see people rabidly deny this and make convoluted arguments for why, say, Belief mechanics are anti-immersive but "combat dice pools" are not. However, for a lot of these players, they say that they seek to make the player's experience as similar as possible to the character's, but then very clearly do not even try to do that.

      I *have* seen some groups try to emulate the speed and chaos of real combat (with things like combat timers, yelling at the table, throwing dice, and slamming their hands on the table), but they are in the minority.

      Having said that, there's certainly nothing wrong with having a preference. I've yet to see an argument for such preferences being universally "correct", however. (Though I would welcome some!)
      \nImmersion is a personal psychological experience and everyone's psychology is different, but there appear to be common trends (not shared uniformly by everyone). Also there is a huge difference between saying that you find something "fun" or even "fun and immersive" and meaning that it gives you the psychological experience of identifying with your character (which is more what I mean when I use the term "immersion"). I do not rule out that some gamers might get that from social combat mechanics, but, I think, they would be in a very, very small minority. It is not about "enjoying the medium". It is about the game provoking a particular psychological experience, i.e. finding it easy to suspend your disbelief in the proposition that you are your character, broadly speaking such that you have heightened empathy with your character, and identify with them. In the general sense of "game", I can enjoy a game immensely without even the beginnings of that experience (e.g. a game of Go), but with RPGs, my preferred sort of enjoyment is when that sort of immersion is there.

      It is NOT about preference alone. It is NOT about trust alone (placing trust in a system etc.). How can I explain this so that you understand? You really ought to trust what I say about how the immersion process works for me and others like me, rather than thinking that you can figure us out by observation (can you read minds? - I didn't think so), based on disbelieving our reported experience. Hypothetically, I could write a system of personality mechanics and social conflict mechanics that provides a 100% believable simulation of personality and social conflict. That would be at best only marginally better at providing me with an immersive experience (in the sense in which I use the term) than Burning Wheel or D&D 3e by the numbers or the most objectionable social combat system I could think of (i.e. still terrible). My ability to believe in or not believe in the outcomes dictated by the dice is NOT the point. The point is whether the process engages my brain in the way it would engage my character's thus tricking me into feeling almost as if my thoughts are my character's thoughts and that I am my character.

However in a minor way some forms of combat resolution might work better for a given person's immersion than others.       \n Let me try and explain it in a less ranty way. (This is almost bound to fail due to strength of feeling.)

      For me, immersion is the "in-character feeling".

      To get that "in-character feeling", to feel "in character", I generally have to actually, really do things "in character". Not just talk about doing them or describe doing them - I have to be actually doing them for real in the real world, in an "in character" way.

      In a tabletop RPG, above all, this means talking "in character".

      It also means reasoning "in character", i.e. having the cognitive thought processes my character could have.

      Live roleplay is more immersive for me, because there is more I can do "in character". I can walk around like my character, dress like my character etc. But for tabletop, talking in character and reasoning in character are at least a start.

      What matters is that it feels convincing to me. OK, so my voice doesn't sound as thunderous as my fictional wizard's anger, and my thoughts aren't an exact perfect duplicate of an immortal vampire's thoughts, but it's close enough to feel real to me.

      If I am not actually doing anything "in character" (i.e. doing it in the real world in an "in character" way), there's relatively little chance of me really getting that in-character feeling. If I'm doing LARP combat and it doesn't feel like how my character would be feeling, that may be a distraction from my immersion, the same as rolling dice or listening to a GM's description of me getting stabbed might be suboptimal from an immersion point of view.

Immersion in character is a much more distinctively roleplay-based phenomenon (acting and roleplay being materially similar for these purposes). Given the desirability of a special term for immersion in character, and the frequent use of the term "immersion" as that special term, using the term "immersion" in RP circles to refer to engrossment in general is what, in my opinion, muddies the waters.

      In my games Soul's Calling and Implodarc, both works in progress, you make rolls for social mechanics prior to commencing key social interactions, and the GM uses the results of those rolls to bias how he GMs the scenario, both in his roleplay of NPCs, and in hints and descriptions he gives (on a neutral outcome of the dice, the GM just GMs the scenario as comes naturally). The main examples I can think of, in a fantasy game, are when giving detailed directions to get from one place to another (when the players don't know the local geography), or when making complex legal or perhaps theological arguments, debating the ins and outs of magic and the like.

Even the best olympic fencer with a PhD in the most pertinent field (English Language? Classical Physics? Biomechanics?) is going to be incapable of giving a totally perfect verbal representation of an olympic fencing contest. Even a five-year-old can give a perfect representation of what his character says, if you are willing to imagine that the character speaks the way the five-year-old does.\n

To divide the RP world into "narrative" (or maybe "indie") and "tactical" is completely ludicrous. Many, many gamers reject both shared narrative authority / a shared storytelling agenda in all its forms, and all forms of tactical / competitive agenda, at least as a fundamental aim. So how do you divide RP into your two schools, then, Leo? Do you put folks like me in with the wargamers and deny our interest in immersion and exploration, or do you put us in with the narrativists and deny our rejection of shared narrative authority or storytelling aims?

      (I do not mean to imply that I am always against shared narrative authority in all its forms but merely that my optimal preference is that when I play I do not have it, and when I GM there are some players who do not have it.)\n
      I mean, why your immersion has to break mine, when it's so easy to design inclusive games so we can all play together? Why should games divide us by how do we have fun, when we can fix them to have fun together?
      \nBut it's not actually that easy. Implodarc is my attempt at an inclusive game, a trindie game if you will, a genuine trad/"indie" hybrid. But Soul's Calling, a more purist game, is much more optimal for me. The sacrifices I make in Implodarc for the sake of broader appeal will detract from my immersive enjoyment somewhat. And even in Implodarc, the social mechanics get out of the way of my immersion as much as possible. Where I make a concession, it is that indirect speech is fine... but direct speech is encouraged. Because if I am playing with you, and my direct speech is met with indirect speech from you, that spoils my experience of the game; it spoils my excitement, and my immersion. And this coming from someone who had used direct speech in roleplay only in LARPs, and indirect speech consistently in tabletop RP, until probably some time in the last 5 or 6 years or so.\n
      Because I thought those years when designers were accused of ruining immersion were gone for good.
      \nIt is not a bad thing that designers are accused of ruining immersion; it is inevitable. People have different tastes (some value immersion; some do not), and even among people who value immersion, people's minds work differently. There will hopefully always be designers designing games catering to immersive gamers, and designers designing games to cater to other sorts of gamers. What is optimal for one, will rarely be optimal for all the rest. So if all of us are to have access to games that optimally suit us, we have to accept that people will argue over whether designs ruin immersion. Or ruin collaborative creativity. Or ruin tactical depth. Or ruin the atmosphere of horror or mysteriousness. Or ruin the possibility of indulging one's trainspotter-like zeal for gun stats or for a vampire aesthetic. It's not a debating tactic used to justify clinging to narrow, grognardy horizons out of a fear of change or inhibitions against artsiness. It's a genuine sentiment and it deserves respect.

      It's a common theme among "narrativist" gamers and storygamers (and I've even done it myself on occasion) to refer to "simulationist" or immersionist games in terms of passivity, or a show put on by the GM. That is accurate only within a very narrow frame of reference, where you assume that the "activity of play" is the active and conscious control of the "narrative" (the active and conscious shaping of the broader events of the game). If you look beyond that narrow frame of reference, what the players in such games are doing is often the epitome of pro-active, energetic gaming. The only thing is that their actions are performed entirely within an in-character mindset, entirely through their characters' agency. Really it is a misconception to assume that this deprives them of influence over the broader events of the game. Generally, in "simulationist" and immersionist games, unless the GM actively railroads the players, it is in fact the players who are driving the "plot" of the game, driving the "narrative", making all the key choices about what the game will focus on - the difference to "narrativist" games being partly that they are not doing this consciously and deliberately, but rather that they are doing it subconsciously or, perhaps it may be more accurate to say, merely incidentally to playing their characters.

      The spotlight is always on the player characters. Unless the GM controls where the player characters can go, the spotlight goes where the players choose for their characters to go. The GM is merely a facilitator. It is the players who are exploring.

      Let me emphasise again that the problem with the "GM Mystery Cult" is when GMs do not realise that particular players care about impact on the fiction, and want their choices not merely to seem meaningful, but to be meaningful, or when for whatever reason a GM does not respect that autonomy. For a group of players who are not motivated primarily by the opportunity to contribute creatively to the "narrative", the "GM Mystery Cult" is absolutely unproblematical (as long as any necessary illusion is successfully maintained).

      "Narrativist" players tend to woefully misunderstand the "mystery-GM" of the "GM Mystery Cult" because of a common facet of human psychology:- our first assumption, until shown otherwise, is that others are like us. Thus, the "narrativist" player assumes that the "mystery-GM" cares about conscious creative control, and the "narrativist" player assumes that the "mystery-GM" knows that the "narrativist" player also cares about conscious creative control, and if you make those two fatally flawed assumptions then you are bound to take great exception to the "manipulative" behaviour of the "mystery-GM". It is all a big misunderstanding between the "narrativist" player who cares about conscious creative control, and the "mystery-GM" who cares about any one or more of atmosphere, character portrayal, immersion, believability, mechanical simulation, investigation and/or exploration.

      You refer to the "GM Mystery Cult" being the dominant paradigm of roleplaying as if it were somehow puzzling that that should be so. It is not puzzling. It happens because a lot of people prefer to game that way as players, and thus, when they GM, they assume that other players want the same thing too (because of the very same facet of human psychology that I have mentioned above, that we assume that others are like us unless shown otherwise). I am not saying that the "GM Mystery Cult" is in fact the dominant paradigm, by the way, but I do think that, interpreted in a broad way, it is a very significant and widespread paradigm.

      I doubt that many "mystery-GMs" are offended by attempts to create games that pull back the veil, or would be if they understood or knew about them, but they might well be offended by deprecatory and derogatory references to the "GM Mystery Cult" as if it were necessarily a toxic or broken thing.

      What is toxic is not the culture of GM-as-Wizard-of-Oz, but rather the collision between the culture of GM-as-Wizard-of-Oz and the smaller movement of RPG-Play-As-Collaborative-Oral-Creative-Writing, when a player desperately wanting the former finds a GM blinkeredly expecting the latter, and neither understands how their motives differ or can be made to work in harmony.

      My own view is that once you* understand the problem there is no difficulty finding a place for a person of the newer school in a game GM'd by and for the older school. The answer is the assistant GM's role. Only a hardcore purist of either school will not be willing to make this work once shown how, if it is the only way to get the gaming group to continue in harmony.

      * (the impersonal "you", not you personally)

      You (now meaning Paul_T again) talk about prioritising a framework for fun, trust and meeting each person's creative interests, but the point is that immersive players (often/mostly?) do not really have creative, so much as experiential interests. That is not to say that they are purely passive; they may drive the game, even with "narrativist" players present. Anyway/also, what is fun for or inspires trust on the part of immersive players may be very different than what is fun for or inspires trust on the part of "narrativist" gamers.

      You talk about some people "letting it slide" when the GM lies to them. I think it's worth distinguishing here between illegitimate lies and legitimate lies. If the game text, available to the players, explicitly says that the GM can lie about certain things, and the GM does so, then in the absence of a house-rule to the contrary, and assuming that the players are reasonably likely to have read the rules, I think that's absolutely fine. (NB players can also lie to each other subject to similar reservations.) As for "letting it slide", in line with what I have said upthread, I have had players who have been really upset when I have shown them some of the cogs of the machine; they are not just "letting" lies "slide"; they positively demand them! My assistant GM in a game of Soul's Calling was even quite upset at me for basically openly telling the players (including the assistant GM) at the end of one session that because a particular player, Pete, would be absent from the game the following week, we needed to come up with some in-character reason for Pete's character to be absent too. The problem wasn't that I wanted Pete's character to be absent when Pete was absent (although the players may not necessarily have seen that as being as vital as I thought it was). The problem was that the players wanted me to pull the wool over their eyes so that they (as their real world selves) would not realise the true metagame reason for the absence of the player character, and they were really annoyed at me for pulling back the veil! All or most of the players felt this way including even the assistant GM who I would class as a storygamer. I had had no inkling that they would feel this way, as to me it was just obvious that a player's absence would mean contriving the best excuse we could for the PC's absence, so I wouldn't have expected hiding that from them to have any chance of working.

      \nBut the more the players are forced to think constantly about each other's comfort zones and to roleplay with kid gloves, the more that in itself distracts them from simply roleplaying their characters, and hence ruins their immersion. I encourage players to just go nuts as their characters and let the GM try and handle any fall-out. Between sessions may be another matter but sharing of information about the fiction with a player should be avoided if at all possible unless his character would know it.

You can learn the tools of the trade more or less as quickly as in real life if you watch a couple of how-to videos. Next in efficiency are APs in comic format, specially if they represent both what's happening at the table and what's going on in the fiction. After that comes written AP examples, and then actual rules description.

      Anima Beyond Fantasy would possibly need 1/5 more pages to be properly explained in text, and maybe a series of 15 minute videos to offer a proper walkthough, like 15 of them maybe to include proper character creation and GMastering. Maybe reducing the manual to the half and adding 32 pages of comic to explain the mechanics could do the deal. \n

      Speaking of tastes and skillsets, for some reason much of this hobby seems to think we all (at least should) share the same ones and gets defensive when we try to identify how we don't. But different games favor not only different interests but different abilities. For example, a player who's good at math but bad at reading people in not as likely to enjoy a game which relies on improv, and more likely to be frustrated when that takes precedence over calculation. On the other hand I'm really good at playing to a GM's expectations and tend to ignore the written rules entirely.

      But most RPGs are so vaguely defined you have no idea which skillsets are favored before you play. Add houseruling and the honesty/clairity issue this hobby has when it comes to explaining what these games actually do and you get a hobby deeply inaccessible without a friend involved as a gateway. And since you have to get through the Trad barrier before you get to the Indie titles, the latter are doubly inaccessible.
      \nIt's more like too many people assume everyone out there thinks in the same way, while in truth our mental skillsets make us totally different people, able to do and enjoy way too different things becaue of that. And of top it's easier to believe people who can't do what we do have an extremely rare flaw, instead of thinking we are the ones who have a somewhat rare gift.

      It's somewhat sad that ultimately, all depends on marketing principles. The faster you can sell it, the more people you will reach, the more sales you get. Since RPGs are still somewhat hard to explain, it's most of the time not fast enough to keep possible buyers attention. Specially when the best part of the game only comes up after 5 minutes of play at the very best, using premade characters, a really streamlined system, and railroading a lot. It starts when players have answered the question "what do you do", roll a high number and the GM narrates what happens.

      But then all you've got to catch a potential buyer attention is possibly 8 seconds best.
