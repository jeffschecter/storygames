\n Any of you hip to Al Bruno III? He's a perceptive writer. Here's an excerpt I was just reading today:\n

My first Dungeon Master had us perform feats of endurance and strength to gauge where we fell in the 3-18 range, our Constitution was determined by holding our hand over an open flame. The more seconds you held it, the more points in Constitution you had, but inversely the less Wisdom points you had because you were, after all, burning your hand for a stupid game.
\nHorrifying... and yet interesting, as well.

What are some of the most unorthodox methods you've seen or used to create characters for a game? (These can be examples of things you've tried, or things in published games.)

    \n Best friends has an amusing method where you describe what you hate each of the other characters for. The more people hate you for being pretty, the prettier you are , the more hate you for being cool, the cooler you are, etc. Your character is defined entirely by the other character's perceptions of you. Which really worked for a game about high school girls.

    \n Back when I used to play games with "stats" (insert grin), I had heard of and once implemented a method where the players never saw their character sheets. The first few sessions, I would just observe their behavior and intuit that this guy is a lethario and this one is a bad-ass and so on. That informed their stats.

    Not really inspired, but I think this one is...

    I also ran a game once, where the snacks they chose before and during game led to (balanced) bonuses to their D&D character. Sort of a "magical potion" perspective on things.

    And of course, there's the Everway method.

    \n I did an Unknown Armies game where I started with:

    "You're in a bright room. You can't see anything. The light starts to dim. You can make out a figure. Dims a little more. What do you see?"

    I went around the room, filling up a portion of an index card each time, asking them more of what they saw. When the index cards were filled, I handed them to the players. What they saw was their characters, which the clicked on.

    Then we played the scene. They were trapped in a maze where every surface was mirrored. No character sheet or stats -- in fact, they didn't know what game system we were using. We just winged it until I felt like they shared camaraderie.

    Then I whiped out the Unknown Armies core book and they got excited. Then we made the characters almost normal-style, street level with a Soul skill that was undefined.

    This was the first scene of the campaign, and a clue as to what they actually were -- archetypes in the Invisible Clergy kicked out by ascending Godwalkers. So they were literally being recreated in this room in the House of Renunciation. It was a few sessions before that understanding was dropped on them.

    \n That reminds me of a con game of Over the Edge that Robin Laws ran. For the first half hour, all that happened was we players asked yes or no questions, and Robin as GM would answer them "Yes". Sometimes "Yes and" or sometimes "Yes but" but never "No" anything.

    After we had our starting PCs and situation fleshed out, we started playing regular Over the Edge.

    (Somewhere, Robin Laws detailed this procedure in his column for Page XX, but I don't think it's online for free any more.)

    \n To prevent the lone wolf syndrome, we had all four players pitch in three proto-pcs. They picked race, career, and goal. Once we had 12, they went in to the hat.

    I read off the information, then each player secretly voted for which person at the table should finish that character.

    So each one had between two and four characterst that they were "qualified" to play. Plus we all had a little insight into the other ideas, and how to work them in to one another.
