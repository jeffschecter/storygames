    Remember the game Zork? Interactive fiction is often a lot like a single-player, text-based virtual world. However, it tends to focus more on telling a particular story than on gaming with other people. Due to the difference in focus, it usually relies on a different family of codebases than, say, MUDs or MUSHes; but the basic interface is similar, e.g.:

    go east

    "You enter a room. There is a hat on a table."

    look at hat

    "It's made of felt."

    wear the hat

    "You put the hat on your head. You're being attacked by head lice!"

    kill lice

    Etc.

    Of course, I'm sure most interactive fiction enthusiasts would expect something much more interesting and well-written than that. Whereas, your typical MUDder's main concern would be whether or not the hat gives a good stat bonus. :) I'm painting with overly broad strokes, of course. It's kinda like the difference between "narrativists" and "gamers" in the tabletop RPG community. Oh, and then there's the MUSH player who cares most about how the hat benefits them socially.

    Here is the one interactive fiction site I've ever paid much/any attention to: http://grandtextauto.gatech.edu/

    I also just found this FAQ: http://www.plover.net/~textfire/raiffaq/ifaq/

    Out of curiosity, what were you hoping for?

    I've been following Chris Crawford's work for a while. There's a lot of interesting technology in the Storytron, although right now only the Story World Authoring Tool has been released. It appears to be a tool that makes it easy to create a bunch of pseudo-intelligent agents (characters) that will go around and interact with each other. I'm still not entirely clear about how players get inserted into the world, and what they do. (Apparently, in a predecessor to the Storytron, there would be all sorts of complex character-interaction stuff in the background, and then the player would occasionally be presented with about 4 choices of how to proceed.)

    After quickly looking over and trying out the SWAT, it seems to lack tools to easily drive a story towards a climax--basically, pacing tools. I think there's a little bit with "Fate" and "Plotpoints," but I'm not sure. I'd have to look deeper, which I don't currently have the time for. Given the structure of the program, I'm sure it's possible to build pacing tools, but it would be nice if there were pre-existing ones I could play with.
