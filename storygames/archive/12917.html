Again, our "official" (scarequotes!) rules summary for these threads:

-----
1. Feel free to respond here in-thread to another link posted.
a. If you're looking to start a conversation, perhaps take your comment and create a new thread with it in the Stuff to Watch category.
b. Otherwise, to prevent too many comments in the thread, number your response (first response: [1]; second response [2]; third response [3]). If you see that you're the second person to post, "Wow, that looks cool!" to a post here, then put a "[2]" at the top of your post. Three posts ([3]) is the limit: The fourth person to post should just take their comment and create a new thread, to carry the discussion over there further.
-----
Why?

Because these threads are great: We can link to little things being talked about on the web in general, on blogs, or other forums that might be noteworthy, without having to dedicate a new thread to each and every one (although that, too, is fine if you want to discuss the item further or "open up a discussion").
Responding to these posts with a comment or two is understandable (other times there might be no comments, just cause the link was self-explanatory: nothing to say, lots of heads nodding in agreement). But we don't want to create a huge discussion in the middle of the thread: Some folks get a lot out of these threads, and don't want to look back to see the "10 latest posts" just to see that it's a heated discussion of something from a previous page. Cool? Cool.
-----

    \n Kinda weird.

    I just got word back from a friend who went to JGC "Japan Game Convention", basically the GenCon of Japan. Not nearly as large, and a far greater focus on community-building, company announcements, meeting designers and playing games far over sales stuff.

    Anyway, I told my friends to take lots of pictures. Turns out that taking pictures is forbidden at the con (and apparently many convention-like events, not just gaming related, have similar policies). On top of that, apparently people were not allowed to blog or twitter.

    Totally bizarre: Such an opportunity to spread the word, get others excited, etc, and all viral media outlets are forbidden.

    I asked my friend why: Apparently the blog/twitter thing is so that people don't spread "baseless rumors" or other information (like to-the-minute updates of announcements, etc?). Weird, because a lot of folks live vicariously through attendees blog updates on announcements and the like.

    The official word on pictures is that, well... it's Japan. In Japan, they be takin' motherfuckin' PICTURES, see? So basically the official reason is that, outside of signing events and the like where apparently it's ok, they don't want 20 people hovering over a table where some designer is playing, taking tons of pictures (apparently there may be staff photographers though) and interfering with that events.

    On the other hand, there is likely an unannounced reason: There are cosplay folks at these events, and they want to prevent otaku perverts from taking upskirt or just uncomfortable pictures of the women in outfits. Or women gamers. I checked around after hearing that and found that this was kind of the Unspoken Reason at many events. Heavy-handed, but in the end it's better to say "no pictures" then have to keep an eye on all the people all the time in case some fucko abuses the privilege and drives off the already small amount of women in the hobby.

    Anyway, I found it an interesting cultural thing: Viral marketing through the fanbase is pretty much dead: No pictures or blogging allowed (although it was noted that there were people breaking the rules, however they weren't particularly chastised, but people noticed and in some places were like "WTF, man? If you're going to set these rules the con staff should enforce them better").

    -Andy

      \nHow small publishers can improve their presence in gaming stores.
      \n
      \nGary Ray, owner of [url=tp://www.facebook.com/l.php?u=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.blackdiamondgames.com%2F&h=b7358]Black Diamond Games[/url] in Concord, CA wrote a guest blog post about third party publisher (applicable to small publishers as well) and retail stores.\n
      \n
      role-playing games occupy about 20% of my consciousness, assuming they have an equal role to play (they probably don't, but I like them, so it's not far off). Of that 20%, about half my sales are Dungeons & Dragons and Pathfinder. I think this is fairly typical.
      \n
      \n
      \n
      So all non-D&D; role-playing games are now occupying about 10% of my consciousness when it comes to purchasing. Of course, purchasing is probably about 20% of my job as a store owner, so in reality, all things RPG that aren't D&D; are more in the range of 2%.
      \n
      \n
      \n
      At last count we carried over 30 different game systems, but most of the 3,000 or so stores out there that carry games probably have far fewer. I put myself in the top 10% of stores in this respect. I'm not bragging, just providing some perspective. Maybe 200-300 stores will have that 2% on their radar.
      \n
      \n
      \n
      ask your customers to buy from us. This may seem against your own interests if you sell direct. It may seem futile if my numbers are correct and 90% of store owners won't care, but 10% of stores is 300 sales. I'm not saying you give up direct sales. Just tell your customers that if they've got a good store to support it by asking for your product and buying it there if the store owner brings it in.
      \n
      \n
      \n
      Why give up half or more of your margin? The FLGS is yet another sales channel and can be a big source of marketing for you. Once we buy your product, we have some level of interest in generating further demand. That one guy might have bought it, but a hundred more people might see it on the shelf. You've also created a sales partner. We post your product info on Facebook, talk up your product with potential customers, and possibly hold events if it's a big enough deal.
      \n
      \n
      \n
      Foster your sales channels equally. If I visit your page and I see you encouraging direct sales over store sales, such as with discounts, early release or telling your customers to please buy direct and not from the grubby game store (this has happened), we're done. I am not afraid of the PDF product, but if I see you're already selling your print product before the release, I'll generally believe the demand is soft and probably pass.
      \n
      \n
      \n
      Digest size books are popular, especially with the D&D; Essentials line moving to them. However, many stores hate them. Think about how your book will be displayed. We use magazine racks, so your digest sized book is invisible unless it's in front. This means it's likely to be shelved on a book shelf instead, spine out.
      \n
      \n
      \n
      If it's not a one shot RPG product, already begin marketing book two on the release of book one. Maybe you don't have the money for it yet. So what? Bang the drum. If you take a wait and see attitude, so will your customers and many won't buy your book. They have this crazy perception of what is alive and what is dead. They want "support" for the product line, reprints with errata, additional supplements to spur their imagination. If that's not forthcoming, they have deep suspicions.
