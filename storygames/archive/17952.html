\n So I have been modifying some of the characters from my pulp villains larp, "Shadow Centurions Assemble!" - which takes a few characters from Spirit of the Century but mostly is based on original pulps. I've been having trouble with it, so I thought I'd think out loud some. The original character list is here:

http://www.darkshire.net/jhkim/rpg/spiritofthecentury/shadowslarp/

My approach to this has been to take pulps villains as they are written, and add in some real-world history into their backgrounds. The primary situation is that the uber-villain is essentially holding try-outs for what group will be his next partner - with three contenders being Fu Manchu and his allies, Gorilla Khan and his allies in Africa, or the secret Incan Empire in Peru. I've run this twice previously, and I noted that some characters are picked last: Yen How the pan-Asian political, Zanaka the Bantu Warlord, and Agent Maita the Incan secret agent.

The first isn't a very strong archetype to modern players. Yen How is from the novel "The Yellow Danger" and is a twist on Sun Yat Sen. In the book, Yen How united China and Japan to threaten the West, so his power is political which is tricky in a larp - and in personality archetype he is too close to Sun Yat Sen.

As a replacement, I decided to pick Lai Choi San, the Dragon Lady. She is from the 1930s comic "Terry and the Pirates" inspired by a supposedly-historical female Chinese pirate - and is the origin of the term "Dragon Lady". This is definitely a strong archetype. I've tried to make more characters gender neutral (so the Incans have a "ruler" rather than a king or queen), but there's no getting around this in some cases.

There is some secret background with the Incans related to their concern for the threat of being exposed by Dr. Henry Jones Jr. So I want to keep Agent Maita, but I decided to bind him more to an archetype by giving him the tagline, "Man of a Thousand Faces". This was the tagline for a pulp hero, Agent X, so I'm mixing my sources here - but the Incans are a mixed source already. They're loosely based on Indiana Jones and the Crystal Skull, but in that one the Indians were all wordless savages. Similar sources include Tintin's "Prisoners of the Sun" and similar plots about secret Incan gold and flying saucers. The archetypes are a little weak, though.

The more difficult has been the Africans. I feel like people accept playing an Asian villain from a racist source like Fu Manchu, but it is more uncomfortable to play a black villain. My other non-gorilla African character is not from the pulps at all, but rather a semi-historical figure, Andr\xe9 Matsoua. I'm actually OK with there being a little discomfort - but I'd prefer it to be relatively equal among the character choices so that some players don't feel put upon. I had been thinking of making a character as La, Emissary of the Lost City of Opar. The city of Opar is from the second Tarzan book - inhabited by the degraded descendants of Atlantis. However, the secret lost city in Africa overlaps a lot in archetypes with the secret Incan Empire in Peru.

Ideally, I want a distinctly African villain archetype that is still fun to play - but I'm having trouble with it.

To connect this with bigger ideas, I think that archetypes are often very important for play - and in particular are probably a key behind the explosion of Apocalypse World variants.

    \n I was sketching on the expansion of This Is Pulp, and because the first (not published) edition only had American white people as archetypes, the edition should be about people from other continents (and one robot). So I started to write down a couple of archetypes where one was tribal warrior. I looked at it and I realised what I was doing. I wanted archetypes, not stereotypes. In my first edition, I randomized the gender of the archetypes, and I ended up with a young woman as a mad scientist, just because I wanted to break the stereotype.

    What you want with an archetype is something that people can (even vaguely) recognise, but I think it's equally important that you also make something new of it, so it wont turn into a stereotype. Stereotypes are good as a quick starter, but they get tiresome in the long end.

    But to answer your question, I think Tahitian voodoo magic is really cool, but voodoo is actually from Christianity. One thing that is really African is zombie creation. Perhaps you can do something out of that?

    \nNo, Vudun is an indigenous West African religion, although its variants and descendants in the New World have Christian elements mixed in. I don't think it has anything to do with Tahiti, though \u2014\xa0Polynesian religions are entirely different.

    Back to the original issue: Distinguishing between archetypes and stereotypes is difficult when there are so many strong negative stereotypes around already. The best solution I can think of is to try to make your character be the opposite of the stereotypes: if he's African, make him cultured and superhuman, not primitive and subhuman. If he has to be a villain, make his villainy aloof, not savage.

    One idea that comes to mind is an inverse Josef Mengele \u2014 a brilliant doctor obsessed with African racial superiority who performs evil medical experiments on human subjects to try to find a way to wipe out the Caucasian race.

    Or maybe he's obsessed with the Malian Empire and wants to restore its capital Timbuktu and take over the world from there. He might think he's the reincarnation of the emperor Musa I.

    Of course these aren't based on pulps \u2026 but I think it's going to be effectively impossible to make any real vintage pulp African character palatable to modern players, so you'll have to get creative.

    \nWell, I agree that it is quite difficult, but that is what I am trying for.

    The South Americans characters are composites that draw mainly from Prisoners of the Sun (1946) and Indiana Jones and the Crystal Skull (2008) - though the latter itself is a pastiche of many pulp sources. I wanted lost gold and flying saucers, which are common pulp themes. The funny thing is that the 1946 comic has natives that are more human and two-dimensional than the 2008 film, where natives are more like monsters that come out of the walls.

    The East Asian characters are all drawn straight from period sources: Fu Manchu and Fah lo Suee from Sax Rohmer, Shiwan Khan from The Shadow stories, and the Dragon Lady from Terry and the Pirates.

    I think you're right that there simply are no vintage pulp African characters that would be palatable, but I would want to at least approximate. A zombie master seems possible. While zombies are mainly Haitian, there are at least some are set in Africa. I'm reading "The Forbidden Trail" by Jane Rice (1941) now - which is set in Liberia.

    \n Rob - It's true that Egypt is in the continent of Africa, but the other characters of that faction are all from West Africa - specifically French Equatorial Africa, just north of Tarzan's jungles. Two of them are gorillas enhanced by weird science, and one is a legendary messianic leader of Gabon.

    Really I'm looking for iconic opponents to Tarzan, but I don't think there are any recognizable ones. The closest I've read in the books or adaptations is the Russian ne'er-do-well Rokoff, but that's a stretch and not very fun in the pulp sense.

    Actually, the zombie-making itself isn't that vital to the situation of this particular larp (since no one has troops or backup). However, the witch doctor is definitely a recognizable archetype who could have a background including zombies. Now the trick is how to make a witch doctor palatable enough to be fun to play, but still true to the pulps. Plus it's distinct enough from the others - whereas my lost city Atlantean was much like the Incans.

    In the original E.R. Burroughs Tarzan stories, Africans are always superstitious and foolish - they would never have real magic power. However, there were many Tarzan adaptations that strayed from this to take up other pulp archetypes.

    \n Yeah, if it needs to be about jungle Africa, witch doctor is the way to go. Sell it by grounding it with some real material on Vodun.

    And Snej is right, you could twist the especially derogatory stereotype parts a bit. Put him in a suit instead of a loin cloth. Just don't do the top hatted Baron Samedi thing, or you've left Africa and gone Haiti/Louisiana.

    Make him the cultured son of a village witch doctor who is using a combination of magic power and political/economic power to seek vengeance for the homeland of his people that has been taken by the westerners. Maybe he was taken out of the jungle by well-meaning missionaries who saw him through college, but he secretly held on to his pagan faith and magic, along with a burning anger for what was done to his culture.

    That would add enough depth to keep the archetype elements from drifting into negative stereotypes, I think.
