\n There are lots of online games, just take a look at the popularity of Tabletop Forge in Google Hangouts.

However does that mean we should treat them any less than we would treat a game at the local club / friends get together?

It seems it's far to easy to sign up to a game (online) then not turn up with no hint of a reason why (until maybe after the event)?

However if you couldn't make it to your friends house, you would be sure to tell them, no....?

    \n Given that almost all of my gaming is now online, after the group gets to know each other the attendence problem stablizes. While you might think the anonymous thing leads to more drops, it's also balanced by that the computer is right there. Compared to my pre-internet FLGS games, I'd say attendence is actually about the same. The key to my success it two fold. For game groups like Sunday Skypers and Porcelain Llama, having a consistent time to play and always planning at least one game session in the future is important. For gaming with friends and strangers at something like a virtual convention, web sites like ConPlanner are essential for organizing people and events.

    \nYou can get 14 people, ON AVERAGE, to show up to your games?!?

    I can't get 2 other people to show up on a regular basis - in person, face-to-face - for more than a couple sessions at a time (I cried and hugged myself when I managed a full Primetime Season of 5 episodes once!)

    To bring the discussion back to topic: As you can see, face-to-face has problems. People have lots of gaming options and, well, they go where the action is. Or they help out their grandma. Or they have a hot date. Part of the roleplaying ethos is the implicit understanding that there are probably better things you could be doing with your time. I have no plans to do them, but sometimes you just can't get free.

    Also, we're not as socially-invested as other groups. Simple courtesies, like warning people that you might not show up to a game, aren't as ingrained in our subculture. For one thing, we don't get as strong a reward from the results of those courtesies as others who attend to them. For another, many roleplayers have gained the benefits of alienation (we have more than our fair share of geniuses and eccentrics) and they aren't as intimidated by the implied social threat of rudeness as others.

    \n I have a long standing FTF game on Thursdays and a long standing On-Line game on Wednesdays. I find that attendance to both are equally abused. And I find that recruiting for either is equally as painful.
    I used to worry about on-line gaming with a lot of the same concerns. But I think that the stereotypes around on-line gaming just make us notice the crappiness that is recruiting and scheduling players to show up for a game more.
    I mean, think back to the last time you organized a FTF game and think about how many times someone you knew who acted interested in your RPG game night flaked out the moment you actually asked them to attend. Or how some people can only make it to your FTF game about 50% of the time and the trials and tribulations about whether to keep them or drop them, etc.
    It is just the same as Online gaming, but confirmation bias just makes you notice it more. Or we have just become conditioned to the pain that is FTF game scheduling, lol
