Creating House Rules

As Dungeon Master, you wear several hats: storyteller, rules arbiter, actor, adventure designer, and writer. Some DMs like to add a sixth hat to that stack: rules designer. House rules are variants on the basic rules designed specifically for a particular DM\u2019s campaign. They add fun to your D&D game by making it unique, reflecting specific traits of your world.

A house rule also serves as a handy patch for a game feature that your group dislikes. The D&D rules cannot possibly account for the variety of campaigns and play styles of every group. If you disagree with how the rules handle something, changing them is within your rights.

This advice can\u2019t turn you into an expert game designer - we\u2019d need more than a page for that. Instead, this is a basic introduction to the concepts behind rules design. Once you\u2019ve become familiar with these ideas, the best way to learn more about game design is to play, see what\u2019s fun and what\u2019s not, and use your discoveries to guide your own work.
\nNow I'm not sure the bredth and depth of this rule is understood. Not that it needs to be, but it'd make any discussion I have about roleplay alot easier. So here goes.

Probably the biggest issue is a perception that if any use of the rule feels bad, then it must have been used wrong.

In the other thread I was tempted to give examples like turning up for a game of 4e, and being presented with poker, since every rule had been altered. But I knew this would get rejected "That's an absurd use! No one would do it like that, it's clearly an incorrect use of the rule!"

But it isn't. It's like a gun - the results from firing a gun might feel bad, but that doesn't mean the gun itself was used incorrectly.

This is the bredth and depth of 'The GM can change any rule'. If you think about it without the restriction of 'If it feels bad, it was used wrong' you realise it can ahnihilate every rule in the book. That it can drag in poker and call it 4e. That there are no rules which will definately be a part of 4e, except one. The only rule you will definately encounter in 4e's is that the GM can change any rule. All 4e consists of is 'The GM can change any rule'. Everything else is an option. Options that you can't count on to be present.

If I offered this equation, where the contents of each bracket are optional to add, does this add up to a correct answer?
(1)+(5)+(2)+(3) = 0

Is that a correct answer? Given that each bracket is an optional add? If there are any math guys in the audience, yeah, I'm just making up the bracket thing (if there's a more elegant way of putting it, hit me with it).

Now, what is 'The GM has the right to change any rule' but to put everything in brackets?

Which means it can add up to zero*, correct? Even if that sucks complete ass. Even if it feels bad. Even if it seems morally bent.

"But we can tell him when it sucks and he wont want us to feel bad so he'll change (or if he doesn't, he must be a jerk!)"

And sadly, this, in the face of the rules bredth and depth, is dishonesty. To make it more fun, imagine you'd given him a licence to kill (cue bond music). But then you complain about someone he's about to kill. Did you or didn't you give him a licence to kill? Did or didn't you give him a licence to ditch any rule?

You can't give but not really give, as a way of managing the bullshit the absolute power invokes. Not unless you want to trade honesty like a commodity.

    \n Sooooo....going back to the original topic...here is a bit of my experience with it and maybe this is where the whole thing started (I think at this late date, the 'change the rules if you want!' rule's intent is lost to the mists of time).

    When we played regular ole red box D&D, there were rules arguments. Well and good. What I find interesting is that while we, in our 7th-9th grade male way, were completely obsessed with both following the rules to the letter and simultaneously finding loopholes to demonstrate our cleverness/prowess, we valued being able to find and cite the appropriate rule as being the ending of an argument. In other words, the authority whose view we were seeking was the designer's view. None of us asked, "Well, between rule interpretation A and rule interpretation B, what is the best one to select for our group in this moment?" It was always "between rule interpretation A and rule interpretation B, which one is clearly the most supported by the game text."

    Yet this was problematic for us for a very simple reason: it was clear that the designers did not follow the same rules they gave to us. D&D modules were rife with characters with too many hit points, too low an armor class, special magic powers that broke the rules for magic powers, permanent effects in dungeons that violated the rules of what permanent magic effects did, or worse, what the rules told us permanent magic effects were like in the setting (really important! hard to set up!) We became increasingly frustrated with what we perceived as inconsistencies in the rules that we were constantly seeking to find some authority in. And if we hadn't been 7-9th grade boys and therefore fucking morons we would have seen that this was only to be expected when dealing with a property that was worked on by multiple people over the course of many years, and even when it was the same person, they clearly refined their designs and changed their views of what the best sort of rule was, as you might expect any human being on god's green earth to do if you thought about it for five fucking seconds.

    Faced with the knowledge that the designer's intent A) didn't matter, was stupid, C) changed all the time, there was really no choice. We couldn't just keep searching the rules for the rule we wanted. Someone would have to decide. The game identified an authority, the GM. This kicked it back to the social level for a while. ("Young JD's games are like this, whereas Young Brian's games are like that.") Eventually we started to work out criteria for when a rules interpretation, patch, or replacement was a good idea or a bad idea, usually based around the needs of the individual group, their preferences, their fucking schedules, their experience level, their comfort level, etc. And that is how I eventually interpreted "change the rules if you want!" rules - to push people who had not been as far down our road as we had further down the road.

    \n It sounds like Callan's issue is not with the rule itself, but with the flavor of GM-player relationship it presupposes in its current written form. I mean, whatever the rule is intended to mean, as written it clearly presupposes an competitive relationship between the GM and the other players in terms of deciding what the fun is.     Mature, uber-experienced roleplayers, and particularly those who design their own games, should have no problem ignoring that presupposition. Sometimes rose-colored glasses are worth putting on, and many people have a good idea of when to do it.

    But I think Callan is correct (if I'm not putting words in his mouth) that there will be some GMs for whom the writing of this rule will confuse the real issue: not whether the players are still showing up, but whether the group fun is being maximized. Hell, even great GMs can forget to distinguish between those two goals on occasion. Each and every rule should clarify that distinction, not muddle it even more.

      A little history recap, about things I think you already know, but I don't know if you have ever considered in this context:

      The very first rpgs didn't have no rule zero. They had a very different thing: being games that didn't cover much apart combat, once the characters got out of the dungeon (and often when they were still inside) there were a lot of situation not covered by the rules. So what happened in these cases? The referee made an ad hoc decision, or ruling. "I want to seduce the king's daughter" "well, there is no rule about this... well, roll against your charisma, with this big fat penality, will you?". But this happened when there wasn't already a rule about this.
      Add to this a social context where (1) everybody picked rules, classes, monsters, etc from a lot of sources and from all over the place, from Arduin Chronicles to Dragon articles, and (2) often the GM was the only one to have access to a lot of these sources (the GM guide, for example), and you get GMs that have a lot of control about the rules used in the game. And this by necessity, because there wasn't really any other way.

      Well. Even in this social context, when there was every reason in the world to delegate to the GM the choices of the rules and the power to make a ruling at any time, you would be hard pressed to find text in role-playing games or publications that advocate what we today call "rule zero": the authority, given to the GM (only) by the game, to ignore at whim any rule and any roll or any results, at his discretion and without having to talk to anybody about this, "for the good of the game"

      When we have the first texts that, at first, inch by inch, by innuendo and inference, and then more and more boldly say this? When we begin to have universal system that can be used for any possible action, when we have games playable using only the player's book, and when more and more GMs ask for more "story" and less dungeon crawlings from their players. When there is no need anymore for all that GM's control "for the good of the game", but they need it "for the good of the story".

      It's rather disingenuous, thinking about the history of that rule, to say it wasn't used, and abused, against player's authorship. When that, really, it's its very reason of being. To help the GM against player's choices that would "hurt the story" (it's rather difficult to say seriously that it could be used "for the good of the game", intending with that the gamist part of play: making the loser win anyway to make him feel better isn't a very good way to have a good game...). When it was introduced ONLY when there was no so much need anymore of the old ad hoc rulings from the GM.

      Do you know a lot of GM that didn't use it that way? From the way you talk about it, in concrete detail, it seems that what you are talking about is house rulings, discussed with the other people at the table. If that is rule zero, even Poker has rule zero. Every game has house rules. Or rulings for the parts not covered by the written rules, or unstated social rules. That's not Rule Zero AT ALL.

      How many GMs I know that abused Rule Zero (if and when they used it) to stop and rein player's input, thinking that stomping would have as a result a better game? Lots. So much that I don't know even a single GM who didn't do it at least once, in my presence, playing with me, counting even one-shot at conventions. Me included. It was the "normal" way to play. Tons, tons of printed papers told us, over and over away, that it was the only way. That if we would have given too much (meaning "any") meaningful input to the players, the game would have been screwed, that we would have been very bad GMs, wussies, no better that B.A. from KotDT. And that players who wanted more input were muchkins or power players.

      When we played regular ole red box D&D, there were rules arguments. Well and good. What I find interesting is that while we, in our 7th-9th grade male way, were completely obsessed with both following the rules to the letter and simultaneously finding loopholes to demonstrate our cleverness/prowess, we valued being able to find and cite the appropriate rule as being the ending of an argument. In other words, the authority whose view we were seeking was the designer's view. None of us asked, "Well, between rule interpretation A and rule interpretation B, what is the best one to select for our group in this moment?" It was always "between rule interpretation A and rule interpretation B, which one is clearly the most supported by the game text."

      Yet this was problematic for us for a very simple reason: it was clear that the designers did not follow the same rules they gave to us. D&D modules were rife with characters with too many hit points, too low an armor class, special magic powers that broke the rules for magic powers, permanent effects in dungeons that violated the rules of what permanent magic effects did, or worse, what the rules told us permanent magic effects were like in the setting (really important! hard to set up!) We became increasingly frustrated with what we perceived as inconsistencies in the rules that we were constantly seeking to find some authority in. And if we hadn't been 7-9th grade boys and thereforefucking moronswe would have seen that this was only to be expected when dealing with a property that was worked on by multiple people over the course of many years, and even when it was the same person, they clearly refined their designs and changed their views of what the best sort of rule was, as you might expect any human being on god's green earth to do if you thought about it for five fucking seconds.

      Faced with the knowledge that the designer's intent A) didn't matter, was stupid, C) changed all the time, there was really no choice. We couldn't just keep searching the rules for the rule we wanted. Someone would have to decide. The game identified an authority, the GM. This kicked it back to the social level for a while. ("Young JD's games are like this, whereas Young Brian's games are like that.") Eventually we started to work out criteria for when a rules interpretation, patch, or replacement was a good idea or a bad idea, usually based around the needs of the individual group, their preferences, theirfucking schedules, their experience level, their comfort level, etc. And that is how I eventually interpreted "change the rules if you want!" rules - to push people who had not been as far down our road as we had further down the road.

      So that is why "rule zero" is a no-brainer to me (godawful though the piece of jargon may be.) All rules are rule zero. Everyone chooses either to apply a rule, mis-apply it, or ignore it, at every moment of play, and it's the choice that controls the game, not the written rule.
      \nI don't have a lot to add, I just wanted to note that Jason's account here is exactly like mine, and that this kind of thing was a strong influence on my game writing. (Perhaps most fully in the chargen chapter for Dark Ages: Vampire.) And yes, to those of you wondering, questions like "Do I actually have permission to change this rule?" actually do arrive fairly regularly at game publishers. White Wolf was routinely getting several a month when I was there, and I hear from friends at Catalyst Labs that they keep getting them to this day about Shadowrun.

      In fact, come to think of it, I do have something to add...

      Rule zero and its kin are responses to preexisting experiences. I'm not sure how much that reality has ever penetrated into story-gaming discussion of them. They aren't primarily manifestos created as what seem like good ideas on their own - they're things put together to clarify principles that game writers thought were as obvious as most story gamers would find them, but turned out not to be. The context of dealing with existing player and GM concerns, ranging from the very sensible through the simply naive to the outright weird, strongly shapes the way such principles are formulated, positioned, and modified (or left alone).

      This isn't surprising, of course. Most efforts at formulating principles have some element of reaction in them. And the field of gamerdom is broad enough for wildly different experiences to be all be common enough to have inspired someone to try and establish their reactions as a foundation for new effort that will escape the inspiring problems. But it can be easy to look at a response to a problem you've never had and think "But does that ever happen?", even when the answer turns out to be "Yup!"
