So, the other evening I had the pleasure of leading a troupe through a short Seven Leagues game. I play infrequently with a group of indie-minded gamers, and there is no steady game night or "campaign"; we get together catch as catch can and play whatever happens to strike people's fancy. I'd been wanting to take Seven Leagues out for another spin with these players for a while; we've only played it together maybe 3 or 4 times.

I had prepared a one-page outline of the scenario, with vital stats and some very basic relationships sketched in.     The handful of 7L games we've played have all felt very non-traditional-Fairy-Tale. For instance, our first game had the Cockroach Queen, a tiny giant, and two members of a psudeo-Incan pantheon, plus someone else I can't recall. Definitely further along the contemporary weird-shit-o-meter than Hansel and Gretel. The comic Fables would run very nicely with the 7L engine.

    In 7L, I think a play group can very easily paint itself into a corner in terms of group cohesion; more accurately, I think that each member of the group can paint themselves into a separate corner. Hieronymous' decision to thematically link the PCs by using the phrase "velvet box" was pretty significant, more so than I thought at first. 7L has a lot of Develop-at-Start (are we still using that terminology these days?), requiring a lot of up-front thinking about a character and their place in the world. There's no sense in having a "party" of Fairy Tale characters, since there's generally no rich NPC to hire the characters to retrieve the McGuffin.

    In a nutshell, thematically linked character creation can help solve the "why are we all together?" and "why do I care?" problems.

    MDK

    Nicely put, Rossum. It is true that in designing and play testing Seven Leagues, "why are we all together?" came up as one of the more persistent issues. I think that's exacerbated by the fact that there are few mechanical templates on which to build a character. Sure there are examples of Protagonists and Antagonists in the rules aplenty, but no classes or ready-made professions, or even races. That's deliberate of course; the last thing I wanted was every one to play the same old flavor of goblins and faeries.

    As a result of this conundrum in the section on Troupes I tried to offer suggestions of some natural ways to resolve this that fit the context of "classic" fairy tales -- typically basing Protagonist affinity on kinship. The thematic affinity I employed in this particular Tale however is more consistent with modern "fairy tales", such as Sandmand or any number of magical realism stories, and I'm glad Anemone picked up on that.
