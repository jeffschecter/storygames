\n Two things:

1) If you are willing to play online via Skype, IRC or other means (not in a convention, just as a single group), post a reply! I'd like to know who is able to play online, and I think others could see the list and form groups. If you want, post what you'd like to play and what platforms you're on.

I'll start:

I'd like to play via Skype on weeknights, weekly for 2-3 hours in a session max. I'd play a lot of indie games. One I'd love to play is PTA, but the want-to-play list is really long. I'd prefer not to run a game, but that's not a requirement. I'm eager to play, but I'm also going to be a choosy bastard on who I'm playing with. I've done this before (play online, that is) and it doesn't always work out very well. So, if you contact me and I decline, please don't take it as an insult. It's not. I just want to get the most reliable fun out of my time.


2) Has anyone ever participated in or organized an online convention? I've never participated in one. My employer (totally not gaming related) hosts online conventions for enthusiasts related to their magazine titles. The software platforms they use would be cost prohibitive for our teeny story game crew. But, maybe there are other platforms and/or ideas? What about activities? What did you do? What worked? What didn't?

A story game online con would rock -- like a winter weekend or something. And, really, it could be a great time to learn and buy more indie games, so maybe IPR or indie publishers would help out either with supporting money, discounts or offering to run events and the like.

    \n Something I've considered, but not yet tried, is variant on the indie-netgaming method.
    \n
    \nFor any not familiar with how ING does it, they use IRC, and typically split a game into two rooms, one Out of Character, one Narration. Dice rolls, kibitzing, rules questions and clarifications all go in OOC. Descriptions of in-character actions and speech go in NAR. This has the effect of creating an AP log of just the events in play for those who care about the story but don't want to look under the hood. For those that want both, it's not too hard to follow along by reading through them both if the logs are timestamped, which I believe is the rule. I've done a few playtests using this method, as well as a couple casual games, and it works well.
    \n
    \nWhat I'm considering is using skype for OOC discussion, as it would flow notably more quickly than text-based OOC, and use IRC for NAR, that way you get a log of the in-game events as well. You'd lose the OOC log, but many times, that's not important, unless it's a playtest, where rules discussions are more important.
    \n
    \nAs for playing: I don't think I'd be able to commit right now. Also, if you're being picky, well, you hardly know me (read: not at all) anyhow. But if this works out for you and you'd like to try again, or if anyone else is interested in trying in the near future, I might be interested.

    \n Vassal came up a couple of times when we were discussing this previously. Basically, the weaknesses I see with it are:

    1. Development requires some degree of programming skill - posing a barrier to entry for someone non-technical (or for a programmer who doesn't know Java, like me) who wants to set up a playtest for a new game they've just written.

    2. It's not generic, as you just noted - which I see as a big, big weakness, and which is closely related to problem 1. There's no real reason why, with the right design, you can't have all that Filip listed above (Gabob appears to be heading in that direction - I didn't realize it was PC-only, though). You just don't encode game rules into the app - only ways to manipulate objects, like dice, cards etc.

    3. It requires a download and install - it's not totally web-based, turn up at a URL and you're up and running.

    It's probably the best already-completed solution out there that I know of, though.

      Posted By: WolfeDice rolls, kibitzing, rules questions and clarifications all go in OOC. Descriptions of in-character actions and speech go in NAR. This has the effect of creating an AP log of just the events in play for those who care about the story but don't want to look under the hood. For those that want both, it's not too hard to follow along by reading through them both if the logs are timestamped, which I believe is the rule. I've done a few playtests using this method, as well as a couple casual games, and it works well.
      \nIf you just toss both logs into a spreadsheet program (assuming the logs treat each entry as a single line with a carriage return) then you can just sort them by "Column A" and the OOC and NAR will automatically be put in exact time order (assuming the time stamps use a HH:MM:SS format with a zero before single-numeral hours, of course).
