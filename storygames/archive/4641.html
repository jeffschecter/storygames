    \n Personally, GNS makes a lot of sense to me with LARPs, even though that's not what it's designed for.

    You can play many LARPs to win (e.g. to discover the murderer) and play them in a Sim way ("I just want to play my character"). As with tabletop games, the two can conflict badly if different groups wants different things.

    In a jury game, winning would probably consist of swaying the jury your way. So, you'll get a lot of conflict if some players play to win (sway the jury their way), while others just want to play their character. Usually, the ones who want to play their character will accuse the ones who want to play to win of powergaming.

    Graham

    \nIf you don't know this by now, then the organizers have failed somewhat in their preparations. Or maybe they will tell you on the day of play. The problem that you have in tabletop with people expecting totally different things is only a larger problem in larp. The degree of freedom in larp is greater and so is (usually) the amount of people.

    But, on the other hand, in a larp the culture and habits of the other players is forced upon you in a much more forceful way than in tabletop. People who play together will quite easily develop a common style and agreements. My personal experience is definitely that the people that are most easy to larp with are those who have never larped anything before and who has never played tabletop! When I started larping I had spent my whole life throwing dice. I had to unlearn everything! Those new people you can mold in a day. (And they will never be the same again...)

    \nI enjoyed myself, that's for sure

    so yeah, I'll definitely go back next year

    as I said, this convention was really aimed at small games, accessible to beginners (though my two friends and I were the only beginners out of the twelve people in our game)

    the logistics were great, which is nothing to be sneezed at

    acting was cool, and some of the players were pretty impressive (and fun to interact with)

    RP-wise, I'd say the experience was pretty much in line with my old Sim habits, just dressing up, and with more people

    the number of players made things interesting and more "believable", but also generated some weirdness

    well, not weirdness... it's just that I feel there's a lot less confusion in tabletop RPing

    example: when someone gets stabbed in tabletop, everyone instantly knows what happened

    in the LARP, there'd be a commotion, people running around, people asking questions to the GM, and things would only become clear after a 30 seconds or so

    it's not a bad thing per se, but it was unexpected for me

    now, back to the Sim part...

    I did feel a little frustrated at my lack of control over the narrative, just like with regular ol' Sim RPing

    more precisely, I felt like I wanted to skip over some of the "down-time" to go straight to the meat of the story

    but this might be due to the fact that with more people playing, the story is spread more thinly
