\n There is so much I *want* to like about Houses of the Blooded and one major thing holding me back: The concept of Privilege. I simply do not like the idea of resolving player intention divorced from in-fiction character desire. That came up a few times in The Roach game I was facilitating this weekend and each time it made me uncomfortable. The only reason I didn't clamp down on it is because I was too tired to do so and the cases were minor. If you want to know my feelings on the manner see the Character Advocacy sections of my play passionately gaming blog.

Now that said, can someone give me a detailed break down of how exactly resolution in Houses of the Blooded works in terms of player-fiction relationship. This really is a make or break thing for me, so be as concrete as possible. What kinds of situations invoke the system? What is established (fictionally) before the system is deployed? What, if anything, fictionally does the system once deployed lock into place? How are consequences moving forward decided? That kind of stuff.

Oh, and I don't need all kinds of details about options and what not. Just the basics.

Thanks.

Jesse

    \n Jesse. The game can support your preference for player advocacy, but it by no means does that exclusively. You could play 100% for success and push hard for it. You could choose to take a more authorial stance and use privilege to decide how your character fails, if that were of interest to you as a player. The system, used in that way, is more about directing the narrative, and less about the PCs in-fiction desires. I know that's not your thing, but others find fun in doing that. The wager system allows you to add one fact per dice wagered to the fiction. Those facts generally stand, subject to veto for dickishness and tone.

    \n I've read the game, and I'm pretty good at that - but no actual game experience here. Insofar as I can discern, the stuff about "rolling to lose" is there as a half-formed vestigial thought perhaps cribbed from some other game, with no real impact on anything else. (As far as I can guess, the reason for this thing in the first place is that the game wants to encourage full-body dramatism from the entire group; it's a bit top-down in that you first need to sell the stance to the players and only then will the mechanic be at all relevant. Unfortunately there's a slew of other mechanics that become happily irrelevant if the players take narrative fun seriously like that.)

    Of course, I'm sure that somebody out there has latched on this particular rule and considers it central to the play experience; if so, I'd love to hear about it, even if it's not my cup of tea.

    \nContested risk is only for one action at a time. You cannot add something as a wager that would require a contested risk. With privilege you decide if you succeed and add details to the scene. It seems to me that John wants you to wonder what the other players are going to do with that power the same way you wonder what a GM might do in another game. This multiplies the tension and engages the players in a way that I have seen in few games.

    As an aside, this game solves what was a central problem for me with Amber Diceless (players tending to go off on their own and play in a corner of shadow). My players jump on contested risks even when they're not around in character especially Wisdom and Cunning risks (these tell you what you know and what you discover).

    So to shorten my answer both of your examples could happen to you character if you have privilege. It is up to you what you choose to do with that power. My son really hates thinking like a hippy gamer so he has his character always succeeding at his intentions with privilege and only rarely uses wagers (except to injure people). My wife likes a deeply political game so she puts her character in these convoluted and difficult situations. They both play the same game at the same time and get something very different (and personally enjoyable) out of it. To me this is very, very cool.

    \n Perhaps it's useful to note that the tagline "Ambition, Love and Revenge" definitely is all about the players manufacturing stories of this sort with a top-down approach - the players want the stylistic tropes of drama, and thus drive the play towards that. The game facilitates and encourages this by having lots of fiction pointing to that direction, basically. In a sense it's a propaganda trick - you get Ambition, Love and Revenge by telling the players that that's what it's about and then giving them enough narrative power to narrate whatever they want into it, including those things. Provided that you laid the groundwork correctly, the players themselves fulfill the promise. It's like Vampire in that sense; tell the players emphatically enough that your game is about lofty drama, and they might do just that, regardless of what the game's system encourages.

    Which is to say that I wouldn't whole-heartedly recommend Houses of the Blooded simply because somebody liked to have those things appear in their game - if it's important to you that Ambition, Love and Revenge rise naturally from the motivations of the characters, Houses of the Blooded does very little to ensure this. On the other hand, if you need a spark of inspiration and are willing to pour in the Ambition, Love and Revenge in the form of your own character narrative, then you're playing the game the way it's intended. The best way to phrase this is perhaps that the designer will be glad if you decide to appreciate his aesthetic and play the way he intends, even if there is little in the game to structure or encourage such.

    \n Eh, I guess I should just write a review of the game, apparently my viewpoint is stealing the thread. I'll try to explain that throw-away comment in short:\n
      \n
    • Relentless censure: The way I read it, Houses of the Blooded shares the typically '90s notion that you get players to make the correct kind of fiction by schooling them into it with lots of fluff (to be read, appreciated and emulated) and a concept of "good roleplaying" shared by the play group and used to create social pressure that keeps players in line. One might say that the game designer has precreated a vision of what sort of things the game is about (theoryspeak: theme-focused simulationism), and the game book's job is to transmit the vision and the tools the group can use to cohere themselves around that vision, managing to replicate it faithfully.
      • \n
    • Narration resolution: The original topic of this thread touches on how the players in HotB are not supposed to be advocating for the true interests of their characters, but rather they should be objective storytellers who share responsibilities for creating a story to the preagreed standards; that's why players get narrative authority in the game, while characters only rarely or not at all get their way, unless explicitly so allowed by a player at the table. Effectively, it's about passing a conch to determine which player gets a turn to shape the narrative, like Once Upon a Time and a slew of other games since then. As far as I understand the goals of the game, the reason for using narration resolution is that it allows players to conflict over characterization of characters and preplanned plots (made by players and characters) - the rules are there to determine whose plans for the game go through. This is very familiar to me from Amber and some other games that share extensive character backstory and some other bits with HotB.
      • \n
    • 90's movement of game design: From the viewpoint of somebody with relatively little exposure to these games, it seems to me that games like Vampire (and pretty much all other WW games), Deadlands, 7th Sea and probably a whole lot of others as well (I haven't read or played very many of these, so I won't try to guess on which games might belong in the group), all share to some degree of the same qualities I'm seeing in Houses of the Blooded. I found Houses of the Blooded to be a very clear and inspiring example of the priorities of play evidenced in all of the above titles, while Wick also did a good job of illuminating the style and techniques related to it. Wick explicitly recognizes the struggle between simulationistic and gamistic priorities evident in these games, and tries to resolve it by the means of explicit social contract; meanwhile, the game rules themselves continue to strive for a seamless combination of rules that control player input with rules that inspire it, continuing the tradition of rules-incoherence (and, one assumes, practical rules-drift) from the '90s. I'll be interested in seeing what sort of responses Wick's work (which I consider substantial within its frame) will generate in its intended audience.
      • \n
    Again, I might just not know what I'm discussing here. The '90s style of game design all but skipped my personal experience at the time, as the Finnish countryside spent most of the decade happily playing '80s games such as Runequest, Call of Cthulhu and Paranoia. My personal play experience of these games is really slim, even if I've read some titles such now and then. In general I've been impressed by the passion these writers bring into their fluff, but the actual rules-sets seem to have little to do with transforming player interaction and lots with orthogonal simulation of the in-fiction reality - an approach that I find relatively dull and confusing myself.

    Even more significantly, I don't really feel any need to contest my impression with anybody who happens to love this sort of game. So nobody needs to come and prove me wrong just to win the Internet. I'm sure that the huge heritage of gaming that Houses of the Blooded represents includes much depth and value, if you happen to get excited by that sort of thing. Feel free to tell me about your viewpoint, but don't expect me to go down in flames to defend some impressions I gathered while reading the game on the flight back to Europe from Gencon.

    \nIt depends on wagers.

    Each wager allows a player to say "Yes, and" or "Yes, but." So, if you and I get involved in a duel, and at the resolution, you have two wagers and I have one, it could go like this.

    YOU: (w/privilege) I lose the duel! (You now decide who spends their first wager. You decide it is going to be you.)
    YOU: (first wager) AND I am hauled off to jail.
    ME: (first and last wager) AND you lose respect in your lover's eyes.
    YOU: AND she thinks you are a cowardly brute.

    Or, it could go like this.

    YOU: (w/privilege) I lose the duel. (You now decide that I spend the first wager.)
    ME (first and last wager): AND you get hauled off to jail.
    YOU: (first wager) And my lover cries for my loss
    YOU: (second wager) And she thinks you are a brute.

    Now, because I know you want a certain outcome, I can use my wager to give you what YOU want. As the Narrator, I'd give that player a Style Point for helping another player accomplish what he wants for his character while sacrificing his own wager. That's Good Form.

    Wagers allow everybody to say "Yes, and" or "Yes, but." How you use your wager is up to you, but if you do Good Things with those wagers, there's a rule that rewards you for it.

    \n John: the original issue was over how you can screw up your own character in HoB by narrating stuff that undermines his success - so you win a contest just to screw over your own character. This goes against the established habits of many; its fundamentally opposite to the model of roleplaying that's developed out the dominant Forge strain of narrativist games, wherein the role of the player is to provide a voice for their own character's independent desires and protagonism. A game that explicitly says that your character is supposed to be a protagonist, but he really has no rights to advocation by anybody at the table (that is, nobody is responsible for advocating his interests), has a very different social mechanic going on as regards story creation. I don't know that I'm right or wrong in my personal interpretation, but to me the game reads like you're supposed to pretty much play the director and script motions for your own character to go through without making a vicarious commitment to taking his side yourself. It's like a given player is committed to play a given story instead of a given character, and the conflict rules are there to find out whether we play the story I have in mind or the one you have in mind.

    Jesse's example illustrates the fine line that can be found between a player advocating for a character and a player advocating for a story: his favorable example has the character achieve the thing he really, truly wants out of the situation, while the other example has the player undermine his character in every conceivable way - so instead of being the character's advocate, he's just using the character as a tool of his story.

    (Not saying that this is fundamentally a problem and that all games should work the same way or anything like that; it's just how I understood Jesse's original query set-up. I also realize that the game's not really written within a paradigm where there is just one right way to play and read it - it's just that we need to read some sort of game into the text when we read it, and that is the interpretation that I see as the most natural and dominant one.)

    \n What I mean by "preferred social dynamic" is what I've extrapolated from your GM Advice in various sources: 7th Sea GM Guide, Play Dirty, your LJ, etc.

    With regards to my example here is what I'm talking about Eero has the right of it here:\n
    Posted By: Eero Tuovinen
    Jesse's example illustrates the fine line that can be found between a player advocating for a character and a player advocating for a story: his favorable example has the character achieve the thing he really, truly wants out of the situation, while the other example has the player undermine his character in every conceivable way - so instead of being the character's advocate, he's just using the character as a tool of his story.
    \nHere's what I believe. Playing from the point of view of character advocacy preserves tension in the fiction but removes tension at the social level. You declare your character's intention. I declare my character's intention. No tension because you and I are the sole arbitrators of what our character's want. If these intentions are in conflict that produces tension in the fiction but there is still no tension at the social level because whether the intentions are in conflict or not is fairly objective. Finally, the system is deployed and it tells us whose intention wins out. The consequences of that inform our next declaration of intentions and so on. Thus story is an unpredictable and uncontrollable by product of these clashes.

    This is not to say that we can pick character intentions at total random and AWESOME will happen. That's bunk. Quality input produces quality output. Crap input produces crap output. This is about processing the input in a non-socially tense matter.

    To move things from the character level to the player level introduces all kinds of social dodges and manipulations that create a social dynamic where story is something someone *makes* happens or forces into the fiction by wrestling over the Privilege stick or endless negotiations about exact outcomes when using Stakes in PtA.

    Jesse

    \n Heck, it well might be. Or rather, I'm pretty certain that its rules do not actually prevent that sort of play, among a range of other interpretations. HoB is written in an (I'd like to think) intentionally flexible manner, so it's up to the group to decide how they do things. As we established at the beginning of the thread already, you can decide to preserve your character's intent in every challenge, without ever narrating against him. The rules do not prevent that, but the instructions, insofar as I understand them, do advocate a kind of play where you put the good of the story (in the dramaturgical sense) first and decide on your character's success accordingly. That's why the option is emphasized, it seems to me.

    Interestingly enough, "choosing to suck" can happen on many levels and mean many things. In HoB it's the player who chooses for his character to suck because he thinks that brings about more interesting events; in Dust Devils the character chooses to suck because he knows that pulling his guns would win him the battle, but the sheriff would then never trust him again. These events have very different meanings for what you're doing in the game, even if in both cases it's the player making "non-optimal" choices for his character. (Non-optimal in scare quotes because in truth that latter choice is optimal: it's more important for the character to gain the trust of the sheriff than win the fight.)

    (Also: I'm sorry if it looks like I'm hogging this thread or something. I just came out of a game design wherein I did a lot of hard thinking about the topic of player-character relationship, so I'm interested in this stuff in other games, too.)

      The character advocacy issue is really two layers for me. One I think is actually a rather vital social organization tool. And works just as I described in my previous post. If you go to the bottom of this page:

      http://playpassionately.wordpress.com/concepts-from-elsewhere/

Thus I am likely NOT to spend fan mail on the GM's behalf because I am that committed to my characters as people with worth while goals, beliefs and passions. At minimum I don't try very hard for success if my commitment to the conflict at hand is very low but I almost never actively undermine my character. I prefer to play with people who share this connection with their characters.

      Edited Note: And all this applies only in the moment of conflict and crisis. I often take a very gleeful roll in *establishing* those moments of conflict and crisis. This isn't about keeping my character out of harms way. It's about standing up for him when harm arrives, even harm I've engineered myself.

      \n Speaking for typical set-ups, the point of advocacy is in letting players take on character roles and make choices from the viewpoint of that character. This is not the only technique to get themeful play, but it's a popular one. Having your own character intentionally lose a conflict signals that we're not really contesting a matter between the characters; you could just give up if that were the case. Instead, that sort of resolution procedure seems to be pointed towards (among other things, I'm sure) resolving player-level disagreements about where the story should go. Advocacy-based play rarely has this sort of player-level planning in the first place, as players are willing to let the chips fall where they may, in the interest of giving their characters complete protagonism (that is, freedom of choice).
