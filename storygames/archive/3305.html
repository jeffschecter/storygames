\n So, as I understand it, this is how its supposed to work. One guy buys a big book of rules and another big book of setting information. The first guy reads through both big books thoroughly and writes a plot and a series of encounters that makes use of as much of the information from both books as possible to run some characters through. Then he gets a bunch of other guys together and, first, instructs them as thoroughly as possible about the contents of the setting book, then has each of them make a character according to the methods outlined in the rulebook. These characters are then run through the plot the first guy came up with and, if it's a hit, that guy will then go on to write up another plot.

That, as I understand it, is how gamers are expected to pick up on new RPG's. And it simply does not work for me nor, as far as I can tell, does it work for any other gamers I know. It's a process that expects nearly all the effort of getting a session of play going to come from one person, the GM. It also demands that the GM serve as the lone aperture of a large amount of information, such that I have to wonder how much of those detailed campaign settings actually ever reach the game table. In terms of actual play, I've only ever been able to establish the broad strokes of a setting effectively, and fine details such as the particular names of all the mountain tribes never have a chance to appear or, as often as otherwise, are superseded by the improvised details that come up in play. I don't buy into "big-box" settings anymore because I know from experience that most of them will never be of any use to me. The same applies to rules systems. Light, quick and functional beats expansive and detailed every time since, even if I care to adjudicate something in particular focus, by the time I've looked up the rule I've probably house-ruled as good a solution anyway.

Increasingly, I'm seeing RPG's as something that works best "DIY and on the fly," a simple set of guideline rules, a few broad strokes of background (one page of text, some pictures) and a lot of improvisation at the table. I've begun thinking of RPG's as a group creative effort to be nurtured and grown, not a technically oriented script to be adhered to. Spares me a lot of work, too.

Which puts me at increasing odds with the rest of the hobby, or at least the commercial aspect of it. For instance, I think the concept of Ars Magica sounds neat, but I'll probably never buy into the gameline because I'll get no more use out of a new book than I would with just FATE, Wushu or Fudge and the phrase "you're a coven of wizards in medieval Europe". I can't even conceive of ever buying a D20 product again.

I am curious how many other folks here find that their tastes are evolving similarly, and how they relate to the rest of the hobby.

    \n I dig indie games. I podcast about them with some friends. Story Now is a cool thing indeed. However I haven't yet abandoned my affection for the trad RPG style of big book of rules and big book of setting.
    \n
    \nThe traditional RPG setting book and GM-doing-the-background work is something I tend to enjoy for a few reasons:
    \n* I think of RPGs as if they are a very small play (as in theater project). When I run games, I like to have enough control of a few scenes to "set them up", from practicing NPC voices, scoring some scenes I suspect will happen within the session, mapping stuff out for conflict scenes and the like
    \n* I like having a reference document to skim between sessions to spark ideas or give me different looks on situations or help me to reincorporate player actions or concerns into the Big Picture (and many players like the Big Picture to come from a big ole setting book)
    \n* With a traditional RPG, I have stuff to keep me imagining between sessions, background tasks that entertain me away from the table. In an indie game where everything happens at the table, I don't know where to put that energy.

    \n Re: the big setting book. I honestly think this kind f thing works most effectively when the players have read the sourcebook also.
    \n
    \nThe, your players and the GM have a shared language of references, much like the cool feeling when you meet someone who likes the same movies as you do, and you can quote it back and forth. If your players have read the book, this means that by simply saying one name or another, you can immediately introduce a whole family of concepts and feelings related to that name, because they've all read about it.
    \n
    \nSay, for example, there's a secret guild of assassins called the Red Hand. Sure you could run the campaign with the players knowing nothing, and have them pick up what they need to know about the Red Hand by having NPCs exposit at them over time. Or, you let them read the book, and then you can just drop references to the Red Hand freely. And as soon as you do, the players will immediately be like "Ooooh shit, the Red Hand!"
    \n
    \nThe problem is not with the books themselves, but with the advice which says that the players shouldn't read them, in order that they be surprised. But surprise is over-rated, and the idea of "stuff that is secret to the GM" equally so. Besides, people nearly always read that stuff anyway if they bought the book. Of course they do.
    \n
    \nIf you're no letting the players read the book, then the book is much less useful, although it still provides an aid for creativity, a source of cool pictures and stats, and a go-to source when you desperately need an answer to a player who just went off in a an unexpected direction.
    \n
    \nBut far and away the best use of the setting book is to let the players read it, and then exult in the shared concepts you now have access to.

    \n Looking over my original post, I think I wandered a bit into vague groussing, so I'd like to restate what I think is my main point. Simply, I have become intolerant of gaming literature, whether rules or setting, which does not have immediate value for play *now*. Multi-hundred page tomes of rules and background have become an unacceptable obstacle for my fun, and I suspect, after some observations of other games in progress that they have for others as well. While I understand that game literature can be entertaining in and of itself to read, frankly I think that has no bearing on its use at the table, and I'd rather spend my lesuire-reading time on "real" books, which in turn I find have more value at the game table in the end anyway (why read a space opera version of a faux Battle of Iwo Jima, with stat blocks, when I can read about the actual battle).

    I find that a "DIY on the Fly" approach is working best for my games, where there's alot of room for improvisation and the story, the setting and the rules all grow over the course of play. Note, I am not advocating total improvisation, sitting down and starting from zero. I've tried that, and in the end it does not work. But I do like starting loose, with a rules framework (preferably one that can be explained completey with just a one-page character sheet) and a simple background overview (5 pages or less) and a handful of striking pictures to set the mood.

    Aggravating matters is that the products put out by hobby game publishers are increasingly superfluous to my approach. Why would I pay forty dollars for a multi-hundred page book that I have every reason to believe will by 90% useless to me in play? I would much rather pay one or two bucks for five pages of setting or rules information at a time, downloaded when I need it. Harn used to be distrubuted in such a way, via a base set and a modular expandable rulebook, and I was always struck by how practical tat approach seemed. Frankly, the modular approah seems so superior and obvious to me I'm suprised no one has taken it up yet.

    \n Sometimes I think we could use a word that's the opposite of "lasersharking." What's the thing where instead of adding incongruous details one on top of another in the pursuit of More Awesome, you're adding more and more "congruous" details in the pursuit of More Awesome?

    Just as Lasersharking can result in Awesome, but can also lead to absurdity and loss of "rightness", or can drown out story/character in sheer over-the-top Color, "logicsharking" can lead to Awesome by intensifying the "reality" of the game, or it can lead to Suck through closing off choices or by reductio ad absurdam of genre conventions.

    The economics of cargo ships in Star Wars, 9 times out of 10, are either going to Suck (because any kind of sensible treatment of them probably breaks genre enough that what you get "isn't Star Wars" in some significant way), or they are going to be Awesome, but force you to discard so much other Star Wars-iness that you have to ask yourself "Why am I playing the Star Wars RPG, again?"

    Posted By: E.T.SmithOne guy buys a big book of rules
    \nI want to throw some love to the Big Book of Rules.

    Lately, I've been running GURPS 3E. This is far, far from my preferred game. We use it for my Skype/Maptool group because the guys with kids don't have time to learn a new game and want to stick with what they know really well. And I love playing with these guys, so GURPS it is.

    For those who don't know, GURPS is horrendously fussy and obsessed with matters of game balance. Character creation is fiddly enough to require a computer. There are hundreds of skills to choose from, many of which branch into narrow specializations and/or have complicated prerequisites. There are many disadvantages, which are mostly enforced with a "roleplay this or be penalized" rule. There are character quirks, which, as presented, are mostly things that influence the dead play between the start of the session and the presentation of the adventure hook. All this minutia is aimed at neutering the munchkin player and promoting "role playing," but none of my players are munchkins. They don't have the time to be good at it, and they recoil at even the notion of such impropriety. Really, on the surface, GURPS has absolutely nothing to offer my group.

    So, awhile ago, I was looking over characters sheets and thinking about the upcoming adventure, when I realized that every GURPS character is pure flag soup! There's like 20-40 flags to a GURPS character and they're all tidily prioritized by their character point cost. How can adventure design be hard with this kind of input? So I tried picking three flags from each sheet and then structuring an adventure around that set, and bingo, instant better fun.

    The climatic scene of our last session involved an Animal Handling roll -- one of our characters had a challenge vs. his alt-timeline NPC double over the loyalty of his/their dog. The dog went with the NPC double, and it was A Big Moment with its alpha and omega in the clunky old GURPS mechanics. Everyone was into it. The post-game chatter was really upbeat.

    I feel kind of embarrassed to admit this revelation, because it really ought to have been completely obvious to me in 1986 when I ripped the cellophane off my first GURPS Basic Set, but there it is for ya.

      As a former GURPS lover, I feel obligated to point out that the system can be stripped waaaaay down if you want. For a while I ran it with, like, a dozen skills to choose from and almost all the complexity flensed from its weary bones.
      \nWhen we started our GURPS campaign, I was like "We're using Lite and Magic and that's it, because I am way too old to deal with 400 pages of rules anymore."

      But then, the Basic Set kinda crept in during the first session, during an unarmed combat. "Just this one rule lookup," I told myself. And with the nice searchable PDFs that I have, it is really easy to look up other rules too. Now, ten sessions in, we run GURPS with the full advanced combat system, pretty much unflensed, and it runs pretty snappily. (We're in the good part of the GURPS power curve, at about 130 points, so things haven't gotten stupid yet.)

      I went through a long stretch of loving rules-light games that substituted GM judgment for rules -- things like Over the Edge, Star Wars d6, Castles & Crusades, that kinda thing. Now that the Forge and its diaspora have made me reconsider the role of the GM and the value of engaging mechanics, I'm finding that it's kinda nice having a rules system that'll arbitrate the controversial stuff in a detailed fashion. If only the GURPS pre-play weren't so damn onerous...

      So, to bring us back to the business of the thread: I guess I can't agree with the "It's a new hobby" sentiment. All this stuff that happens online, this forge business and this story gaming and knife fighting and indie press revolution and whatnot, all of it has made gaming much better for me in a wide variety of situations, from hoary ol' GURPS to cutting-edge story-games.
