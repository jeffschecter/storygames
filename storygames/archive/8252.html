\n Hey friends, I'm putting together a book of lists to share this holiday season, and one of my lists is "The Six Phrases Every Traveler Should Know". I'd love to present them in as many languages as possible, and thus I turn to you, my polyglot posse. I've already got German, Danish and French covered, but that barely represents half the world's languages! Here are the six phrases every traveler should know:

1. I would like a room for the night.

2. A feral cat has bitten my penis.

3. You gangsters are all stupid weaklings!

4. My primary interest is hard drugs but what have you got?

5. I need an axe handle, three bibles and a Faberg\xe9 egg.

6. I am a United States senator; your rules don\u2019t apply.

I know these boards are haunted by speakers of Polish, Tagalog, a variety of Chinese dialects, whatever they speak in Scotland, and many more languages! Help make my holiday bright while showing off your crazy linguistic skills!

    \n However, Petri, my translation fits Finnish idiom better. In Finnish we don't use the perfect tense when describing something that happened recently and requires immediate attention. Rather, we would only use the perfect tense if we wanted to imply that the event and its consequences have all passed and are not a concern anymore. The past tense is used whenever the issue is still on the table and requires attention. Assuming that Jason wants medical attention for his penis and not just make small talk about something that happened to him a while back, the past tense is the natural choice.

    The same effect in English is achieved by switching to a passive voice and a different tense. "My penis was bitten by a feral cat (a while back)." vs. "A feral cat has bitten my penis (and I need help)." is pretty close to the difference that tense makes in Finnish; you'd use one of those at a soiree when describing your adventure, one at the clinic while getting treatment for the wound. Not that you wouldn't be understood either way, of course, especially if you take the penis out and show the wounds to your audience. I'm sure they'll understand what you mean.
