Im planning to run a game at a Non-RPG convention next year and wanted to run a game set in the "Adventure Time! With Fin and Jake" Universe, a Cartoon Network show thats totally Algebraic!

I want a game that is easy for new players to pick up, and allows alot of player participation in world burning too as Id like people to play using non-canon characters and settings but with the theme of the show. It has to be fun, fast and slightly silly.

I had thought of using In A Wicked Age with a player generated Oracle set - but Im not sure if this will be too complex a rule system for novice players and maybe a little too PvP though I am not against that.

Fiasco is also a possibility, but Id like to give players the feel of a GMed game to an extent too, but again I can be persuaded!

Any suggestions?

    \n Adventure Time is D&D.

    There is no question about it. It's self aware, surreal and psychadelic D&D but it's D&D.

    Characters go into dungeons and face gelatinous cubes and mimics. Characters reference their alignment and experience gain. Wizards cast D&D spells.

    Perhaps there's a tinge of Gamma World in there, but it's not sci-fi enough, just post-apocalyptic. And D&D is post-apocalyptic.

    However, 4E is too slow, tactical and mechanical. You want something light, fast and freewheeling.

    Something like Swords & Wizardry White Box edition or World of Dungeons. It would be really easy to create some custom classes/races for World of Dungeons to fit into the Land of Ooo.

    My second choice would be something like FATE or Cortex+ or PDQ# allowing for a more descriptive character creation and assertions of weird stuff, like Jake's growth abilities.

    \n There are a lot of games that would seem to match the setting (D&D et al) but wouldn't encourage people to play in the fast and awesome Adventure Time style. Dungeon crawl systems will not encourage you to pickpocket the giant and then fly away in a paper airplane folded from the huge dollar you stole from him.

    What you need (need!) is a hack of Keep It Sunny. It's built to encourage fast play and give players strong hooks into their character's shticks. If you nailed the character cards it would play itself: "Jake gets a coin whenever he takes the lazy way out and cashes in three coins to get out of a tight spot with key-hand!" (or whatever). The episode format would get replaced with the stages of their quest and the difficult morals they didn't learn along the way.

    If you wanted a safety net you could easily insert a GM to present new wrinkles and keep it flowing.

    (here's a discussion thread about the game)

    update: Won't work as well for generating new characters at the table, because that would require the players to have a strong sense of witty-but-not-overpowered shticks. You could let them pick from a pre-gen list of abilities and build their characters from that.

    \nI had actually been working on this, called Kingdoms of Ooo. I've been a bit sidetracked lately, what with other projects getting in the way (compounded with my inability to prioritize), but I'll take this long holiday weekend to finish it up (in tandem with playing a RECON adventure and trying out Durance for the first time).

    There are, I think, many ways people could play an Adventure Time RPG, depending on what games someone had played in the past that gave them that feel of freedom and adventure, and what they focus on when watching the cartoon. For some, they liked the heroic feeling of D&D4e, and want to focus on that but with absurd-sounding races and monsters (such as the aforementioned 4e hack). Some found the fun and heroism in other games, either less structured, more structured, cinematic, etc. Some see in the cartoon the innocent heroics, others see the tongue-in-cheek pop-culture references, or the morals of the stories, or even the breaking-the-fourth-wall comedy.

    It's a matter of finding what you want in the game, and what you get out of the show, and balancing the two.

    In my case, I want to recognize the D&D roots while having mechanics that are flexible enough to allow for random stunts and improvisation...thus, World of Dungeons. I also want to allow for both the absurdity of the cartoon, as well as its drama and heroism. That informs how I'd use the WoD rules, or, in my case, adjust them to my needs. All while still fitting on three or four double-sided letter-sized sheets of paper.

    Hopefully I'll be able to post it on Monday.

    \n The way I see it, an Adventure Time game needs:

    AD&D alignments (Flame princess is Evil, Marcelines dad is Chaotic Evil, Finn won't act out of his alignment)

    Monsters. There needs to be monsters with a clear level structure, where one monster is stronger then the next ( a rock is a low-level enemy! Vampires can't beat ghosts.)

    Magic items (demonic witching eye, The Enciridian,)

    It's a weird, surreal show, but it works within a certain set of rules (most of them seem to have been lifted from the dungeon masters guide!)

    If you have a totally freeform game where players can just go nuts and narrate anything, it's going to start out gonzo and just get silly pretty quickly.

    A game where I could play a talking ice cream cone needs some boundaries or its going to fall apart.

    One key element of the show is how the zany humour is balanced with despair and sadness. Some of the monsters are pretty terrifying, and there's the whole deal with the Ice King's back story.

    I think you need something rules light, but fairly traditional with a GM as a strong adversary, to bring those moments of sadness.

    If not D&D maybe something like Mouseguard or even (shudder) an AW hack?

      \n I know it's superlight, but the wackyness of Adventure Time just comes out naturally in Baron Munchausen.

      Just make some new prompts that fit the AT universe. Ask the prompt to TWO players. They are always Finn and Jake. Finn and Jake then start telling the story of "that time when...". They're best buds, so they back each other, also when telling tall tales. They ask each other questions when they're stuck, and finish each other's sentences. They both know how the story went, so one of them can go "I'll be Ice King. Jake, go on, tell them what you told Ice King!". Deal out math points to the best story. WInner is the most mathematical!

      That's all AT needs, for me. Or perhaps something that ties Baron Munchausen-ish tales together.

      Oh and if anyone can pull off a rules-light evocative thing like this, it'd be John Harper. Please listen to your urges John!
