\n With all this talk about live gaming , I've been considering the notion of props.

Water pistols, fake jewellery, coins, card board cut-outs, hand crafted suits of armour...

It's objects like these that can really add a degree of "authenticity" to a live game, but they can also be brought to the table for a memorable experience.

I've just written an entry in my blog about the idea, but I'd love to hear from a few story-gamers.

What sorts of props have you used? Did they help or hinder the game? Would you use them again? Do you consider yourself a "prop" player?

    \n I ran a 7th LARP about 10 years ago at my local convention. Underground tomb left by previous visitors to the planet kind of stuff. If you've not read or played 7th Sea, it has some alien tech in it.

    Anyway. My buddy Mike and I would hit thrift stores all the time and pick up anything "weird" we could find as a prop. There were something like 40-50 props in the room. All little things the PCs could touch and interact with. And eventually they had to figure out how to operate everything so they could escape the tomb. Well. In the end, it would only release one person, but they didn't know that.

    I think Patrick Kapera (crafty games) can top this story, though. He ran a Cthulhu game in his house. And everything in his house (including his car keys, which led to his Jeep which had documents in it pertinent to the game) was "LIVE" and part of the story. He even (I shit you not) tied up his girlfriend and left her in the closet. The other player did not find her for over an hour.

    Love the Lacuna phone idea.

    \n As per the blog entry which started this thread idea...\n
    The most impressive prop I ever used in a game was at a convention held on the Kensington campus of the University of New South Wales, it was the mid to late 1990s. We had an unused 12 story building at our disposal, with working elevators. So we made the elevators into a system of portals and defined each level of the building as a different alternate reality. The majority of the game occurred on the ground floor (with 20 odd players), while certain groups would be sent up and down the building when they went off to explore other parts of the setting. It became easy to determine who was where in the game because different players would physically be on different floors of the building. Of course a prop of this magnitude requires a few GMs to handle properly.

    \n Lets see....

    You know how there are those "Light reflecting" puzzles in video games? Well, I took a pocket laser and a photo sensor, and built one on the table with little mirrors from compacts. All while an interesting battle is going on around them.

    In high school I had this big project to build a miniature and real canal with sand, and little fake trees. Awesome Savage Worlds battle to save people crossing the canal during a flood. At the end of every players turn we ran the garden hose for 5 seconds to see if anyone got washed away. As the sand around them got wet they took additional penalties and all that tactical combat stuff.

    In a modern game I ran back in early the earlier 2000's, when there were all kinds of weird interesting tech services. I found one that would send pre-recorded messages to a number at a specific time. So it was set up in game that players would receive a call at certain points throughout the night. The climax of the game was resolved, and they left for the evening, and then they each got another call on their cells on the way home that told them there was a big surprise.

    There were many times we would all go to dinner as a group, and would go to some big important dinner in the game as well, so we had to play in character the entire time at dinner.

    I made a "Find the pieces of the McGuffin" sort of quest for our game, and in turn made a scavenger hunt across campus.

    I built a website with hidden content and messages, with MySpace pages for NPC's all as part of a conspiracy investigation.

    \n I ran a Warhammer 2E campaign where the Sigmarites were considered not-very-friendly-at-ALL. So when the characters were about to battle their agents, and it turned out one was a wizard, I described him casting some strange incantation that sucked the metal from the nails in the floorboards, the armor his enemies were wearing, and even from some dental fillings, that created a giant Sigmarite hammer in the middle of the battle (which was inside a city hall courtyard). And BAM, I set down a giant black hammer made of Legos right next to the bad guy's mini.

    There was also a dark supers game I ran, and the resurrected Alestair Crowley was sending the heroes clues. So I went to Uwajimaya (my local Asian import grocer), bought some pigs blood, and proceeded to paint snippets and symbols on cryptic-looking newspaper clippings, and then stuffed them all in a small, brocade box (once they were dry, of course).
    I think that disturbed some people, mainly because those things stank.

    \n For a larp I wrote and ran about a secret society of cannibals based in Western Massachusetts:
    Two creepy leather bound journals with carved faces on them. Purchased from an eBay vendor (cut-faces). In the game lore one of the practices of the society was to make a book out of a victim, then all the secrets of the society were hand transcribed in blood by the book maker. The book was called, aptly, The Book of Blood and Flesh, and naturally it had mysitcal powers. One ofthese books was "alive" and one was "embalmed".

    A coyote skull. A deceased NPC was a werewolf. It was his skull.

    I set up a website for the society The Anthropophagy Society (sorry all you iPad/iPhone users, it's Flash based).

    Naturally I served food at the game. All vegan. One PC noted that it was a marvelous thing to have human flesh shaped like vegetables.

    \n Dunno if this counts as a prop or not but I think the thing I got the best response for as a GM was diegetic music. (Music that was actually being produced by characters in the game.)

    In a Necessary Evil campaign I was running, one the PCs (most of whom were supervillains) had begun kidnapping citizens, bringing them back to the hideout, tying them up and dressing them as the Village People. He still had two to go.

    A short time later, after the PCs had been away, they returned to find their hideout had been taken over by the Zombie Master, a supervillain NPC who had the power to animate and control the dead using music. For the most part, any music he had playing would do, but most of the time it was music being played by his undead jazz band. (Yes, I was going for something cheesy and over-the-top.)

    I put in a CD with a jazz mix I'd put together for the session and told them this was the music they could hear the band playing as they fought their undead minions.

    Then, when the first round of zombies was down, I stopped the CD and had the Zombie Master say, "I hope you don't mind, but I took the liberty of completing your set." Then he pressed play on the boom box one of his minions was carrying. I put in the second CD, which was a mix of the Village People, at which point five more zombies (three of whom had been the PC's kidnapping victims) emerged, dressed as the Village People.

    So, yes. I made my players fight undead Village People. While listening to the Village People.

    \n The most impressive prop I've seen used at a tabletop game:

    We were playing a Cthulhu game set in the 60s. Our characters were going to a concert. The GM gave us concert passes to wear with funky pictures on them.

    Naturally, weird stuff started happening.

    Then, about halfway through the game, the GM describes a funky mist creature appearing on the road. "It looks vaguely humanoid, but with shapes trailing off from the appendages. In fact, it looks kind of like that." And he points to one of the guys' badges.

    At that point, we looked at all the different pictures. Some of them looked like things that had already happened. Others were clues about upcoming things and how to deal with them.

    We'd been wearing clues the whole time! It was awesome!

    \n Probably the video of a dead dictator giving a speech that got played on a loop throughout one dystopian, Orwellian larp. I went online, and recruited Remi Treuer and Jason Morningstar to play the dictator and a government propaganda news reporter. Then the dictator's speech played continuously in several areas throughout the game area, so even though he was dead the dictator felt like an overbearing, omnipresent Big Brother type NPC. And partway through, you started to hear the dictator's speech patterns work their way into the speech of the players.

    The soviet-style propaganda news report, meanwhile, played on a bank of old TVs near the snack table. I borrowed a signal spitter from work, so all the TVs showed the same thing constantly. And I replaced every framed photograph in my house with pictures of the dictator, sometimes with propaganda slogans alongside. This was several years ago, and I still am finding pictures of the dictator staring at me.



    (Another larp had a lot of success using QR codes and a cellphone barcode scanner to replicate a sonic screwdriver/tricorder type device.)

    \n At the boffer larp I participated in for a near decade, the small group of folks I wrote with was affectionately known as the "gore squad" because of our love of introducing splatterpunk to an otherwise rather staid adventure fantasy.

    On of my greatest props was the final encounter of a module where the players approached the throne of a hugely morbidly obese necromancer (yards and yards of vinyl, several wigs, about a quart of latex, 1 actor), who parlayed with them for a little while as he ate disgusting greasy snacks. They were interrogating him about where a missing girl had ended up. At the end of the scene the girl burst forth from his belly, covered in slime and viscera, and led then led the party back to town thankful they had saved her from her prison.

    I repeated the trick 4 times for 4 different groups that night. One guy almost threw up.
