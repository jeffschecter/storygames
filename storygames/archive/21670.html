On a similar note, I'm toying with the idea of Otherkinding D&D (its because I really want to run D&Dish looking things but I'm always dissatisfied with my options - 5E, OSR & DW included).

So you roll a couple of d20 every turn and distribute it to:

Typical d20 uses in a turn
- Initiative (depends on edition)
- Attack/Skill check

- Enemy rolls

    \n In a hindsight 'Fortune in the Middle D&D' would be a better title.

    Based in @Rafu idea
    1. Every turn roll 4d6.
    2. Distribute as many dice to your current action as you want.
    3. Sum vs DC: success or fail.
    4. Distribute these used dice to
    - Make effect (damage, healing, etc.)
    - Raise your initiative for the next round
    - Decide narration rights (1-2: GM, 3-4: other participants POV!, 5-6: you)
    5. The dice you didnt use for you action could be 'burnt' for various side effects (small compensations for shitty rolls).
    - Mayhem: every die you burn this way causes 1 point of damage for someone.
    - Reorganize: every dice you burn gives you back 1 HP.
    6. Unused dice goes to your pool, which you can use later to raise
    - your passive defense / saving values
    - your further actions (this function accelerates the fight in later rounds!)

    \n Ah! You just reminded me of the Dark Souls RPG (2017, Katou Hironori/Group SNE) whose combat system is basically what you described, though with more granular numbers and mechanics inspired by the video game.

    Each turn you roll up to 5 stamina dice. Weapons have a cost to attack that varies with the weapon (lighter weapons cost less, deal less damage; heavier weapons more, and making multiple attacks usually costs n+1 for each additional attack, so lighter weapons are also easier to chain attacks with). Everything else you just burn a die of any value for things like using an item, swapping equipment, or changing your positioning in the fight. Shields and weapons also have set costs to block attacks with, and dodging has costs that vary depending on your encumbrance level. Various character special abilities also have associated costs, a lot can only be triggered by spending a pair of dice that rolled the same number for example.

    Probably too detailed and fiddly for what you were going for, but the variation in weapon costs and their effects on encumbrance might appeal to people who like the "twelve different kinds of minorly differentiated polearms" part of D&D :P

    \n The crunch level is limited only by your imagination.

    Roll 3d20 when you attack. Assign 1d20 to your attack roll, then add Strength to it. Assign 1d20 to your defensive roll, then add Armor Class to it. Assign 1d20 to your aim roll, then add (whatever ability score feels best, maybe Intelligence). Add more or less rolls according to taste.

    Change these up based on the style of thing you're doing. Ranged attacks might use Dex, AC, Wisdom.

    There are a whole lot of interesting and fun things you could do with this. The downside of Otherkind dice being d6s is that it doesn't offer a lot of room for granularity (this is a boon and bane), but the d20 does.

    \n There are a few problems with that.

    First of all, play will come down to people doing the math for each action, and seeing which dice can most effectively be used there. Maybe your 14 isn't enough to hit the enemy, but it's just high enough for your defense roll, for instance, so the choice is clear.

    That's a very slow process, and not particularly fruitful, in my opinion. It allows you to optimize your choice of dice and actions to have the best outcome in each round. ("OK, how much damage does that thing do? Can I take two more hits? Or should I use my die to defend?")

    In comparison, the kind of conflict resolution you get in Otherkind makes you choose between weighty and different, consequential resolution points. Each die assigned (in a well-designed system, anyway) transforms the situation and means something in terms of how the fiction develops next. Did some of your allies die? Did you manage to make it into the castle? Will the tribe hate you forever?

    Given the implementation described here, it's not a terribly exciting prospect, I think. But that doesn't mean it couldn't be.

    Anyway, based on what I played, when stakes are announced, player rolls 3d6 (or 3d10, or whatever), with an additional die if they\u2019re especially prepared (ie a fighter who is fighting, a thief who is thiefing), then allocate to Outcome, Risk, and Fallout (do you get what you want, do you get hurt, does a friend get hurt) with low rolls being bad, high rolls being good, and mid-rolls being a mixed blessing. Mess around with extra dice for specific conditions and *maybe* die-roll mods to handle small differences.

    \n Two things, @Paul_T.

    First, in the matter of battle as a form of conflict resolution, a more involved rolling and adjudication system may be desirable. In a combat-heavy game like D&D, the depth of the combat engine is important to the feel and focus of gameplay. In editions of True D&D (by which I mean, somewhat tongue in cheek, those older editions, retroclones, and assorted OSR games), combat is a weighty and dangerous affair. A strong mechanical focus on this allows players a greater degree of control over the flow of battle through tactical depth. Do I use my highest die for attack or defense? What if one succeeds and the other fails? Ideally, each of the rolls would have a bit more heft to them if you\u2019re using Otherkind methodology (e.g., an attack roll is almost certain to kill or seriously maim as opposed to doing a meager 1d4 damage against a hit point blob).

    Secondly, and a bit glibly, there\u2019s an expected degree of min/maxing when playing D&D. Player skill matters. I think you have to accept that with all the other conceits of D&D.
