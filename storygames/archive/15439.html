    \nDo avoids character death by not having death be the stakes.

    The biggest problem with most adventure game systems - D&D, Savage Worlds, Shadowrun, etc. - is that death is the default stakes, and it's really easy to forget to add other stakes in because you have crunchy combat systems with death as the default failure point.

    And most players don't really want death as the stakes most of the time, even when they say they do.     Those are not all the same thing.

    ~90% of the rules in all the forms of D&D are about tactical combat.

    So there are basically 2 ways to deal with any given situation - engage with the system and have a tactical combat or ignore the system and talk. Because the system only effectively covers tactical combat, it highly incentivizes interacting in that way.

    If death is really the consequence of failure, then the player is encouraged to avoid failure because failure is not fun. So you are not really incentivized to seek actual danger. You are seeking tactical combats that are weighted in your favor from the beginning. It's like a 3 year old playing a board game with their parents - they expect their parents to play at less than their maximum ability so that the kid will either win or at least be allowed to get much closer than they would if the parent were playing to slaughter them.

    You don't go into each fight with a relatively even chance of not surviving it and needing to start a new character afterward.

    In a game like Dogs in the Vineyard, for example, you go into a fight with a fairly good chance of getting some fallout. The odds are typically in your favor regarding pure survival, but you come out of it with consequences. And those consequences are a major source of the fun of the game. You are strongly incentivized to put your character in danger of facing fallout because fallout adds awesome stuff to the game.

    When I run adventure style games, I remove death as the default negative end condition of combat - for both sides. Roughly 50% or so of the defeated NPCs survive - more or less depending on how appropriate it feels for the situation. This does a couple things. For one, the story of a fight continues after it is over - I get a lot more mileage out of each encounter, as the PCs decide what to do with the survivors afterward (my current players are a pretty moral group in a pretty realistic setting, so they don't just slit everyone's throat without a second thought). And secondly, it sets the stage for what would happen if the players lost a fight - they would be captured by their opponents, and the story would continue from there. So it removes combat failure as a full stop ending.

      \nIt's not amazing at all.

      Both games encourage a relatively passive form of entertainment. The GM comes up with a storyline and then feeds it to you bit by bit. It's not purely passive by any means.       So it serves a broad range of consumers reasonably well, which is a good formula for success - if by success you mean sales.

      \n But Spiderman has some character-driven goals. He wants to impress Mary Jane. But he wants to keep his identity secret so that the bad guys don't attack his family.       Hero helps you play the fight. It's all about the how.

      Smallville helps you play what you're fighting about. It's all about the why.

      \n I come quite late to the party, but I still wanted to chime in.

      It always baffles me when someone claims to have fixed a classic game like CoC or proclaims to have found the definite incoherence in its design (as some discussion at the Forge a while ago). Because, usually, those fixes are about a problem that is not there.

      The goal of CoC is not to see your PC die or go insane. Nowhere in the books is such thing stated. The goal of the game is to investigate some horrific problem and try to solve it. Emphasis on the"try." The rules try to emulate a world that is not kind to the PCs: no metagaming currency to steer things towards any resolution, harsh combat rules, and an insanity mechanic that simulates how people goes nuts in the literary source of the game (which has nothing to do with real life, by the way). There is nothing more than that, and I think that the attempts at 'fixing the game' are misguided, as is the pretense that people is not enjoying the game very much.

      In the OP ccreitz says:\n
      Posted By: ccreitzFromDo: Pilgrims of the Flying TemplePilgrims get into trouble.
      \nGames like D&D or Vampire, where getting your character killed really does reduce your ability to contribute to the fiction (rules as written), are fundamentally broken as potential literatures, because they incentivize players to "protect" characters, "advocating" for them. D&D is a set of rules for making short, bearded Mary Sues. Vampire is a set of rules for making Mary Sues with heavy eye makeup.

      I disagree, because I don't see why games should be potential literature. Actually, I think that any game will suck at that, and if you try to force it into being literature then you will have a sucky game. Then he adds:\n
      Posted By: ccreitzAnd then tells you, in a hundred pages of rules, to make a flawless immortal. It's great that it tells you that your character will get into dangerous situations and all, but that's just color. The mechanics agree: You've only succeeded when you've taken away the DM's ability to murder your guy. There are an even half dozen problems with that.
      \nThat is a very weird reading of any edition of D&D, ever. The character succeeds by surviving the adventure in one way or other, either by defeating the threat or by fleeing, because then he can try another way of solving the problem, or just decide to go on with his life. You can never take away the DM ability to kill a PC.\n
      Posted By: RobMcDiarmid
      So there are basically 2 ways to deal with any given situation - engage with the system and have a tactical combat or ignore the system and talk. Because the system only effectively covers tactical combat, it highly incentivizes interacting in that way.

      If death is really the consequence of failure, then the player is encouraged to avoid failure because failure is not fun. So you are not really incentivized to seek actual danger. You are seeking tactical combats that are weighted in your favor from the beginning. It's like a 3 year old playing a board game with their parents - they expect their parents to play at less than their maximum ability so that the kid will either win or at least be allowed to get much closer than they would if the parent were playing to slaughter them.

      Some folks don't play like that. I know. But those are still the exceptions that prove the rule.
      \nExceptions don't prove the rule. Exceptions destroy a rule and make it invalid. And it's not "some folks", it's an awful lot of people. Part of the thrill of a D&D game is knowing when to fight, when to flee, and when to talk. Just like in real life, an skilled player learns to weigh his chances.

      Death does not need to be the default consequence of combat, not as a result of rules application, but because most people won't fight to death. The will flee or surrender when wounded or outmatched. My players don't go around with a "no prisoners" policy: combat is usually self-defense, and only to the minimum needed to fend off a threat. Sometimes they will take the initiative, but because I roll in the open and don't fudge they try to avoid combat, as they could die. No fixing of the game required. You just have to roleplay most characters as people with a normal reluctance to kill other people, unless they are hardened soldiers or utter psychopaths.\n
      Posted By: David BergThat's a sensible reading, but how has that worked out, really? I can't tell.       Since I started reading indie RPGs messageboards I've got the strong impression that many fixes on traditional games are attempts to fix sucky GMs and groups, motivated by a bunch of anecdotical evidence that amounts to "I played a shitty game of X, therefore the game needs fixing."

      It's OK not to like CoC for whatever reason, or to think there are better ways of playing a game of lovecraftian horror. There are great options out there. But to pretend that the game is not successful at what it does is baffling.

      As it happens with any other hobby, RPGs are strongly dependent on the group that plays them. But RPGs are the only hobby where, instead of just changing groups, people thinks that the very foundations of the hobby need a fix.

      \nThe difference here is that David is looking a things from an authorial/narrative control POV and you're looking at things from a character/roleplaying POV.

      It is the goal of the author to push his characters into situations where their sanity will be reduced, because that's the nature of Lovecraftian fiction. Ergo, it makes sense for the mechanics of an STG to incentivize sanity loss: It will encourage you to create stories in which your character will go insane (while, presumably, the character himself is resisting).

      Posted By: ccreitzThe first step to freeing that game from its shackles is to agree that if everyone dies or goes insane, that's actually a good and appropriate thing, way better than the alternative. There, I fixed it for you!
      \nSomething which is probably under-emphasized in the CoC rulebook -- but nonetheless essential for the game to succeed -- is that you create investigators. In other words, the primary goal of the character is to investigate. If curiosity is not a major part of the character that you create, then you will almost certainly run into problems unless the GM can find other ways to incentivize the investigation.

      Similarly, the traditional D&D adventure doesn't work if the characters don't have a strong motivation to go into the dungeon. (OD&D provides that mechanically: You want treasure. Treasure is in the dungeon. Get to it.) If you don't have that motivation, then there's no rational reason to go into a death-laden dungeon.

      Frankly, I find dissociating character goals from player goals in a traditional RPG extremely ineffective. I also think trying to take the stick of Sanity loss and turning it into a carrot ("you want to go insane!") is a mistake. It flattens the narrative structure. Better to leave the stick in place and focus on the real source of the problem: Insufficient carrots.

      \n I find something similar applies to GMing, too.
