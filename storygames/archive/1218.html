Inseparable Characters

shreyas
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Story Games

So I was thinking about some things said in the Puppetry thread, specifically John Kim's example of Mary Kuhner:

I remember in particular a story I think of Mary Kuhner's where out of game, the GM discussed with her at length various hypotheticals and how she thought her characters would react. However, when the actual game happened and he presented the situation, her character reacted completely differently than any of her predictions.

This struck me as important and in need of separate discussion; how is it that we can make characters inextricable from play? Let me try and stumble toward an answer...

It's not strictly an immersive phenomenon, as John's example is; in Sol Invictus, where I play a character Birds-of-Trinity, I have the same pattern of failed prediction; it's an IRC game, and about as non-immersive as they come. But it remains the same - I can try to write and talk about Birds, but outside of play, I can't produce correct Birds output. It isn't her. Other players in the same game (who can chime in if they choose) report similar effects.

So, there is a sort of obvious conclusion here, but to me it feels unnatural; for some reason we can't depict those characters without the presence (not necessarily participation; one-on-one scenes with just the GM or just one other character are not uncommon) of the other players. I don't have any clue what process would underly that, and that is where I am puzzled.

    As Shreyas puts it, it is, in a way, the presence of the other players. Writing up "background" for a character, for isntance, doesn't have the same quality as coming up with background details in play, does it? Why is that?

    RPG play is about communicating values back and forth, and getting instant social reinforcement for doing do. The other players smile at the creative way in which you've managed to say something about the human condition with your narration of not only the character's actions, but also of the situation in which the character exists from which the actions emerge. Everything you do creatively.

    If you do this from script, then it's, quite simply, not nearly as much of a feat. Worse, you may end up suffering from Jason's phenomenon, and not be able to think of anything creative when the time comes. Scripting isn't likely to be effective in any case, in a medium where everybody else is improvising. You're likely to be off your script after the first five minutes anyhow. And then all it's going to do is to make you do additional analysis trying to figure out if you can finagle things back onto the script, etc.

    Put another way, if you've predicted to another participant what your character would do in situation X, you've played out that situation effectively. If the other participant says, "yeah, that's cool" then the moment of transmission has come and past. Doing it again later can only be a repeat performance, the results of which we already know.

    I've actually seen some OOC play go like this:

    Player A: "OK, what would my character do in a situation like this?"
    Player B: "How about he reveals that he's secretly a transvestite?"
    Player A: "Oh, that's awesome. GM, my character does that."

    You see how the planning is the play? When this happens, players usually want to skip the narration of the scene. Because the important part, the decision of what happens vis a vis the plot, has already been made in front of all of the players. The last statement is merely getting GM affirmation that the events in question are entered into the SIS. Being that normally you'd have some narration involved, which upon not being struck down, serves the same purpose.


    This explains, too, another phenomenon:

    Player A: "OK, what would my character do in a situation like this?"
    Player B: "How about he reveals that he's secretly a transvestite?"
    Player A: "Oh, that's awesome. Buuuuut... I have the character kill yours instead."

    Call this the "Stealing Thunder" syndrome. I agree that OOC conversation is almost always a good idea, in that it helps ensure that everyone will enjoy play. But if play is about getting social rewards for value transmission, then if you simply say, "Uh, yeah, what he said" you don't get the reward, the player who made the suggestion does. At best you get the weaker, "Thanks for taking my suggestion" social reward. So, even if the player makes an awesome suggestion, players may go with something else, merely because they want some reward, and know they won't get it if they say yes to the other player's idea.

    When playing, therefore, I think that it's a good idea to give very general advice as to what sort of things you'd like to see, instead of specifics, unless the player is really, really stuck. Because otherwise you're just "planning" for the player, which has the same problems as if he planned - even worse, really.

    Mike

    I think what's been said is all good stuff. However, I come at this from another angle, one that can be an "also true" rather than a "this instead" thing.

    To my eye, talking about characters outside of play is talking about them outside of context. Roleplaying characters in actual play is talking about them very much in context. What I mean by that is this: in play, characters are surrounded by a vast landscape of details, other characters, motivations, mechanical support and reinforcement, player audience, et cetera et cetera et cetera. All of these things inform our decision-making process, and when we try and predict character behavior outside of play we're trying to make decisions without that context.

    In a similar illustrative vein, this is one of the big things about Dogs for me. Making big weighty moral decisions is easy when it's just the question and no context -- killing people is wrong. But then putting those questions in the context of desperate and hurting people turns everything on its head -- is it wrong to kill a man who's been sleeping with your wife while you raised up his kids because he's a paraplegic since the accident? The context of details completely changes the process of answering the question; for me the same applies to the in-play and out-of-play character questions. You can speculate, but until you're "there," in context, you don't know.
