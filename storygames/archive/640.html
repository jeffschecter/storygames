Give me a rocking moment at your gaming table and the mechanics that made it so if the moment is play related. Non-play related mechanical moments could include good food, a good joke or I dunno, surprise me.

If comments on one particular game are going to go any longer than, "Cool!" please take it to its own thread.

Here's mine, from last night's weekly Burning Wheel game:

Aaron's bad-ass sword-wielding elf had killed (almost) every damned opponent I'd put on the table in ONE HIT. Son of a bitch.

The Dwarven Traitor to the Troll Warlord...one hit.

Fuck that.

His brother, the Raven King of the North, was being choked, hung from the rafters by a whip that was held by He Who Rules by Whip, Sword and Spell, the biggest, baddest Orc in the campaign, an NPC listed in the back of the Burning Wheel book.

They fenced for a little while, circled, parried, let their armor do a little work for them and then, at the same moment they went for the Great Strike, the most brutal hit possible in the Burning Wheel system. When Aaron saw my script and I saw his we high-fived, knowing full well that it might mean death for his beloved character.

Great Strike allows the player to either add to the power of the hit or add to the hits ability to penetrate armor. They both went for the armor penetration.

And they both got it.

Aaron got lucky, he was just maimed, sword stuck through his chest.

He Who Rules was not so lucky, his sword arm was chopped off and the blow continued on through his chest, stuck in his sternum.

Aaron's elf, The Herald of the Dawn, collaposed in his brother's arms and his brother called for a healer. It took him four months to recover from his wounds, one iota short of death.

When Aaron's bad-ass elf, The Herald of the Dawn, took down He Who Rules with Whip, Sword and Spell, I realized right then that I need never see an elf fight an orc at my gaming table again. It will never be cooler than that for me.

I'm satisfied.

What I really liked about the fights I had in this session is that there weren't any bells and whistles. It was just mechanics driven play.

I attacked the PC's Beliefs with big ugly orcs, clean and simple. I didn't have to make the PC's fight on upright logs or set the house on fire or fill a room up with water. I just had orcs attack your beliefs. Bam! BW's Fight! mechanics did the rest for me.

Now I feel like I've got Fight! down as much as I have Duel of Wits. Next up (though not this campaign) is Range & Cover. This is prompting me to cultivate the Troll Hunt mini-scenario after school chills out.

Fun.

One of them is now running for the escape shuttle she has ready in the docking bay while under heavy fire, after having risked her life to go back and bring the people she loves with her.

    So, are we gonna let the season end without settling this once and for all? Surely not. Even if we later can't scrounge a personal scene where the other two parts of the triangle beat a decision out of our heroine, at least we, the players, are gonna find out who our fellow player loves the most before the season ends.

    Non-canonical PTA conflict resolution follows. I set up 3 different simultaneous conflicts for that player: one for each of the NPCs that were running with her to the shuttle (her father, her fiance, and her lover). At stake was the sucessful escape of each of the NPCs. As the producer, I told the player upfront I would only roll one die for each of the conflicts. As for her, she would get her one screen presence die for each of the conflicts, plus, she could divide up her limited Fan Mail and Traits/Connections dice in whatever way she saw fit between the three.

    After a few minutes of the player working things out inside her heart, this was the result we got: she left her father with only the 1 default die, her fiance got an extra die making 2, and her lesbian lover got a total of 4 dice. And finally, finally, finally dear God, now everybody at the table knows who's more important to the character/player! Woohoo!

    The story would end here, but the dice on the table still had an additional magic to work on us. We start rolling them: it's only a 50-50 chance, but the father goes through the small arms crossfire unharmed; the fiance has much better odds and makes it through too; as for the lover, who we all have just found out to be the love of the player character's life, four dice againt one roll get nothing better than a tie, and the tie-breaker roll rules in the Producer's favor... the NPC is shoot and goes down!

    The look, the expression, on the player's face was just priceless. We had an endless suply of heart wrenching moments from that series, but that one... it just topped them all! Just perfect for the season's finale. What a way to go!

    It broke my heart breaking PTA's rules, but it was so worth it. And so far, I have only gone beyond the rules again to pull a very similar stunt (with the same player, in her spotlight episode of the second season of the same series).

    And that's my story.

    From last weekend's WHFRP game.

    Our characters -- Anders (me), Jurgen (Jon) and Lisl (my wife Allison) have spotted a Beastman camp in which a bunch of humans are being held prisoner. There are tons and tons of Beastmen in it, Centigors ... and a Minotaur. We know that they're preparing for something big, and we're pretty sure they're about to sacrifice the captives. We do a bunch of Magnificent Seven stuff, we rope in various local factions to help, etc., etc.

    But in the end it comes down to it that Lisl has to sneak into the camp and free the captives. Obviously, this is impossible because the place is crawling with Beastmen. We're going to have to provide a distraction.

    (Backstory: Jurgen, Lisl and Anders grew up in the same small town when they were kids. They all left, and then returned many years later for the funeral of a friend. Ever since then, they've been companions, and their bickering and fighting is really the engine of the game. In particular, Jurgen and Anders, a civilized scholar and a grimy engineer, are always doing their two-dogs-in-a-small-room act, with Lisl as their sort of big sister.)

    So we come up with the World's Dumbest Plan. Jurgen, a fire wizard, has a spell that makes people angry, and we know that minotaurs are unstable engines of fury. We figure that we're going to sneak toward the camp, cast the spell on the minotaur, he'll go bugfuck and start smashing beastmen, the beastmen will spot us and charge out of the camp to attack, and while that's happening Lisl and her guys will sneak in the other side and free the hostages.

    Except the spell has a 12-yard range, and the camp is like 40, 50 yards from cover. We've got archers waiting in the trees, and we've got these ridiculous beastman costumes we made from the skins of a patrol that attacked us, but even so we're going to have to run like hell to make it to cover before they get us.

    Out of game, we know that Warhammer movement is fixed -- the Centigors, who are faster than we are, will simply run us down unless we can delay them. But we have to do it anyway.

    So here we are, with these Beastman skins on us (to stop their senses of smell detecting us, actually, which had happened before), about to walk out to sacrifice our lives to cause this distraction, just two guys with a blunderbuss and some magic, and Jurgen turns to Anders and says "well ... it was nice knowing you."

    And as they start to walk out, Jon says, still speaking for Jurgen, in this quiet surprised little voice, "you know, it actually was."

    Mechanically ... the constraints provided by the range, the effectiveness of the spell, our knowledge of what those centigors would do to us if they caught us. But also, I don't know, the feel of the game. Jon, Luke and I all played first edition WHFRP (we're gamers + we're British (sort of in my case) = we've played WHFRP) and we all seem to have imbued it with the same sort of slightly whacky, caper-movie kind of feel. A lot of what we do has this quality, and I think it's part of a sort of tradition associated with the game that isn't expressed in toto in any one document.

    I think a lot of older games have that, and it has a lot to do with how they're played.

    God, it's hard to tell a story about a character who's in the middle of a long-term game without either making no sense or sounding like the game is made of CHEESE. Nonetheless, I am going to try, since this was a really great moment.

    This is a birth-to-death Ars Magica one-on-one game. My character, Basilia, has had an ongoing battle with a powerful demon since she was about eight, when she became a victim of one of his plots. He can't touch her directly because she's very pious, but she's not powerful enough to actually hurt him, so they basically try to screw up each other's plans. At this point she's leading the Guernicus and is trying to get the Order to care more about demons (among other things), but Chris is playing the Order as having gotten fat, lazy and corrupt. It's fun.

    In any case, she goes to the Mount Etna covenant because she suspects that some of the magi there have been corrupted by the demon. This covenant is built on the edge of the volcano, and when she arrives, no one is there. After some investigation, she discovers a secret door down into the bowels of the volcano, where the four corrupt magi of the covenant have murdered their un-corrupted peers and are praying for the demon to rise and save them, in front of a big, black stone which Basilia thinks is the demon's altar.

    Basilia hears these magi say incriminating things, but before she can try to arrest them, the demon appears. It seems that he'd taken up residence in the pit of the volcano after being driven out of his last home. The demon and Basilia end up in a war of words. Basilia's trying to save the souls of the men, while the demon is trying to convince them that if they show their faith in him by leaping into the pit of the volcano, he'll save and protect them.

    This is where my partner got mean.

    One of the demon-worshiping magi starts shouting about how Basilia is just going to have them all executed by Tribunal for worshipping demons, and then she leaps over the edge. Basilia starts screaming at the other three to wait - but she does plan to have them tried and executed, and refuses to lie to them about it, and so two more leap off, putting their faith in the demon rather than face justice. There's just one more magus left, and he's wavering. Basilia rushes forward and struggles with him on the edge of the pit, trying to physically stop him from going to what she believes to be a fate worse than death. Of course, she's a total wuss so he easily overpowers her physically, but when he realizes that she's just risked her life to save his soul (since he could easily have tossed her into the lava) he decides to surrender and be judged by Tribunal.

    I could have had the character leave with her prize, since she knows she can't even hurt the demon, but then I thought of something HARDCORE . . . .

    Tired, wounded and furious, Basilia turns to the demon. "I may not have been able to save those men," she said, "but at least I can prevent others from falling to you." And then she destroys the big black rock that's the demon's altar with her magic sword, which was established to have big Perdo Terram effects.

    (Brief not-quite-digression: using the sword gave the act a huge amount of resonance with their previous encounters. After finding the sword, she wounded the demon with it, as it's immensely powerful - though she can't swing a sword for the life of her, which is the main reason why the blow didn't kill him. Then he attacked her apprentice, murdered her protege and stole the sword. Finally, not one week of game-time before this encounter, Basilia's son had taken up the sword to use in the service of the demon against her, and she'd lost most of her left arm in the encounter - not to mention almost killing her beloved youngest son whom the demon has corrupted. She did, however, manage to get the sword back again!)

    In any case, Basilia points the sword at the rock and speaks the command word . . . and my partner asks if HE can be HARDCORE. Of course I say yes . . .

    The rock explodes into tiny, tiny shards . . . and the demon laughs. "You think you've destroyed my altar?" he asked. "That was put there by Verditius himself to ward this place - and now you've done yourself more harm than I could ever do you." The demon disappears as the lava starts to rise, and the group starts to run out of the cavern, trying to stay ahead of the coming eruption. It was bad. But oh, so, so good!

    In the end, everyone made it out alive (mostly thanks to Basilia's hoplite physically picking her up and throwing her over his shoulder), but the covenant was destroyed, with even its aura being gone. Nonetheless, it was a fantastic, dramatic, rocking scene - and I'm so grateful that I have a play partner with whom I can run things like that, not just once in a while, but every damn time we play.

    --Jess

    PS: I apologize if this sounds like cheese. I swear, every element in that game came out of about a real-time year of play, and is grounded in concrete things that the character did and had done to her.
