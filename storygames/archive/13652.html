\n A thread for posting experiences and speculations on tempo and rhythm in role-playing games.

For the sake of discussion I've provided two definitions to base our talks on:

- Tempo = the pace by which we play the game, including how fast we set scenes, the effectivity of any resolution system, how we organize the interaction, and how we use time-shortening as a tool in the storytelling.

- Rhythm = changing the pace of the game, including changes in the use of a resolution system (or switching it), speeding up or slowing down the scene-framing, the interaction and the overall storytellling.

How have you done it?
How do you imagine it can be done?
What impact can it have on game-play?

    Posted By: David ArtmanOr are you asking why making the game feel more exciting helps drive sales?
    No, I'm wondering why higher tempo makes the game feel more exciting?

    And why don't we do high tempo more? Having "more time" is no excuse for not having more fun, or is it? I wonder if this has to do with our traditional way of playing the games? I've done a lot of development in tools and play-styles myself, both in modern and classical role-playing games, but my old rpg-group have never shared in my developments. They are too set in their ways for me to even bother trying. I suspect that this is true for most groups out there ...

    Why? Maybe due to the fact that creating a conscious rhythm in the game needs some authoritative leadership to work (at least in classical rpgs). That kind of leadership is not supported in most classical games (in spite of the use of a "game master"), and are indeed virtually impossible with the slow and cumbersome resolution systems in most of these games.

    And then it is frowned upon in most player-groups too. The players don't want to be "bullied" around, and any leadership interfering with the pace of their game-play is hard to accept as positive by players with such a mindset. I suspect that the vague idea of an egalitarian group-structure makes it hard to work with rhythmic tools in classical games.

    In more recent classical games the trend is more mixed, in my view. It may be that Dogs in The Vineyard is a step forward regarding tempo (if not rhythm). I don't know it enough to say for sure. 3:16 Carnage Amongst the Stars certainly is a step forward, in tempo, and creates some wonderfully intense game-play as a consequence. Burning Wheel and its offspring is a step back, in this respect, as I see it.

    As for modern games I do not know. Most of them seems to materialize the egalitarian group-structure we've dreamed of, by getting rid of the GM (and creating equally shared tools for influencing the game-play), and as such I would expect them to be as bad on rhythm as the classical games. But I would love to see some evidence to the contrary. In my view tools on rhythm would be the next big step forward, in fulfilling the potential of modern role-playing games. Players of modern games should be made equally able to "bully" each other into more intense game-play! ;-)

    On tempo, though, I find modern games to be much bolder than the classical ones. Most of them are played at a much higher tempo. I believe the higher tempo of modern games is much of the reason many of them feels more intense and entertaining. The higher tempo is a key to their success, in my view. The curious thing is that tempo has still not been much discussed in relation to them. Why is that?

    (my terms "classical" and "modern" pertains only to the presence of a GM, or not. Classical games got a singular GM. Modern games are organized and led in other ways)

    Posted By: jdfristromI'd be interested in some mechanics for tempo & rhythm in GM-less games...anything fit the bill?
    \nFiasco (truly GMless) and Montsegur (rotating GMish authority) both have hard limits on the number of scenes that will be played in a game. I don't know if these limits are precisely tempo mechanics, but they can definitely inform tempo.

    Combined I've played these games at cons ~6 times. Both games always fit nicely in a ~4hr con slot, and both games always produced crammed and satisfyingly eventful narratives.

    Usually this happens without my intervention, but when I'm facilitating one of those games, I do try keep an eye on the clock, and I'll periodically divide the number of scenes remaining by the available time, so that I know if we need to crack the whip or not.

    I wonder if formally imposing a time limit on the game might be a good tempo mechanic? In GPNW08's Heart of Darkness game, we meandered chaotically for three hours and then, when we discovered we had ~20 minutes to be done and be out of the hotel, we fused our brains and arrived at an unexpected burning-hot ending that wrapped up the story of every character in a deeply satisfying way.

    Rhythm management, for me, has exclusively been in the realm of technique rather than mechanics, so that's a tougher question to answer.

    Posted By: johnzoHave you looked at discussions of task resolution vs. conflict resolution? It seems like one of the primary virtues of the latter is the speedier tempo it brings to a game.
    You are right, of course. Speeding things up is considered a virtue in those discussions. \n
    Posted By: johnzoI wonder if formally imposing a time limit on the game might be a good tempo mechanic?
    I started my classical game Fabula out as a tool for GMing children, in 2 hour slots after school (back in 1995). Due to the time-frame I had to design a game with simple mechanics, to facilitate high-speed interaction. This proved to be very effective in other ways too, as it empowered the GM in ways I had not foreseen. As the years went by, and the game developed (I led it professionally from 1995, published it in 1999, and I am still on it), I continued to work mainly within this time-frame. So much of what I'm saying on tempo originated in my experiences with such an imposed time limit.

    The time frame has had other consequences too. The fast flowing mechanics supports shifts in tempo, including going into the slow "reflective pace", and this flexibility has facilitated the development of some sophisticated storytelling techniques.\n
    Posted By: johnzoRhythm management, for me, has exclusively been in the realm of technique rather than mechanics, so that's a tougher question to answer.
    My design-principles includes a simple divide of the game method into resolution/task-system (mechanics) and storytelling techniques. The techniques are mainly focused on how to lead a group of players through engaging drama, and how to accentuate their individual part in that drama. During the years I have come to lean more and more on rhythm for this, shifting the pace according to the situation, demanding players to gear up and gear down their pace of interaction in tune with developments in the drama.

    Mainly this has been:
    - gearing up tempo in conflicts
    - gearing down tempo in reflective scenes
    Nowadays it also includes:
    - gearing up tempo in towns, castles and cities
    - gearing up tempo when the characters attends a party or celebration
    - gearing down tempo in any aftermath-scene pertaining to a conflict, city or celebration (this is reflective, of course)

    In general I'm gearing up whenever the characters dive into a chaotic and densely populated social setting.

    The relatively simple shiftings between high-action scenes and the calmer reflective scenes, does much to support in-depth character-interpretation, and is wonderfully effective in building and releasing dramatic tension.

    \n My up-tempo play-mode for cities, conflicts and celebrations is strictly turn-based (each player will be focus for at least one scene, or gets to frame a scene, each turn). The scenes are often played out in seconds (in a major battle a typical scene may last for 3-7 seconds). The point is to run through lots of scenes, to simulate the chaos of human interaction in situations with a lot happening at the same time. Most scenes are dealt with outside the resolution system (although the res-system in Fabula is fast).

    The "audience time" in this play-mode is so short no one have any reason to leave the table.
    - And this is important: all players present makes all players part-take in the full effect of the play-mode; the whole group experience the chaotic nature of the situation. This works to strengthen the PvP-interaction.
    - Equally important: when we go around the table again and again, the scenes start to feed into each other, and makes the social weave develop an interesting fibre of its own. It's like the town/celebration/tournament becoming something of its own, through the eyes of all our characters. Even though the mode allows for a lot of characters going their own ways, the players still create a shared story out of it.

    I find this play-mode to be easy to do, effective, popular amongst the players, and great fun for the GM.

    \n Actual play, up-tempo mode, barn-party (reposted from the "downtime"-thread):

    Player A (Pront): me and Gonder sits behind the barn, drinking.
    Player B (Gonder): Jihaaa! Cheers! How about Mulia, Pront; she's nice!
    A: Cheers! No, she's too prickly!
    B: Prickly!?
    A: Ja, she said no to that man from Kos, you know!
    B: Asked her to dance, did he?
    A: Ja, and she just refused!
    B: But you do like her, eh?

    GM: Cut! Next scene!

    Player C (Mulia): me and Gonder are dancing.
    Player B (Gonder): Nice of you to ask me, Mulia!
    C: Dance! Don't talk!
    B: Sorry! (is doing his best to dance well)
    GM: Roll for dancing! Both of you!
    (B rolls good, C rolls abysmal)
    GM: Continue, with rolls woven in!
    C: Sorry!
    B: No problem! Got solid toes! Humph!
    C: Sorry again!
    B: No problem! Share a pint of beer?
    C: Oh yes, thanks! (goes off the floor)

    GM: Cut! Next scene!

    Player D (Roona): me and Pront standing by the door.
    You like her?
    Player A (Pront): Well ... she is good looking, but ...
    But what?
    A: She's not very friendly.
    Looks friendly enough with Gonder, over there.
    A: What? Where?

    GM: Cut! Next scene!

    Player B (Gonder): me and Pront out back.
    B: I only bought her a beer to get her off the floor! She dance like a mad bull!
    Player A (Pront): Oh, ok!
    B: (indicates the scene is over with his hands. Players may cut their own scenes)

    GM: Good! The next scene is mine!

    GM: Pront, Gonder, Mulia and Roona is out by the fire. Your father is there too, Mulia.
    GM (Mulias Pa): How dare you say no to a nice man, Mulia!
    Player C (Mulia): But I did dance with Gonder, Pa!
    Player B (Gonder): Yes, she asked me to dance, sir.
    GM (Pa): Oh, and that's ok, is it; a woman asking a man to dance! (he spits in the fire) I'm talking about you being a proper woman! You just don't say no to someone asking politely!
    Player D (Roona): (rolls her eyes)
    C: (notice Roona, but keeps her face serious) Sorry, Pa!
    GM (Pa): Good! (suddenly smiles) Now, Gonder, you seems like a nice man. Who's your father?
    ...

    - and so on.
    ___________________________

    The scenes don't tie into each other thematically like this all the time. Mostly the focus will shift from one thing to another, and a third, especially in the early stages of the play-mode. Keeping several balls in the air is easy, and fun, and one of the reasons I invented this particular play-mode.

    The key to it is building speed; as a GM you must be ready to cut a scene, and start the next, with no words to "fill the gaps", just: "Cut! Next scene!" (Cut & Go). It has modifications for different situations, but essentially it works the same for all kinds of situations with lots of people interacting.

    \n I like that example, because you can really see how the frenetic, relentless push to move to the next bit adds some dynamism and interest to a situation which doesn't really carry a lot of inherent drama or conflict. What could have been a bunch of players sort of flailing around looking for some interesting direction to move in turns into a series of little virtuoso moments: here's the immediate situation, here's the question that's before your character right now, tell us your character's answer! Instead of the barn dance being four characters in search of an interesting event, it literally forces each of them to focus very intently on making a particular, specific moment interesting. Neat trick.

    Although it does make me wonder, do you ever find it useful/interesting/fun to slow down the pace and linger over a particular scene, or is it always "Cut! Next scene!" rapid-fire pacing? Other types of fiction often vary their pace and produce insane amounts of intensity by slowing a scene down, so that the emotion and the drama sink in rather than being expended in one crazy push.

    \n On calm scenes in the midst of chaos:
    Posted By: johnzoJackson Tegu and Ben Robbins are masters of this kind of scene.
    Good! How do they make these scenes? What are the techniques for initiating the scene, setting the focus, and maintaining it?

    I use voice.

    One such moment of calm came in the midst of a war-scenario some years back. It was in the first developments of the play-modes I'm talking of here, a scenario set to a civil-war in a small kingdom. The characters were soldiers attacking a village in the dim light of morning, burning and killing all they could see (they were ordered to). The carnage was played out in a whirlwind of action- and anxiety-filled scenes, and then, suddenly ...

    - (I change my voice, and force myself to calmness): Private Lommen sit on his knees in the mud, utterly bloodied and weary. He has a spear, but it is not his own. He don't know where he got it. It is full of blood. In spite of the battle raging around Lommen, no one is attacking him.
    - The player was really fired up by the play mode, so he did not quite fathom the situation at first. He asked; "Eh ... what? Is no one attacking me?"
    - I responded in fiction (using a very calm voice, almost soothing): You sit there, no one attacking you, blood on your hands, dead bodies all around, and the fight still raging in all directions. It's hard to fathom, but nothing is threatening you. It's like being in a magic bubble of calm. You sag in the mud, and you notice a yellow-winged sparrow on an overturned cart, chirping ... What kind of emotion is heralded within you, by the sparrows chirping?"
    - And he answered: "I feel nothing. I am empty. There are no feelings in me. I just ... sit there."

    Our hearts all went out to the player, and his character, for the blink of an eye. And then the carnage continued!

    Posted By: TomasHVMReflective scenes is regularly used in the aftermath of the up-tempo play-mode, but are also used in-between more speedy scenes, to contrast them, and to make "the sudden calm moment in the midst of chaos" a part of the game.

    You need these reflective scenes to measure the depths of the emotional drama connected to the action.
    An important thing to note on reflective scenes, is that they don't have to stretch for ever and ever. It's the tempo within the scene that matters, not how long it is. The key to reflection is introducing a reflective mood; to breath slowly and talk calmly. As a GM you should lower your voice, let out a sigh, look the players in the eyes, and then open up the scene with soft words.
    Like this:
    - And now you sit here, in the silence of a private room in your inn, pondering the fight yesterday, and the violent death of your friend Oland. You get the food, you eat and drink, and you spill some, and you smile over the memory of Oland with a hot potato in his mouth. And you remember how he used to go over his gear the day after a major fight, oiling the leather, sharpening the sword one more time, humming to himself. But there's no humming today ...
    - You suddenly feel that someone should say something ...

    Thus you open up the imaginative field for "insignificant details", emotional consequences, and characters in serious, personal dialogue.
