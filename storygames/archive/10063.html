    \n Definitely. It works just fine here in Finland. Anybody want to multiply their audience by a magnitude, feel free to offer your game to a traditional publisher; just be careful to make sure that the game is wholly a book, and wholly understandable to the publisher's editor.

    We've had four rpg books published in this way in Finland through the last decade, I think. Not many sales numbers are available, but my understanding is that there is just one Finnish rpg that might compete with their sales through the traditional hobby distribution. Everybody else is left far behind.

    (Part of this is no doubt that Finland is such a small market that any publisher worth the name will be able to sink more books into the book trade in a month than the average indie rpg sells over its life. So it's not so much that rpgs sell well in book trade, but that they sell so little through hobby distribution. Still, rpg books fare no worse than poetry, as a local rpg writer is fond of saying.)

    \n We are maybe talking about a different kind of respect. And a different kind of bookstore. Mainstream bookstores, where quantity is king, may not be the kind of market that is really right for many indie games. In Portland (where I live) there are many, many bookstores that sell books by local publishers, small press publications, zines, mini comics,short run books and all kinds of other stuff. Most of these stores also carry comics of some kind or another (since Portland is a huge comics town) and some games of some kind. I've had some luck selling my own games to these stores. Out of the five stores I presented my games to three were very receptive and ended up carrying the books. Two reordered. Unfortunately I haven't followed up on this, since this came right at the end of my initial publisher enthusiasm, and I've been focusing on the making stuff side of what I do for the last two years instead. but there is a market in small bookstores, as well as comic shops, record stores and a ton of other businesses.

    \n Also, I think there was a lot more to comics getting taken seriously then just getting into book stores. First, there was a huge push away from the traditional comics genre of superheroes, and a real exploration and establishment of new (for comics) genres. Second, there was a whole generation that grew up reading non-superhero comics and wanted to continue reading non-superhero comics. Third, there were plenty of non-superhero books that gained mainstream (mainstream media, not comic mainstream) attention, even without making it in the bookstores.

    None of this stuff is happening in mainstream gaming. Really, we're not seeing much of it in our own community either.

    \nAnother type of respect that comic books get in the sense of being taken seriously is due to -- ta da -- the money that Hollywood can make off of 'em.     \nThis. A thousand times this.

    The first time I ever started thinking about comics as a serious medium was when I randomly ran across Julius Knipl, Real Estate Photographer at the public library. It was the first time I'd ever seen a comic that involved something other than a "good" dude in spandex beating up a "bad" dude in spandex, and the first time I ever realized that you could combine the immediacy and visualality of film with the depth and invitation to reflection of prose into something that incorporated the best aspects of both film and prose at the same time.

    Of course, none of that has anything to do with RPGs, because role-playing games aren't Works of Art. They're tools, which might be used to create Art, if you must, but aren't necessarily intended for that purpose. Instead, one should judge RPGs in the same way in which one would judge a chair, or a guitar, or bottle of wine -- that is, in terms of both their craftsmanship and their usefulness.

    Posted By: JuddMaus and Will Eisner and Manga are great but it was the ability to sell books in book stores that did it. Book stores don't care if they are selling Captain Poop and the Toilet Cadets or The Best of Snobby Brilliant Commentary about the World Around Us.
    Though there's another factor at work here: The average quality and insight of the writing in comics has seen an absolutely generational shift in the past few decades.

    Hey, I loved the Judas Contract arc in Teen Titans, and Claremont's Phoenix Saga in X-Men as much as the next guy (probably more) but I've been reading through the wonderful DVD-ROM compilations of Marvel comics from those years and I will tell you that those well-crafted, carefully balanced and morally insightful story-lines were absolutely and without question the exception to the wonderfully campy rules of the time.

    The thing is, for a while now we've been seeing comics made by a generation of authors who grew up with those stand-out story-lines: They're people who have a sense that their genre can aspire to greatness, because they spent their youth treasuring those stories (and possibly because the rose-tinted lenses of memory have gently erased the junk that stood alongside them). They have a rock-hard certainty that the genre can do worthy things, and that doing less is a failure, and that really does make a palpable difference in the material that comes out. The comic companies can put out so many trade paperbacks of recent comics because there's a serious richness of material that (if not always great) consistently measures up to at least a basic level of minimum quality.

    I honestly don't know whether I think there is or should be a parallel for roleplaying games. Yes, looking at My Life with Master convinced me that game design could do worthy things ... but years before that, really good gaming experiences that I'd had convinced me that the act of play could do worthy things. And I suspect that the number of gamers who believe that actual play can be worthy and serious is vastly greater than the number who believes game design is a serious business. Honestly, for all the time I spend trying to design worth-while games, I spend (and hope to continue to spend) more time trying to make worthy experiences come out of the fleeting and ephemeral medium of actual play.

    I don't know that that's a bad thing.

      Posted By: Joshua A.C. NewmanI think pornographic RPGs might be a damned fine idea.It's funny, but I'm still serious.
      IIRC, that was one of the notions I floated in the last "how to get mainstream" or "how to get newbies" thread (of which this is yet another variation, through the analogy of comics). Simple games with simple situations, color-rich, that are primarily built to be arousing foreplay. ONE little step beyond freeform, maybe a simple resource mechanic (clothing comes to mind), and a color-rich "setting" and situation (a Bang to start the gang bang, if I may be cheeky).

      BUT... at the risk of going back to the original thread....
      I think it is safe to say that the notion being batted about ("comics taken seriously") involves these components:
      * Mainstream literary critics now take note and write commentary.
      * Intended for adult consumption, often including mature themes.
      * Available through non-specialty channels (Borders, not FLCS).

      SO... the notion is that RPGs can look at the popularization and increased respect that the medium of comics has gained and learn from it. On the surface, that seems to make some sense: look, here's the games right next to the comics in the specialty store... now, wow!, the comics are spreading to the non-specialty store... so surely we can follow.

      Well, um, not so much. As others have said, RPGs are more like board games or sporting goods or tool kits than comics. Very active, very participatory, very component- and/or accessory-rich (mostly). Now, one might say, "But they're books!" to which one might respond, "Except when they're PDFs and playing cards and miniatures and pounds of polyhedrons and require roulette wheels and..." And the debate then rages over to, "Well, some or all of that stuff shows up in Borders." To which another would reply, "Maybe some playing cards, because they go with the 500 books on playing Texas Hold'Em. Maybe even some poker chip sets. But packs of 10d10?!?" And the proponent then replies, "Look, here's the Star Wars TCG with the pop-out space ship models" and the opponent says, "Yeah, uh, STAR WARS. You could put the Rebel insignia on a dog turd and sell a couple a week in a Big Box," implying that mass mainstream popularity with a license is what encourages diverse products on that license. And so on. And so on....

      In short, this thread suffers most not from definitional uncertainty, nor from radical thread-jacking to talk about genre, nor even from equally radical thread-drift to trust issues. Rather, it suffers the oldest and simplest breakdown of debate: arguments from analogy fucking suck, because there ARE NO true analogs. RPGs won't follow the market-expansion path of comics because they are an utterly different medium serving an utterly different activity agenda, requiring oftentimes unique or specialized accessories to maximize playability or achieve the intended effect of the game system. You're better off with using computer games as an analog, if you remember when the "console market" was bulky units with one to four hard-wired games and the "PC market" was build-your-own Altairs?).

      The idea of a Major Computer Games Market was laughable, back when the only games were Pong and Adventure. But somehow they became not only "mainstream," but they now out-strip the movie industry's gross revenue, are considered a serious medium for training and exploration of ethics, and there are even some brushing at the doors of hallowed halls like Modern Art museums and galleries. Mainstream, big money, intelligentsia respect. MORE so than comics, in maybe a quarter to an eighth of the time (depending on when you want to say "comics" started). And the folks in THAT industry STILL talk about "how do we make more addictive casual games for moms and pops" or "how can we expand the game market for women and girls" and so-forth. They got the BIG, BIG bucks and entire stores dedicated to their one medium... and they still wring their hands regularly in Game Developer magazine or on Gamasuta about how to grow and be more "mainstream" or how to be... "taken seriously."

      THAT could be a worthwhile thread: What can TT RPGs learn from the meteoric rise of computer gaming and simulation, and for that industries continued push to achieve the REAL mainstream levels of things like, oh, TV shows?
