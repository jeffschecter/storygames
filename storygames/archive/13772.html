\n I have posted some notifications of this game's release on various other forums, but mostly as a kind of advertising. I do want to have a serious discussion on here about the viability of moving storytelling out of the individual RPG model and into a kind of national/state model via diplomatic/adversarial storytelling. So here goes.
\n
\nI released this game called Statecraft: a Grand Strategy Roleplaying Game as a public beta today. Free download is here: http://statecraftgsrpg.blogspot.com/2011/02/statecraft-public-beta-release.html
\n
\nIt is a 40-page RPG designed to be played on the web via forums or e-mail. I am about to start Beta testing it.
\n
\nStatecraft is my attempt to take a lot of the forum-driven faux-roleplaying that I experienced when playing grand strategy computer games online in small groups for fixed multi-hour sessions over a time interval (i.e. every Tues and Thurs from 8pm to 12pm EST). Outside of this timeframe, I found I was engaging in a ton of instant-messaging and e-mail diplomacy to maximize political positions when the game started up again. Sessions would end in the middle of huge wars, we would come back and as soon as the game started things would restructure based on the diplomacy done in the interim. Very interesting, I thought I would try to replicate it as it's own game.
\n
\nOriginally, I had retained a lot of the wargaming aspects but over time and multiple revisions a lot of that was stripped away. Now the game is down to what I consider a fairly streamlined version for easy use on the web. Obviously, there is a lot more to do before I really have a good feel for how the game will end up playing out. I am currently recruiting testing groups to start finding out how it plays, so far I am just going on conjecture based on my RPG experience and my wargaming experience.
\n
\nMy question for the Story Games community is: how do you think switching from a self-centered individual avatar to a multi-generational dynastic avatar that rules over the fate of millions of people will alter the non-mechanical aspects of the game (i.e. the story / the shared experience)?
\n
\nIn a traditional RPG design where you have a single character, you are going to be viewing actions through that lens. In Statecraft, I specifically created structures to judge the player based on the size of the ripples they make in the pond. Dramatic failure on a national scale can play out in a way that benefits the player via a reward mechanic based on being bold. If we strip away the character's insistence on the protection of the self and we see time not in the small scale of a single life but played out over generations, how do you think player behavior will change from what they are used to?
\n
\nFurthermore, the players are cast in opposition to one another by being in command of opposing nations and they gain power in the game based on their ability to pull off really amazing feats of diplomacy and skill. How will that change their storytelling and worldbuilding behaviors? Especially when we are heading into an anachronistic world that runs off the rails of history as soon as the player starts making choices.
\n
\nI feel like I am kinda heading into a brave new world here, very interested in the thoughts of others on how it will play out.

    \nAs I said in my initial post, this was designed to capture the excessive amount of diplomacy that was occuring off-screen in my PC games. My game is very much not about moving resources around, but about the interaction between players. That is why diplomacy is the longest chapter and combat is resolved with a few die rolls and generic units. I had to have rules to adjudicate combat, but they are designed to be as simple as possible so that people can focus on the real heart of the game; the interaction between people.

    \n Hi Greg,

    None of the issues you bring up (playing a dynasty, commanding feuding nations) strike me as a real problem. RPGs have done much weirder things before.

    The main questions I have are:

    -- How do you expect the narrative to really emerge in this game? Is it does as in a history textbook ("Well, I'm going to send the Spanish Armada to attack England"), depersonalized from the monarch that you are presumably playing, or is it more personal ("I call the Fleet Admiral to my chambers." "What do you want, my liege?" "I have decided that we should attack those English fools"), playing out interactions between historical persona, with the GM playing NPCs?

    -- If it's the former, it really sounds like you're playing the state in abstract (including all its resources) and not really the sovereign. That's fine and is typically what you do in Diplomacy and so on, but then I wonder if it's really best to think of it as a roleplaying game instead of a board-less strategy game. If characters are never really going to interact, if you just have these situations that you handle by rolling dice and manipulating resources, it's just a strategy game without the board, yeah?

    -- Right now, the 40 pages of dense description are hard to parse at first glance. Is it possible that you could make a quick-play guide or some cheat sheets that summarize everything in a couple of pages, especially things like: "It's my turn; what am I supposed to do?" That would make it way easier to get a sense of what you are trying to do with this game.

    \n Greg, I guess that 40 pages of 12-point is pretty terse for a lot of "mainstream" RPG writing, most "indie" games these days are much more concise and to the point, especially when it comes to rules procedures. That's what I'm used to now, which is why you text seemed a bit intimidating. It's not a problem, unless you're hoping for a particular audience for you game that isn't used to these kinds of game texts. I was hoping for something much more like the procedures for a board game, not necessarily instead of your text, but at least in addition to it:

    On your turn:
    -- Do this thing...
    a. substep 1
    b. substep 2
    c. substep 3
    -- The do this thing...
    a. substep 1
    b. substep b
    etc.

    With less explanation about why you might want to do something or how you should think about it or what it represents. Just the straight up procedures for play. This would be useful both to help understand the rules more easily and as something to consult during play rather than having to look things up constantly.

    On the other issues, I have a couple more questions:

    -- Do the GM-played NPCs then have either 1) mechanical significance (it matters whether you have a good admiral or not) or 2) their own interests, apart from the sovereign, which the GM can have them pursue? Because if they have neither then, in play, I would find it difficult to care about them because they will simply be doing what I instruct them to do. They are my fingers and toes then, so talking to them is like talking to myself, even if the GM plays them. Do you see what I mean?

    -- The danger I see in giving them complete independence is now the GM has to run the domestic politics of multiple different countries, which is a lot to manage. The stats of the sovereigns and their states certain are some assistance in this regard, but it might be interesting to consider tying certain PC character traits to specific NPCs or groups of NPCs, such that the GM can assert some fictional change in their status or relationship with the sovereign when there are mechanical changes in abilities and resources. For example, if my state gets most of its income from mineral weath, perhaps that's tied to the state's mining conglomerate, which is a monopoly with a managerial staff apppointed by the king. If there is a sudden domestic change in wealth, then the GM can bring in the NPCs from the mining conglomerate and we can try to deal with it. Better yet, maybe the GM can create some event in which the mining conglomerate, seeking their own interests, tries to take over control of the state's mineral wealth from the sovereign. Adding some domestic political issues, on top of international interactions with other states, would make the whole thing really meaty.

    -- Lacking that, the main danger I see is making rolls or taking mechanically significant actions (going to war, etc.) but then not really translating them back into the fiction so we have a sense of what's actually happening in the world, what changes are occuring, etc. Else it's just numbers changing and we can only care about that in a mechanical sense ("I'm losing the game!") rather than a fictional sense ("I'll show those mercenary bastards in the mining conglomerate!").

    \n Ah, one last thought. Trust. In these online games, trust is a huuuuge part of the tension of diplomatic negotiations.
