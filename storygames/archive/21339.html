\n In Korean Shamanism, the shaman is an integral part of the community's personal, spiritual, and cultural life. After years of apprenticeship, the younger female shamans will be tested on their ability to become vessels for the pantheon of spirits, generals, and deities that they will then evoke through dance, movement, and language. I don't want to cheapen the complexities and importance of the Korean Shamans by saying that what they're doing is role-playing, however, there are some potentially useful comparisons that can be made between the performance aspects of their craft and the performative elements of role-playing.

Before turning this into a long-form essay, I wanted to state that I believe there are unexplored concepts in role-playing games that could be extrapolated through looking at role-playing games not just as play spaces, but also as ritual and performance spaces. How can exploring the connections between magical practices, rituals, liminality, and role-playing games lead to innovations in design practices/mechanics/ ways of thinking about games?
Rpg materials
This continues to the level of the ways we think about materials. When we consider that dice have a long history of being used as divinatory tools, how some dice are hand-picked from stones in the earth and the great care that's invested in considering the materials used in ritual spaces could lead to changes in thinking about the ways materials are used in games. The Inuit belief system focuses heavily on the use of miniatures as a soul-link between the object and the thing the object represents.
Note on appropriation
Now I'm NOT saying that we should by any means APPROPRIATE or otherwise DEVALUE these cultural systems by removing them from their original contexts without proper understanding. I am saying that we consider how similar practices to role-playing are utilized throughout the world, and how that could shift our way of thinking of our own design practices.

    @Hopeless_Wanderer I definitely can see why there's some interest from people who like games in shamanism. I do want to restate however that I'm not suggesting that game designers be Shamans becuase i think that can get very very very problematic, but rather what are some potential takeaways from understanding ritual and performance that can help us understand how games can function in different and exciting ways.

    Game space as liminal space
    Game spaces are sites of ritual and liminality, obviously. There's been a lot written on the ritual spaces of DnD so I won't rehash it here. What I will do is note that a game text is a codified example of liminality. You have the original experience of what it is to play the game, and then you have the rules that shape the behavior of players and what it means to exist in that space. Within that space, you have the potential for transformations, disruptions, interventions, insight, etc. Within that liminal play space you become semi disoriented as the rituals designated by the game text are interpreted by the players.

    What I'd like to suggest we continue to think about are the kinds of interventions and disruptions that the rituals of play allow for (in addition to/beside the point of getting lost in a story, character, having fun), and how those insights/transformations etc can continue outside of and beyond the traditional circle of play (which I will here define as people sitting in a room at home).
