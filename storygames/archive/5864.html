\n I played a three person game (and I could feel the strain by having too few, mostly I had too many NPCs).

A side note, I had just moved, and couldn't find my deck of cards. So, we used a Tarot deck instead, removed all the Major Arcana and the Knights (used Pages for Jacks, mostly because one Knight was missing already). This seemed to add a bump to the ritual flavor of the oracle process. Next time, I think I would dim the lights and light a candle, just for the Oracle reading.

So here's our Oracle:
QS: A warrior-priestess of a truly bloodthirsty cult.
3C: The arrival at a way house of four unusual travelers.
10C: The marriage of a region's most beautiful girl, necessarily virgin and without blemish, to the dead stone effigy of a harvest god.
QC: The seizure of arcane powers by an arrogant and brutal wizard.

The player characters end up being the beautiful girl. Asha, who is also one of the four travellers with magical bells. And Balashi, vizier to the warrior-preistess with convincing words.

Some of the NPCs was the way house innkeeper (who wants to get Asha drunk and take advantage of her), the warrior-priestess (who wants to scar Asha to prevent the marriage), one of the other travellers (seeking to destroy the cult), and the brutal wizard (sister of Balashi, who wants her brother dead at the hands of his own fellow cult members and to steal Asha's magic).

The first conflict I feel I did wrong, although it worked out just fine. The innkeeper strides over, gives Asha a drink on the house, and proceeds to try and get her drunk to take advantage. The player lost, but we negotiated that Asha went upstairs with him where the Innkeeper passed out (but he thinks that he got all the way) and got a little drunk dropping one of her forms by a die.

The second attempt at a conflict was the Warrior-Preistess had another pretty lame Best Interest, to embarass Balashi. Balashi went along with the ridiculous costume she gave him so he would look proper in the village. I discovered it wasn't a really good conflict driver.

Anyway, we carried on. Balashi takes Asha to the temple to prepare for her wedding, the wizard mind controls some cultists to kill Balashi, and then goes to kill Asha while he's away. These go normal and good. But I'm still framing conflicts in the way I'm used to, like "here's what this conflict is about. Do you get drunk and taken advantage of? Do you notice the guy following you? Do you get murdered by four dazed looking cult members?" And so on. Let me be clear, this worked okay. We had a good time and the only problem was that I felt I didn't have quite a handle on how to engage with the rules and make the shift from scene narration to conflict more smooth. As far as I could tell, I was the only one with this problem (it might also be the players were still learning the rules to notice). It just felt like I had to wrestle a gator to get the game to play right, but I've grown used to that in my last 20 years of gaming. It felt odd to be doing it in a story game, but wasn't a huge deal.

And then I do this:

After the attempt on Asha's life, she was able to best the wizard and knock her out. A man comes in who Asha thinks is one of the temple guards. She tells him to bind up the intruder and take him some where. Balashi returns and I tell his player: "That's no temple guard." And I go, "He walks past you, and only briefly looks over you and the wizard. He heads over to the stone effigy and lifts it over his head. Hurling it to the ground, it smashes into a thousand pieces." Immediately both players respond "No he does not!" And we grab dice.

That's the moment I realize, "So that's how conflict works In A Wicked Age!"

    \n We had the same thing happen in our game, we all had trouble deciding when to roll the dice and what was a conflict. Then while watching a scene I was not in, everything kinda clicked and I said "Guys treat this like Polaris, say you do stuff - go for the throat and if someone dosen't like it and has the ability to stop it then they can say F you and grab the dice" After that we were all on the same page regarding conflicts and the game smoothed out and much awesome was brought. I think that this type of understanding is something that each table needs to hash out together in play, and I really wish the book was clearer on conflicts or offered more help in reaching that point - cause before that frustration and after that amazing game.

    \nYes. That's what I've come to realize. IAWA is not a conflict resolution mechanic, its a conflict domination mechanic. Its "Give me something shiny, or I'm gonna pick up these dice and hurt you again!" That's mindblowingly new stuff.

    And that is why people sometimes stumble; they're still stakes setting. They "win" the die roll, but they haven't gotten what they "wanted." Injury/Exhaustion is generally not only a punishment for the loser, its a dissappointment concession for the winner. Unless of course they just wanted to hurt you in the first place, and yes Colin, I'm looking at you

    EDIT: Another thing I struggled with was why Vincent says a lethal move gives you a strong place to negotiate from. I mean, the default damage from "I chop you with my sword" is the same as "I make you eat cotton candy with your feet." Injury/exhaustion. And then it struck me, the conflict resolution is dice + negotiation. The lethal move is a signal to the opposition. You're saying "I don't intend to stop with just a little hurtin'. I ain't stopping until you're dead. You better start negotiating hard."

    \n The importance of narration in IaWA has been mentioned in a couple discussions, but I didn't start to understand until I finally understood the narration rules for dicing things out. After playing IaWA a couple times, reading the rules and Vincent's answers to questions on the Forge, I'm convinced that some key narration rules are in the text, but easy to overlook. I believe these are:

    Narration Rules
    - always roll then narrate.
    - always describe within the forms you're rolling.
    - win an answer -- describe how you gain advantage.
    - lose an answer -- describe how you prevent the action but end up at a disadvantage.
    - lose completely -- admit the Challenger's last statement.
    - win completely as an answer -- describe complete turnabout and your finishing action.

    Except for the free narration that triggers the start of dice rolling, every mention of player description in the dicing rules comes after the player rolls! This is different from most games I know, where one describes action, then backs it up with a roll. In IaWA, you roll dice, then back it up with description!

    The narrations are chained together by the context and logic of described events. The last surviving description sets up the action that must be addressed by negotiation when proposing consequences. The severity of that final action demands to be addressed, so the nastier it is, the more pressure on the loser to give more.

    At the same time, the option of taking Exhaustion or Injury puts a cap on the amount of mechanical harm that a player may choose instead of a consequence -- until they've suffered an injury or two and have a dice at zero!

    But in the end, whether we negotiate to a story consequence or just go to Injury or Exhaustion, the action description still demands an explanation of how we go from what was declared to the result we negotiated! There may be some retcon and different groups may have different standards of how much retcon is acceptable.

    Eg If my winning declaration is "I hack off your head!" but our result is you lose two die sizes and are still in the game, then we have to reconcile these. If we demand high fidelity to our winning statement, then we have to come up with some way the character can lose their head and still be in play -- ghosts or animate headless body or eldritch resurrection maybe (and this brings up questions of how much magic we want in the story.) Alternately, we may accept some retcon -- "the sword slashes across his throat, blood flies as he falls into the chasm. .... Later, we see him on a ledge, his throat not lethally cut but bloody...." (this is more in line with pulp and Robert Howard's writing.)

    This is how narration matters in IaWA dice contests.
