    \n Mike, yeah, I mean not "share alike," but "attribution only".

    Jason, Clinton's stuff is all "share alike" from my reading of the licenses on them.

    What stuns me is that gamers never get to where they realize they aren't going to commercialize their setting and decide to release it into the wild to see what happens. It says something about our psychology, I think, how we value what we create, and the lasting dreams we have for our creations, about our exploitation anxieties and the fear of losing something we never had, and about how we think about the creative commons that we never do this. If I was a setting guy I'd create one now just so I could release it "attribution only" and be the first to do it.

    \nI think that, honestly, the main jump there is one of time/effort.

    ...but that's a lot of content to just dump on the web. You can probably find thousands of homebrew campaign notes and stuff in raw text form on blogs, enworld, wikis etc from people who don't care if it's used or not (but hadn't simply thought about releasing it).

    \n Um... say what? If I have parsed your question correctly, putting a CC, fully open mark on a work is more like a "please go run with this, even to the point of selling it!" sign on it, while not doing so puts the default "it's MINE!" sign on it (\xa9). And then there's the weird middle ground of "free to extend but not copy and resell," which is how I will release GLASS. And other, similar middle grounds like the d20 license, OGL, etc.

    It's all just the last gasps of a monetized information economy, which will be utterly obsolete if we can break our energy deadlock. But that... is another thread....
