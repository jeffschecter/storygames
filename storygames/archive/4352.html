Rewarding Inaction

\n
\n
\n
\n\nTonyLB \n\n\n
\n
\n\nSeptember 2007 \n edited September 2007 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n So, I had a little mini-rant at Formulae, pacing, landmarks and conflict about how all of the landmarks of our game-stories seem to be conflicts, or some close variant. Wouldn't it be nice, I said, if we could use the rules to draw attention to stuff that isn't a conflict?
\n
\nI stand by that, but hey ... people wanted specifics. And, really, specifics are ... specific. They'd be tangential. But they're also fun, so I figured a new thread would be good.
\n
\nAnd here's me going off the deep end here: Let's go beyond landmarking stuff that's merely not a conflict. Let's talk about rewarding people for stuff in the story which is not an action.
\n
\nYes, I'm very fond of "Get two widgets and a foober if you betray your group to their death" and similar stuff. I think that it drives ... well ... action. But there's stuff you can do other than action. Sometimes the best landmark you can aim for it "Your character is sitting perfectly still, looking out a window as the rain falls."
\n
\nSetting aside which formulae need that kind of stuff (hint ... there's more than one) I'd like to wrestle with the difficulty of providing it. Do you get a widget when you first look out the window? What if we enter the scene in media res, with you already looking out the window? What if you look out the window for a long time ... do you get more widgets?
\n
\nThe above ridiculous questions are aimed to make something clear: Rewarding someone with an instantaneous "This happens" sort of reward is in the wrong category if the thing you're trying to reward is not instantaneous, but rather continuous. It's like trying to give your child a dollar every time they're blonde. Either you never give them a dollar at all, or you give them an infinite amount of money.
\n
\nSo what sort of mechanical rewards would make sense for passive, inactive conditions? Passive, inactive rewards. Check out this example: "If your character feels all alone in the world then you may spend a widget point to flip up to three foobers. If they don't feel all alone in the world then you may not."
\n
\nWould this type of setup encourage people to have whole scenes in which nothing actually happened, just to establish a state that's advantageous? I tend to think that it would. And, since I've got formulae that require such scenes, I think that's a damn fine thing.

    Calm the fuck down.

    "Being lonely" *is* an action and a conflict *in the context of a narrative medium.*

    Loneliness as a shared narrative event is a conflict. Real lonbeliness isn't. Real moral positions are often reflexive. Game mechanics that deal with morality are not. For instance, Vampire's Humanity system is actually a check to determine the existence of moral conflict in-world (if you fail, you don't care and there's no conflict), but the game mechanic ensures that it is a type of conflict in the game's narrative, no matter (and preceding the) result.

    "Begging," in the form of tagging, spending, using or performing any other almost-totally player-controlled action does not work well for these subtle narrative conflicts because the player simply asserts privilege regardless of the quality of performance. If I say I'm lonely IC and nobody else gives a shit, the loneliness mechanic was a stupid waste of time for the group. This is why appealing to the group as an audience, and basing a mechanic off of their feedback rather than individual player privilege, is in my mind a superior way to go about this.

    \n Building on the ideas you've proposed, let me suggest a mechanic.

    My brainstorm is a "track" (similar to a damage track) with let's say five boxes. Could be more, could be less, I like five. On the track, one box will be marked as the current position. Each end will be tied to an emotion. Any time your character feels one of these emotions, you may move the current position on the track any number of spaces toward that emotion. When your track is on one side of the middle, each box toward a particular emotion and away from the middle provides an incremental bonus to a related skill or roll or whatever. It also gives you a penalty to the roll related to the opposite end of the track. In the middle, you get no bonuses or penalties to either roll.

    It might look like this:

    Lonely [ ][X][ ][ ][ ] Outgoing

    For the purpose of the example, we'll say that when you're lonely, you get a bonus to mental rolls, since you have more time to think. When you feel outgoing, you get a bonus to social rolls. In this case, the character in question would get a +1 to mental rolls, and a -1 to social rolls. If the track were marked like this

    Lonely [ ][ ][ ][ ][X] Outgoing

    that would indicate a +2 to social rolls and a -2 to mental rolls.

    Of course, without some further restriction on when you can change your current position, this would allow players to choose whatever bonus they wanted based on the situation at hand. Perhaps an appropriate restriction would be that any time you narrate a demonstration of this emotion, you can change the position. I think this could be pretty cool if the players were allowed to select or create these tracks for their character, somewhat like Keys.

      \nI think I understand what you're driving at here. I've actually got something like this working in my game Gnostigmata (free for download at http://legendaryquest.com). The mechanics for what I'm talking about, though, feed directly into conflict resolution, so maybe it isn't what you're looking for. In any case, it might spur some ideas.
      \n
      \nGnostigmata is a game about the nature of reality. Specifically, it deals with the conflict between the belief systems of Catholicism and Gnosticism. It is GM-less, so the players control everything. It is also competitive, so the players are always in conflict. Each player has one attribute called "Coincidence" (or "Divine Grace" or "Fate" - depending on the character's perspective), which applies to all situations in scenes where a player's character is absent. The combination of Fate and the fact that attacks can be either physical or intellectual sometimes produce circumstances where a character is essentially emotionally attacked by happenstance.
      \n
      \nFor example, suppose it has been established that a character, let's call him "Stan", used to have a daughter, but that daughter accidentally killed herself when playing with Stan's gun. Further, suppose another player decides to use his "Coincidence" to perform an intellectual attack on Stan, who is currently in his apartment. One possibility would be for the vibration of a passing train to cause one of his daughter's red rubber balls to fall off of the closet shelf and, further, the closet door just happens to be open. The ball bounces a few times and then rolls to stop at Stan's feet. The dice are rolled, Stan loses, and consequently suffers Agony at the reminder. No characters, including Stan, did anything. Further, nothing physically threatened Stan. And yet, Stan suffers. Stan's player would, of course, be allowed to narrate Stan's reaction.
      \n
      \nIs that kind of what you're looking for? Gnostigmata is still in Beta, and doesn't quite meet my ultimate vision yet. But, this part occasionally provides some nice effects.
