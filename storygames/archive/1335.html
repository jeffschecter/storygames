    It's dangerous to fall asleep right when a new idea is introduced to my waking brain.

    So, assuming that all you need to play the game is a ring of some kind (like a carabiner) and stuff to put on that ring (like random novelty trinkets), that's a very interesting limitation. (You know me, I'm all about using the fewest, cheapest components possible.) It seems like the primary engine of play for this kind of charm-bracelet game is the collecting, trading and carrying of trinkets out in the open.

    Thinking in a completely abstract sense, a game can be based on a typical bidding system. For example:
    If you have your key chain on display, you are declaring that you would like to play the game with other people. Kind of like Button Men. Just to make sure they're an actual player, ask them "do you know what time it is?" If they answer, "It's time to play" then you know they're playing. (This is blatantly ripped off of Continuum.)

    Once per round, every player chooses a number from one to infinity. This number is called a "bid." The person who chooses the lowest bid without matching anyone else's bid wins and collects a trinket of her choice from her opponent. Anyone whose bids matched must exchange a trinket. If you're playing with just two people, then a matching bid still means you and your opponent must exchange trinkets.

    The next round, the winner gets two trinkets and matches must exchange two trinkets. In the third round, the winner gets three and matches exchange three, and so on. The number of the round is the number of trinkets that are won or exchanged.

    After the first round, a bid must be lower than than the lowest previous bid. If anyone bids 1, they automatically exchange trinkets with their opponent and end the game.

    A player is out of the game also ends when he doesn't have enough trinkets to exchange in the next round. (If you have three trinkets on your chain, but you're entering the fourth round, you're out.)
    Something like that anyway. I like the idea of a persistent alternate reality game, but it's pretty dry without a metaphor to lend spiritual weight to the system.
