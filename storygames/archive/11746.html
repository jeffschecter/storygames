    \n I am Christian. In some ways, I feel my faith runs deeper than most Christians. In other ways, it is vastly shallower. I have spent quality time with Satanists (LeVey version) atheists and pagans of various stripes, and I believe that NO single faith has the monopoly on Truth. I believe there is an absolute Truth, and the sole purpose of existence is to seek it; Not to find, but to seek. I happen to believe that the conglomerate of Christian faiths have a bit bigger chunk of the Truth than other faiths (else, why would I classify myself as one?) but that we also have a chunk of self-deception and obfuscation that's bigger than it ought to be, too.
    \n
    \nI believe that morality and faith are divorced; A belief or lack of belief in a greater power shouldn't be the reason you do the right thing. You do the right thing because it IS the right thing. My faith shapes what I feel is right and wrong, but does not dictate it. There are things I believe are not wrong that my religion says are wrong, and there are things that I believe are wrong that my religion is well known for.
    \n
    \nThis shows up in my gaming in that I rarely like to represent real-world or pseudo-real-world faiths in a bad light. My Dog was a genuine believer, compassionate, fair-minded and convinced of the rightness of his cause. He acknowledged that the Faith was imperfect, but believed it was the best thing going. Hence, when he passed judgment, he did it without doubt, but not always without regret. When a sorcerer was to be executed, he spent the night prior praying with the sorcerer, trying to help her get right with the King before she died.
    \n
    \nIn my younger years, I was uncomfortable playing devotees of fictional deities. Hence, I used the loophole in the D&D; text that said a Paladin could be devoted to an ideal, rather than a deity. In time, this reluctance faded, but I still tend to play good guys completely straight.
    \n
    \nOn the flip side, I like to explore the darker side of things, usually in solo ventures like video games et al. These are things I couldn't, wouldn't, ever do in real life because of what I believe, so I indulge in fictional context. I do those wrong things. I explore what it is to be dismissive or doubtful of greater powers. I find, however, that while I can play an actively evil character, I cannot recall ever playing a character devoted to an evil deity or devil.

    \n I was raised in a vaguely Christian household, went through a typical adolescent "Ha ha, Mom & Dad, I don't believe in God!" phase, then became a devout Christian in my late teens and early 20s. Went through a major spiritual crisis and ended up a secular humanist.

    I try not to be one of those tedious jerks who thinks that just because they've quit something it's now their sworn duty to viciously attack anyone who still does that thing, but get a couple beers in me and talk to me about religion and phrases like "anti-human, life-hating slave cult" are likely to slip out with alarming frequency. I know a number of truly decent religious people and see the positive effects that their faiths have in their lives, but I have a hard time reconciling that with my firm belief that if left unchecked, religion will eventually destroy the human race. But then, when I was a Christian, I had a hard time with "love the sinner but hate the sin".

    In my gaming, I use religion the way I use everything else: as a tool to create interesting conflict and satisfying drama. In my D&D settings, I tend to put more emphasis on mortals acting on their beliefs rather than on the direct influence of the gods. Usually the gods are distant, difficult or impossible to communicate with. I ran a heavily religious themed D&D campaign where the gods had walled themselves up in a sort of celestial prison after realizing they were hopelessly addicted to meddling in mortal affairs. A number of religions sprang up to explain what had happened to the gods; none of them agreed and none of them could prove they were right. When the PC cleric began to have mysterious visions of one of her religion's saints, she couldn't know if they were real or not, she could only act on faith. The player found it a lot more interesting than the typical D&D approach to religion, where Pelor calls you on his divine cell phone to tell you where the next dungeon is.

      I'm Jewish, with some Buddhist practice. I consider the personal pursuit of science to be a necessary counterpart to Buddhism, for reasons that are irrelevant to this discussion. My thesis project in college was a mystical allegory about a particular branch of computer science and its similarities to a particular branch of Jewish mysticism. I have a great deal of respect for atheism, as I think it helps people get away from a lot of horrible baggage and look more closely at the Universe as it exists. Jewish and Buddhist practice (broadly defined) help me look closely at the World, but I've known a lot of people who have only felt comfortable reading articles on science once they've formally rejected their religion, and if they need to reject something in order to gain the ability to explore, then awesome.

      \n

      I've played characters who were atheist (many), Gnostic Christian (A Byzantine alchemist who returned to England with a Crusader to bring civilization back), Catholic (a Jesuit vampire, embraced by Jesus Christ himself in the secret catacombs of the Vatican), an ex-Catholic Buddhist (another Jesuit who went to India to convert, wound up making a journey to the Himalayas and had his mind blown), a number of Muslims (even not counting my characters in Thou Art But a Warrior, but the most notable one was a knight caught on the edge between poet/ and warrior/leader). I've played a few games as Jewish characters, but the only one that I remember clearly was Rabbi Loew, the guy who putatively created the Golem.

      \n

      I don't, personally, have to think that a religion is valid in order to explore it fictionally. I mean, that's the point of exploration, isn't it? I don't think any of us have a strong grasp on the actual structure of the Universe, so while I'm fairly confident that some paradigms are more wrong than others, trying on a wig won't make me blond.

      \n

      That's not to say that I always portray a religion sympathetically. Not by any stretch. I think that religions are very often used as an excuse for material horrors at the hands of humans. Ultimately, after all, religion is something that people do. Their beliefs aren't at all interesting until they become evident in action*. I think that some religions have a lot to answer for as moral systems that encourage socioeconomic division and imperialism, for instance, and I might play a character who either enacts that moral system or is wrestling with the privileges it gets them.

      \n

      Curiously, it's very rare that I play a member of the dominant religion of the setting (though that might be because things are interesting according to their distinction). Sometimes, the character is just far from home, and sometimes the character is just weird.

      \n

      * This is an important feature of Judaism, which is not really concerned with what you believe at all. This gets applied to characters I play and games I design, so this might be the part of this post that actually answers the question.

My faith was at its strongest when I realized that, amidst my immersion into these other beliefs.
      \n
      \nTo tie this back into the original topic a bit further.. I am attracted to roles which have that unshakable confidence in the rightness of their beliefs. Paladins are amazing, completely aside from them being a force for righteousness, because the basic concept has them being totally assured in their faith, something I've never really had. A different facet of that same preference is the anticipation of what happens if that faith is well and truly shaken... It's the flip side to my own voyage of discovery. Where I was never confident in my faith, the shattered paladin was, and is no longer. How does that compare and contrast with me? Will his journey mirror mine, or was the damage too much to recover from?

When I play a character like that, I usually haven't decided *why* they're atheistic. That's one of the things I'd like to leave to be potentially discovered through play.
