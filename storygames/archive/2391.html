Here I am, ready to rock like a hurricane. I am all but confirmed for my application to be a Judge Nominee and would like to know if there are any questions people might have about me.

Here is the bio that I expect will soon be posted on the ENnie webste:

Hi! My name is Mark Causey. I've been roleplaying for about 11 years
now. I've bounced between traditional games (Marvel FASERIP, D&D (1st
Edition - 3.5), Vampire) to 'indie' games (Primetime Adventures, The
Shadow of Yesterday, Shab-al-Hiri Roach). I'm also into Japanese RPGs
(Tenra Bansho Zero, Alshard ff, Double X Cross).

I feel that I can provide a fresh mindset to the Awards. I will
honestly play any kind of RPG once I know what kind of fun the authors
want to provide (which is hard to ascertain, sometimes). I believe
that any game book or supplement, if written from the point of view of
the fun that occurred from actual play, can be a good purchase. My
philosophy on gaming is based on getting my group excited and having
fun, using the best system for the concept we want to play. I think I
can help award those authors and companies for their efforts based on
that philosophy.

So, ask your questions! I will try to keep up. Also, other SG Judge Nominees feel free to make any comments you want. This isn't my pulpit.

    Posted By: AndyOK, just got back from vacation and I'm trying to understand this all, so I'll drop a few questions for any interested judges:

    1) Why do you want to be a judge for the Ennies?
    My RPG reviewing is very important to me, I take a lot of pride out of it. This is a natural way to take that to the next level, allowing me to further refine my craft. It's not as cool as all the talented folk who produce the RPGs I love, but it's important to me.

    2) What do you think of the importance of the Ennies?
    I think that, like reviews, that bring attention to products. Being made more aware of a broader variety of products is a wonderful thing, and I think it helps draw folk deeper into the hobby. They generate discussion about these products, and so I think they're important and useful but I wouldn't run out and buy a game just because it won an ENnie. I would take a look at it online, however.

    3) What was the most fun you had gaming last year in a single session? Give details, and tell us briefly why.
    Probably a game of Faery's Tale I ran that ended up being a lot of fun, largely because all the players seemed to buy into the game and got into the habit of contributing their own ideas as the tale came together. Here's a link to the actual play for that session: http://cwricheson.livejournal.com/tag/actual+play

    4) About how often do you actually play games (not just read them)? What's your split of doing GMing to Playing?
    Between 5 and 10 hours a week, depending on if I'm participating in one or two games. In the last three months it's been 5, but on the whole 10 has been more common. I GM the vast majority of the time, largely because the games I most want to play tend to be less well known and more difficult to find games for.

    5) If you were like one of those "Nobilis Gods" that only controlled one aspect of the universe, and that aspect was "RPGs"/"The RPG Market"/"RPGs in General" and the like, and you had the godlike power to influence the world of RPGs (from the way they are written, to how they are received and played, even to the market in general), what would you change about RPGs or 'the market' over the next 3-5 years?
    I'd inspire more people to self publish and experiment with game mechanics. I'd inspire more consumers to buy those games. I'd make it to where instead of a few RPGs dominating the market RPG stores frequently sold lots of varied RPGs. I prefer playing new things when I can, and as much as I enjoy a good game of D&D or GURPS what I'd really like is to meet more folk that have a broad interest in RPGs.

    6) What's the best 1-3 things that have happened in the world of Roleplaying/Gaming over the past three to five years?
    It's back a bit further, but I'd put the d20 movement at 1. Not because everyone playing the same game is a great thing, but because it seems to have strongly reinvigorated the hobby and brought in a lot more people. It also showed that an open license could be successful with mainstream RPGs, a concept I hope to see again in the future. I'd put the continued rise of small press RPG publishing at 2, which has resulted in most of my favorite games. I also hope that it's the wave of the future, especially with the development of portable PDF readers. I'd probably say the successful use of licenses for 3. Buffy, Angel, Stargate, and other licenses have been handled well and have both drawn new people into the hobby and made existing hobbyists more aware of different systems and publishers.

    7) What is your favorite gaming-related accessory (could be in general like "This Product X", or even specific, like "My Lucky Pencil")?
    I have a big goblet and several treasure chests sitting on my coffee table. The goblet contains d6s, one chest contains d10s, another contains a dice mix, and the last contains playing cards, tokens, sticky notes, index cards, and other materials. With a pencil and pen jar next to all of that, I'd say my gaming table generally h as really contributed to a relaxed atmosphere where no one needs to bring anything with them and materials are always close at hand.

    Why do you want to be a judge for the Ennies?
    I have been working as a volunteer for the awards for two years, helping to professionalize our voting systems and practices.     I find the group of people who run the ENnies to be a phenomenal group of individuals to work with and find there is a spirit of camaraderie, sacrifice, service and shared fun in being part of this volunteer enterprise that one would be hard-pressed to duplicate anywhere in the non-profit sector. I would really like the chance to continue to be part of that and continue helping to guide the awards as they mature.

    What do you think of the importance of the Ennies?
    In an industry in which so many designers are quasi-volunteers, it is obviously important to create and foster institutions that recognize people for their hard work and innovation because this is not a sector that is going to produce many millionaires.

    I also think the ENnies are important because they are a kind of hybrid award that does not merely reward products for good marketing and popularity; the extensive judging and nominations process creates chances for high quality small publishers and obscure products to be introduced to a large body of gamers.
