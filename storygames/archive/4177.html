    \n So, I've played more Amber DRPG than is probably healthy, and it's greatly influenced my opinion on this. See, in the DRPG, resolution of _everything_ is handled in basically the way we're talking about social things. Need to talk your way out of something? Get talking. Need to fight someone? Start describing what you do. Sure, stats set the baseline, but clever play ultimately determines the outcome. Bottom line, you describe and you play, the GM does some hidden math, and things are resolved. It's fiat - there's a bit of sleight of hand to obscure it, but that's what it is.

    This is, to be frank, a lot of fun, but two things get revealed very much over time.

    1) Anyone more socially skilled than the GM or who groks how the GM is thinking stylistically is going to dominate play, whatever the rules and
    2) Man, it makes the shape of GM fiat crystal clear.

    Now, #1 is a real problem, but one that people are fully capable of denying. #2 is a bit more of an issue since I don't find it a _problem_ so much as something that slowly wears on me over time. Sometimes it's awesome, but after a while I want to be able to do more than game the GM. So I turn to rules. Combat, research, socializations - whatever it is, when I move to rules it is because I am looking to explicitly address the gaps I find in fiat-driven play, which are primarily in the realm of predictability and fatigue. I desire predictability in the sense that I like having some sense of the potential results of my action, and I wish to offset fatigue by not having to run every decision through a filter of how the GM is going to interpret it.

    Does this thinking distinguish between social rules and other rules? Well, no. Not really. And that's not going to be to everyone's taste - some people really want that small piece of fiat mixed in with the rest of their game. Sometimes it's a patch over a flaw in the rules, sometimes it's a reflection of what the players really wish the system was doing, but most often, it's just how people want to have fun. This is a hobby with such varied levels of social function that it could be reasonably argued that it's almost impossible to plan for every group, and GM fiat is the ideal form of customization. The arguments that are going to work at an (to stereotype horribly) Ars Magica table are not the same ones that are going to work at a FATAL table. If your group consists of polished, socialized players, then a well socialized GM can roll with the punches. If it consists of mouth breathers, then maybe a bonus for a really good fart joke is exactly what the tense negotiation needs.

    The only problem I see, and it's a bit of a catch-22, is that it assumes some uniformity of socialization. If there is a discrepancy, then the odd man out can get punished in any number of ways. The shy guy stays clammed up, the sharp guy gets laughed down, the friendly guy gets walked on and so on. This is, of course, a risk in any social group, but gaming seems to work like a force multiplier for this sort of issue.

    On a purely personal level, I don't like this. Not all my friends are eloquent, some of them are intimidated by the rules or some other element of the game, and as a _group_ we try to make sure they don't get forgotten. Rules help. If a player is uncertain and unwilling to give that big speech, but we just give them a big bonus out of sympathy, that is condescending and doesn't solve the problem. By letting them use the rules, they get bonuses that they have _earned_, which promotes comfort with the system, which in turn leads to confidence in play.

    Are these things always issues? Of course not. But this is a very important, purely social, issue for me, and to that end, I am happy to have rules for such things.

    -Rob D.

    PS - And on a less thoughtful level, I like games where people who can talk get to *kick ass*. I like a civilized world where the guy who can negotiate a deal is at least as valuable as the black belt over there. This is not a play experience everyone wants, but by god it's exactly what I'm looking for, and a game which allows that makes me very happy indeed.

    \n I think there are two important things we haven't talked about yet in this discussion, and I'm kind of hoping that Alan and other folks will stick around and throw down about those two things a couple more times after this post.

    First of all, as a preface, I'm pretty much against any game bonus that arbitrarily rewards people for elaborate talking, emphasis important. Someone will always get the shaft there, because no GM can be objective about that, period. That has nothing to do with what I'm about to type, but may distantly explain where some of it comes from.

    1.) The mere presence of social mechanics in a game doesn't obligate anyone to engage them every time two or more characters are having a disagreement. There are good times and bad times to use them.

    2.) The mere presence of social mechanics in a game doesn't obligate a player to put elements of his character "up for grabs" to change as a result of a social conflict unless it would be cool or make sense, or whatever criteria you want to insert. It *does*, however, obligate you to be clear about what's precisely at stake.

    These two things render most any example anyone could come up with to defend either side of the "pure roleplay vs. social mechanics" debate useless, because they reveal that the dichotomy is pretty much false. You can just narrate social stuff as much as you want, and you can turn to the dice where there's an impasse and both parties are open to a mechanical resolution for the result.

    In other words, if you're afraid that a social conflict failure will ruin your character for you, and that's even possible in the group you're playing with, your group is doing it wrong. If you're having a good time talking stuff out, and you use the dice because you feel obligated and it trips up your scene, your group is doing it wrong.

    Let's take a really basic example. Bob has high social skills stat-wise, and he's trying to convince John, who has recently been disgraced at court, to leave the Princess Shelly's harvest day ball because he's making Bob and himself look bad. John retorts that as his friend, Bob should join him in protesting the silly mandates of the court. The players start talking, and it becomes clear to everyone else sitting there that after five minutes of the players arguing in character, no one's going to convince anyone of the other's position.

    Now what? First, you have to find out if either player is even interested in letting their character be changed here - if not, it's dysfunctional to engage the social mechanics at this point to resolve the dispute. If they are willing, awesome - go to the dice and settle the matter once and for all.

    But, so I can finish this post, suppose neither of them is open to the possibility of change - John's got a chip on his shoulder he's interested in exploring, and Bob doesn't want the baggage of going against the court. You have two options at this point:

    1.) Call it a stalemate. Neither side gets what they want from the other, but neither has to worry about their character changing, either. Suggest that Bob will have to change tactics if he wants John gone, like get him arrested or something.

    2.) Find something relevant to the conflict that you *can* resolve with mechanics, as a guide to how you break the deadlock, showcase Bob's high Persuasion skill, and allow the scene to proceed.

    How do you do #2? By using the magic wisdom of Rob Donoghue, who once said to me, "More often than not, social conflict should revolve around a question of sincerity, rather than a question of influence. It's much more palatable to suggest a social roll to cement that a character perceives something as sincere and true, instead of dictating a course of action for that character."

    Back to our example. Bob's player goes, "Okay, clearly my dude's not going to talk John into leaving, but can I convince him beyond the shadow of a doubt that if he does this, he's taking my reputation down with his?" John's player says, "Sure - if you can get past my awesome Willpower skill, we'll say John totally believes that." Now this scene is infinitely more interesting, because John is still free to do whatever. He could totally acknowledge that he has to harm his friend to get what he wants... and do it anyway, and that's way more dramatic than it was before.

    Now, a small challenge: I assert that the last paragraph inserts way more meaning and power into the scene because it's backed by mechanics, than it would be if Bob had simply said the same fact as part of his verbal retorts to John. Because the mechanics say, "This statement is true in the fiction," whereas with just the verbal, John can claim that his character just didn't buy it at the time or whatever, and try to weasel out of all the drama in that choice with nothing to dispute him.

    Consider also that you can shortcut everything I just said by focusing purely on questions of sincerity as the motivation for using social mechanics. Don't couch it as, "Does the hot catgirl seduce my character," couch it as, "Does my character genuinely believe the hot catgirl is attracted to him for reasons that aren't purely selfish?" It's still a seduction, either way, except in the second case, it doesn't necessarily have to end with, "My character sleeps with the hot catgirl."
