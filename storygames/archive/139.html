    There have been a few people looking at this for sometime now, and the answers are a mixed bag. Before I get into my own thoughts about it, I'd like to just drop some links to things other folks are trying.

    Wizards of the Coast is doing a program to push literacy and D&D at a younger audience by doing "Adventures in the Afternoon" a program where librarians start D&D campaigns in local and school libraries. Their page is here: http://www.wizards.com/default.asp?x=dnd/library

    Chris said some cool things about this while talking about getting poor and disenfranchised kids into gaming at his blog right here: http://bankuei.blogspot.com/2006/01/loaded.html

    There are a couple of communities of teachers and educators who have been using RPGs at schools in various ways and for various reasons (Paul Elliott and Colin Chapman being among them), as well as parents playing games with their children.

    Paul Elliott's game Totem was designed to be run in a school environment: http://www.geocities.com/zozergames/totem1.html

    There is also a Yahoo group for teachers who play and use RPGs in the classroom (it is a closed group, but some of you might meet the entrance requirements, so you can check): http://uk.groups.yahoo.com/group/rpgteacher

    I also know there were several threads about someone playing Heroquest with his son -- but due to the lack of search I can't find it now. So if anyone has that linked, could you toss it up?

    Actually the socio-political environment of the average highschool is way more complicated than "cool" and "geek" and has been so fragmented across focused market groups that...

    Okay, to say it without moon speak: kids are ideal subjects for focused marketing. That can include marketing that targets them as geeks, as geek pride is a growing segment that has a lot of money to throw down. However, the game has to then be specifically targeted at geeks and not just fall into the trap of being "kinda generically geeky." You want to geek? GO ALL GEEK. SHOUT NERD UP BIATCH!

    Same deal with targeting other segments. All "kids" aren't the same, after all, any more than all adults are.

    The big issue with coming at them not through adults (as all the links I was posting above do) is that you then have to show them something cool enough that it gets past the cellphone, videogame, music glut. Kids aren't lacking for entertainment clammoring for their dollar. So if you want them to pay attention you have to give them something different, something they can't get other places.

    Of course, tying it into those other things can work too. Something like the Code of Uranis without the massive background, designed to be played via text-message while IN CLASS could have some real possibilities.

    I think the first question you need to ask is: do you want to design a game that teenagers would have fun playing? Cause I don't.

    Josh,

    Historically I think part of the reason was because people got into gaming young, then left as they got older. Thus it became an imperative in the industry to get more young folks in, because it was the only way to keep the hobby alive.

    However, as we're really starting to see a lot of games for adults now -- I mean games that adults who never were geeks nor played RPGs in their teens could legitmatly use to get into gaming -- it seems we could be moving into an era in which story-games could stop looking at that focus.

    Adventure games and such may still need ways to get to the youth market, but I don't know that Zero at the Bone or Dogs in the Vineyard need to get at teens in order to grow.

    Of course, there are still other reasons: money, market share, because teenagers can be cool sometimes, because we want to try something different, etc.
