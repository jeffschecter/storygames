\n I'm not talking about one-shots or, obviously, con games.

But increasingly, I'm considering adopting the following rule for ongoing home campaign games I start up: you may only play in my game if you are also running an ongoing game of your own, or do so frequently enough that you occupy the category of "GMs regularly."

People in this category are generally the best players with whom I have the most fun, but also the fewest problems: they're least likely to whine if they don't get their way, they're least likely to flake on game sessions, least likely to be incredibly passive as players, etc.

What do others think of this idea? I'd specifically love to find a few folks who are not in the S-G orbit but are the sort of D&D GM who'd just love to play more often but is the only one in their group willing to GM. Someone like that would probably be a really fun and appreciative player.

    \n Personally, I'm a huge fan of rulesets where the GM can be quite "hands off" from the rules, and the mechanical aspects of play are passed off to the players.

    A really simple example is The Pool - a game like this can quite literally be played without the GM interacting with the rules at all. (Particularly if you use a variation where the GM doesn't hand out 1-3 dice, as many do.) I think it frees up a GM to do what she does best.

    Alternately, why put so much pressure on yourself? Give yourself room to breathe and look things up - quite likely the players do this sometimes, too. It's hardly a game-breaker unless you're maintaining a frenzied pace.

    Of course, though, it's nice when you don't have to slow down to look things up. Pick a game which lets you do that, and/or delegate the bits you can't remember.

      \n Honestly, if it were me, I'd take the guesswork out of it and have a frank conversation with her.

      My knee-jerk reaction is to ask her, "What the bleep is your problem?"

      But that's not nice.

      From personal experience, I've never responded well to, "You do these things, and that makes you a bad player, and that's why we're giving you the boot."

      I respond much better to, "This seems to be an issue. Let's work something out so that everybody can have fun."

      In my experience, most of the time, conflicts in the game don't come from one person deliberately trying to ruin everybody's good time but from some other thing that needs to be addressed.

      With that in mind, here's how I would handle it.

      I'd give myself some time before the session starts to take her aside and say, "Hey, I need to ask you about something regarding the game. Do you mind hearing me out for a couple of minutes?"

      If she says no, I'd say, "I understand. Unfortunately, this means I can't continue to play with you because I need to be able to address things that are happening in the game."

      Then I'd thank her for her time and participation then wish her good night and a safe trip home.

      Now, if she says yes, I'd tell her, point-blank, "[Name], it seems that you're not having much fun in this campaign. I've been trying to find a middle ground between the vision I have for the game and your needs as a player, but nothing I've tried seems to be working. Do you still want to play this game, or are you looking for a different gaming experience?"

      If she says no, again, I'd thank her for her time and participation then wish her a good night and a safe trip home.

      If she says she has to think about it, I'd tell her to feel free to take this session off and come back next time (or not). If she doesn't come back, the game goes on without her. If she returns, I'd let her have her treasure and XP and not penalize her for skipping a session because taking a step back to reassess is something that makes the game fun for everyone.

      If she says yes, I'd tell her, "That's good to know because some of the things you say and do at the table make it seem like you'd rather be anywhere else."

      Then I'd describe the specific things she does, how it affects the game, and how that makes me feel. I'd focus strictly on her behavior and its effects. I would not try to tell her what she thinks and feels because I can't stand that when people act like they can read my mind. (Wanna see me get angry? Tell me how angry I am and insist that I'm angry when I tell you I'm not.)

      Then I'd ask her, "I need your help to turn this around. How can we change this?"

      If she has good ideas and offers something I can work with, great! This is the outcome I'd be waiting for. I'd want her to admit her real issue and offer options.

      Regardless of her answer, once she's said her piece, I'd ask, "May I offer a couple of suggestions?" or, "Could you do me a favor or two?"

      If she says yes, I'd say, "Having your phone/iPad/whatever out while we game can be a bit distracting. Why don't we try putting away the electronics at the table? Just for this session to see how it goes?"

      (If she needs her mobile device to keep track of the math or something like that, I'd offer her another way to do that.)

      If she refuses, I'd thank her for her time and participation then wish her a good night and a safe trip home.

      Once I got the yes about putting away the electronics, I'd thank her then say, "Another thing to try this time: if I make a ruling that seems off to you, do you mind waiting until we take a break to bring it up? And can you do it without raising your voice?"

      If she says yes, I'd thank her and say, "Let's try this. I think this will work."

      If she forgets or slides back into the habits that make the game frustrating and disheartening to run, I'd gently remind her.

      At the end of the session, I'd thank her for trying my suggestions then ask her, point-blank, "Do you think this will work in the long term?"

      If she says no or keeps doing the things you've stated you don't like, I'd thank her for her time and participation then wish her a good night and a safe trip home.

      (Noticing a pattern here?)

      \n I personally interpret the cavalcade of personages floating around our gaming lives with something similar to this ability matrix:

      MOTIVATION - some are here and already bored, others need this
      FLEXIBILITY - some have a singular vision of how Gaming Should Be, others are willing to observe and engage in a dialogue
      ERUDITION - some have a wider and deeper cultural background with interesting unique ideas, others less so
      CHARISMA - some are quiet and difficult to connect with, others are friendly and encouraging

      (Yes, the recent D&D ability discussion inspired this particular stand-up routine.)

      Being "GM-like" does not directly enter into this, except perhaps in that people who GM tend to have high MOTIVATION and ERUDITION, which makes for enjoyable people to play with. Add a reasonable amount of the other two qualities, and you've got yourself a good all-around roleplayer.

      Reflecting on Paul's post, my personal experience is that highly motivated character actor roleplayers often have a critical lack of FLEXIBILITY: they often have a preordained idea of what roleplaying is, they have difficulty engaging with game mechanics, and consequently they may get sidelined by the processes of a game that is not very similar to their ideal game (a slow-moving, atmospheric freeform with strongly GM-managed plot). This would presumably be caused by the fact that they've already got their shangrila in sight: they know what they want, so the only question is whether this particular gaming group is on the same wavelength or not. (This is not so different from the committed fantasy adventure gamer who is unwilling to entertain the idea that a rpg can be unlike D&D.)
