\n So I've gotten in good with a brand new group. It's a Meetup group, and there's close to 20 of us, so we never know how many people are showing up or who will be there or when! It's all very exciting. The problem I've run into is that games tend to drag along with only one or two people agreeing to meet together to play, and then like, the day before, five or six others decide to hop on and give the game a shot. Problem is, I don't really have much experience with games for that many people! Four has been my max, and a lot of the stuff I'm prepped to run kind of drags along with that many people.

So what's something good that works with a large group of players? I'm looking into any and all suggestions, but you're my best friend if you can show me something: with weird or quirky, yet solid rules; that doesn't resemble any kind of Lovecraftian setting (the gang is pretty stuck on their CoC, so we're covered there); and that can easily be played in a one-shot.

Help me out!

    \n I just played a game of Zombie Cinema with 6 players, with is the upper end of the 3-6 designated on the box. I was worried it would be too many, but it turned out to work beautifully. One key technique that we used: someone suggested our characters all be grouped together by a fictional purpose: in our case, a Vegas bachelor party. That way, instead of building little scenes gradually connecting the characters, they all start out together for instantly comprehensible and accessible reasons, and can all set to teasing each other, yelling and freaking out together, and so on. As the cast started to get picked off by zombies, separated, etc. the chaos settled down into more focused storytelling (and the players of the dead characters get to play the zombie threat in earnest, so there's still full engagement from everyone).

    \nThe Trouble with Rose can seat from 3 to 7 people. With 3-5 people, they get five dominoes each. With 6-7 players, you get four dominoes each. TTwR should fit your 'quirky but solid' rules requirement. And, because the current player can draw in PC and NPCs for each scene, if you don't have a big lag as the game goes from player to player around the table. Also, since the audience scores and can 'embellish' a player's scene, there's motivation for everyone to say involved each round. Finally, it's a freebie. :-)

    \n Yah, The Mountain Witch sings with 5-6 characters. I wouldn't try to fit it in one session, though. I do have a method for skipping right to the "good stuff" for conventions and whatnot, though, if you're interested.

    InSpectres is great with lots of players. I've run it for tables of 6 and 7 and it's gone well. You can play a single mission in one session, and then continue playing missions later down the line without any disruption, really. Also, it's great for a changing group, there's no real reason any particular character has to be in every game.

    I will mention my Vietnam soldiers-with-issues game carry as well, as it works just fine with 6 players (+GM), and is designed to play in a single session. It's def something you probably want to have everyone on board with playing, though.

    \n I know this is an answer to a question you didn't ask, but one useful thing to do with a large group is play multiple small games. If you have eight, play two four-player games. GMless games make this easier as an impromptu option.

    If you want to get really fancy, tie the games together and swap a few players, NPCs, or plot threads occasionally.

    \nThe Burning Wheel works well with 6+ players when the PCs are competitors or rivals. They may share a common goal that foster cooperation, but have individual goals that conflict. Burning Wheel scenarios reflecting this idea are 'the Sword' (for 4-5 players but additional characters could be added) and 'the Gift' (for 6 or more). Also in this vein is the adventure I wrote and have ran a few times, 'Quest for the Holy Grail', at the wiki or the Internet Archive. After starting the adventure by pointing the characters at the Grail, my job as GM was mostly rules referee and playing King Arthur's, and sometimes Merlin's, reaction as the PCs bring the Grail to Camelot. Grail has eight pre-generated characters, and you could add any of your favorite Arthurian figures.
