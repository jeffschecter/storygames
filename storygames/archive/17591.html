    \n I'm sorry, but Jason, you couldn't possibly be more wrong about the Elf-whip/triple-axe thing. That conflation dates back to a weapons appendix entry in the fourth edition of Muzzfidgit's Villainy, the inaccurate (and racist!) monster field guide that was mass-duplicated by Wizardstock Press (they rope in illegal immigrant mages to cast shoddy Duplication spells on their tomes, with predictably degenerating quality) and sold in every tavern this side of Netherblack Pass to zero-levels who didn't know any better, up until the release of the (still problematic but nevertheless better) fifth printing, which omits both the "elf-whip" and "triple-axe" entries for tax reasons. The term "elf-whip" more properly refers to the springsteel batons which trainee moblin-wranglers use before they're cleared for full-length mobliprods, and can be traced etymologically to the wide variety of elf comparisons used as derogatory insults which moblin-wranglers have to memorize in Year 1.5 of apprenticeship.     \n Well, see, there's a couple things going on there with the ol' six-tone.

    Originally it was a small simple hand instrument made out six nails of varying lengths sticking out from a rounded sounding box. They were about the size of an open palm, and were played by being plucked with the thumbs. You'd hold it like a prayer book and pluck 3 with one thumb and the other 3 with the opposite.

    But that version got a bad reputation, because they also served as makeshift weapons. Many's the time a sneaky assassin pretended to be a poor beggar busker to get close to a target, only to hurl his instrument into the target's throat. It was considered a particularly skillful kill if they were singing along to your playing at the time.

    But almost certainly what would have been played for a padishah would be the much larger Royal Six-Tone. This is a much larger version, built around a fancy inlaid sounding box the size of a man's torso and played with small hammers. Instead of nails, it features 6 triangular extensions, each bent unevenly so that it strikes a different tone if you hit one side or the other.

    Of course, that means that the Royal Six-Tone actually strikes 12 notes. Go figure.

    Together the 6 triangles extending from the sounding box make for a lovely star shape with uneven but cross-balanced legs. And the playing is a fascinating mixture of gong and chimes. 20 of them playing together would be quite the impressive display.

    [Edited to illustrate]


    The Six-Tone Star on the left, and the Royal Six-Tone Star on the right. Note that these are on massively different scales. The one on the left is about 6 inches across. The one on the right is about 3 feet across.

    \n Ahh, yeah Fisherman's Lament.

    It's actually two things. Certainly the origin comes from the "moving barnacle" called Fisherman's Lament. That's the reason that sailors from the Flayed Coast are kind of a stigma in other seas: In truth, seafarers fear that particular nasty ship-eater, and being a suspicious folk, they lash out unfavorably against folks from the Flayed Coast.

    Perhaps even because of that stigma, sailors from the Flayed Coast are actually paragons of their craft: Organized, hard-working, and they keep their ships meticulously clean.



    The barnacle itself can move, it doesn't root into wood like its common ancestor, but can travel slowly. They are voracious consumers and can eat through a hull if uncontrolled, but they are at the same time easily stripped and removed from a surface.

    But the totem called the "Fisherman's Lament" is simply a piece of wood (usually rotten) from a ship that sank, nailed to the door of a sailing family to ward away bad fortune at sea.
