\n So, let's say a game has (a) scenario sheets and (b) character sheets and (c1) cards and (c2) a resolution flow chart.

Would you rather see these in the book right where it tells you the steps of (a) making scenarios and (b) making characters and (c) resolving?

Or would you rather the play aids all be in the back of the book, and the steps just say "see Play Aids for (relevant play aid)"?

Or both?

    \n I can offer at least one reason; not sure how compelling it is.

    Let's say your play aids take up a sizable number of pages. Single-side-printed cards or some such. Let's also say your instructions are intended to be concise, taking up few enough pages that readers can flip through and not be daunted by the prospect of reading it all. So, sticking play aids in amongst the instructions triples the page count from first instruction to last, and now flipping through it looks daunting.

    I'm not sure there's a correct answer from an information design perspective - a lot depends on context. I think as a reader I'd generally prefer to have all the materials in the back, or segregated clearly in their own section, with clear pointers in the text. In your case, it sounds like that's way too much material to put in with your instructional text.

    Posted By: Ryan MacklinCan someone offer a compelling reason *against* both?
    \nThe only one I can think of is effort. I can think of at least two games that did both, but where the data in the "in text" table disagreed with the "at the back" table. (In one case, this was made even worse by both tables disagreeing with the game text.) Obviously, this is an avoidable editing mistake, but it does happen. And, when it does, it creates more confusion than if there had been only one table.

    And, of course, there are page count and cost issues and such, though in the PDF world this probably doesn't matter as much.

    \n How We Came To Live Here reproduces a play aid (the village map) in chunks, with stuff written on them as examples. I do sort of the same thing in Fiasco. If it aids understanding and is visually clear, diagrams can certainly work intermixed with instructional text. I'd make sure that your text is luminously clear before you add visual references. That way it becomes a decision about effective communication and accommodating different learning styles.
