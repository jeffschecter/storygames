    \n i asked Richard Garfield this question a long long time ago, a few years ago i actually got a reply!
    \n
    \ni asked about external group playtesting and what kinds of questions you should ask
    \n
    \nQuote: Richard Garfield
    \n
    \nThere are several types of playtest. Rules playtesting involves just seeing if people 'got' the rules and played properly with the given material and no designer input.
    \n
    \nMost of my playtest is more personal though - I am telling people how to play, and modifying the rules based on what I am observing. I am personally involved and have enough experience that I don't need to really ask them much, my observation is usually better than their feedback in any case. For example, when they say that the 'flavor' doesn't make sense they often are actually criticising the mechanics without realizing it.
    \n
    \nIf you are sending the game to people for this sort of feedback (often done with big games or one in which you or your personal playtest group are not the targte audience) then you will have to treat it differently, my experience here is much more limited. I would be cautious about how to interpret thier feedback though because playtesters have to be very good before they understand a lot of what you are looking for. Absolutely ask about the basics they do understand, however: how long was the game, did they make mistakes playing, were there rules they didn't get, was there a lot of time that players felt like they couldn't win, were there strategies they felt like they could follow or were they playing randomly. You can ask questions like "was it fun", and the feedback may be useful, but it will definitely require interpretation.
    \n
    \nBest Regards,
    \n
    \nRichard Garfield
    \n
    \nend quote:
    \n,
    \n
    \nIf your new to play testing asking questions can be helpful eventually you will understand by how player's are acting if the game is doing what you want it too. but even after years of play testing I have been amused by a few people's feed back after playing a game. apparently some people like games where them make confused constipated faces while playing.
    \n
    \nAs for taking notes and preparing a game to be tested i sugjest, taking a really good look at the back of a mouse guard character sheet, or what is more commonly know as a player aid. If I'm working on an rpg, board game, or card game I always make a player aid first! This is a outline of the procedure of play. It's also the very best place for yourself or players to make notes about what they liked and don't like about each part of the game. i make games that use components and i encourage my players to mark up the prototype directly.
    \n
    \ni think when people write a game that is untested with the idea that what they are writing is "final text" they become over invested in their mechanics or procedure. so i highly recommend using just a player aid during design and development. they cut to the chase and and are easy to change.

    \n Perhaps you could offer your playtesters a trade, they record (Audio) AP of the test session in exchange for credit in the published work. The audio could be from blind playtesting or just permission from your regular group to be recorded. Then you could include those audio files in an online design diary. You could then reference the audio files elsewhere and turn the design process into its own creative product. When you publish the game, you could put the audio files and design diary into a CD that would be an "extra" to go along with the book (in the same way that they include the "making of" in the bonus material for DVDs). You wouldn't have to include the CD in all of the books, but you could take it to conventions to sweeten the pot.

    Alternately, and not to derail your post, people might bet interesting in working on a generic playtester questionnaire form, that anyone could use, which asked questions like: what worked, what didn't work, what happened in your game, how long did you play, etc. After each playtest session, you could have people fill them out and post selected responses. The benefit here is that you have a defined question and answer that illustrates your point of discussion when discussing your design on the forums.

    \n
    \nI dont post too much about playtests in public forums, but when i do i guess this is the formula follow
    \n
    \nIntroduce the portion of the game play you want to discuss
    \n
    \nClearly define the problem or reasons you're interested in discussing the topic
    \n
    \nIf you have any potential solutions or are leaning in any direction about the topic say that at the end
    \n
    \nSo an example from my own game would be
    \n
    \nI'm working on an rpg where each player choses a character type, types are things like, comic relief, brigand, and hero. They determine how the players are rewarded for their role play or storytelling. so if the comic relief does something that makes everyone laugh they earn a point! stuff like that
    \n
    \nMy problem is no one wants to pick the hero character type. the game defines the hero as "a character who develops a story ark, and sacrifices to accomplish their goals,"
    \n
    \nSo can you think of a better way to describe this character type or maybe a better way to reward playing the hero archetype? I was thinking of changing the description to something like this "the hero is an epic character who suffers great setbacks and thrilling victories" but I'm not sure if this communicates how that player is supposed to develop their story.
    \n
    \nso I guess that is the way I would go about discussing play testing on forums.

    \n We have two testing methods. The first is my table, trying out the rules. We play RAW in Oder to keep the number of variables low and we record all sessions. After each session we write p major observations and change the rules. Iterate.

    The second form is external play testing and for this we are at least in part testing the rules as a delivery system, so we don't do any teaching -- we just give the game text to interested parties and ask them to report. We get a hit rate of about one in six on this...most will not respond or respond uninterestingly but you will get a few golden response. One type is the actual playtest, recorded well. The other is the awesome edit of the text, which oddly happens slightly more often.
