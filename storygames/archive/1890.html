For those who missed the original thread, go here. In short, this is a collective design thread for a fantasy d20 supplement called Permafrost. Feel free to put your two cents in.

So, here are some of the major items that the reviewers brought up, in no particular order:
1. No hint as to what the original disaster was
2. More detailed adventure ideas needed
3. Updated "back-cover blurb" needed that reflects what's really inside
4. No art
5. No map
6. No NPCs
7. No info on the cities

They said other things too, but those are the ones I care about, and the ones I want to focus on.

Here are my personal constraints:
1. I'm writing my thesis, which means I'm mostly going to be in an editing capacity here.
2. I'm poor. I have a total of $50 for artwork, which I think might best be used to have someone make a map of the Great Dark. I can probably get public domain pictures of frosty places without too much trouble.

Matt, Tad, I'll be relying a good amount on you guys for interesting setting details. As I've said, this is not my forte. The current setting info for Permafrost is in the next post down, for those who don't have a copy yet, and anyone else who has ideas is more than welcome to put in a few words (or more).

I'd like to start by "growing" things off the existing setting description. Pick one place, person, or group, and expand on it. Point out something that seems like it should naturally be there and explain why it's missing or secret. Invent NPCs to fill major leadership roles, but avoid the Elminster Problem (i.e. make sure they clearly need the PCs for something).

--Colin

P.S.: What do you get for helping out? Mostly your name in the book - see constraint #2 above. I can also hand out complimentary copies of any of my other games, and will be glad to do so.

    Twenty years ago there was a major planar accident, an epic-level screwup of such magnitude that it pushed the prime material plane out of its place in the cosmos. Ever since then the world has been getting colder and colder. After two solid years of winter, the people in the north started moving south. They went a little farther each time, hoping it would all just stop so they could live their lives. Five years ago the northerners finally traveled so far south that they ran into the people who had been moving north. The whole world is in the worst ice age you can imagine â€” the equatorâ€™s frozen and the poles are wide-open portals to the paraelemental plane of Ice. If not for magic, everyone would be dead. There are only a quarter million people left alive.

    There are three major outposts in this world still capable of supporting life. First is the Steaming Mountain, where the Magma Mages live. This active volcano is one of the few places thatâ€™s still warm enough to grow crops on a regular basis, as the ground itself produces enough heat. The mages and their protectors, the Core Seekers, are looking for a way to tap into the warmth left in the worldâ€™s core and heat everything back up. About 80,000 people live on the slopes and foothills of the Steaming Mountain.

    Next is Crystalhome, a vast domed city made of magical crystal. The wizard Laccia created this whole place on her own before traveling to the outer planes to look for help. That was six years ago. Crystalhome is run by the Adepts, a band of monks whoâ€™ve had to put their training on hold to deal with this town showing up on their doorstep. Crystalhome is about thirty miles away from the Steaming Mountain. The Planar Initiates, disciples of Laccia, make their home here as well, using the crystal to peer into the multiverse, seeking an answer. Crystalhome has about 150,000 inhabitants.

    Last is the Great Dark, the vast system of catacombs beneath the world where the cold has not yet penetrated. The core seekers delve into these underground vaults and tombs, but they do not make it their home. That right belongs to the Naga Cultists, who draw their power from allegiance to the great snake-people, and to the Servants of Death, who solemnly watch over those buried here. Both of these groups have been seen above-ground in recent years, and their influence is growing as the Great Darkâ€™s population increases. There are only 20,000 humans and demihumans there, but the population of monstrous humanoids is much larger.

    In the snowy wilderness, one can find few survivors. Those who refuse to move to the Mountain, Crystalhome, or the Dark eventually succumb to the blistering cold. Only three kinds of people can be found here: the Knights of the Snows, whose fey goddess protects them from the cold; the Iceborn Raiders, who steal from others to survive; and the Mad Hermits who survived when others perished, and have paid the price in their sanity.

    There is one thin ray of hope. Last year some people seemed to fall deathly ill, eventually entering glowing cocoons and emerging hale and healthy, with unexpected powers and different personalities. Most of them were young, none more than twenty years old. Each had a message: they were archons, sent by the gods of the Upper Planes to be reborn in living flesh, to help the people of this world. Some take this as an indication that Laccia succeeded in her quest, but others point out that the cold is still growing.

    And the existing plot seeds from the back:

    Things that Might Save The World
    1. Finding out what happened to send the world towards the paraplane of ice in the first place, and reversing it.
    2. Drawing the paraplane of magma closer.
    3. Stoking up the inexhaustible heat of the Core until the ground itself grows warm.
    4. Calling on the gods for aid (either the old ones, or an entirely new set that might care more about your world).
    5. Fighting the masters of the paraplane of ice to convince them they don't want to be near you.
    6. A massive enchantment that lets everyone adapt to the cold.
    7. Retreating into the Great Dark until the conjunction is over and the world returns to balance.
    8. Traveling back in time to prevent the original mishap.
    9. Defeating the Snowflake Queen, who is the real mastermind behind all of this.
    10. Stealing a piece of the sun to warm the planet.

    Things to Do in Social Games
    1. Keep the peace in the crowded halls of Crystalhome.
    2. Settle a growing dispute that could leave Crystalhome without food and the Steaming Mountain without any tools.
    3. Make peace with the denizens of the Great Dark.
    4. Claim the overland world for the nagas. Alternatively, infiltrate the naga cults to prevent them from taking over.
    5. Become the leader of the iceborn raiders.
    6. Use your persuasive ways to seek aid in the outer planes.
    7. Become the Snowflake Queen's consort.
    8. Seek out and cure mad hermits.
    9. Control the Crystalhome/Mountain trade route.
    10. Play a band of mad hermits who want to make everyone mad.

    Other Plot Hooks and Questions
    1. Perhaps victory is really impossible, and the only answer is an exodus to a new prime material plane.
    2. Travel north. Find dead bodies. Go on resurrection spree.
    3. Stop a mummy lord from animating all the bodies that froze.
    4. Where is Laccia and what really happened to her?
    5. Is this happening to just this prime material, or all of them?
    6. What are the dragons up to during all this?
    7. Can you tell who's going to become a reborn archon?
    8. Are the mad hermits' ice spirits organized? Do they have a plan? Are they working with/against the Snowflake Queen?
    9. Is there anyone who wants this conjunction to continue?
    10. Why do the nagas care so much about what we do?

    And Now That You've "Won"...
    1. Is the world headed towards a collision with the paraplane of magma now?
    2. Are enough plants and animals left for the world to recover?
    3. Is an invasion from the Great Dark imminent?
    4. Who's going to run the world now?
    5. What will become of the knights of the snow?
    6. Will the Steaming Mountain explode from all th extra heat the magma mages pumped into it?
    7. Will the adepts go back to being just monks, or are they more interested in the social arena now?
    8. Will the magma mages be overwhelmed with madness as the warmth makes them more powerful?
    9. How can we prevent this from happening again?
    10. How much does it cost to buy a tavern? Can I hire an old guy to sit in the corner, wear a beard, and send young adventurers on bogus quests? Man, would that be funny.

    First thoughts:
    You could probably mitigate the lack of art by creative layout tricks. The Window, for all its sins, is a very attractive pdf despite a total lack of art. You might be able to do something similar with creative use of the snowflake motif you're already using. Still - graphic design takes nontriial time and effort that may interfere with your thesis. I'd offer my own services, but its been a fair while since I used a DTP program.

    Maps I've never understood the appeal of. My instinct is to turn the bug into a feature and say that the relative location of the various places is deliberatly left unstated. In part so that GMs are free to intriduce locations wherever the plot needs them, and in part to represent the lack of knowledge of the exact shape of the world. The world's slow freezing has uprooted people from familiar lands, reshaped the geography and made exploration difficult all at once. Not conducive to accurate cartography.

    Diplomatic language may be required to properly sell that as a feature rather than a copout - I think it's doable though. I'm not sure it's necessary to completely lose the "barebones setting" aspect in the process of putting some more meat on the bones. Plus, a part of me thinks it'd just be giving in if you respond to the criticism by making Permafrost what they expected rather than what you wanted it to be.

    Random setting ideas:

    People moving into the great dark seems like an ideal excuse for conflict. I can imagine existing (prehaps formerly unknown) subterranean entities resenting the surface people spilling into their realm. This could either be distinct from the naga cultists - they would be the people that have "gone native" so to speak and turned on their fellows - or merged.

    Dragons. It seems wrong to have a D&D setting not mention dragons. What if most of the dragons ae gone, and make this a mystery of the setting. Some say they saw the permafrost coming and fled to another plane. Others that they caused the disaster and most perished in it. And so on. You could restrict dragons to whites, who are right at home in the current world, excepting oddball individuals the GM might introduce for plot purposes.

    The warmth from underground magma seems a nifty element, and I have an unreasonable love for "between a rock and a hard place". What if you personified the heat of the earth? It may provide respite from the cold of the surface, but its nature is hostile to the surface people. Say that this world has a very active core (perhaps a direct link to the elemental plane of fire/magam?), hungry rivers of magma always reaching up to the surface. Whole societies of fire-aspected creatures worshipping its divine (sentient?) power. The arctic apocalypse has sent people hunting for heat sources and they've found this ancient hostile enemy. So yeah - rock and hard place - make the geological heat not just dangerous but hostile. They're not the big bad, but they're there. Also supernatural cold+supernatural heat = being able to say "fuck you" to plausibility when you want cool magma vs ice imagery.

    I am also a big fan of complementry opposites. What if you de-personified the cold. What I mean is, try to make even the cold-based monsters like mindless forces of nature. Say that certain of the weirder, nastier cold-based monsters only made an appearance with this eternal winter and that they seem to be of it. Not sure exactly which ones - but one or two well-chosen "iconic" examples might be enough for flavour.
    One crazy idea would be if the undead are a new phenomena in this setting, directly caused by the frozen conditions. Creatures that are killed by exposure, frozen to death, or the like don't rest easy and become forces of the unnatural cold. Rimed skeletons. Corpsicle zombies. Snow wraiths. Which gives an extra justification to the "no turning undead" thing. It also opens room for fun colour text about how societies cope with this knowledge - cremating frostburnt corpses and so on. It interacts badly with the death-aspected character classes though. You could distinguish between cold-born undead and the "natural" kind I suppose, but that may require flavour backflips.

    To keep the character classes (which are the real core of the supplement) central, I'd explicitly tie them to setting elements. So maybe, for each class there's a couple of examples of how they exist in the world. For the reborn archons, for example, maybe detail a religion that believes they are evidence that the gods have died and that the archons are inhabitted by fragments of their pwoer that fell to earth. And maybe a singularly powerful reborn that's posing as a fallen angel and gathering other reborn archons to his side with his compelling rhetoric (and numerous "mortal" followers too, natch).

    This has been quite a long post so I'll stop. But a couple more random undeveloped ideas:

    *A weird, sheltered area that's been flash-frozen. Trees, people and buildings all preserved inside a layer of ice. A freaky snapshot of how the world was before this winter.
    *A nomadic people with odd customs that lived in the arctic areas of the world before everywhere froze - now they're found across the whole world. They probably think the way everyone else copes is quaint.
    *An area of fragile, snow-covered lava tubes. It's dangerous to cross because many of the tubes will crumble under bodyweight, and the thick snow makes it impossible to tell where it's safe to tread. Visuals include pillars of steam rising from where the magma has been recently exposed, flowing across the snow.

    The path was barely visible now that the snow was falling again. He hurried through the forest of dead trees, petrified and made immutable by the terrible cold. The crunch of fresh snow underfoot was the only sound to each his pained, blistering ears. Above his head, a cold bluish sun hung high in the sky and he had brief memories of running through this forest as a child, naked and laughing, enjoying the summer warmth.

    The walker wore woven vests under a mantle of animal skins, some older than he was for the use of animals for their skins was now anathema. Not when they could be used for warm milk, breeding stock and body heat. Under his many layers, beside his skin to keep them from freezing, he kept a bottle of milk and some breads with butter and cheese. In his right hand he clutched a walking staff, used to test the depth of snowdrifts and in his left, a crystal periapt. If the cold became too much, it would save him, but only once. His skin was ruddy and cracked from the bitter chill and he constantly had to brush ice from his eyes and nose. He wriggled his toes in his great boots, made from ten layers of the finest threads and wools. He was warm from his exertions.

    At last he could ignore his hunger no more and sat down on a frozen log to eat. His hands, staying inside his layered poncho, grasped the cheesy breads and he submerged his head into his clothing to eat. His fingers were cold, despite his layers but the bread was chewy and the cheese warm and soft. He thought about his home, twenty miles behind him and then his destination, the volcano of the Magma Mages. A few miles more and the great column of steam would be visible and his journey would nearly be over.

    What had brought him out of his crystal dome? What was important enough for a lone traveller to be given a crystal periapt and sent on his way? He didn't know. The Initiates had called and he had been there to answer. What could have been so important? Sated from the breads, he closed his eyes and mentally prepared himself for the next part of the journey.

    He woke with a start. His vision was misted from ice crystals on his lashes and there was a layer of snow upon his chest. He tried to stand but realised with a sickening lurch that he could no longer feel his toes. His hands flailed for his stick but it was nowhere to be seen. He wiped the snow from his eyes with his useless frozen fingers stared into the white.

    The Periapt, he thought. It was still in his hand, encased in frozen flesh and wool. He tore with his teeth at the cloth and tasted frozen meat - he was tearing the flesh from his fingers but there was no pain. Eventually he could see the periapt, crystalline and calming in his ruined hand and he concentrated upon its faceted surfaces. A glow began to spread from that hand and with it, the pain of returning sensation. He muttered a prayer to Laccia, wherever she was, as the periapt brought sensation back to frost-bitten limbs and the pain took him. His tears, freezing upon contact with the air, fell from his cheeks into the snow as he started to black out.

    A memory of sun through the trees was his last thought as the cold took him.

On the second day, purely out of compassion, they raised a hundred such stones and set them to burn eternally to provide warmth for the folk who flocked to them. During the day they summoned mighty feasts to feed the hungry but still it was not enough. Those who could not get close enough to the stones were taken by the cold. This was to be their new home. The mountain could house hundreds of thousands and keep them safe and warm. They would never again fear the cold.

Still, every now and then tales arise of Wizards whose touch will sear the flesh from your bones, whose hair is that of fire and whose eyes smoulder like eternal embers.     The Dwarves have a distorted view of the world. They do not see themselves as short, squat humanoids, but rather that the world has elongated. Humans and Elves are tall, willowy creatures and trres are dark petrified spindles.

    -=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-

    The wind blows. The water ripples. The fires flicker and dance. Other adepts sway with the freezing gale. In the meditation yard, Brother Uwen alone is unmoving.

    The scene endures for a timeless instant.

    The gong outside the monastery rings, jolting some of the monks from their meditation. Brother Kaibu walks towards the door as the others try to regain their postures and their calm. Brother Uwen remains motionless.

    Kaibu opens the door. Outside are some peasants from down the hill. He can tell at a glance that they carry their lives on their backs - their packs are stuffed full, their mule is laden down, even their children bear burdens. Live chickens peck at the ground behind them, tied together on a long string like mountain climbers.

    "Yes, honored travelers," says Kaibu, "what can I do for you?" He knows the answer. He has heard it from many others in the last year. Nonetheless, it is polite to ask.

    "Y'all the Adepts, raht?"

    "Yes, we are the Adepts of th-"

    "We gota question for yew."

    "If you are seeking lodging, I suggest the crystal villa in the valley. I understand they accept all-"

    "No, no, we came from there. It's full-up."

    Some of the monks crack an eye open and look at each other. Full? The ever-expanding crystalline town is... full? Brother Uwen remains motionless.

    "Naw, we wants you to settle a dispute fer us."

    "A dispute. Oh." Kaibu's brow furrows slightly. "Can you not ask the wizards who live there?"

    "Nope."

    "No?"

    "One'a them's gone. Other one's all busy."

    "Gone?"

    "You gonna repeat everythin' I say, baldy?"

    Kaibu is taken aback. Not by being called "baldy;" how others see his hair or lack thereof is not a concern to him. No, it is the loss of the wizard that captures his attention. This will be brought up at dinner tonight. Some of the monks come out of their stances and look towards the door, and Kaibu thinks, or perhaps before dinner. Perhaps right now.

    "Well, I cannot say that we have much experience in settling other's arguments..."

    The man's wife speaks up. "Please. We ain't got no other authority to talk to. We need yer help."

    Kaibu sighs. "Very well. I will come to your home tomorrow and speak with you about this." He learned from them where they lived - on a set of stairs in the villa, as it turned out, gods above, how did they live in such a way? - and promised his presence in the early morning. The villagers, barely satisfied, began the ever-shorter trek down the hill towards the crystalline town. Kaibu closed the door. Uwen meditated.

    Conversation began with koans, quickly turned into proverbs, and moved from there into genuine debate. Kaibu was a senior student, but a student nonetheless, and he watched and learned as the masters twisted words and ideas around. Isolation was near to sacred for these people, as was cleanliness, and going into town meant a lack of both. There was the law of Crystalhome to consider as well; the monks were not well-versed in the regulations and decrees of that place, and helping would mean taking time away from their studies to learn. Soon there would be scores of peasants clamoring for assistance of one kind or another; the situation demanded a response.

    "We are being called forth."

    Brother Uwen had spoken, and all were quiet.

    "We are becoming part of the world. We will move there and help, or we will stay here and freeze. There is no desire, there is only truth."

    And thus the adepts came to Crystalhome, where they met Laccia and learned of her love for her husband and for the world.

They decide that retreating into the Great Dark to wait it out is the best option.

      Suggested Party Composition: Having a Core Seeker and at least one underworlder (either a Naga Cultist or a Servant of Death) will be essential for navigating the Great Dark both physically and socially. You'll also need someone who can convince the upworlders that they'll be safer underground.

      Major Challenges: The overworld population is large, and finding a suitable place to put them all will almost certainly require displacing an existing group. The Nagas will be glad to help point out groups that "need displacing" from their point of view, and it will be a challenge to make a home in the Great Dark without becoming someone's thrall or making major enemies. In short, this kind of campaign is equivalent to having to clear the largest dungeon in the world for at least ten levels straight down and miles out to the side.

      Twists: There are areas suitable for habitation that are not currently occupied, but this is thanks to some of the powerful undead monsters lurking in the Dark. There are mummies, liches, and worse down here, plus vampires who haven't tasted human blood in thousands of years. Many of them have class levels: old classes, SRD classes, with powers and abilities that don't exist in the Permafrost base classes. The core rules have good examples of such monsters already fully statted out. (If not for the fact that Beholders are considered Product Identity by WotC, I would totally put a giant hive of beholder-kin in the depths. Damn you, you eleven-eyed freaks!)
