\n How do you handle a situation where a person feels uncomfortable with a certain topic, but that topic is at the crux of the game that is being played. This for the most part only happens at Cons, but occasionally I can see a situation with a home game.

For example, someone signs up for my Monsterhearts game at a convention, but says they are uncomfortable with sex in their RPGs. The sex, sexuality, and identity confusion is a major point in that game and without it, you really are not coming at the game from an authentic angle.

So when players and core game concepts come to blows, what do you do?

    \n I've seen people excuse themselves from convention games, after learning that the subject could be "edgy" to them. I've also seen games made dull and less than entertaining by people who chose to stay in, but were unable to engage with the topic. If you've ever excused yourself from a con game you didn't feel well about, then you deserve a thank you IMHO \u2014 maybe you found something else to do, maybe you didn't, but you were a grownup about it and allowed other people to have a good time as planned.

    Not that the above happens often, in my experience. Maybe with the sort of conventions I go to, everybody likes to read what the games are about before signing up? Might be because I favor very small conventions (small by non-Italian standards, at least).

    There's the potential for communication failure, though! You can't always anticipate which topics can be touchy to other people. When I write a summary or intro blurb for a convention game, I try to focus on what the game is really about, but sometimes a detail I didn't think it necessary to put forward turns out to be a serious issue trigger for a player.
    This never happened to me with a strictly The Forge-y tabletop RPG (in which case, anything not worth putting forward as "what the game is about" can usually be customized to players' tastes on the fly) but it's a very real risk with any games employing secret information, such as freeform scenarios which include detailed character backgrounds/write ups.

    For example, I co-wrote a game which a friend was supposed to run at a convention recently. I pitched it as being about summer loves as viewed from different times in one's life, and I maintain that's what it's about. But the game includes 2-3 pages of "secret" background per character (a nod to the Italian tradition of chamber larps). It was reported to me that, after picking up their characters, one player found that one defining event in her chosen character's biography was trigger-y to her, for personal reasons. In retrospect, I could maybe have seen that coming, and maybe add a list of trigger warnings to the game blurb (however slightly spoiler-y), but at the time of writing I didn't.
    Due to the inflexible design of both the game (requiring exactly 4 players plus a facilitator) and the convention itself (where everybody was already signed up for a similarly inflexibly-designed game), they had to cancel the game. I believe they absolutely did the right thing, under all those constraints. I'm just a little sorry that the scenario still lies unplaytested, but there will be other opportunities.

    All of the above strictly concerns convention games, of course. When hosting a game at my place, we would of course switch to a different game should some issue come up, making sure everybody's needs are being met.

    \n I don't game at cons usually, except with groups I already know, so I have no direct experience. However I do run community games and games at stores. I'm not a fan of majority rules nor minority exception, so instead use either dm rules, or homogeneous fragmentation (i.e. organize groups based on interest and/or ideology to prevent diversity of preference at the table).

    The result of those is that people are forced to resolve their own discomfort (since they're the only ones who can anyway). If they aren't happy with the game choice or play style they can go do something else, or find and form a group of similar minded people. I'll help facilitate as much as I can, but the bottom line is that it's not up to others to make an individual happy, and attempts to do so generally make things worse for everyone.
