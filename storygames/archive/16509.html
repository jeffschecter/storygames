\n Quite a few gamers within my social "reach" don't know the first thing about indie games and the current trends in the area. There's a group doing an awesome job introducing palyers to new games here in Poland, called Orient Express. They run games on demand, do presentations and talk, etc. Now, that's great but I'd like to take it a step further.
\n
\nSome of these people oblivious to the newest & shiniest story game developments and trends are wannabe game designers. Think "played D&D; or Warhammer, put together his own setting and crude set of game mechanics". I feel this group would benefit from a slightly different, more structured mode of exposure than Joe Gamer.
\n
\nIn short, I'm thinking of a way to show the evolution of the genre(s). A way to show the interconnections between games, trading and adoption of ideas from "generation" to "generation".
\n
\nThis would give us a list of games budding designers could explore (in sequence, if they so wish) and understand how the ideas behind them work and how they enrich the overall gamescape. I find this kind of info intriguing and very handy in trying to take things in new directions (helps to know where we're coming from as a hobby).
\n
\nHas anybody ever tried to put together such a list (or a graph)?
\n
\nIf not, any ideas on where to start? I fear I don't have a broad enough overview of what's been happening in past years to get this off the ground, so I thought I'd toss the idea out there.

    Lucas, here's what I'd do. Find some games you enjoy that you also think would be interesting or representative from a design standpoint. Identify their predecessors and identify the games they influenced (this forum could help), just so you know. Then play them with people and, if a design conversation results, share the immediate family tree.

    So for Dogs in the VIneyard, you'd tell them to check out Trollbabe, a game that strongly influenced Dogs, and one of the million games influenced by Dogs, say Cold City. Predicate this on play and keep it casual, since once you get them started people can do their own research and reach their own conclusions.

    \nThat, and asking the designer to pinpoint their influences, are the most reliable ways of tracking influence. The poor man's alternative is to have a well-read roleplayer arrange games on the basis of the appearance of obvious, discrete ideas that come into the culture at known points and then disseminate. For example, we can reliably say that Fudge influences FATE because of the considerable similarities between the systems, while we also know that TSoY is less so influenced, because there was a clearly independent 1st edition that relied on a different basis.

    Obviously influence is not a binary condition like depicted here, but it's a complex topic, so it is perhaps useful for anybody interested in rpg history in general instead of the specifics of some particular game to simplify like that.

    \n If I were you, I'd try to avoid putting together the big list of names. For the kind of people you mention, who (at most) rarely played most of the games on the list, it would be just boring and incomprehensible, anyway (and, probably, rather strengthen the border you're trying to dissolve). I'd rather start with descriptions of various techniques and design solutions: key phrases, end points, GM-fullness, stuff like that, and draw a history (including metric fuckton of examples) for each of these.
