Simple directed question for consideration. I wish to keep this separate from all settings and companies; if you wish to reference a mechanic, do so without reference to published versions.


So, what effects on play and player psychology do the following methods of dice manipulation have:

A. Roll and add (2d)
B. Roll and add (3d)
C. Roll and add (4d+)
D. Roll and select (2d through 4d)
E. Roll and select (5d+)
F. Roll and assign (2d through 4d)
G. Roll and assign (5d+)
H. Percentile roll

Are we missing any other dice mechanics?


Roll and add: Die1 + Die2 + . . . + Dien = Roll Result

Roll and select: Die1 + Die2 + . . . + Dien; player or rules "picks" xDie or Dice to = Roll Result

Roll and assign: Die1 + Die2 + . . . + Dien = Roll Result; player or rules "picks" xDie or Dice to = specific Roll Result(s)

Percentile Roll: Die1 (d10) + Die2 (d10) = Roll Result, with d10s representing the tens and the ones positions of a number representing a percentage (either 00-99 or 01-100).


EXTRA CREDIT: Do the answers for the above change with differing die types (d2, d4, d6, d8, d10, d12, d20)?

EXTRA CREDIT: Do the answers for the above change with change Roll Under to Roll Under or vice-versa?

    Agreed, agreed.

    In my experience, systems that have rolling (say) 2 to 5 dice are most often Roll and Add or Roll and Select (Single) systems, while those with rolling 5+ dice are most often Roll and Select (Successes) or Roll and Select (Sets) systems.

    That division seems to be a function of things like:
    1. It's easier for the player to Add a smaller number of dice.
    2. A high number of dice makes the player feel that he/she has a better chance of achieving the desired number for a Select System.

    Adding systems give the benefit of building (natch); roll x dice and add them up into total y. With multiple dice, even if one die is "bad" the other one(s) may be "good" and all work together.

    Select systems offer more chance to get the desired "good" total y. . . but "bad" rolls have no feed into the system (unless there are no good rolls, and/or there's a "very bad" category).

    I suspect that with Add systems, a player has a better and more solid chance at getting "something" while being easy to work with. A Select system feels riskier and is riskier, with a potentially higher payoff alongside the higher risk.

    Well, D&D's a fascinating example of why the dice mechanics _can_ matter a great deal. Because it has so many interdependencies, a simple change (like substituting 2d10 for 1d20) has large and frequently unforeseen consequences. Mechanically, it still _works_, but by changing certain frequencies, the game can change drastically. But D&D is also an oddball in that very few systems have as many separate subsystems of dice and resolution, and even ithas fewer than it used to.

    Anyway, that reminds me of the bit I think is most key with dice. Dice mechanics absolutely affect the feel of a game, if nothing else, based on their relationship to the range of character capability. If the range of potential randomization greatly outstrips the range of possible skills (or other modifiers) that feels very different from a game where the range of the skills outstrips the range of the dice.

    Specifically, let's say a game has skills that range from 1-5.
    In system 1, the mechanic is roll d20 and add the skill.
    In system 2, the mechanic is Multiply the skill times 5, and add 1d8.

    Clearly, in system 1, the randomizer vastly outstrips the skill, rendering differences in skill virtually meaningless.

    In system 2, Differences in skills are so profound that the system is only a smidge away from being diceless.

    While either of these solutions will appeal to a certain set of the gaming populace, I don't think either really has legs, so ideally the system shoots for a point between those two points looking at three axes: The modifiers, the range, and the curve.

    Modifiers are, collectively, all the things that influence the dice roll, skills usually being the most pronounced. The modifier may influence the roll (as a bonus or penalty) or it may determine the number of dice to be rolled, or even both.

    The range is simply the range of possible outcomes on the dice.

    The curve is the statistical curve the dice produce. Often a bell curve, young et some wacky shapes with the roll and keep games, but ultimately, the curve tends to serve one purpose: to allow a broad range, but still maintain consistent results. This can be a little odd at times. Because Fudge has a range of modifiers that is only about 6 steps, the fact that their dice can generate a range of 9 steps seems crazy.

    The reason I bring it up is that I think the cog neuro practice of examining cognitive processes by searching for patterns in the ways they break down under various stresses (lesions, chemical imbalances, &c.) strikes me as an interesting way of examining your "effects of dice mechanics" idea.

    I'm willing to accept for the sake of investigation that a basic, unperturbed dice mechanic does have some psychological effect, but how are you to determine that? You can't have a control case of a non-affecting dice mechanic.

    So you observe the effects that a constant perturbation has over different baselines, in order to compare those baselines. (Conveniently, unlike in cog neuro with humans, you have the ability to precisely control your "lesion.")

    Unfortunately, that's just an avenue for investigation, and I can't really provide conclusions based on it. But, maybe there are systems in the wild that have very similar perturbations, set on top of differing baselines?
