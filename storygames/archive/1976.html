From What Don't You Understand (about Gaming)?:
Posted By: Brand_RobinsWhat I don't get is, aside from a desperate need to feel that your choices are validated by everyone else liking exactly what you like, why the fuck is it so hard for RPers to honestly say, "Well heck, that's not what I like, but if it works for you then awesome!"
Okay, fess up. What parts of gaming out there are not what you like, but you have nothing but praise and encouragement for the folks who do pursue it?

For me, the Number One item is Immersion. It fascinates me that folks get so into the experience and into their characters that that constitutes the primary enjoyment of the game for them. And it totally, manifestly, profoundly doesn't work for me that way. But I love hearing about it, and I want to learn how to design for it better.

Also on my list:

The Hardcore Setting. Not "oh, we are hardcore setting junkies, grrr." No, I mean Warhammer and Rifts and the like. The settings that I swear had to be inked in testosterone. I so don't get it; playing it so doesn't interest me. But you know, I love hearing about some guy talking about killing vampires with his glitterboy.

Fantasy. Seriously, I think I have forgotten how to appreciate the fantasy genre any more. It doesn't compute. But I see that other folks dig it, so rock your bad selves out.

    As for me, I can totally see "Not what I like, but awesome", with one caveat: It's a very fine line between the honest sentiment that "I like hearing accounts of it even though it's not my bag" and the dishonest technique of pseudo-innocuously bringing up concepts that you don't like and taking them to task (iow, 'being patronizing or condescending'). No one's crossed that line yet, but I just wanted to throw that out there. Also, it would help if you are thorough in your reasoning behind why you think it's awesome, so people don't automatically assume that you're being condescending. Come up with stuff you don't like that you STILL think is Awesome, not things that "you just don't like" and make a hand-wavey gesture of saying something "nice" about it. Awesome is awesome, so tell us why.

    For me, here's some non-gaming stuff:
    Fantasy Football. I have no interest in the sport of football (the only sports I watch are the bizarre ones like Sumo, Badminton and Full-Contact Karate). I never participated in the "office pools" of yesteryear, but I've noticed that those office pools ('which team will win?') have been replaced by this deep, strategic social game of Fantasy Football. When I hear my coworkers talk about it, it seriously sounds like the exploits of two dudes playing Warhammer, complete with building their armies. But I have no idea who the big players or teams are, so I cannot participate (and I'm not interested enough to learn). But I do LOVE to hear tales of the flow of tactics and choices between the various folks in my office.

    Now, gaming stuff:
    * I'm with Jonathan on the face-stabby thing. For the folks wondering what J-Dogg was talking about, I think he's talking about those games where you are basically encouraged to be put into a situation where your character indulges in machinations against the other characters, usually in a way that involves eventual ruin and death, or where every conflict is high stakes. Like The Mountain Witch, Cold City, Polaris if you squint your eyes, Sorcerer (not by necessity, just by habit), AMBER, etc. It looks like Jonathan is looking for games that aren't so "big Big BIG" in terms of stakes, and don't implicitly encourage you to gak your fellow protagonists, even if the PLAYERS are totally down with that, going "Damn, dude, that was awesome the way you killed my dude's girlfriend and ruined his life, forcing him to commit suicide. That rocked!" Also, see "All of Luke Crane's convention demos" :-)

    I like high stakes fast-to-the-conflict play and character-gakking when everyone is on the same page. But not all the time. There's some folks out there that seem to love it all the time, and that seems to be the only way they play. Which again, rocks, because if you've played these types of games it takes some excellent skills to keep on your feet, come up with awesome ideas quickly, and also separate yourself from your character so you don't get beat up when your character does. But I don't like playing that type of game more than once in say, every five games or so. I tend to prefer games where the players are protagonists, and aside from key moments, tend to work loosely like a team.

    ... I'm trying to think of something else that I don't like that I think is awesome (and not just that "I don't like it") but I'm having trouble. I'll leave it there for now, and join in again when I eventually think of something else.

    As to why I would say, "I don't like it but think its awesome" and why I'd want to and why it isn't condescending -- because I'm a student of humanities, and I love the glorious diversity of humanity. I celebrate the fact that we are different, I revel in it, I embrace and enjoy it.

    I've spent a huge part of my life studying religions that I do not believe, but that does not mean I do not respect or admire them, that because I do not personally believe them means I think anything less about those that do. I can't think of anything that would have made my life poorer for having ignored, say Hinduism or Islam, or refusing to talk about them because they are not my religion, my ritual, or my little corner of the world. I do not believe that Brahman is Atman, but I can see the wonder and potential and beauty in the idea -- even though it is not my thing.

    In a much, much smaller and much, much less important way I think the same about the different ways people approach games and fun and telling stories. Just because they do something differently than I do does not mean that I cannot celebrate them, and just because I tell them that what they are doing is awesome does not mean I am patronizing them. I think the world, even just the pathetic little world of RPGs, is bigger than I am and I WANT IT TO BE BIGGER THAN I AM.

    I can think of nothing sadder or more pathetic than a world in which everyone played the way I play, or did only things that I thought were awesome for me. I cannot even conceive of why I would want to be so bound up in my own narrow paradigm that I was unable to see the value in people who do things differently than I do. And because I want there to be more than my philosophy, I will tell others that I can see the cool in what they do, even if it is not my cool. Beginning neglect and "'don't ask/don't tell" are not good enough if we want to actually support communities of difference rather than just communities of indifference.

    As for things that I see mass Awesome in that are not my thing:

    Immersion: I play with immersives a lot, and have talked with even more. I find the ability to subsume into character to be fascinating, and have found that if its supported with a play structure amenable to it that it can make the game experience much richer for everyone at the table. I, however, can't do it. I've tried, and I just can't do it. Nor do I think it would get me, personally, what I want out of game even if I could.

    Highly Constrained Games: I do like my games to have some limits, but if you give me an overly constrained game I will start to chafe under the limitations. However, I have seen that these games can work for a lot of people -- especially non-RPers getting into RP. The ability to have a very narrow focus, to zero in on and perfect one thing, to have a simple and straight forward mechanical play structure that allows for an easy mental model of how to do something (tell a story, have an adventure, whatever) can do amazing things to bring joy to those who don't need to be a free bird or a unique snowflake.

    Boffer LARPs: Once upon a time I was interested in these things. But I gained the same problem I had with MUSHes, they didn't give me story, and I am story's whore. But the way that people get into these things, the effort they put into costumes and latex weapons, to creating a full and fully interactive world of fantasy and physical martial contests is something to behold. I can totally see why people get into the dirt and the struggle and the joyfully whacking away at each other, trying really hard to land the killing blow.

    Con Games: Sorry guys, but con game are a mild form of diversion for me, a chance to maybe see something new in action lke a pony prancing before the judges. I don't get the emotional, personal connection from them that I get from my "real" games. But, I totally see the point of con games. Getting out there and trying new things, meeting new people, getting a chance to put a game through its paces, to do things your normal group might not want to... that's all awesome. It just doesn't work out for me as being any more fun than playing Snap. Of course, I'm more than willing to have my mind changed about this one.

    Just as a little follow-up and rationale for my thread here, over at NerdSoCal.com we are building a community. Members of that community come from many different places, and have different likes and dislikes. The goal of NerdSoCal is not to build up some giant edifice of consensus; it's to create a place where people who do agree and who enjoy the same things can connect and run off to do that thing. As the head cheerleader on that site, a great deal of what I do is welcoming people in and telling them that this is a place where they can let their hair down about their nerdy hobbies. So needless to say, there's all sorts of guys who are all into d20, or the techie brigade, or the Ren Faire folks, who are all into stuff that I'm just not. But that doesn't mean that I can't see the appeal or appreciate that it's a thing that brings them enjoyment. Maybe it's just something about me, but I can't help but stand up and cheer when somebody finds something that brings them joy. And that's the sort of vibe that I'm trying to build over there -- of welcoming and mutual respect, rather than indifferent tolerance.
