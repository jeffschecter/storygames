    Don't we have to choose a meaning for the word "story" before we can really answer that question? What is it and how is it created?

    In computer games, the relationship of user to designer allows a direct delivery of a scripted series of events that we can call a story. Some role-playing styles aspire to this as well, but few succeed. Others are interested in merely putting up challenges for the players to meet and to hell with overall continuation. And then there's people like me, who want to my play to generate a story that has some meaning for me.

    I think that both traditional and small press rpg designers come from both camps. I don't think the market or the business is big enough for eather to have the power to prescribe in the same way such a movement might with the huge electronic game industry.

  • Josh Roby
    November 2006
    Firstly, this is mostly all I see when I read that article: whinewhinewhinewhinewhine. If the games this guy likes aren't being published, he's got three productive options: (a) stop buying the other games, (b) get off his ass and build a better game, and (c) scratch his itch some other way, like German board gaming. This article does none of this or proposes any of it. It boils down to "the entire industry that has perfectly good reasons for doing what it's already doing should change everything to suit my preferences" which is total and absolute bullshit.

    Secondly, the guy totally overlooks one of the big reasons that story (properly narrative) is so huge in video games and every other media on the face of the planet: crossovers, licensing, and brand recognition. If a game has a story, it can have a sequel. It can have a prequel. It can have the characters on lunchboxes, backpacks, jackets, and wallets. You can make completely different games with those characters in it for no real reason, and have it sell (MarioKart, Dead or Alive Volleyball). You can spin off a movie. And all of those produce more revenue. There is no Tetris: the Movie. Nobody has Tetris blocks on their jackets. There are Tetris sequels, sure, but why do I need another incarnation of Tetris when I have the original? I get that the guy is concerned with his in-the-moment play experiences, but spouting off about the industry without considering, well, the industry is kind of stupid.

    His analysis of "story = reward" is really superficial. Stories in video games also provide context, motivation, ambiguity, color, and a ton of other valuable stuff. To say that story only ever makes you happy or excited for a few seconds is really underestimating the powerful uses that story can be put to.

    Overall, it seems to me that he's not criticising stories in games, he's criticizing poorly implemented stories in games. I think it's telling that he offers no examples of games that would have done better without stories, and the examples he cites of good games include Grand Theft Auto, which does indeed have a story involved, complete with cutscenes.
