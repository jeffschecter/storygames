\n
\n
\n I like playing pathetic characters. Normal, mundane people who would not normally put themselves in harm's way, but are pushed into it by circumstances and possibly rise up to the challenge.
\n
\nCharacters like that have seldom been as rewarding to play as I had felt, because often GMs (and other players) have failed to push me hard enough. Most people, hearing about some weird cult in their hometown, wont infiltrate it. Now if a loved one (spouse, friend, lover, child, etc) gets brainwashed by said cult, they might be interested in a more proactive decision, especially if the cops wont do anything (or are controlled by the cult).
\n
\nIn contrast, I often play very energetic, enthusiastic, idealistic characters, who will jump into the action and push other characters' buttons and allow his to be pushed. These characters are more often satisfying because they help the plot move along. It's definitely a safer choice, especially if I don't know the group that well.

    Posted By: Jumanji83I'm a coward, so when am I actually courageous, what conditions do I actually need to be courageous? I'm lazy, so what will push me into action?

    The opposite is also true. My character is courageous, but then, what does he fear? He's honorable to a fault, so what would make him lie and cheat?

    \nIs it okay to tell the other people at the table the answers to those questions (or at least what your best-guess, hypothetical-situation answers are), or do you get the charge out of deciding on the spot what the answers are?

    I could see it going either way:

    If you tell people, then you're essentially putting up some flags and pointing out what buttons people can push on your character. "Threaten his family," you say, "and he will set aside his cowardice immediately and jump across the table and try to shove all ten of his fingers through your eye sockets." You're inviting them to push those buttons over and over, but mostly you're doing a performance; you know approximately what your character will do, they know approximately what your character will do, and you're all having fun going along with that. Possibly the downside is that you may feel like a button is getting pushed too often and no one is as happy with the 30th iteration of the show as they were with the first time.

    If you don't tell people, you're basically making them guess. "He's a coward," you say, "but with the right motivation he would do anything he could to murder you; now, go find that motivation!" It's very dynamic -- even YOU don't know what will do it, you're just waiting for a moment that feels right -- and it's a lot of fun to just start with a powerful reaction and only go back to explain the why of it later. The downside here would seem to be that you're basically asking everyone at the table to do the hard work for you (keep trying new motivations, you haven't found the one that moves this character yet!), and if no one hits upon the magic combination that works for you, nothing happens. Their efforts are wasted, your desire is stymied, no one enjoys it.


    I tend (as I suspect most people would) to lie between those two extremes, although I'm definitely closer to the "tell everyone everything" pole. I can usually find enough novelty to inject into a performance to partly satisfy my desire for a dynamic "I'm not sure what I'll do next" feeling, and I just feel really shitty about making everyone else pixelbitch my character. I'd rather let them know what works on my character and try to sell the reaction as hard as I can than sit back and tell them "No, that wasn't it."

    \n In an Archipelago game, I played a girl who was completely zen. She didn't have to do anything. She was an uncarved block. She mostly just walked around and said true and obvious things to people she met. In that game, it worked great. I had my scenes, same as everyone else; I had my destiny points that other players wrote for me; I had NPCs around me, interacting with my character, in all my scenes.
    \n
    \nIn other games, that character would probably have been frustrating and boring, and I would have had to make her more active.

    \n Accounting for Taste: I work closer to the second, but I will provide clues into what might help, usually. Like, if I create an NPC that my character loves a lot, this means "here's a button you can and should push". But pushing it wont work every single time. What's interestring is to find out where the line lies.
    \n
    \nSo, I'm a coward. Can I be sprung into action when my mother is threatened by a mugger? Maybe. How about an angry mob? Say she's hiding and the mob are threatening to kill me if I don't give away her whereabouts? What if they promise not to kill her, just imprison her and rough her up a bit? Now it's unclear.
    \n
    \nIt's interesting to me even if I pick cowardice in the end, as long as the choice was tough.
