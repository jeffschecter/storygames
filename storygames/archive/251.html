On Dickery

Jason_Morningstar
February 2006 edited February 2006 in Story Games
Yesterday at the fantastic Troublicon, some of my peeps played and hated Once Upon A Time. "The rules say the ending only counts if everyone agrees that's cool" somebody said. Clinton called it an ultra dirty hippie game, which is pretty funny and accurate. The whole thing got me thinking about dickery.

You know dickery - some games address it explicitly and some don't. You can be a total dick and break any game, but it is generally assumed that nobody will tolerate playing with a dick more than once, so it is a non-issue in design, right? Or is it?

My question here is where to draw that line. In the Roach I address dickery obliquely, by adding an "I call bullshit" rule - but it only applies to narrating in NPCs, which I perceive as the place most open to unwitting abuse. The Roach is a game wide open to dickery and, as a result, very easy to ruin if that's what you want to do. I didn't even worry about it. I'm interested in your thoughts on dickery, and whether it should be addressed in design, and if so, how.

(and just to be clear, I'm totally OK with you guys hating OUAT! After all, I hate .. well, lots of things you think are the cat's pajamas!)

    Resisting dickery (great word!) has been a design goal of mine since I began writing Ends and Means. LARPs thrive on lots of players, but lots of players means it's all the more likely one will have a PHD in Dickery, and it really only takes one or two such players to run a LARP into the ground.

    My design resists dickery in a few ways:
    • It's a resource-based system in which winners pay losers, so if someone's messing with you, at least they're making you more powerful in the process.
    • It's fluid: if someone's messing with you this session, you can completely shuffle your numbers around so that next session they can't do it again the same way.
    • It's very stop-at-the-skin: it is impossible to do long-term damage to anyone's character without their explicit buy-in.
    • Character advancement reaches a plateau: long-term play doesn't make it too much easier to mess with others.

    I'll throw in some thoughts from board and card games here.

    So I personally dislike games that I feel amount to popularity contests. A great many multiplayer games do this, in my opinion. For example, in Illuminati you can spend down any other player at any time. I find that effectively, this comes down to the players voting which person they would like to win for personal reasons. I like better games where such interaction is restricted. In something like Settlers of Catan, you have relatively few options to stop a winning player. You can refuse to trade with them, but that hurts you as well as hurting them. In my opinion, better designs reduce the popularity effect, and also keep all players engaged and in the game with a chance of winning. If you make mistakes, you can lag behind but should still have a small chance of winning.

    I have seen the same thing in role-playing games. In particular, I have played in games with player-voted awards that became more like a popularity contest. Worse, I've seen vicious cycles where the rewards to the popular mean that newbies, shy players, and passive players are subtlely edged out of effective participation while they're still in the game. These are dangers that I worry about in my games. The leading players are being dicks to a degree, but they were also doing what the game rewarded: doing cool stuff and gaming the system.

    Recent popular designs vary in this. For example, Primetime Adventures has open social awards -- but Dogs in the Vineyard has no parallel. It very specifically rewards the mechanical options of Fallout / Taking the Blow. I like the latter because it has a clearly defined way to get a reward.

      In the original post about Once Upon a Time there was the glimmer of a really interesting observation: OUAT has a really incoherent ruleset. At once it says two contradictory things.

      1. This is a game. The object is to win. You can win by following these rules and applying them the best.
      2. You will only win by the consent of the other players, who also want to win.

There are games that actually are about winning, and the a win is acheived by "best" play, and there are games where there's a winner, but the winner is chosen essentially arbitrarily. Approaching either as the other is a receipe for a bad time.

      Honestly, I really tend to think of the latter, more hippy games as being more "activities." Because you play a game to win, and you participate in an activity just to have fun. The trappings of winning are largely unimportant.
