\n This is possibly a generalizable and (for me) important idea.

There was a specific, important event in the development of the Society of Dreamers where one player did something unexpected, which changed the way I played the game forever. It turned into a magical session. However, not all games I've played have had the same level of magic, even though I incorporated the new technique as a standard.

It wasn't the technique he used in itself. It was us listening and accepting and incorporating that technique, because it felt right at the time. We were a bit scared, we didn't know where it would go, and this heightened our awareness and the creative tension within the group.

Now, we all know about accepting contributions to the fiction. Say yes! Build on others' ideas! Etc.

Is it possible to develop a playstyle that is not about the specific toolbox of techniques you have, but about a philosophy of spontaneous incorporation of techniques? Of saying "yes" to others' mutation of the game you're currently playing? Not in a formalized way, but in a human and spontaneous way.

"The rules of this game will change, as all participants do things that fit. Follow each others' lead. Explore the new territory. Feel your way. Touch others. Find out who you are together."

    \n There is a magic that happens when you follow each other's leads. A moment where the things that you've created together blossom into something none of you could have imagined on your own. As though the creative world takes on a life of its own. That's what your story makes me think of, Matthijs.

    It can happen in fully structured games that are highly collaborative--Universalis for example often brings in many crazy, possibly unrelated elements, that all of a sudden click into place and the game gains a wholeness. Swords without Master often has this effect: the way you play has you add your ideas and combine them with others, so often, suddenly, something happens that grabs everyone's attention and becomes what the game is about for the rest of the session.

    Approaching a game with the intent to be open to what matters to the players, in the moment that they happen rather than determining them ahead of time, or knowing what structures will make it happen, makes you pay attention. And if the play is long term, there is a lot of opportunity for the world to organically take on shapes you'd not dreamed.

    Agreed, even beyond what rules you're using, the fact of listening to one another and playing off of that makes for amazing play. Sounds like a principled approach you're looking for.

    \n I have a suggested technique that might be adaptable for your use with a fairly regular group of players.

    It doesn't come from gaming though.

    The Party Poster

    Back in my college years and my early 20s, we used to throw some pretty decent, kinda raunchy house parties. Everyone would get goofy and no little bit chemically altered. Usually at some point, I'd end up doodling a little party chronicle of cartoons and snippets of converstions that had been particularly funny. Since lots of us had some interest in drawing or painting, there was usually a largish sized sketch pad around to use for it.

    It became enough of a habit for me that a few of my pals still keep those created that have survived as treasured possessions.

    For game purposes, you might try something similar.

    It really isn't an organized sort of thing. It's just an almost collage looking collection of funny, cool things that people liked in play. Snippets of dialogue. A quick note about something cool and fun ( not an explanatory paragraph, more like a one sentence description). Stick figure cartoons. Whatever.

    Put info about what happened down close to the time it happened, not later when the urge is to analize. and edit.

    Then just save it and add to it. Use a new sheet each session. Let anyone write on it, or hand it off t someone that's good at it.

    Then, occasionally, let people leaf through it, between or before sessions, to at as a bit of a reminder or to spark memory, or to let new players puzzle over and absorb the group culture by osmosis.

    Edit: Sheesh! Talk about unformed thoughts! I forgot to tell you why I thought this might be useful!
    1) It encourages a culture of sharing and recording things that other folks did that you thought were note-worthy and which happened spotaneously.
    2) It's a messy sort of record, open to all sorts of interpretation and retelling of events (not thefictional events, but the play group events)
    3) Because it'smessy, it is a bit confusing to the newcomer. Which, hopefully is good. It means they have their own initial interpretation, but then need to talk to an earlier participant to get the actual story behind the doodle or quote. Hopefully that more rapidly brings the newcomer into the group culture.
    4) It's a record of fits and starts and experiments and changes, none of which were intentional, but were ultimately considered important enough to record by someone in the group.

    \n I'm maybe thinking something like:

    We gather in a room. There's not much there - some paper, some writing utensils, some open space, some pillows&chairs&tables on the side. Everyone has the option of bringing stuff, and maybe they did: A hammer and nails, a Ouija board, a veil, a bottle of vodka, a painting of a woman with child. We sit around the table as we discuss before play.

    We talk about Playing With Intent (or whatever it's called). We lay out the basics:

    - We're here to explore. Our aim is to listen, to incorporate, to build, to listen more. To do what feels right, spontaneously.

    - These are (some of) our principles:\n
      \n
    • Be attentive! Listen to what the others are doing. Feel what the group is doing, see what the situation is, while playing.
      • \n
    • Stay with it! Don't chicken out on boredom, tension, pain or discomfort. Like massaging a hurt muscle until it warms up, go through it.
      • \n
    • Be in it! Be your character, be your description, be in the situation.
      • \n
    • Take action! Step in and do something. Your character can act. You can use techniques, give instructions, set scenes.
      • \n
    • Challenge! Make others feel alive by providing them with challenges and hurdles.
      • \n
    • See the real people! Help and challenge the actual players. Give them themes they might find interesting, provocative, hard. Scaffold them.
      • \n
    • Sense the spotlight! When someone's doing something cool, help them do it until it's done. When there's not a spotlight, feel free to grab it, or point it at someone.
      • \n
    \n- We have loads of roleplaying techniques we know. Let's mention some of them, write them down on a big poster. We put the poster on the wall.

    - We generate the seeds: A setting. A basic situation. Some characters.

    - We should expect the start to grate. Things can be slow and painful, but warming up we'll get into it. Stay with it!

    - We start out with a simple, but functional structure - round-robin scene framing, for example.

    - Further play is guided by the principles.

    \n What principles can be implemented to avoid silliness that derails the overall tone? In a setup like this, is tone important?

    How do we create the initial situation as a group? How do we elicit buy in? How do we negotiate different points of view?

    How do we avoid "survival of the fittest" in terms of the loudest / best / most social players gaining more authority or spotlight than everyone else?

    How do we manage pacing?

    Who plays the NPCs? Are there NPCs?

    How do we motivate characters? How do we build situations tied to those motivations?

    How do we build in the unexpected? Twists?

    How do we push when someone instincts say pull? Many naturally move away from drama; how do we short circuit our instincts?

    How do we build a safe space?

    How do we externalize intentions?

    How do we negotiate disagreements?

    How do we remember elements to reincorporate?

    How do we remember who everyone is and what's happening?

    How do we remember all the principles?

    \n In my experience, people without related specialized skills get confused if principles are vague or too numerous (often to avoid vagueness, you end up with too many principles so it's a tough balance).

    I suspect most people I know wouldn't know what to do with:

    - "Make others feel alive by providing them with challenges and hurdles."

    But I think these would work really well:

    - "When someone's doing something cool, help them do it until it's done."

    - "When there's not a spotlight, feel free to grab it, or point it at someone."

    As a GM, I often write principles like these for myself. Things like, "the person who's said the least goes next" and "if they don't want to go next, add a check next to their name and offer them extra spotlight later." I find that saying to myself "challenge the players" only draws blanks, mainly because it's a multiple step process. It's like saying, "build a car" except I don't have the parts, schematics, tools, or a garage.

    I really love how in Fiasco you can frame your own scene or let everyone else do it. Couple this with the person who didn't frame the scene gets to end it... pure brilliance! But the take away point is that someone who isn't good being put on the spot creatively has methods to use the group for inspiration and motivation.

    I've found the 3 most important principles to teach players of improv type games, more than say yes, is "listen more that you speak", "make decisions that entertain your audience, not just yourself" and "don't plan".

    \n JOHN TOO MANY QUESTIONS

    But I think you're highlighting something important (or at least making me think about it): What I want here is a basic attitude towards play. This is made up of a set of principles, probably, or a set of attitudes and reactions and internalized "rules". So it's a (meta) rule set. It can, in theory, be described; in practice, it's almost like a personality trait. Everyone knows what "being extroverted" means, but the more you look at it, the harder it is to describe in concrete, simple, non-ambiguous rules.

    I think the attitude of play I'm looking for probably needs to be painted in broad strokes.

    (Paulo and others who want to include more people: That's a great goal, but not my primary goal in game design. I would love everyone to play, but I don't have the patience to spend my time honing games that fit everyone and help them get better. I often make games that presuppose a whole lot, but I think I mostly presuppose specific attitudes, certain personality traits, and experience at being human - not specifically gaming experience).

    \n Edit: cross posted with Matthijs above.

    I want to be clear that I'm not against these ideas or against GM-less play (which usually isn't GM-less). I also don't want to throw monkey wrenches into the early brainstorming of new ideas. It's too easy to kill a baby before it has had a chance to grow into an adult, make mistakes, learn, and be able to defend itself!

    What I've noticed is that the players who tend to rock at this sort of play, don't even need explicit principles beyond the skills they already bring with them. I could simply say, "game of thrones, we're wildlings, the next 2 hours is the end of our story, and it will be sad" and bam... the next 2 hours are possibly some of the best roleplaying experiences I've ever had!

    So if these amazing players (who do exist, they aren't even fringe examples, I play LARPs and Call of Cthulhu with them every year at Gen Con) don't need principles, who are the principles for? Once we know who the intended players are, we can come up with the most useful principles for them.

    My concern over systems that don't have a GM(s), is that they don't compensate for or ignore a lot of what a GM brings to the table. As WarriorMonk says above, I've seen GMs constantly and repeatably take a table of shy and outgoing people, experience and inexperienced people, even people with different play styles... and get them working together to create beautiful experiences.

    All the questions I asked above are 100% serious. I want this sort of play that also addresses those questions. If I can create these magical moments with an assortment of people with minimal structure and distributed authority... I'm all for it! I'm excited for it. My money is practically already in your pocket (or whatever currency we want to exchange if any)!

    Again to be clear, the above questions aren't meant to be obstructionist. I think they are vital. I want this to succeed!

    \n I'm having difficulty latching onto a particular thread here, so I apologize for a bit of unincorporated thought, but here goes.
    \n
    \n1: Thinking about 'how' to do things tends to distract from creativity. If you are looking up rules or procedures you are in all likelihood activating the parts of your brain which are not going to help you be creative and spontaneous. The greatest value I find in 'ritual phrases' is that they have the ability to encapsulate a logical structure, but keep your creativity active. In the end it's like riding a bicycle; the more you think about how you are riding, the less capable you are. The trick is to silence the part of your brain which is thinking about what you are doing.
    \n
    \n2: GMs can provide an important leadership role, particularly when a group isn't gelling, but when groups are working well, focus on the GM's exceptionality should diminish. In best groups I have seen or participated in, the GM dissolved into a mere title that one of the players hardly admitted to holding, but in the worst groups, the GM almost always should have been providing stronger leadership and structure to coordinate the group. If you are looking to get your group to create something wonderful, it helps to have a skilled leader who knows when to interject and when not to. However, don't be fooled into thinking that you need an official GM for a player to exert powerful leadership.
    \n
    \n3: Try to defeat the 'play to win' mindset. Throughout the capitalist world, but particularly in the English-dominated parts of it, the idea of individuals pursuing their own interests to achieve the best for all is prevelant. Unfortunately, this doesn't tend to lead to good play experiences. Story-games have developed a slew of ideas around ideas of helping others to be awesome and failing in an awesome way. Replacing 'game' with 'play' is one way to help here, but so is backing off from mechanics which inspire 'winning' as an enticement to be creative. Well-functioning groups can usually handle the distractions of winning and even build on them to create better experiences, but if you are having difficulty consistently making the magic happen, this can be an important hinderance.
    \n
    \n4: Know what you are trying to do. I see story play as falling into a range between the points of games, improvizational acting, and storytelling. I shy away from games as they tend to have a strong bent toward 'winning', so most of my focus is on finding the right balance between acting and storytelling. Acting is immediate and visceral. Good LARP and Jeepform leans into this realm where the focus is on the interactive experience and emotional connection to the role. Storytelling is a bit more structured and cereberal. I don't think that the tools we have developed for cooperative storytelling are nearly as well understood or refined as we might like them to be, but this is the realm where we need to consider pacing and arcs and such and the overall shape of the story becomes much more important. While there are synnergies throughout this spectrum, there are conflicts as well and learning to manage those conflicts can be aided by understanding what sort of activity you are actually trying to accomplish.
    \n
    \nI find myself rambling a bit so I will cut myself off. I hope some of this is discussion worthy.

    \n I'm going to try to answer some of John's questions. Because... my geekish nature won't let me avoid a list of questions, I guess? And because I know John is a smart and serious guy, and I've learned from him before. So I trust I'll learn something in the attempt to answer.

    What principles can be implemented to avoid silliness that derails the overall tone? In a setup like this, is tone important?
    This is a general problem in play with no set authority. Giving everyone tools for saying "stop, too silly" works pretty well - like Archipelago's "Do it differently". Different groups will do different things (I mean, it's supposed to be an open gaming philosophy), but I do want to help those who aim for serious play. Startup is very important as well: A pre-game ritual, collective emotional experience or similar that makes everyone feel serious and bonded can help. (Like, sometimes, the banishing rituals of "Society of Dreamers").

    How do we create the initial situation as a group? How do we elicit buy in? How do we negotiate different points of view?
    This is covered pretty well by a lot of games. Collectively, as a group, we can assume that we have a bag of techniques. Maybe someone says "let's do an R-map", or someone says "let's use Anitas garnn\xf8ste" (Norwegian larp technique), or someone says "PC-NPC-PC triangles", or whatever. Or maybe someone starts describing in a soft voice how things are, and others silently nod. You know what I mean? I'm, as always in this thread, talking about a basic attitude towards play where people pick and try techniques deliberately, on the spur of the moment.

    Negotiating different points of view: This is where listening comes in, strongly. You must at all times be aware of others' reactions and ideas (as far as humanly possible). When you feel something start to grate, or get that feeling like a rope getting tighter, move more slowly. Look people in the eye. Let them take over. Do it in dialogue, in conversation, in interactive physical play. This way, it's not about my view or your view - it's about what happens when we interact, the synthesis that emerges. We do not stop and say "roll dice!" We pause and look at each other, waiting for a moment for the other to speak, willing to be surprised and follow that surprise.

    How do we avoid "survival of the fittest" in terms of the loudest / best / most social players gaining more authority or spotlight than everyone else?
    By spotlight techniques, probably. Making sure to move it around, to not grab it too much. Perhaps, even, phrases like "Shine on this person now".

    How do we manage pacing?
    I don't think we do. I think we are open to the experience as it unfolds, without expecting it to necessarily become a story. We can jump in time, if we want. But there's much to be said for unity of time and space - isn't that what they do in Greek drama? Or am I confused? (Well, of course I'm confused. It's what I do.)

    Who plays the NPCs? Are there NPCs?
    Anyone and everyone. This, again, depends on the choices we make about techniques. Maybe someone says "You be this guy", or "Let's have these two women meet and see their conversation". Maybe someone stands up, lights a candle, has a solliloquy in the voice of a minor character. Again, techniques chosen in the moment, to explore something specific that someone wants to explore.

    How do we motivate characters? How do we build situations tied to those motivations?
    A central conflict is always easy to latch on to. It's an age-old roleplaying tradition that works. We might create one to start with - someone says "let's have an ancient war between demons, that is creeping into the world we know", and someone else says "some of us are demons, but we don't know", and someone else says "and some of us believe we are, but might be wrong".

    How do we build in the unexpected? Twists?
    ? This one surprises me, and makes me wonder if I've been unclear (duh, obviously I've been unclear). When five people play in a room, using different techniques to explore an improvised narrative, it's going to be twisty all by itself. We actually need something to keep twistiness down a bit. A rule of thumb saying "one plot twist at a time, resolve before moving on" could be good.

    How do we push when someone instincts say pull? Many naturally move away from drama; how do we short circuit our instincts?
    We don't. We let them be in the moment and experience play as they like. In a balanced group of four or five people, this won't be a problem. With two or three, however, we might need to pick participants that are active and take initiative.

    How do we build a safe space?
    Good question! Answers are welcome. I've been in safe spaces, but how to create them? Starting rituals, showing people you trust them, being open, making sure you're in a closed room with no interruptions/interference, existing real-life relationships...

    How do we externalize intentions?
    Not sure what this means.

    How do we negotiate disagreements?
    See note above re "listening". We try to see disagreements before they appear, and try to create things together instead of based on one person's idea. We're ready to abandon our ideas anytime to accept whatever else is going on. Nobody has a strong, central vision. Everyone has a piece of a puzzle, and we don't know if we'll even get a coherent picture at the end. It doesn't matter, necessarily, that all pieces fit.

    How do we remember elements to reincorporate?

    We accept that some will be lost.

    How do we remember who everyone is and what's happening?
    Sometimes, we sit down and plan a little. In "Society of Dreamers", each improvised scene is separated from the next by a sit-down with a oujia board and some notes. Here, we can remind each other of things, ask questions. Little pauses in between the intensity, to help each other move on in the same direction.

    How do we remember all the principles?
    Put them on a poster on the wall. Keep them few, focus on the most important ones. Discuss them, with examples, before play.

    Thanks, John! I think this helped me, at least. I find that much of the above is my vision of Society of Dreamers, which has mutated/matured way much since the published text.

    \n Matthijs, thanks for tackling these questions. Lots of fruitful answers!

    By "externalize intentions", I mean making subtext transparent. Making the reasons for our words clear.

    It's usefulness depends on how we play. Are we mainly speaking in character, first person? Only third person? Do we go in and out of first and third person? Do we only say what our characters say? Do we vocalize unspoken thoughts? Do we somehow reveal the real meaning of our words?

    Why and when does this matter?

    Sometimes "always in first person" games (which may not apply to your games) can create unintentional communication obstacles. If I'm always talking as my character, the person I'm speaking to may not understand my intentions, especially since many of us are not professional actors and may lack the body language and voice tone techniques to help communicate our meaning. My character may say something sarcastically but the person I'm speaking to may take my words 100% truthfully. Which can be hilarious, but if hilarious isn't the goal, it can be a problem when the person I'm speaking to wants to build on what I've said and we're currently miscommunicating. This is where I usually add some way to externalize intentions to make it clear what's really happening.

    Matthijs, thanks again for answering the questions above. I have a lot of thoughts and experience creating safe spaces that I would like to brainstorm with you at some point.
