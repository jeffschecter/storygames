It's Story-Games book chat!

Barbara Tuchman: Anything. Fantasy lovers should read A Distant Mirror.

Dee Brown: Anything. Many would recommend Bury My Heart at Wounded Knee but my favorite is The Galvanized Yankees. I liked this book so much I stole a copy. Shelby Foote's Civil War trilogy is also fantastic if you are into this time period.

Kevin Kelley: Out of Control, for approachable and fascinating science. Also: Jared Diamond is gamerific.

Robert Hughes: The Fatal Shore. Epic and disturbing and overflowing with awesome.

Kate Marsden: On Sledge and Horseback to Outcast Siberian Lepers. ON SLEDGE AND FUCKING HORSEBACK TO OUTCAST SIBERIAN LEPERS.

Apsley Cherry-Gerard: The Worst Journey in the World. Upper-crust hard cases take on Antarctica and hunt Emperor penguin eggs. Amazing lunacy, beautifully written.

Norman Davies: Rising '44. A very comprehensive and endlessly readable account of the Warsaw Uprising.

Slavomir Rawicz: The Long Walk. So what if it is mostly fabricated - it's a great story of a Polish POW walking from Lake Baikal to fucking India.

    Yay, nonfiction!

    Ti Ping Tien Kwoh: The History of the Tiping Revolution Including A Narrative of the Author's Personal Adventures by Augustus Lindley (Lin Le), formerly Honorary Officer, Chung-Wang's Guards; Special Agent of the Ti-Ping General-in-Chief; and Late Commander of the "Loyal and Faithful Auxiliary Legion" (1866) -- a former British naval officer describes running goods and fighting pirates while assisting in China's pseudo-Christian Taiping Rebellion (1851-1864), including long tracts where he gives his own government hell for supporting the corrupt imperial government over their fellow Christians. Beijing Foreign Language Press just came out with a 2-volume hardcover slipcase edition with the original full color illustrations and a replica of Lindley's commission from the one of the head rebel generals. Holy crap is this an amazing piece of work.

    Steven Mithen's After The Ice: A Global Human History, 20,000 - 5,000 BC (2003) -- A terrific account of post-Ice Age humanity for the lay reader. Mithen creates an imaginary literary persona, John Lubbock, and describes him wandering around thousands of years ago. A great account of where we all came from and of hunter-gatherer and early agricultural societies in general.

    Christopher Dewdney's Acquainted With The Night: Excursions Through the World After Dark (2004) follows the hours from dusk until dawn, describing different aspects of nocturnal existence, from bats to ladies of the night to dreaming to lunacy.

    The Book of the Cosmos: Imagining the Universe from Heraclitus to Hawking, edited by Dennis Richard Danielson (2000), is a collection of short historical papers on cosmology. From the earliest discussions of the music of the spheres to modern discussions of dark matter and black holes, all by renowned thinkers and scientists.

    I'd suggest a good overview of contemporary China or contemporary Chinese religion, but there really isn't one.

    walter benjamin, the Arcades Project: an encyclopedic escavation of 19th century Paris and the origins of modern consumer society, with entries on topics like barricade fighting, fashion, Baudelaire, and the glass & iron arcades that are the precursors to shopping centers. Tragically unfinished (and long considered lost until a manuscript was relocated) as the author killed himself when he was seized by spanish customs when fleeing the nazi invasion of france.

    Christopher Alexander et al, A Pattern Language: contains hundreds of 'patterns' or design suggestions from the municipal level to architectural details- all aimed at creating livable places.

    Richard Rhodes, the Making of the Atomic Bomb: gripping history on an explosive crucial topic, touches on science & engineering, biography & personalities, and the politics of the race for the bomb.

    Rose Macaulay, Pleasure of Ruins a history of how people have been fascinated by ruins, overflowing with melancholy (someday I want to create a d&d dungeon based on the mood of this book)

    Holldobler and E.O.Wilson, the Ants amazing illustrations and all kinds of fascinating info on ants (as well as the diversity of life & sociobiology)

    E.M Cioran, A short history of decay morbid aphorisms and short essays wherein the meaning of life is punctured in limpid prose

    James Buchan Frozen Desire the most interesting, readable book about the history and meaning of money that I have read (try Simmel's The Philosophy of MOney for a much denser look at the same topic)

    Eduardo Galeano Memory of Fire trilogy a history of the Americas through the end of the 20th century, written as a series of short events and stories, with the author adding fictionalized details and color that bring the facts to life

    David Cannadine the Decline and Fall of the British Aristocracy surrounds the topic from multiple angles, showing deaths in the great war, declines in land holdings and seats in parliament, through a combination of stories and figures (almost makes you feel sorry for the subject at hand)

    Mike Davis, City of Quartz overly pessimistic, but still a fascinating analysis of late 20th cen los angeles, and one of the reasons I was glad to have a chance to move out here

    West of Jesus: Surfing, Science, and the Origins of Belief by Steven Kotler: The classic surf quest adventure that tries to explain away the classic surf quest. The author starts out trying to find the origin of a surf story about the Conductor who controls the waves with a human bone, but he finds something much deeper.

    Mountaineering: The Freedom of the Hills: Could be considered the text on Mountaineering and backcountry travel.

    Expediton Canoeing by Cliff Jacobson: The text on long distance canoe trips taken in remote locations. Heading to an Arctic river? Read this book.

    Beyond Backpacking by Ray Jardine: An expanded version of Ray Jardine's lightweight backpacking philosophy. The influence that this book has had on wilderness travel and the outdoor industry is just beginning to be felt.

    Hope Against Hope, by Nadezhda Mandelstam. The wife of Russian poet Osip Mandelstam writing about the years directly preceding and surrounding her husband's imprisonment, mysterious release, re-imprisonment and eventual death in a Siberian labour camp. The title is a pun on the meaning of the author's name (Nadezhda = 'Hope', approx.) Osip Mandelstam is an incredible poet, but this book is easily as illuminating as his poetry, especially to a non-Russian reader like myself -- I haven't read a ton of Russian history, but nothing else seems to come close to explaining what it was like to live as an artist in Stalinist Russia.

    Essays, George Orwell. 1984 sucks, but that doesn't mean Orwell isn't worth reading. It's all about the essays.

    Fearful Symmetry, Northrop Frye. The first serious literary criticism of William Blake, and in my opinion more enjoyable than actually reading most of Blake's poetry. Blake's symbolism and philosophy -- often confused for or passed off as 'mysticism' by scholars and readers alike -- are explained and elaborated, and it turns out that they're completely fascinating. Angels and Devils -- and not the kind you know -- engage in a war to remind human beings that they themselves are the only true God, fallen from Eden through the denial of Imagination. "Drive your cart and your plow over the bones of the dead"!

    First, a few "yeah, I agree" comments.

    Yes, Godel, Escher, Bach is pretentious, but it also a great, interesting read on a valuable topic.

    Jared Diamond's books Guns, Germs and Steel and Collapse are essential to anyone who gives a hoot about verisimilitude in setting. The former is a bit more enjoyable, but the latter is probably a bit more relevant to the sort of settings that RPG designers are interested in. Screw it, read 'em both. You won't regret it.

    Goldman's Adventures in the Screen Trade is a barrel of monkeys wrapped inside some great commentary about how to create an engaging story. The sequel, Which Lie Did I Tell was more fun for me, simply because I was familiar with more of the movies he talks about.

    Fermat's Last Theorem is amazingly interesting, since the story of how a mathematical mystery was solved seems like it should be dry as a bone. SImon Singh is just an amazing storyteller, though. His book on cryptography, The Code Book, is also great, but probably not useful to RPG design or play.

    As for original recommendation....

    Anyone designing a contemporary spy game that hasn't read Spycatcher by Peter Wright is just hurting themselves. There's a lot of great non-fiction about the worldwide intelligence community, but this is the best one. Robert Baer's See No Evil and James Bamford's Body of Secrets (possibly in conjunction with the older The Puzzle Palace) are also very important.

    All the President's Men is the story of how Bob Woodward (in his pre-sellout days) and Carl Bernstein brought down Richard Nixon, from the horses' mouths.Sounds like it should be boring a hell, but it's one of the most interesting books I've ever read.

    The Dreams Our Stuff is Made of by Thomas Disch. The best critical survey of science fiction literature I've ever read, from one of the leading lights of The New Wave. It has a little dish on some of the authors, too, which may make it more fun for fanboys Not quite a deconstruction, but very useful to understanding the tropes of the genre on a less superficial level.

    Song of the Dodo by David Quammen is an entertaining and slightly heartbreaking introduction to the ecological theory of Island Biogeography. After your read it, you won't look at a Monster Manual the same way again.

    1867 a history of how Canada was born out of deal making, backstabbing, and a lot of beer and champagne. An object lesson in how the birth of a nation can be fascinating even when there is no war involved. (not that I'm biased or anything

    There's so many more, but this is a good start. I've been on a non-fiction binge more or less since I graduated from university (many many moons ago), and frankly a lot of great stuff has gotten lost in the blur of memory.
