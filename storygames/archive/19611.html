    \n I love Quade diagrams. Those are charts used in Mutant City Blues to help players investigate superpowered crimes, but do so in a logical, playable manner. It connects all the possible mutant powers together in a way so the players can make informed, meaningful choices and speculation as they play.I love this technique very, very much. I love it so much that I wrote a game all about how great Quade diagrams are for investigating mysteries.

    \n I almost never play one shots or conventions, but we do enjoy props from time to time. I've used puzzle boxes, mazes, cryptography puzzles, games, various miniature elements (even a couple working traps to be disarmed), or simply requiring each player to have a single character item on them for immersion.

    Handouts of some form are in almost every game...scrolls, riddles, maps, announcements, puzzles, clues, gossip, etc.

    \n For a Dark Matter game, I used a corkboard with pictures and clippings representing various conspiracy threads pinned to it and connected with string. The players modified/added to it throughout the course of the campaign.

    Those full-color hand-outs from the armitage files are basically props, and were delightful for the first few sessions. It's hard to make it work in the long run, though, because the adventure doesn't really point anywhere.

    \n One time we were playing a Gundam game, using Chris Perrin's Mecha rules.

    All of the players made a characters the week before. So I went over everyone's character sheets with neon gel pens. I typically play music during my games. But when they went into space to fight- I turned on nothing but ambient spacey battle sfx, cut off the lights, and made them work under blacklight with glowsticks.

    The look on their face was priceless, and every time they were about to go to space they'd get excited and nervous as if they were really going into battle. It was great.

    \n I should probably also mention the puppet... I used a puppet in a few games to represent the lead character, while everyone else played supporting characters: it began life as the 10th Doctor but with a costume change and a sewn on beard it became Blackadder the 2nd.

    Do chairs count? I've used the actual chairs the players were sitting in as props in the game: the characters were in a busy bar/nightclub so I provided only half as many chairs as there were players and characters could engage in a conflict to get another character to give up their chair.

    \n Played our first game of John Harper's Bootleggers last week. The opening scene had the gang arriving at night at a distillery to pick up two barrels of Canadian whiskey. The parked their Model T by the loading dock, and I produced a flask and handed it to one of the players, saying, "Holly, you pull out your flask." She then took a sip of whiskey and passed it around the table.

    Half an hour later, four NPCs were dead and the PCs were on the road in two trucks loaded up with 10 barrels of whiskey, as the distillery burned behind them.

    We also used paper money that session, which was a great way to reinforce the economics of the game.
