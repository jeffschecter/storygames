\nMap of house is a solo-game by Jackson Tegu that was designed for the Solitaire Challenge competition (held here I guess). I discovered it in the second issue of Playground Magazine. It's a short game (1 page) that invites you to write a map of a house you've inhabited in when you were a child, then place obstacles to overcome in the rooms, that reflect real-life problems. Then, through your imagination, you enter the house, defeat the foes, collect items after each victory that you might use for the next fight.
It's played in your imagination only and rules are to be bent if it allows a more interesting game.

I've begun an interesting Mooc about Gamification (not sure if it's still possible to join in, I'm following the course but it's already finished) where I was asked as an assignment to play three games and assess what were their respective goals, challenges and rules. Map of house was one of the game I picked.

I put on some introspective music to establish the right mood (I chose videogame Dear Esther soundtrack : melancolic beautiful music, mostly cello and piano) then began to draw a map of the house I lived in most of my life and the totality of my childhood. I enjoyed this part. I used to draw but stopped a while ago by lack of motivation. The game induced me in doing it again. I then created various monsters that I draw a different color. Each one reflected something that causes me difficulty (for instance 'tiredness' or 'lack of focus'). Then I laid down in my hammock and envisioned myself entering with a screwdriver...

I won't further into the details of the journey which is personal on some aspects. But I really enjoyed the game. It was a bit like a dream, induced me to remember what my childhood house looked like and allowed me to be inventive with the story. I didn't plan anything ahead and I think it's a good way to play the game. One action after the other and let oneself to be surprised by one's imagination.

I've the map created on my desk, along a second one that I played later in my current day flat. The second run was played much quicker since I knew the game. It was less surprising (I used the same monsters). But I spent more time on the drawings. I will give it an other try in my first apartment mostly for the pleasure of remembering (a skill I'm usely not very good at) and producing a picture.

The game can be found at page 52 of the second issue of Playground Magazine and all issues can be downloaded for free.
