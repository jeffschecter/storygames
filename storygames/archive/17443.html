    \n #2: Yeah, the text seems to imply that any damage severe enough to be locked requires intrusive measures to correct. Sort of a genre issue, I think, injecting some of the cyberpunk flavor in there and showing what sort of toll is taken by the lifestyle the characters lead. With some severe psychosis maybe you have hardware installed that triggers a release of chemicals in your brain to counteract it or something. Personally I'd probably just work with the fiction and the players towards a fitting solution, whether it required actual object installation or not.

    #3: My impression here is that vector is king. Determine who the helper is acting against and that should lead you to your opposed reaction rating. Remember too that you're not rolling against the Rig so much as the operator. The operator being backed up by the scale of the Rig is what requires you to band together and help each other or find some other plausible vector. A character and a Rig operate at such different scales in the fiction that special vectors are required to have any effect at all. So unless you've got a plausible vector you're unlikely to be able to produce a helping adjective by acting directly against the Rig operator.

    #4: Not as far as I know, which seems like an oversight to me. I mean, I can figure some things out but it would have been nice to have a little direction for how the roles tie into the Mechnoir milieu. Like, why are Priests so important that they're one of the training programs? Where do Marshals fit in to the setting? And so on. The exposition on the last page of the pdf gives some insight, but there should probably be some explicit text as well regarding the training programs.

    #5: What I've been doing when a Connection is part of the mission seed is basically ignore page 58, since it doesn't cover that scenario. The procedure the text gives is meant to help you add depth to the Plot Map, but if that Connection is part of the mission seed then it's already integrated into the Plot Map. So I allow the two favors and usually don't draw any more lines to nodes. If something inspires me I'll draw or change whatever lines I want. The mission seeds and Plot Map are meant to be guidance and inspiration, not a straight jacket you have to maneuver within.

    Hope some of that helps!

    \n I think you're tying yourself in knots because you're not leading with the fiction. You don't have to band together for mechanical purposes. I mean, only one adjective from a helper is all that's technically needed in that regard, right? And even if it wasn't you're not likely to have enough Push dice at your disposal to take advantage of more than three or so helping adjectives at a time anyway. You band together because the fiction requires you to do so.

    So, vector and scale. In regular Technoir if you're standing in the street and some dude is coming at you in a large truck, and you want to stop him, you've got a few options. Maybe you shoot the driver through the windshield or you shoot out the tires. One thing you very likely can't do is grab the truck and throw it, right? That's just beyond what you can do because of the difference in scale between your character and the truck. It's not something you can do in the fiction of the game, so you can't even roll to try it. But if you were in a bulldozer you would then have a vector to go head on with that truck, because you'd then be operating on a similar scale in the fiction.

    Mechnoir works the same way, but instead of a truck coming at you there's a ginormous mech. How do you stop it? What, to you and the rest of the group, sounds like plausible options? Do those options sound like they'd require a bunch of people to work together to succeed based upon how you see mechs in the fiction? If so then you need help, however much you think makes sense based on the fiction, and those helpers need to try and create fleeting positive adjectives to show that they're actually helping. If, like in my radio and leverage example in my post above, the PC is in a fictional position that doesn't require assistance, then no help is needed.

    As to the opposition for a roll, once again, look to the fiction. Maybe I take a dozen people and create a distraction by giving the rigger another target to worry about. Then I'd probably be making a Coax roll against the rigger in an attempt to create a negative fleeting distracted adjective on the rigger. Why a dozen people? Because that's about the minimum number we decided would be needed for that action, right then during play based on our understanding of the scale of mechs and the ongoing fiction.

    When it comes to helping remember that helpers have to create fleeting positive tags for another PC. Since you can only create tags for your target a helper's target has to be the PC they're trying to help. Maybe I'm a Marshal and I'm helping someone by calling in a drone airstrike, giving them the positive fleeting supported adjective. No roll required as the person I'm helping isn't opposing my efforts. Maybe to get access to the drones for the airstrike I had to go through a Coax contest with someone first. But once I've won access through that contention I can just apply the positive fleeting adjective on the PC I'm helping based on my ability in the fiction to now call an airstrike. Of course you might decide that the drone airstrike is something at a scale similar to a mech and the Marshal can have an Operate, or maybe Shoot, contention versus the rigger to put a negative bombarded adjective on the rigger. That's for you to decide.

    Technoir definitely has a different approach and edge cases can crop up during play. Just try and remember to lead with the fiction, that you can only place adjectives on your target, and that contentions only happen between characters. That should get you through most of the difficult spots that come up.

Likewise, there's sabotage in the fiction, and "sabotage" in the mechanics where the players "roll to apply a negative adjective to the rig". While, by the rules, you have to get help (in the form of fleeting positive adjectives) in proportion to whatever makes sense in the fiction to bridge the scale gap between the PCs and a rig, doing so isn't particularly helpful mechanically.     \nI'll just reiterate something from my last post here, that being that you can only apply an adjective to the target of your roll. So there's no way, by the rules, for a roll against the rigger to apply a positive adjective to a PC. The target of your roll would have to be the PC for whatever action you're taking. Which that player could then decline to oppose, making the application of the adjective automatic.

    As far as I can tell, the "banding together" rule only exists to engender a certain type of fiction. It's a genre emulation rule basically. Like you said, nothing is going to break if you ignore it, as long as you make sure the actions the PCs are taking against a rig/rigger make sense given the differences in scale and the vectors involved.
