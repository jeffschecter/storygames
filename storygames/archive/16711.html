    \n Murgh, it is a structured freeform game. The setup owes much to Fiasco - in collaboration you build a planet and colony, both in crisis, and a bunch of characters (everyone plays two and there is no GM), also in crisis. Every character has something they have sworn never to do, like "tolerate disrespect" or "abandon my work here". These oaths get broken in play with powerful consequences.

    In play you take turns asking questions (inspired directly by Microscope) and discovering the answers through the roleplaying of your friends. When there is uncertainty, you resolve it guided by a very simple die mechanic that tells your friends what drive is dominant in resolution - savagery, servility, or something unique to your colony like a need for freedom.

    That's Durance!

    \n We thought really hard about this, and honestly it is an experiment for us. I don't think we'll know if we'll do it again until the last package gets mailed. Ask me at Gen Con.

    Setting an initial goal that is immediately reachable is a best practice. There's business and psychology behind it that I don't personally understand, but it obviously works. Maybe Sage can explain it!

    We're betting that the roughly 10% Kickstarter and Amazon take will be less than the additional attention, exposure and commitment we'll get for the project. There's no way to know for sure but I feel like that's true in this case. Right now 15.5% of dollars contributed arrive via some search within Kickstarter. Not all of those are completely new discoverers, but some are.

    6.17% of dollars contributed come via Story Games!

    \n Setting a reachable goal is a great idea, especially if you're in the space that Bully Pulpit and Sage Kobold are in, where you're asking for help producing a game with a finished design. It means that you get to the "this will be happening" stag sooner. I'm sure there's more complex reasoning, but that's what Adam and I said.

    However, I think it kind of bit us on the rear a bit. We shot past the goal so fast that now we have this expectation of stretch goals that's a little intense. Don't get me wrong, we're excited to be giving people more stuff and to have the money to make this the best project possible, but it's been an extra demand on our time and resources. In retrospect I probably would have aimed higher just so we weren't at 1000% quite so fast.

    Kickstarter as a form of advertising has been great and well worth their cut. Also having the tools to really track pledges and get feedback is amazing.

    At an even more fundamental level, I'm pretty sure the first few hours would have crashed my website. The traffic was intense.

    \n Yeah, whether it was coincidence or not, the BPG site was unresponsive for the first 30 minutes after we launched the project. My guess is that it was traffic from the Kickstarter "new projects" spotlight and from Jason's announcement on Google+

    The main reason that we decided to experiment with Kickstarter was to have a framework for inviting collaboration from people interested in the game. We found with Fiasco that players had great ideas and ways of interpreting the game that made it better, and we ended up putting a lot of the that in the Companion a year later. We wanted to shortcut that and get people's input now, before it's finished. We always do that with playtesters, but Kickstarter gives us the chance to let people literally invest in the success and quality of the game. We wanted to see how that would work.

    It's also an experiment in spending more money up front than we might usually- buying more and better art, doing a larger print run, going full color. We can take those risks because we know exactly how many books to print, and what those customers want out of it. Like Sage said, we knew we'd publish the game, but I think without Kickstarter it would be a much smaller release. We'll see if the experiment succeeds!

    \n Hey Jason: I had a followup question as well, re the "Why Kickstarter" thing above. It's actually general for many folks doing kickstarter, but I wagered I'd ask it here than in a new thread.

    ...Why is there a need to keep coming up with further stretch goal reward tiers?

    For example, if you blow past your original goal exceedingly fast, and think "Hey, you know, there's this thing I always wanted to do but didn't think it would be practical. But with X dollars I think it would totally work. Since there's far more interest than I expected, let's go for it...", then I can see that.

    But it seems that recently there's this thing where folks feel the need to keep coming up with more and more stretch goals after their initial goals and all conceivable tiers are met. But that results in extra work, without necessarily a real pool of money to meet that (PDF money and higher donations aside, all the tiers are priced pretty much at cost, meaning that it's not just extra cash, it's all tied down).

    ...why not just work until all early conceivable goals are met, and then just coast for the rest of the Kickstarter run?

    -Andy

    \n Andy that's a good question, and I'd ask it of Sage and Adam who are really going the extra mile. part of it is obviously that you can use incentives to make more money (at least a little) and get cool things done. In our case we made the decision not to add potential goals that involved us physically doing stuff (like flying to your town), because we didn't want to do that. We had conversations with our artists and they were totally game for earning some extra money, so that makes perfect sense as an additional goal in which everybody wins. We have other things lined up that are things we want to do anyway but that are really cool. I hope we get to roll some of those out. I'm probably missing some things too.

    TLDR there's no reason not to coast, to be happy with whatever level of support you get for the amount of effort you want to put in.
