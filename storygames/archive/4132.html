\n Let me try something quickly. If you know where I'm going with this, don't answer.

Here's a story:

A thief makes his way across the rooftops, completely silently, keeping to the shadows. He creeps to a window and, using a diamond, cuts through the glass. He reaches through and opens it. Once in, he turns: and sees a woman with something shining on a desk behind her.

Could you tell me:

1. What you naturally expect will happen next in the story?
2. What you would like to happen next?
3. The coolest, cleverest thing you can think up to happen next?

Keep answers to a sentence or so, if that's OK: it's about the immediate thing that happens next.

    \n So, basically, I wanted to test out an improvisation theory. There's a full explanation on my journal.

    The theory predicts that the answers to 1 and 2 will be natural extensions to the story: good things to say at the gaming table.

    It also predicts that the answers to 3 will be bad things to say at the gaming table: overembellished, laser-sharking ideas that seem cool but which would actually derail the story.

    Basically, it says that your first instinct is the best one to go with: if you try to be clever, it's not as good.

    It's an interesting theory. Looking at the answers, there's some truth to it (although, unfortunately, some of you are such good storytellers that your cool ideas are, actually, cool).

    \n David Mamet, in "On Directing Film" (his book of screenwriting & directing advice) and "True and False" (his book of acting advice),
    proposes that actors, writers, and directors are motivated by a desire to impress-- to be well-liked.

    And this motivation will lead to certain pitfalls:

    The first potential pitfall of trying to Impress; is that the mind will chose a "safe" solution, by copycatting that-which has been seen, and which 'worked', before. Clich\xc3\xa9s from TV shows & movies you admire. To remedy this, Mamet proposes holding-off until a 2nd or 3rd idea suggests itself. This is at-odds with the Impro advice Graham advocates.

    Mamet's second pitfall is this: Given a chance to choose What Happens Next in a story, the mind will race to devise something "interesting". (Unexpected, clever, flashy, unique.) The problem with this-- is that the actual goal should be-- to find a solution that is ESSENTIAL to the story. Not novelty-for-its'-own-sake.

    As an example, Mamet rejects a scene in which the concept "a student wants to impress a teacher" is depicted via the student leaping-up to catch the teacher's prosthetic arm, when it falls off in class. Again, Mamet suggests waiting for more ideas, and choosing the un-interesting one; which gets the job done minus grandstanding. This is partially at-odds with Graham's Impro philosophy, because it relies on multiple suggestions. But partly in sync, because a simple, direct, even clich\xc3\xa9d solution is prized.

    In both the above cases, Mamet is talking about pre-production: when the writer, director, and actor have heaps of time to weigh their choices and choose wisely.

    But in the crunch of live performance, when the scene is in-progress and even a scripted performance has an improvised aspect-- Mamet's advice is more fully in-sync with Impro & Graham. That's when Mamet advises that artists shouldn't try to eliminate their anxious confused stagefright fear. Because that would lead to hesitation which kills live spontenaity. Rather, he proposes that the artist should ride-along at the speed of one's bad feelings and incorporate the negative junk into the energy of the onscreen/onstage fiction. To avoid further hestitation distractions, Mamet advocates choosing the most direct, obvious, simple solutions available when last-second choices appear.

    I see two ways to use Mamet's advice in games. The first is to use the not-quite-real-time aspect of play to entertain a few choices before racing into an overly 'safe' or overly 'interesting' solution that presents-itelf immediately.

    The second way is to embrace the almost-realtime aspect of play, and not slow things down in a futile search for the 'perfect' solution, when a workable option is already available.

    Either of these two mutually-exclusive ways could be more desirable, depending on the nature of the particular game, or the style of play preferred.
