    \n I'm not familiar with this product, although I know what the Encounters are like in general (and agree with Christopher that this is basically what they're supposed to be like). I'll offer an idea that I've been musing on in relation to 4th edition:

    I might be more interested in 4th edition D&D if the players had more say in the strategic-level setup of the individual skirmish combat encounters. You could maybe, depending on the specific nature of this adventure, attempt to inject a little bit of that into the proceedings. For example, if there is a common monster that shows up regularly in the encounters, perhaps let the players have one encounter with them and after that leave it more up to them to determine how many of these beasts they can beat in a single encounter; this might make it more interesting for the players, as they'd have to gauge their capabilities against the monster. You could achieve this "bidding" conceit in setting up the game in different ways, depending on what the adventure is about exactly: maybe there's a choice of roads where on one road you encounter 5 monster As and 1 B leading them, while on the other there's just 10 monster As, and the players get to choose which they want to face. Or maybe they can besiege the enemy at the cost of 100 gp per day, and each day spent on the siege causes one enemy to surrender, so it's up to them how much time and money they waste before they decide that they're strong enough for the fight. That sort of thing.

    I guess almost anything I might suggest regarding expanding the metaplot into actually functional strategic concerns would necessarily mean a lot of work in rebuilding the adventure, as you'd need to prepare for when the players do something differently from the assumed plot railroad.

    Perhaps the most realistic approaches to making 4th edition enjoyable without entirely rebuilding its high-order structures are to either give to some dullards to play (somebody who'll enjoy enacting the mechanics for their own sake), or give the players some personal psychological stake in succeeding in individual skirmishes as challenges to their tactical wit (often requires something of the sort I outlined above; players who are being railroaded from encounter to encounter rarely make that personal commitment to victory in my experience), or make it a storytelling game where you focus on letting the players talk about how their characters do the things they're going to do anyway. I've seen all of these approaches enjoy some success, although that last one is probably the most common: narrate engagingly, let the players participate in coloring in the fictional details, smile benevolently and support the coolness of their characters when the players make initiatives about establishing their characters as cool, and in general enable the players to enjoy the railroad in style.

    An example of what not to do, from the first time I played 4th edition D&D: my character was a dwarf, a bit of a jerk and a racist, from a clan that used kobolds and assorted other "animals" in their cuisine; he was dreaming of starting a restaurant business, and had an endless litany of behaviorist psychological doctrine to back up his insistence that his chosen prey animals weren't sapient. The GM reacted to my gallows humor somewhat hostilely because it wasn't clean-brow enough for him, so instead of supporting my concept he insisted on generally causing mundane trouble for my dwarf when an opportunity came up. This led into game time being wasted in e.g. bickering about door repair costs with a tavern owner, and other such highlights of play. Naturally this frustrated me somewhat as a player, and made me enjoy the "proceedings" less: I was forced to choose between entertaining myself and others with the antics of my dwarf, and letting the boring plot railroad proceed unhindered, with myself essentially reduced into a dice-rolling automaton.

    If you can avoid that mentality and instead back the players up, whatever kinds of heroes they envision they'd like to play, I'm sure that you'll have a much better chance of getting into the groove with 4th edition style game. Just let the players immerse themselves in their carneval, whether dramatic or comedic or whatever, throw them some scenes, and you'll probably find that as long as you let them play their characters color-wise however they want, they'll generally follow your plot wherever it needs to go. Combats can be run in a light-hearted manner, as opportunity for showcasing character personalities and varied monsters.

    \n Encounters are a very specific subset of modules - they're meant for people who haven't played D&D before to get a taste of the action of the current edition in a fast, digestible way. For 4e they were each 30-45 minutes long, and yes, continuity MUST be bad for this because you don't know who's going to be in the next session.

    Since D&D (at least Advanced 2e and onwards) is an action game, they don't want to fuck around with anything else.

    Similarly for the desire to have more player input into the setup of set pieces - Encounters are almost entirely set up explicitly for people who wouldn't know how to do that and probably wouldn't want to. Encounters is really a top-notch, highly-focused marketing thing and maybe the first really new innovation in adventure writing in a long time. Releasing them for an edition not out yet is brilliant - but of course playing them when you already have a D&D experience you desire is not going to give you the experience you want. WIthout knowing more about what the OP wants, I would hesitate to answer.

    \n Okay, I run the Encounters at my local game shop. I see people here telling me they think 4E was BadWrongFun, and that's okay -- it's an opinion. 4E is never going to be my first choice, but I know tactical people like it a lot.

    Anyway, Scourge is written for 5E, which is being designed to attract back players of all editions of D&D. It has some of the 4E mechanics people liked, and a lot of customization options, like 3.5, but it's also got a lot of old-school feel. Anyway, I'm liking it as a story-telling role-playing game -- they've used some story-game ideas, like tying skills to backgrounds instead of character classes, and mixing up the options a lot more so that you can combine class features you like in interesting ways.

    Making the game interesting in D&D Next is a lot like making a game interesting in any other RPG -- it requires an investment from the DM and from the players. When I run, I use the agenda from Apocalypse World: be a fan of the player characters, make their lives interesting, and ask questions and use the results. The module tells you the core plotline of who's doing what and to whom; you can turn those into DW-style adventure fronts.

    The Red Wizards (Cabal)
    Impulse: to absorb those in power, to grow
    Grim Portents
    \u2022\tThe Thayans construct Bloodgate Keep
    \u2022\tThe Red Wizards construct the gate
    \u2022\tThe Thayans bring their elemental creatures to Bloodgate Keep
    \u2022\tThe Red Wizards seize control of Waterdeep and the Sword Coast
    Impending Doom: Usurpation

    Baazka (Demon Prince)
    Impulse: to open the gates of Hell
    Grim Portents
    \u2022\tBaazka uses an animal to spy
    \u2022\tBaazka possesses an evil humanoid leader
    \u2022\tBaazka influences a noble
    \u2022\tBaazka is freed from the gate
    Impending Doom: Destruction

    The adventure locales are suggestions. Feel free to reskin them the way that you need to fit the actions of the party.
